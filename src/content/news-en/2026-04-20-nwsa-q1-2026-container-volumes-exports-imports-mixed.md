<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>Northwest Seaport Alliance (NWSA)</strong> reported a mixed start to 2026, with full exports rising 1.8 per cent in Q1, marking a <strong>second consecutive month of growth</strong>. Full imports fell 18 per cent against a strong Q1 2025 comparison period, driven by cargo frontloading ahead of anticipated tariffs. Year-to-date (YTD) total container volumes, including international and domestic traffic, reached <strong>714,719 TEUs</strong>, down 14.2 per cent.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">March 2026 volumes totalled <strong>278,829 TEUs</strong>, a 10.1 per cent year-on-year (YoY) decline. Full imports dropped 9.1 per cent, while full exports edged up 1.2 per cent. <strong>Domestic container volumes</strong> showed marginal growth of 0.2 per cent YTD, with Alaska up 0.3 per cent and Hawaii down 0.4 per cent.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The results come as the alliance continues to advance infrastructure investment aimed at capacity expansion and emissions reduction across the <strong>Seattle-Tacoma gateway</strong>. As a US West Coast gateway, NWSA operates in one of the consular zones where <strong>tariff uncertainty and shipping vessel rotation changes</strong> are most clearly felt. The gradual recovery on the export side is interpreted as evidence of stability in agricultural and forest product flows, particularly to Asian markets.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. NWSA reported full exports up 1.8 per cent in Q1; full imports down 18 per cent.<br>
2. The drop in imports was driven by tariff frontloading ahead of anticipated tariffs.<br>
3. YTD total container volumes reached 714,719 TEUs, down 14.2 per cent.<br>
4. March 2026 volume was 278,829 TEUs, a 10.1 per cent YoY decline.<br>
5. Domestic volumes grew 0.2 per cent YTD; Alaska up 0.3 per cent, Hawaii down 0.4 per cent.</p>
