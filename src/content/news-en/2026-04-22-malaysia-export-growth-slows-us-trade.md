<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Malaysia</strong>'s exports grew more slowly than expected in November — reflecting the weight that higher tariffs are placing on the Southeast Asian nation's trade with the <strong>U.S.</strong> According to the <strong>Department of Statistics Malaysia</strong>, exports rose 7% year-on-year in November — falling short of the median estimate of 11.6% in <strong>Bloomberg</strong> surveys. Shipments to the <strong>U.S.</strong>, one of <strong>Malaysia</strong>'s largest trading partners, fell 0.9% and posted declines for a second consecutive month. Imports accelerated 15.8% year-on-year — effectively cutting the country's trade surplus in half in November to 6.1 billion ringgit ($1.5 billion). From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Malaysia</strong> is <strong>ASEAN</strong>'s third-largest economy — after <strong>Indonesia</strong> and <strong>Thailand</strong>. <strong>Singapore</strong>, the <strong>U.S.</strong>, <strong>China</strong>, <strong>Japan</strong>, <strong>Hong Kong</strong>, <strong>Thailand</strong>, <strong>India</strong>, <strong>South Korea</strong>, <strong>Vietnam</strong>, and <strong>Taiwan</strong> are <strong>Malaysia</strong>'s principal trading partners.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>U.S.</strong>, which accounts for 11% of <strong>Malaysia</strong>'s total trade, is imposing a 19% tariff on the Southeast Asian nation's goods — effective from August. Yet <strong>Malaysia</strong>'s electrical and electronic product exports are up 15% year-on-year — holding firm amid expectations that the <strong>U.S.</strong> will soon tax semiconductors. Strong exports to <strong>China</strong> also help offset the decline in shipments to the <strong>U.S.</strong> — while exports to <strong>Taiwan</strong> have reached record levels. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Penang</strong>, <strong>Selangor</strong>, <strong>Kulim</strong>, and <strong>Johor</strong> are <strong>Malaysia</strong>'s electronics manufacturing hubs. <strong>Intel</strong>, <strong>Texas Instruments</strong>, <strong>AMD</strong>, <strong>Infineon</strong>, <strong>Bosch</strong>, <strong>STMicroelectronics</strong>, <strong>Renesas</strong>, <strong>Western Digital</strong>, <strong>Micron</strong>, <strong>Lam Research</strong>, <strong>Applied Materials</strong>, <strong>Inari Amertron</strong>, <strong>Globetronics</strong>, <strong>VS Industry</strong>, <strong>Pentamaster</strong>, <strong>ViTrox</strong>, and <strong>MMS Ventures</strong> are major electronics manufacturers in <strong>Malaysia</strong>. <strong>OSAT</strong> (<strong>Outsourced Semiconductor Assembly and Test</strong>) services represent the main activity in <strong>Malaysia</strong>'s role in the global semiconductor supply chain — representing 13% of global <strong>OSAT</strong> capacity.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">"It is still good performance for us, as we are seeing," said <strong>Mohd Afzanizam Abdul Rashid</strong>, chief economist at <strong>Bank Muamalat Malaysia Bhd</strong>. "The impact of higher <strong>U.S.</strong> tariffs will need to be more granular in 2026," he added, expecting more moderation in exports to the <strong>U.S.</strong> as higher tariffs reduce demand. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> tariff regime applies regionally differentiated tariffs to <strong>ASEAN</strong> countries under a <strong>reciprocal tariff</strong> mechanism. <strong>Vietnam</strong>, <strong>Thailand</strong>, <strong>Indonesia</strong>, <strong>Cambodia</strong>, <strong>Laos</strong>, <strong>Myanmar</strong>, and the <strong>Philippines</strong> are affected at varying rates. <strong>Vietnam</strong>, one of the leading beneficiaries of the <strong>China+1</strong> strategy, is among the hardest hit with a 46% tariff. <strong>Malaysia</strong>'s 19% rate remains relatively moderate. A <strong>Section 232</strong> semiconductor investigation forms the basis for potential chip tariffs the <strong>U.S.</strong> will apply going forward. The <strong>CHIPS and Science Act</strong>, <strong>Intel Foundry</strong>, <strong>TSMC Arizona</strong>, <strong>Samsung Texas</strong>, and <strong>Micron New York</strong> are the major investments in <strong>U.S.</strong> chip localization.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Bank Negara Malaysia</strong> (<strong>BNM</strong>) manages monetary policy as the country's central bank. The <strong>Malaysian ringgit</strong> (<strong>MYR</strong>) and <strong>USD</strong>/<strong>MYR</strong> parity are critical for exporter margins. <strong>Palm oil</strong>, <strong>petroleum</strong>, <strong>LNG</strong>, <strong>rubber</strong>, <strong>tin</strong>, and <strong>tropical wood</strong> are <strong>Malaysia</strong>'s main export items outside electronics. <strong>Sime Darby Plantation</strong>, <strong>FGV Holdings</strong>, <strong>IOI Corporation</strong>, and <strong>Petronas</strong> are <strong>Malaysia</strong> companies operating at global scale. <strong>Port Klang</strong>, <strong>Tanjung Pelepas</strong>, <strong>Penang Port</strong>, and <strong>Bintulu</strong> are <strong>Malaysia</strong>'s principal ports. <strong>Tanjung Pelepas</strong> ranks among the world's top 20 container ports. <strong>RCEP</strong> (<strong>Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership</strong>), <strong>CPTPP</strong>, and the potential <strong>MEUSFTA</strong> form <strong>Malaysia</strong>'s active trade agreement portfolio. The <strong>JS-SEZ</strong> (<strong>Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone</strong>) is being signed in 2025 — promoting cross-border investment. In summary, <strong>Malaysia</strong>'s November export data serves as concrete evidence of the granular impact of <strong>U.S.</strong> tariffs on <strong>ASEAN</strong> economies.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Malaysia</strong>'s exports grew 7% in November — falling short of the 11.6% expectation.<br>
2. Shipments to the <strong>U.S.</strong> fell 0.9% and mark a second consecutive month of decline.<br>
3. The <strong>U.S.</strong> is imposing a 19% tariff — effective from August.<br>
4. Electronics exports are growing 15%; exports to <strong>China</strong> and <strong>Taiwan</strong> are offsetting factors.<br>
5. <strong>Mohd Afzanizam</strong> expects the impact to be more granular in 2026.</p>
