<p style="text-align: justify">Should the federal government support the struggling <strong>U.S.</strong> commercial shipbuilding sector through subsidies? The <strong>Trump Administration</strong>'s recently released "<strong>Maritime Action Plan</strong>," presented as aimed at restoring "<strong>America's maritime dominance</strong>," includes a new round of proposed construction subsidies for <strong>U.S.</strong> commercial shipbuilding. No one disputes that the sector cannot compete with many other countries in cost, infrastructure, innovation, and order delivery times. But is it sensible to attempt to level the playing field with expensive subsidies? From a supply chain perspective, <strong>U.S.</strong> commercial shipbuilding produces 5–10 commercial vessels annually in a global market dominated by Asian giants such as <strong>Hyundai Heavy Industries</strong>, <strong>Samsung Heavy Industries</strong>, <strong>Daewoo Shipbuilding</strong> (South Korea), and <strong>China State Shipbuilding Corporation</strong> (<strong>CSSC</strong>) and <strong>China Shipbuilding Industry Corporation</strong> (<strong>CSIC</strong>), each of which builds more than 100 commercial vessels yearly. On a <strong>compensated gross tonnage</strong> (<strong>CGT</strong>) basis, the <strong>U.S.</strong> share of global production is less than 1%.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Cato Institute</strong> — a libertarian think tank — scholar <strong>Colin Grabow</strong> says the answer is no. <strong>Grabow</strong> argues that <strong>U.S.</strong> domestic shipbuilding cannot compete even close to other nations "<strong>under current conditions or under any plausible combination of subsidies or mandates</strong>." <strong>Grabow</strong> also articulates his opposition to the <strong>Jones Act</strong>, which mandates that vessels serving <strong>U.S.</strong> domestic commerce be <strong>U.S.</strong>-built, owned, and crewed. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Merchant Marine Act</strong> (<strong>Jones Act</strong>), which went into effect in 1920, imposes strict cabotage rules under <strong>Section 27</strong> on domestic shipping. Consequently, shipping costs to <strong>Puerto Rico</strong>, <strong>Hawaii</strong>, <strong>Alaska</strong>, and <strong>Guam</strong> are 2–3 times higher compared to alternative foreign carriers. <strong>Cato</strong>, the <strong>Mercatus Center</strong>, the <strong>American Enterprise Institute</strong> (<strong>AEI</strong>), and the <strong>Heritage Foundation</strong> have long lobbied for the <strong>Jones Act</strong> to be repealed or reformed.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, Asian dominance in <strong>U.S.</strong> maritime commerce stems not just from cost but from a combination of shipyard scale, steel supply chains, skilled workforce pools, OEM component ecosystems, and the geographic concentration of <strong>shipyard cluster</strong>s. Shipyards in <strong>Ulsan</strong> (South Korea), <strong>Shanghai</strong> and <strong>Dalian</strong> (China), and <strong>Imari</strong> (Japan) have developed competitive advantages over decades through government support, <strong>R&amp;D</strong> investment, and process optimization. In the <strong>U.S.</strong>, <strong>Newport News Shipbuilding</strong> (<strong>HII</strong>), <strong>General Dynamics Bath Iron Works</strong>, <strong>NASSCO</strong>, and <strong>Marinette Marine</strong> focus primarily on <strong>U.S. Navy</strong> and <strong>U.S. Coast Guard</strong> contracts and lack sufficient cost competitiveness for commercial work. While the argument for protecting the <strong>defense industrial base</strong> is the main pillar of subsidy advocates, <strong>Grabow</strong>'s argument is that subsidies fail to address structural problems.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>maritime supply chain</strong> carries more than 80% of global trade. <strong>Maersk</strong>, <strong>MSC</strong>, <strong>CMA CGM</strong>, <strong>COSCO</strong>, <strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong>, <strong>ONE</strong> (<strong>Ocean Network Express</strong>), <strong>Evergreen</strong>, <strong>Yang Ming</strong>, and <strong>HMM</strong> are the major players in <strong>container shipping</strong>, and none of them operate <strong>U.S.</strong>-built container vessels. The <strong>USTR</strong> (<strong>U.S. Trade Representative</strong>)'s proposal under <strong>Section 301</strong> investigation to impose port fees on Chinese vessels aims to support the <strong>U.S.</strong> maritime industry but may raise costs for agricultural exporters and retailers. The podcast is hosted by <strong>Bob Bowman</strong>, editor-in-chief of <strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong>. In conclusion, <strong>Grabow</strong>'s arguments clearly demonstrate the structural constraints of <strong>U.S.</strong> maritime policy and the impact of the <strong>Jones Act</strong> on the supply chain.</p>
