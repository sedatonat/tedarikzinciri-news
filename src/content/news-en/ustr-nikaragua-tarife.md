<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>U.S. Trade Representative (USTR)</strong> has announced plans to implement a new and phased tariff regime targeting <strong>Nicaragua</strong>. The <strong>Trump administration</strong> revealed that Nicaraguan products will face <strong>additional tariffs of up to 15%</strong> due to <strong>human rights violations and labor standard violations</strong> in the country. The decision is based on findings from a formal investigation launched in December 2024 under <strong>Section 301</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">According to <strong>Newsweek</strong>, the new tariffs will take effect <strong>gradually</strong> rather than all at once. Under the planned schedule:</p>\n<ul>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>10% additional tariff as of January 1, 2027</strong>,</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>15% additional tariff as of January 1, 2028</strong></p></li>\n</ul>\n<p style="text-align: justify">These levies will be imposed <strong>on top of existing 18% tariffs</strong> currently in force against Nicaragua. As a result, for products falling outside the CAFTA-DR (Dominican Republic–Central America–United States Free Trade Agreement) scope, the total tariff burden will reach significantly higher levels.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Section 301 Findings: What Is the Rationale?</strong></h3>\n<p style="text-align: justify">The USTR's final report identified that the Nicaraguan government:</p>\n<ul>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">exhibits "<strong>increasingly pervasive abuses</strong>" in <strong>child labor</strong> and <strong>forced labor</strong>,</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>human trafficking</strong>,</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>workplace abuses</strong>,</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">systematic violation of union rights</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p style="text-align: justify">These practices are argued to constitute not merely domestic legal violations but also <strong>disruptions</strong> to U.S. trade relationships in a manner that is disruptive.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Consequently, Washington is converting its sanctions policy toward Nicaragua from mere diplomatic pressure into a <strong>trade policy tool</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>U.S.–Nicaragua Trade Significance</strong></h3>\n<p style="text-align: justify">While Nicaragua is not a major trade partner for the U.S., it maintains a <strong>strategically important</strong> supplier position. The country ranks <strong>approximately 60th</strong> among U.S. import sources. However, the U.S. market is critical for the Nicaraguan economy:</p>\n<ul>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Nicaragua's total exports in 2024: <strong>$7.52 billion USD</strong></p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>52% of which went to the U.S.</strong></p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Primary products: <strong>apparel, knitwear, and textiles</strong></p></li>\n</ul>\n<p style="text-align: justify">This picture demonstrates that the additional tariffs imposed by the U.S. will create <strong>disproportionately large impact</strong> on the Nicaraguan economy.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>CAFTA-DR Exemptions and Scope</strong></h3>\n<p style="text-align: justify">The new tariffs will exclude <strong>products covered by protection under CAFTA-DR</strong>. However, additional duties will apply in full to products outside the agreement. This situation may make <strong>origin and certification processes</strong> in textile supply chains increasingly critical.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">For U.S. importers, this development means increased costs for supplies sourced from Nicaragua. Consequently, companies may:</p>\n<ul>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">consider shifting supplies to alternative CAFTA-DR countries such as <strong>Honduras, Guatemala, or the Dominican Republic</strong>,</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">or rebalance with supplies from Mexico and Asia.</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p><br></p>\n<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>USTR's Open-Door Policy</strong></h3>\n<p style="text-align: justify">The USTR stated it will continue monitoring Nicaragua's progress before the tariffs take effect. If the government:</p>\n<ul>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">fails to demonstrate <strong>concrete improvements</strong> in labor and human rights,</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>accelerated tariff implementation</strong> or <strong>further rate increases</strong> may be considered.</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p style="text-align: justify">This language indicates that Washington is wielding tariffs as a <strong>dynamic pressure tool</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Supply Chain and Regional Impact</strong></h3>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Central America has become central to <strong>nearshoring</strong> strategies in recent years. The additional tariffs on Nicaragua could prompt U.S. companies to redraw their regional supply maps. As Nicaragua's appeal in low-cost apparel manufacturing diminishes, opportunity windows open for neighboring countries.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong></h3>\n<ul>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The U.S. is preparing to impose <strong>phased 15% additional tariffs</strong> on Nicaragua.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Schedule: <strong>10% (January 1, 2027)</strong> → <strong>15% (January 1, 2028)</strong>.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Will be imposed <strong>on top of existing 18% tariffs</strong>.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Rationale: <strong>Section 301</strong> investigation findings on human and labor rights violations.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>52% of Nicaragua's exports</strong> go to the U.S.; textiles are the main product.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">USTR signals willingness to accelerate or increase tariffs if no progress is made.</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p><br></p>\n<p>----------</p>\n<p><strong>News Link: </strong><a href="https://www.supplychainbrain.com/articles/42986-ustr-announces-15-tariffs-against-nicaragua-over-human-rights-abuses"><u>https://www.supplychainbrain.com/articles/42986-ustr-announces-15-tariffs-against-nicaragua-over-human-rights-abuses</u></a></p>\n<p>--------------------</p>\n<p><strong>Author: </strong><a href="http://sedatonat.com/"><u>SedatOnat.com</u></a></p>\n<p>--------------------</p>\n<p><strong>!!!
