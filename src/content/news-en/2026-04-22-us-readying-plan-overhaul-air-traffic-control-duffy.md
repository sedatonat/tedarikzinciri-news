<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports, per analyst insight: <strong>Trump</strong> administration is planning to announce a "brand new" US air traffic control system "within the next several days," according to Transportation Secretary <strong>Sean Duffy</strong>. Changes will include a shift from copper communications infrastructure to fiber lines, as well as new radar systems and terminals for air traffic controllers. Duffy made these comments in an interview with <strong>Fox News</strong> on March 19. "The key to this is speed," he said. Air traffic control challenges are well known—but upgrades take far too long, and then technology changes—with <strong>Congress</strong> needing to "give all the money up front." The comments come weeks after increased scrutiny of efforts to modernize the <strong>Federal Aviation Administration</strong> (<strong>FAA</strong>) system, which manages approximately 45,000 flights carrying roughly 2.9 million passengers each day.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.S. Department of Transportation</strong> (<strong>DOT</strong>)—under Secretary <strong>Sean Duffy</strong> (Trump 2.0; former <strong>Wisconsin</strong> congressman and <strong>Fox News</strong> host)—through the <strong>Federal Aviation Administration</strong> (<strong>FAA</strong>), with Acting Administrator <strong>Chris Rocheleau</strong> based in <strong>Washington DC</strong>, regulates all civil aviation in the United States. <strong>NextGen</strong> (<strong>Next Generation Air Transportation System</strong>), launched in 2003, is the primary air traffic control modernization program, with <strong>ADS-B</strong> (<strong>Automatic Dependent Surveillance-Broadcast</strong>), <strong>Data Comm</strong>, <strong>SWIM</strong> (<strong>System Wide Information Management</strong>), and <strong>NAS Voice System</strong> as key <strong>NextGen</strong> components. The <strong>National Airspace System</strong> (<strong>NAS</strong>) is the primary US airspace infrastructure. Notable 2023 FAA system disruptions included the <strong>NOTAM</strong> (<strong>Notice to Air Missions</strong>) system failure (January 2023) and 2024 <strong>Boeing</strong> 737 MAX safety crises. <strong>SpaceX Starlink</strong> (<strong>Elon Musk</strong>, Founder/CEO, based in <strong>Hawthorne California</strong>) is beginning tests of satellite internet terminals within parts of the <strong>FAA</strong> system in February 2025—a controversial move linked to <strong>Trump</strong> adviser <strong>Musk</strong>'s <strong>Department of Government Efficiency</strong> (<strong>DOGE</strong>) efforts.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, key US air traffic control technology and service providers include <strong>Raytheon</strong> (<strong>RTX</strong>; CEO <strong>Christopher Calio</strong>; based in <strong>Arlington Virginia</strong>), <strong>Lockheed Martin</strong> (<strong>James Taiclet</strong>, CEO; <strong>Bethesda Maryland</strong>), <strong>L3Harris Technologies</strong> (<strong>Christopher Kubasik</strong>, CEO; <strong>Melbourne Florida</strong>), <strong>Leidos</strong> (<strong>Tom Bell</strong>, CEO; <strong>Reston Virginia</strong>), <strong>Saab</strong> (air traffic management), <strong>Indra</strong> (<strong>Spain</strong>), <strong>Thales Group</strong> (<strong>Patrice Caine</strong>, CEO; <strong>Paris</strong>), and <strong>Frequentis</strong> (<strong>Austria</strong>). The <strong>National Air Traffic Controllers Association</strong> (<strong>NATCA</strong>; President <strong>Nick Daniels</strong>) is the primary US air traffic controllers union, with approximately 20,000 members. Key global air traffic control authorities include <strong>Eurocontrol</strong> (<strong>Raul Medina</strong>, Director General; <strong>Brussels</strong>), <strong>NAV CANADA</strong> (<strong>Raymond Bohn</strong>, CEO), <strong>NATS UK</strong>, and <strong>Airservices Australia</strong>. The <strong>FAA Aerospace Forecast</strong> is the primary annual industry forecast report tracking US commercial airline traffic growth.</p>

<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, key US airlines—users of air traffic control services—include <strong>American Airlines</strong> (<strong>Robert Isom</strong>, CEO; <strong>Fort Worth</strong>), <strong>Delta Air Lines</strong> (<strong>Ed Bastian</strong>, CEO; <strong>Atlanta</strong>), <strong>United Airlines</strong> (<strong>Scott Kirby</strong>, CEO; <strong>Chicago</strong>), <strong>Southwest Airlines</strong> (<strong>Bob Jordan</strong>, CEO; <strong>Dallas</strong>), <strong>Alaska Airlines</strong>, <strong>JetBlue</strong>, <strong>Spirit Airlines</strong>, <strong>Frontier Airlines</strong>, <strong>FedEx</strong> (cargo), <strong>UPS</strong> (cargo), <strong>Atlas Air</strong>, and <strong>Amazon Air</strong>. <strong>Airlines for America</strong> (<strong>A4A</strong>; CEO <strong>Nicholas Calio</strong>) is the primary US airline industry advocacy group lobbying for <strong>FAA</strong> reform. Additional aviation sector organizations include the <strong>National Business Aviation Association</strong> (<strong>NBAA</strong>; CEO <strong>Ed Bolen</strong>) and the <strong>Aircraft Owners and Pilots Association</strong> (<strong>AOPA</strong>; CEO <strong>Mark Baker</strong>). The <strong>National Transportation Safety Board</strong> (<strong>NTSB</strong>; Chair <strong>Jennifer Homendy</strong>) is the primary US aviation safety investigation body. In conclusion, <strong>Duffy</strong>'s <strong>FAA</strong> overhaul plan appears to signal a fundamental redesign of post-<strong>NextGen</strong> US air traffic infrastructure globally—with supply chain managers now viewing air cargo disruption risk and <strong>Starlink</strong> dependency as main strategic priorities.</p>
