<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; analyst insight; <strong>A.P. Moller-Maersk</strong> (<strong>APM</strong>); has signed an agreement to extend its lease at the Port of New York and New Jersey for its terminal in <strong>Elizabeth, New Jersey</strong> for an additional 33 years. According to a release from <strong>APM</strong>, the <strong>Elizabeth</strong> terminal handles more than 25 percent of the port complex's annual container capacity — and is the company's largest terminal on the East Coast. The terminal also offers the largest reefer capacity at the Port of New York and New Jersey — equipped with super <strong>post-Panamax</strong> ship-shore cranes capable of handling vessels up to 23 containers wide on-deck. <strong>APM Terminals</strong> and the <strong>Port Authority</strong> are the primary partners.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>A.P. Moller-Maersk</strong>, headquartered in <strong>Copenhagen, Denmark</strong>, with CEO <strong>Vincent Clerc</strong>, and founded in 1904 by <strong>Arnold Peter Møller</strong>, provides container shipping, logistics, terminal operations and tanker services globally. <strong>APM Terminals</strong>, based in <strong>The Hague, Netherlands</strong>, with CEO <strong>Keith Svendsen</strong>, operates more than 70 port terminals across 60+ countries worldwide. <strong>Maersk's</strong> integrator strategy combines container shipping + land transportation + air freight + contract logistics into a unified single-point-to-delivery offering — <strong>LF Logistics</strong> 2022 acquisition, <strong>Pilot Freight</strong> 2022 acquisition, and <strong>Senator International</strong> 2022 acquisition are the major recent consolidations. <strong>Maersk's</strong> principal container shipping competitors include <strong>MSC</strong> (<strong>Mediterranean Shipping Company</strong>; Chairman <strong>Diego Apont</strong>; <strong>Geneva</strong>), <strong>CMA CGM</strong> (CEO <strong>Rodolphe Saadé</strong>; <strong>Marseille</strong>), <strong>COSCO Shipping</strong> (Chairman <strong>Wan Min</strong>; <strong>Shanghai</strong>), <strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong> (CEO <strong>Rolf Habben Jansen</strong>; <strong>Hamburg</strong>), <strong>ONE</strong> (<strong>Ocean Network Express</strong>), <strong>Evergreen Marine</strong>, <strong>HMM</strong>, <strong>Yang Ming</strong>, and <strong>Zim</strong>, which are the principal ecosystem players.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Port Authority of New York and New Jersey</strong>, with Port Director <strong>Bethann Rooney</strong>, was established in 1921 as the <strong>Port of New York Authority</strong>, with its primary jurisdiction being the New York-New Jersey port complex, which serves as the principal gateway on the U.S. East Coast. The port complex terminals include (1) <strong>APM Terminals Elizabeth</strong> (<strong>Maersk</strong>), (2) <strong>Maher Terminals</strong> (owned by <strong>Macquarie</strong>), (3) <strong>Port Newark Container Terminal</strong> (<strong>PNCT</strong>; <strong>Ports America</strong> and <strong>Terminal Investment Limited</strong> (<strong>TIL</strong>; <strong>MSC</strong>)-owned), (4) <strong>GCT New York</strong> (<strong>Global Container Terminals</strong>), (5) <strong>GCT Bayonne</strong>, and (6) <strong>Red Hook Container Terminal</strong> as principal terminals. Other major U.S. East Coast port terminal operators include <strong>Ports America</strong> (CEO <strong>Mark Montgomery</strong>; owned by <strong>OMERS Infrastructure</strong>), <strong>SSA Marine</strong> (owned by <strong>Carrix</strong>; <strong>Goldman Sachs</strong>), <strong>ITS</strong> (<strong>International Transportation Service</strong>; owned by <strong>K Line</strong>), <strong>DP World</strong>, <strong>Yilport</strong> (<strong>Yıldırım Holding</strong>; <strong>Turkey</strong>), and <strong>HHLA</strong> (<strong>Hamburger Hafen und Logistik AG</strong>; <strong>MSC</strong>-owned) as principal terminal operators.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the primary U.S. container port capacities (on a TEU basis) include (1) <strong>Port of Los Angeles</strong> (10.3M TEU 2024 — <strong>Gene Seroka</strong>), (2) <strong>Port of Long Beach</strong> (9.6M TEU 2024 — <strong>Mario Cordero</strong>), (3) <strong>Port of New York/New Jersey</strong> (~7.7M TEU), (4) <strong>Port of Savannah</strong> (~5.4M TEU — <strong>Georgia Ports Authority</strong>), (5) <strong>Port of Houston</strong> (~4M TEU), (6) <strong>Port of Norfolk</strong> (<strong>Virginia International Terminals</strong>), (7) <strong>Port of Charleston</strong> (<strong>SC Ports Authority</strong>), (8) <strong>Northwest Seaport Alliance</strong> (Seattle/Tacoma), (9) <strong>Port of Oakland</strong>, and (10) <strong>Port of Miami</strong> as the principal U.S. major ports. The <strong>American Association of Port Authorities</strong> (<strong>AAPA</strong>; CEO <strong>Cary Davis</strong>) and the <strong>National Association of Waterfront Employers</strong> (<strong>NAWE</strong>) are the principal U.S. port sector organizations. The <strong>Federal Maritime Commission</strong> (<strong>FMC</strong>; Chairman <strong>Louis Sola</strong>) and the <strong>U.S. Maritime Administration</strong> (<strong>MARAD</strong>) are the principal U.S. federal maritime regulatory authorities. In conclusion, <strong>Maersk's</strong> extension of the <strong>Elizabeth</strong> lease fundamentally reaffirms U.S. East Coast terminal investments globally — and suggests that East Coast capacity strategy and <strong>front-loading</strong> alternate routing lanes remain a principal strategic priority for supply chain managers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Maersk</strong> extends its lease on the <strong>Elizabeth</strong>, NJ terminal for an additional 33 years.<br>
2. Handles 25 percent of the port complex container capacity — the largest on the East Coast.<br>
3. Super <strong>post-Panamax</strong> cranes handle vessels up to 23 containers wide.<br>
4. <strong>Port of NY/NJ</strong> is the largest container port on the U.S. East Coast.<br>
5. <strong>APM Terminals</strong>, <strong>Maher</strong>, <strong>PNCT</strong>, and <strong>GCT</strong> are principal port terminal operators.</p>
