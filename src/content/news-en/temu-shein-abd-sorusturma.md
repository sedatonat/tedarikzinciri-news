<p style="text-align: justify">As of December 2025, two major discount-focused e-commerce platforms in the US, <strong>Shein</strong> and <strong>Temu</strong>, are facing significant political and legal pressure. With investigation calls mounting at both federal and state levels, the platforms' supply chain practices, labor standards, and intellectual property policies are once again becoming the subject of intense scrutiny.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to information reported by BBC News, <strong>Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton</strong> has launched a comprehensive investigation into Shein involving allegations of <strong>forced labor</strong>, <strong>unsafe materials</strong>, and potentially <strong>deceptive marketing</strong>. Paxton has noted that the vast majority of the brand's products are manufactured in China and that labor practices within this supply chain require serious examination.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In a statement, Shein said it would engage in <strong>"constructive engagement"</strong> with the investigation and that it continually enhances its processes to protect designers' rights. The company, which has faced numerous accusations in recent years regarding intellectual property violations, emphasized that it has invested in <strong>internal processes</strong> and design protection mechanisms.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Meanwhile, <strong>Temu</strong> did not respond to BBC's request for comment. However, the growing pressure is not limited to Shein alone; Temu has also come under scrutiny over <strong>intellectual property theft</strong> allegations. Arkansas Senator <strong>Tom Cotton</strong> called for federal investigation into both companies, describing them as "<strong>Communist Chinese platforms</strong>." Cotton cited US research showing that a significant portion of products purchased from these platforms are <strong>likely counterfeits</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">These developments are directly linked to a critical trade decision the US made in 2025: the elimination of the <strong>de minimis rule</strong>. This rule had allowed Shein and Temu for years to send millions of small packages to the US in <strong>duty-free</strong> and <strong>scrutiny-free</strong> manner. According to Cotton's statement, following the rule's elimination, millions of packages originating from China are now awaiting processing in US warehouses and can be subjected to <strong>customs inspection</strong> procedures. This situation creates a "<strong>golden opportunity</strong>" to systematically identify risks in products sold on these platforms, including <strong>IP violations</strong>, <strong>unsafe materials</strong>, <strong>counterfeit goods</strong>, and <strong>forced labor allegations</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The supply chains of Shein and Temu have long faced harsh criticism regarding <strong>environmental impact</strong>, <strong>labor standards</strong>, <strong>fast-fashion sustainability</strong>, and <strong>traceability</strong>. In particular, Shein's rapid production model, dependence on low-cost labor, and lack of transparency in manufacturing processes have never escaped the attention of Western policymakers. Texas AG Paxton's investigation demonstrates that these issues are now being elevated to formal proceedings.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The potential impacts of these investigations will not be limited to the platforms alone. Should Temu and Shein's US entry processes face regulatory obstacles:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Transit times</strong> will increase,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Customs holds</strong> will intensify,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Supply chain compliance costs</strong> will rise,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Significant operational burden will be placed on e-commerce fulfillment centers.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">Senator Cotton's statements serve as a signal of an upcoming regulatory period that could fundamentally alter Shein and Temu's business model in the US market. Particularly, IP protection and forced labor issues could drive much stricter certification requirements and data demands for US e-commerce imports in 2026.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In conclusion, this wave of investigations into Shein and Temu demonstrates that not only the two platforms, but the entire <strong>low-cost and fast Chinese supply chain model</strong> is being re-evaluated in the US. As we move into 2026, this issue will be among the top agenda items for both trade policies and compliance processes.</p>
<p><br></p>

</ul>
