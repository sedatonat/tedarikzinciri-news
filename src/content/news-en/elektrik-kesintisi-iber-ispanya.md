<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>At 12:33 CEST on April 28, 2025</strong>, an <strong>extraordinary widespread power outage</strong> struck the <strong>Iberian Peninsula</strong>. Electricity was cut off for <strong>approximately 10 hours</strong> across most regions of <strong>mainland Spain</strong> and <strong>Portugal</strong>; <strong>Andorra</strong> and <strong>southwestern France</strong> experienced brief outages lasting seconds to minutes. The outage affected <strong>telecommunications</strong>, <strong>transportation</strong>, <strong>airports</strong>, <strong>healthcare services</strong>, and <strong>government institutions</strong> across multiple layers. At least <strong>±7 deaths in Spain</strong> and <strong>±1 death in Portugal</strong> were attributed to indirect causes, including <strong>candle fires</strong> and <strong>poisonings from indoor generator use</strong>. ENTSO-E and national sources reported a <strong>"total zero"</strong> condition in the Iberian power system at the time of the incident.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Pre-event and initial moments:</strong>&nbsp;</h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to <strong>Red Eléctrica de España (REE)</strong> data, at <strong>12:30</strong> demand was at <strong>25 GW</strong> and generation at <strong>32 GW</strong>; the system was exporting <strong>2.6 GW to Portugal</strong>, <strong>0.87 GW to France</strong>, <strong>0.78 GW to Morocco</strong>, and <strong>3 GW</strong> was being used for <strong>hydro-pumping</strong>. Solar accounted for <strong>~59% (19.5 GW)</strong>, wind <strong>~12%</strong>, nuclear <strong>~11% (3.3 GW)</strong>, natural gas <strong>~5%</strong>, and prices had turned slightly negative. Two oscillations were damped between <strong>12:03–12:07</strong> and <strong>12:19–12:21</strong>. A generation loss of approximately <strong>2,200 MW</strong> was triggered in the <strong>Granada, Badajoz, and Seville</strong> regions in the <strong>12:32:57–12:33:17</strong> timeframe; between <strong>12:33:18–12:33:21</strong>, frequency dropped below <strong>48 Hz</strong>, and <strong>automatic load shedding (UFLS)</strong> activated; the <strong>France-Spain AC lines</strong> opened at <strong>12:33:21</strong>, the <strong>HVDC</strong> at <strong>12:33:24</strong>, and the <strong>Iberian system collapsed</strong>. On the German side, grid frequency dropped by approximately <strong>150 mHz</strong>.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Impacts – Spain:</strong>&nbsp;</h5>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Renfe</strong> halted all trains; <strong>metro and suburban lines</strong> were evacuated; airports, including <strong>Barajas</strong>, returned to limited-capacity operations; <strong>internet traffic</strong> fell to <strong>17% of normal</strong>; <strong>hospitals</strong> switched to generators. <strong>Nuclear power plants</strong> automatically disconnected from the grid while maintaining safe cooling with <strong>backup power</strong>. <strong>Madrid City Council</strong> activated its emergency plan, <strong>PEMAM</strong>; the <strong>city assembly, stock exchange, and government buildings</strong> were affected. The business federation <strong>CEOE</strong> estimated economic losses at <strong>~€1.6 billion</strong>. Three family members in <strong>Galicia</strong> died from CO poisoning; one person in <strong>Madrid</strong> died in a candle fire.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Impacts – Portugal:</strong>&nbsp;</h5>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Supermarkets, pharmacies, card payments</strong>, and <strong>mobile communications</strong> systems failed on a wide scale; <strong>traffic lights</strong> went offline; <strong>suburban/metro</strong> systems stopped due to signaling problems; <strong>Lisbon Airport</strong> closed in the afternoon and partially reopened in the evening; <strong>hospitals</strong> operated on generators. The government declared an <strong>energy crisis</strong>; it was decided to protect the <strong>black start</strong> capacity of <strong>Tapada do Outeiro</strong> and hydroelectric plants, and to extend it to <strong>Alqueva</strong> and <strong>Baixo Sabor</strong>. A <strong>77-year-old</strong> dependent on a home ventilator lost his life.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Re-energization:</strong> <strong>French</strong> connections were phased back online from <strong>12:44</strong> onwards; support of up to <strong>900 MW from Morocco</strong> and <strong>2 GW from France</strong> was provided; the <strong>Aldeadávila</strong> hydroelectric facility accelerated sequential restart with <strong>autonomous start</strong>. <strong>Spain</strong> fully returned to load by the morning of April 29; in <strong>Portugal</strong>, full restoration was achieved after midnight with <strong>Castelo do Bode (138 MW)</strong> and <strong>Tapada do Outeiro (990 MW)</strong>. <strong>Portugal</strong> limited imports from Spain to <strong>1 GW</strong> between <strong>May 8–12</strong>.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Investigations and technical findings:</strong>&nbsp;</h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>ENTSO-E</strong> preliminary report noted that two <strong>inter-area oscillations</strong> before the event, <strong>voltage increases in the south</strong>, <strong>rapid ROCOF</strong>, and <strong>loss of synchronism</strong> prevented defense plans from mitigating the collapse. The <strong>Spanish government</strong> report emphasized <strong>cascading effects from minor faults in the south</strong> and <strong>insufficient synchronous thermal generation capacity</strong> being operated at the time of the event. <strong>REE</strong> stated that "planned voltage control was adequate, and the collapse would not have occurred if conventional power plants had fully fulfilled their duties," while industry representatives argued that <strong>safety margins</strong> were kept too low. The <strong>Comillas IIT</strong> report identified <strong>inadequate dispatch of synchronous generation with dynamic voltage control</strong> and <strong>limited resilience of the transmission network</strong> among the primary causes. A <strong>cyber attack</strong> was not confirmed following comprehensive investigations.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Policy and system debates:</strong>&nbsp;</h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">The impact of the renewable-heavy energy mix was opened to discussion; the EU Energy Commissioner and various analytical bodies emphasized that <strong>the mix was standard</strong> and <strong>no single source type could be held solely responsible</strong>. Experts recommended increasing <strong>synchronous condensers</strong>, <strong>grid-forming batteries</strong>, <strong>inverter settings</strong>, and <strong>safety margins</strong> to address <strong>low inertia</strong>, <strong>high grid-following inverter</strong> prevalence, and <strong>limited interconnection</strong>. The <strong>Spanish Senate</strong> established an investigation committee; <strong>Portugal</strong> announced an additional <strong>€400 million</strong> investment in the grid, increasing <strong>battery capacity from &lt;20 MW to 750 MW</strong>, raising <strong>black start points from 2 to 4</strong>, and strengthening <strong>critical infrastructure resilience</strong>.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong></h5>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">On <strong>April 28, 2025</strong>, a widespread outage lasting up to <strong>10 hours</strong> struck across Spain and Portugal; <strong>~31 GW</strong> of load disconnected from the system.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">At <strong>12:33</strong>, <strong>generation reductions in southern Spain</strong>, <strong>UFLS</strong>, opening of <strong>French lines</strong>, and <strong>HVDC</strong> caused the system to collapse.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Transportation, airports, telecommunications, and payments were severely affected; deaths and injuries from generator and candle-related incidents were reported.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Restoration was carried out gradually through <strong>France/Morocco interconnections</strong>, <strong>hydro and gas turbines</strong>; both countries fully recovered by the morning of <strong>April 29</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Initial findings converged on <strong>voltage/frequency instability</strong>, <strong>insufficient synchronous capacity</strong>, and <strong>limited network resilience</strong>; <strong>cyber attack</strong> was not confirmed.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Policy responses focused on <strong>synchronous condensers</strong>, <strong>grid-forming batteries</strong>, <strong>expanded black start deployment</strong>, and <strong>increased safety margins</strong>.</p></li>
</ul>
