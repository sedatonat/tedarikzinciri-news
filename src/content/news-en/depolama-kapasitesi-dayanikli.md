<p style="text-align: justify">In recent months, new tariffs imposed by the <strong>Trump administration</strong> and port labor concerns have prompted many shippers to front-load goods by focusing on specific product categories to mitigate port disruptions. <strong>Jonathan Gold</strong>, vice president of customs policy and supply chain at the <strong>National Retail Federation</strong>, notes that many retailers have been cautious about bringing in excess cargo and are therefore in a strong position.&nbsp;</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to a February 7 press release from the <strong>National Retail Federation</strong> and <strong>Hackett Associates</strong>, imports at the nation's major container ports are expected to remain at elevated levels, as retailers continue to bring in cargo ahead of increased tariffs on China and threats against other countries. ​</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>John Morris</strong>, president of the Industrial and Logistics business for <strong>CBRE</strong> in the Americas, notes that some front-loading of cargo has had a marginal impact on storage capacity, but not at alarming levels. Morris adds that current warehouse space is adequate and that third-party logistics providers have leased more facilities in recent years. ​</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Gene Seroka</strong>, executive director of the <strong>Port of Los Angeles</strong>, stated at a press conference on February 19 that the Southern California region has more than 2 billion square meters of storage, distribution, and order fulfillment center space. Seroka expressed confidence that they have the capacity to handle even cargo arriving at these elevated levels. ​</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong></h5>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">New tariffs implemented by the <strong>Trump administration</strong> and port labor concerns are driving shippers to front-load cargo.​</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>National Retail Federation</strong> and <strong>Hackett Associates</strong> expect imports at major container ports to remain at elevated levels.​</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>John Morris</strong> from <strong>CBRE</strong> states that cargo front-loading has had only a marginal impact on storage capacity and is not at concerning levels.​</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Gene Seroka</strong> from the <strong>Port of Los Angeles</strong> notes that Southern California has more than 2 billion square meters of warehouse space and has the capacity to handle elevated cargo levels.</p></li>
</ul>
