<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>International Energy Agency (IEA)</strong> Executive Director <strong>Fatih Birol</strong> warned on April 16, 2026 that commercial flights across <strong>Europe</strong> may have to be cancelled if the war with <strong>Iran</strong> halts oil shipments through the <strong>Persian Gulf</strong>. In his remarks, Birol stated that Europe has roughly <strong>six weeks</strong> of <strong>jet fuel</strong> left to support current consumption, and that if the supply disruption persists, aircraft waiting on taxiways would soon be unable to refuel. The <strong>IEA</strong> chief emphasized that the emerging picture represents a risk of the scale of the <strong>&ldquo;largest energy crisis ever faced&rdquo;</strong> since the agency&rsquo;s founding, noting that European airlines with limited short-term reserves are among the most fragile actors in this scenario.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to Birol, any interruption in oil shipments through the <strong>Persian Gulf</strong> would trigger a domino effect across the global energy market, with downstream sectors such as aviation, shipping and trucking feeling the impact first, as they rely directly on refined-product availability. Shortfalls in <strong>jet fuel</strong>, <strong>diesel</strong> and <strong>bunker fuel</strong> would not only push prices higher but could also create physical supply shortages. Birol reminded observers that most of Europe&rsquo;s strategic petroleum reserves are held as <strong>crude oil</strong>, while the tightest stress is concentrated in refined products. Releasing crude from reserves and moving it through refineries could take weeks, meaning the market effects of any crisis cannot be balanced quickly and passenger airlines would have to revise operational plans rapidly.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>IEA</strong>&rsquo;s assessment stresses that in such a scenario <strong>no country is immune</strong>; major consumers including the <strong>United States</strong>, <strong>Japan</strong>, <strong>South Korea</strong> and <strong>Germany</strong> would all be directly affected by a squeeze on Gulf-sourced flows. Birol highlighted the possibility that global stocks of certain oil products could erode rapidly, with specific product groups facing a real risk of <strong>&ldquo;dry up&rdquo;</strong>. The agency&rsquo;s warning is not confined to price signals: it also draws attention to the physical insufficiency of cross-continental logistics capacity. Fuel prioritization for aircraft, ship and rail operators, <strong>force majeure</strong> declarations and emergency route revisions come into focus in this context.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In the <strong>IEA</strong>&rsquo;s April 2026 <strong>Oil Market Report (OMR)</strong>, the reopening of the <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong> is described as <strong>&ldquo;the single most important variable&rdquo;</strong> for global market balance. The report notes that if this chokepoint &mdash; responsible for roughly one third of seaborne oil trade &mdash; stays closed, <strong>&ldquo;significant disruptions&rdquo;</strong> can be expected. The <strong>OMR</strong> also argues that losses from the Gulf can only be partially offset through coordinated releases of <strong>strategic petroleum reserves</strong>, supply diversification and <strong>OPEC+</strong> cooperation. For Europe specifically, developments are putting pressure on <strong>jet fuel cracks</strong> and accelerating existing fuel stockpiling activity on the continent. The agency is urging governments, airline operators and critical-infrastructure owners to update contingency plans without delay and to step up measures to strengthen the resilience of the supply chain.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. <strong>IEA</strong> Executive Director <strong>Fatih Birol</strong> views flight cancellations in Europe as a plausible outcome if <strong>Persian Gulf</strong> oil flows are cut.<br>
2. The continent holds only about <strong>six weeks</strong> of <strong>jet fuel</strong> cover.<br>
3. Large consumers including the <strong>United States</strong>, <strong>Japan</strong>, <strong>South Korea</strong> and <strong>Germany</strong> are not immune under this scenario.<br>
4. According to the April 2026 <strong>OMR</strong>, reopening of the <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong> is the single most decisive variable for markets.<br>
5. <strong>Strategic petroleum reserves</strong> and <strong>OPEC+</strong> coordination can only partially offset the potential loss.</p>
