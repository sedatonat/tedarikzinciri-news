<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain Think Tank</strong>; <strong>SCB</strong> contributor <strong>Karl Fillhouer</strong> addresses how freight fraud in the supply chain over the past several years has matured into a full-scale crisis — demanding urgent, strategic and federally enforced intervention. According to latest data from <strong>CargoNet</strong>, cargo theft is rising 13% year-over-year in Q2 2025 — with organized crime groups targeting high-value commodities nationwide. The magnitude of financial damage continues to accelerate — far outpacing preventive measures. While cargo theft cost U.S. businesses an estimated 25 billion dollars last year alone, industry stakeholders are calling for a technology-backed and federally unified approach to reducing freight fraud. Freight fraud is not a new problem — but the growth trajectory since 2020 has been striking — with some estimates placing the increase above 1,500%. After a brief plateau at the beginning of 2025, fraud surged again in the second quarter — with average theft values reported reaching 200K dollars per incident.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>cargo theft</strong> and <strong>freight fraud</strong> represent one of the fastest-growing threat categories for the U.S. supply chain. <strong>CargoNet</strong>, a subsidiary of <strong>Verisk Analytics</strong> based in <strong>Jersey City, New Jersey</strong>, is a cargo theft database and prevention services provider. <strong>Strategic theft</strong> is an evolution of classic physical theft — perpetrated through counterfeit carrier identity (<strong>identity theft</strong>), <strong>fictitious pickup</strong>, <strong>double brokering</strong>, <strong>load board fraud</strong>, <strong>email spoofing</strong>, and <strong>phishing</strong> — the primary modern fraud types. The <strong>FBI</strong>'s <strong>Internet Crime Complaint Center</strong> (<strong>IC3</strong>) reported <strong>Business Email Compromise</strong> (<strong>BEC</strong>) losses at 2.7 billion dollars in 2024 — with the logistics sector among primary targets. Hot-spot regions include <strong>California</strong> (<strong>Los Angeles</strong>, <strong>Long Beach</strong>, <strong>Inland Empire</strong>), <strong>Texas</strong> (<strong>Dallas-Fort Worth</strong>, <strong>Houston</strong>, <strong>Laredo</strong>), <strong>Illinois</strong> (<strong>Chicago</strong>), <strong>New Jersey</strong>, <strong>Florida</strong> (<strong>Miami</strong>), <strong>Georgia</strong> (<strong>Atlanta</strong>), and <strong>Tennessee</strong> (<strong>Memphis</strong>) as principal U.S. cargo theft regions.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>cargo theft</strong> prevention technologies include <strong>GPS tracking</strong>, <strong>geofencing</strong>, <strong>tamper-evident</strong> locks, <strong>covert tracking</strong>, <strong>video surveillance</strong>, <strong>license plate reader</strong> (<strong>LPR</strong>), <strong>biometric driver verification</strong>, <strong>blockchain</strong> chain of custody, <strong>AI</strong>-based carrier validation, <strong>FMCSA</strong> (<strong>Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration</strong>) <strong>SAFER</strong> database queries, <strong>MC</strong>/<strong>USDOT</strong> number verification, and platforms such as <strong>SmartHop</strong>, <strong>Highway</strong>, <strong>Truckstop</strong>, <strong>DAT Freight &amp; Analytics</strong>, <strong>Carrier411</strong>, <strong>Carrier Assure</strong>, <strong>Highway by FreightWaves</strong>, <strong>RXO</strong>, <strong>Convoy</strong> (shutting down), <strong>Uber Freight</strong>, <strong>Loadsmart</strong>, <strong>FourKites</strong>, <strong>Project44</strong>, <strong>Tive</strong>, <strong>Roambee</strong>, <strong>Sensitech</strong>, <strong>ORBCOMM</strong>, <strong>Geotab</strong>, <strong>Samsara</strong>, and <strong>Motive</strong> (formerly <strong>KeepTruckin</strong>) — primary cargo security and visibility platforms.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, federal intervention and regulation involve <strong>FMCSA</strong>, <strong>FBI</strong>, <strong>U.S. Department of Transportation</strong> (<strong>DOT</strong>), <strong>Department of Homeland Security</strong> (<strong>DHS</strong>), <strong>Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency</strong> (<strong>CISA</strong>), and <strong>Customs and Border Protection</strong> (<strong>CBP</strong>) as principal federal agencies. <strong>Combatting Organized Retail Crime Act</strong> (<strong>CORCA</strong>) and the <strong>INFORM Consumers Act</strong> (2023) are primary U.S. federal statutes. <strong>National Insurance Crime Bureau</strong> (<strong>NICB</strong>), <strong>Transported Asset Protection Association</strong> (<strong>TAPA</strong>), <strong>Cargo Theft Recovery Network</strong> (<strong>CTRN</strong>), and <strong>Pharmaceutical Cargo Security Coalition</strong> (<strong>PCSC</strong>) are principal industry representation and prevention organizations. <strong>California AB 2390</strong> (September 2024) escalates penalties for cargo theft — with <strong>Texas HB 4128</strong>, <strong>Florida HB 549</strong>, and <strong>Georgia HB 451</strong> as principal state-level cargo theft statutes. <strong>TIA</strong> (<strong>Transportation Intermediaries Association</strong>), <strong>OOIDA</strong> (<strong>Owner-Operator Independent Drivers Association</strong>), <strong>ATA</strong> (<strong>American Trucking Associations</strong>), and <strong>NMFTA</strong> (<strong>National Motor Freight Traffic Association</strong>) are principal U.S. highway transportation sector organizations. In conclusion, as <strong>Karl Fillhouer</strong> emphasizes, the freight fraud crisis represents one of the most critical security issues facing the U.S. supply chain — requiring coordinated implementation of technology investment, federal coordination, and industry collaboration.</p>
