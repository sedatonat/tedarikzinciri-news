<p style="text-align: justify">Commercial shipping in the Black Sea has been hit by yet another attack report. The <strong>Russian-flagged MIDVOLGA-2</strong> tanker reported coming under attack while transiting approximately <strong>80 nautical miles (129 km)</strong> off Turkey's northern coast. Turkey's <strong>Maritime Affairs Directorate</strong> confirmed that the attack information was communicated directly by the vessel but noted that <strong>no assistance was requested</strong>. The ship has maintained its cargo and operational capacity while altering course toward <strong>Sinop</strong> port.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The MIDVOLGA-2 was carrying <strong>sunflower oil</strong> from Russia to Georgia, and all <strong>13 crew members aboard were reported safe</strong>. Authorities confirmed that the vessel was proceeding normally with its engines and that no emergency status had been recorded. Turkish authorities did not provide additional technical details regarding the incident and made no statement about the nature, source, or method of the attack.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The attack report emerged shortly after mounting tensions against commercial vessels in the Black Sea. Last week, <strong>Ukrainian naval drones</strong> had targeted two <strong>sanctioned tankers</strong> linked to Russia. Those attacks were assessed as part of a strategy to pressure Russian oil exports. Whether the MIDVOLGA-2 incident is connected to those attacks remains unclear, though the current regional tensions have rapidly elevated the matter to international attention.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Turkish President <strong>Tayyip Erdoğan</strong> emphasized earlier this week that any attack on commercial vessels in the Black Sea was <strong>"unacceptable"</strong> and issued a warning to <strong>"all related sides."</strong> Ankara views the preservation of maritime security in the Black Sea as a critical priority for regional stability. Following the incident, Turkish authorities are monitoring the situation closely, and it has been noted that regional security coordination may be undertaken if needed.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to information reported by Reuters, despite the attack report, the MIDVOLGA-2's navigational safety or operational integrity was not compromised. The vessel reported only the attack information, with no reports of leaks, damage, or fire, raising the possibility that the incident may have been a harassment operation against commercial shipping.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The security environment in the Black Sea has become increasingly fragile over the past two years due to the Russia–Ukraine war. Kyiv's expansion of maritime drone capabilities has led to an increase in naval attacks on Moscow's military and logistical targets. Commercial vessels operating in the region, particularly tankers transiting along <strong>logistics corridors</strong>, are experiencing greater exposure to these risks. This latest incident near Turkish waters underscores just how sensitive a geopolitical arena the Black Sea remains for commercial trade security.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Turkish authorities are tracking the vessel's progress toward <strong>Sinop</strong> port and maintaining heightened alert for any possible damage or security threat. Following the incident, the international maritime community has begun conducting new risk assessments regarding the safety of commercial navigation in the Black Sea. Although no official findings have been released regarding who carried out the attack, diplomatic repercussions are expected given the sensitive power balance in the region.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The MIDVOLGA-2 tanker incident is regarded as the latest example of escalating threats faced by commercial vessels in the Black Sea. It has been noted that maritime shipping operators in the region may be forced to adopt new measures concerning risk management, route planning, and insurance costs.</p>
<p><br></p>
