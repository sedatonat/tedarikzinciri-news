<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>TPM26</strong>, opening this week in <strong>Long Beach, California</strong>, was expected to feature the familiar crises affecting international container shipping as participants registered for the conference. But no one anticipated the headline that would dominate proceedings. Launched 48 hours after <strong>the U.S.</strong> and <strong>Israel</strong> conducted multiple missile strikes on <strong>Iran</strong> targeting military, nuclear, commercial, and settlement facilities, the annual event in its 22nd year by <strong>S&P Global</strong> has instead become a critical forum where supply chain dynamics are being redrawn around a new geopolitical reality. With <strong>BCO</strong> annual <strong>service contract</strong> negotiations already underway from a supply chain perspective, the conference is reshaping contract pricing dynamics. <strong>3PL</strong> providers and major line operators are convening at the table and agreeing that the <strong>capacity-rate-route</strong> triangle must be redrawn across disrupted corridors.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The conflict is rapidly escalating to encompass more than 14 countries and territories across the <strong>Middle East</strong>, with ocean shipping and other modes grinding to a halt on several key routes. <strong>TPM</strong> participants—a mix of senior carrier executives, third-party logistics providers, and <strong>beneficial cargo owners</strong>—can only speculate about the days ahead. <strong>Jeremy Nixon</strong>, <strong>chief executive officer</strong> of <strong>Ocean Network Express (ONE)</strong>, characterizes the conflict as "<strong>another Black Swan event</strong>." <strong>ONE</strong> is a joint venture of <strong>Japanese</strong> container lines <strong>Nippon Yusen Kaisha</strong>, <strong>Mitsui O.S.K. Lines</strong>, and <strong>K Line</strong>, and is a major player on <strong>Asia-Europe</strong> and <strong>Asia-North America</strong> routes.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">By day three, approximately 750 vessels queue at the now-closed <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong>, with 350 on each side and about 100 registered as container vessels. The <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong> is the sole passage for commercial shipping transiting from the <strong>Persian Gulf</strong> to the <strong>Gulf of Oman</strong> and open waters, and the chokepoint is affecting 15 million barrels of crude oil shipments daily, according to <strong>Janet Yellen</strong>, opening speaker at <strong>TPM26</strong> and former <strong>U.S. Treasury Secretary</strong> and <strong>Federal Reserve</strong> chair. From a supply chain perspective, a backup of this magnitude creates pressure that could translate into added demurrage costs for <strong>Asian</strong> refineries in crude supply flows, rate spikes in the <strong>VLCC</strong> charter market, and could put the <strong>Cape of Good Hope</strong> routing on the table.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Among the conflict's first impacts on global trade and shipping is a 13 percent jump in crude prices, with <strong>Brent</strong> reaching a peak of $82 per barrel. <strong>Nixon</strong> noted at <strong>TPM26</strong> that if the conflict extends beyond a few weeks and producers are forced to curtail production, oil prices could reach $100 per barrel. From a supply chain perspective, the implications are materializing as <strong>liner</strong> carriers are pushing <strong>BAF</strong>/<strong>EBS</strong> surcharges upward and <strong>airfreight</strong> capacity is tightening. The extent to which container freight rates will be impacted remains unclear, but major line operators are rapidly passing war surcharges to <strong>BCO</strong>s. As a result, <strong>TPM26</strong> has become a turning point where global container shipping is recasting its 2026 outlook under a Black Swan wave.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. <strong>TPM26</strong> opens 48 hours after the <strong>Iran</strong> conflict commences.<br>
2. 750 vessels queue at the <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong>; 100 are container ships.<br>
3. <strong>Yellen</strong> notes 15 million barrels of crude oil daily are being affected.<br>
4. <strong>Brent</strong> climbs 13 percent to $82, with a $100 scenario in play.<br>
5. <strong>ONE</strong> CEO <strong>Nixon</strong> characterizes the conflict as a "Black Swan" event.</p>
