<p style="text-align: justify">Persistent congestion in European ports clearly reveals that the region's major ports suffer from <strong>insufficient buffer capacity</strong> in both marine terminals and internal logistics networks. According to Kuehne + Nagel's <strong>SeaExplorer</strong> visibility tool, Europe's three main import gateways — <strong>Rotterdam, Antwerp, and Hamburg</strong> — remain in the "severely disrupted operations" category.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Chantal McRoberts, advisory director at Drewry Supply Chain Advisors, notes that the root cause of congestion is Europe's major port network's <strong>poor elasticity against high volumes</strong>. The current modest improvement does not signify lasting relief; ports struggle both to digest accumulated workload and to manage seasonal transitions.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h4 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Lack of buffer capacity renders the system fragile</strong></h4>\n<p style="text-align: justify">McRoberts summarizes capacity challenges in the European port system across three key points:</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>1. Terminals lack adequate storage and staging areas</strong></h5>\n<p>When vessels arrive with larger-than-expected volumes, buffer areas for short-term container storage fill rapidly. This slows discharge and loading processes.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>2. Hinterland transport (Northern Europe rail and barge networks) operates under severe strain</strong></h5>\n<ul>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Insufficient train slots,</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Barge delays,</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Driver and truck shortages</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p style="text-align: justify">push port exits to bottleneck point.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>3. System lacks flexibility against volume fluctuations</strong></h5>\n<p>"The arrival of even a slightly larger vessel immediately squeezes the system," McRoberts says.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h4 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Congestion repeats as demand waves arrive</strong></h4>\n<p style="text-align: justify">The congestion cycle experienced in European ports throughout 2025 unfolded as follows:</p>\n<ul>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Early summer</strong> saw intensity drop due to vessel schedule adjustments and blank sailing impacts.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>September–October</strong> saw cargo volumes rise again on Asia–Europe lanes.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Terminal shelf systems and hinterland capacity</strong> could not absorb the new volume.</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Result: a "relief–congestion" cycle repeating.</p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">McRoberts notes that current recovery stems only from ports' efforts at load shifting and accelerating delayed vessel clearances.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h4 style="text-align: justify"><strong>The solution is not only capacity investment: load distribution is essential</strong></h4>\n<p style="text-align: justify">According to experts, European ports face difficulty expanding within their own limits; these terminals are already surrounded by dense urban areas. Thus, the solution lies not merely in increasing capacity, but in <strong>distributing load more widely across regional ports</strong>.</p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Recommended strategies:</p>\n<ul>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Greater utilization of secondary ports</strong> (Gdansk, Zeebrugge, Wilhelmshaven, Koper, etc.)</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Strengthening feeder networks</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Accelerating rail and barge investments</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Digitizing port–hinterland synchronization</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p style="text-align: justify">While major ports will continue to serve their primary gateway role, bringing surrounding ports into operation can ease congestion.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h4 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Outlook: Cargo flows will become more volatile</strong></h4>\n<p style="text-align: justify">As 2026 approaches, supply chain cycles will face increased volatility due to:</p>\n<ul>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">tariffs,</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">vessel route changes,</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">growing vessel sizes,</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">block train capacity constraints</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p style="text-align: justify">and other factors.</p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Unless European ports develop a "buffer space approach" to absorb this volatility, recurring congestion is inevitable.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h4 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong></h4>\n<ul>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Rotterdam, Antwerp, and Hamburg are in "severely disrupted" status.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The core problem in European ports: <strong>buffer capacity shortage</strong>.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Congestion is structural, not seasonal.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Solution: <strong>More balanced distribution of cargo to secondary ports</strong>.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Hinterland infrastructure (rail–barge) is a critical bottleneck.</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p><br></p>\n<p>----------</p>\n\n<p>--------------------</p>\n<p><strong>Author: </strong><a
