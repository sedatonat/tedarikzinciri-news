<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Iran</strong> has announced a partial closure of the <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong>, citing what the country's leadership characterizes as <strong>security precautions</strong> during a period when the <strong>Revolutionary Guard</strong> is conducting military exercises in the region. The move creates notable disruptions to shipping flows and insurance premiums at a critical maritime chokepoint through which a significant portion of global crude oil passes, even if only in the short term. From a supply chain perspective, transit windows for <strong>VLCC</strong>, <strong>Suezmax</strong>, and <strong>LR2</strong> tankers are being recalculated during the military exercise period, and crude supply flow schedules for <strong>Asian</strong> refineries face minor adjustments. The situation underscores the geopolitical vulnerability of crude oil supplies originating from the <strong>Middle East</strong> and affects <strong>insurance war risk</strong> premiums.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to <strong>CNBC</strong> reports, the temporary closure beginning February 17 is aimed at ensuring maritime security during the <strong>Revolutionary Guard</strong>'s <strong>"Smart Control of the Strait of Hormuz"</strong> exercise, which is expected to include live missile tests. The closure coincides with a period when the <strong>U.S.</strong> and <strong>Iran</strong> are conducting second-round negotiations in <strong>Geneva, Switzerland</strong> to discuss <strong>Iran</strong>'s nuclear program. It also marks the first time the strait has been closed since the <strong>Trump</strong> administration began threatening military action in the region in January. According to <strong>CNN</strong> reports, the <strong>U.S.</strong> military is continuing a buildup that precedes the negotiations and constitutes a significant concentration of air and naval assets in the <strong>Middle East</strong>. This concentration adds an additional pressure layer to the regional maritime security equation.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In 2025, approximately 13 million barrels of crude oil pass through the <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong> daily, representing roughly 31% of global seaborne crude oil flows. <strong>Jacob Larsen</strong>, head of security and safety at the <strong>Baltic and International Maritime Council (BIMCO)</strong>, stated in comments to <strong>CNBC</strong> that the partial closure will likely cause minor delays but will not result in a major disruption. From a supply chain perspective, this assessment indicates that <strong>Asian</strong> refinery crude supplies are expected to remain relatively resilient provided the exercise remains limited in scale and duration. Flows of crude oil from <strong>Saudi Arabia</strong>, the <strong>UAE</strong>, <strong>Kuwait</strong>, and <strong>Iraq</strong> to <strong>China</strong>, <strong>India</strong>, <strong>South Korea</strong>, and <strong>Japan</strong> are expected to return to normal rhythms outside the exercise window.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">During this process, negotiations between the <strong>U.S.</strong> and <strong>Iran</strong> are focused solely on <strong>Iran</strong>'s nuclear program, with crackdowns on protesters—which <strong>Donald Trump</strong> initially cited as justification for military action—not on the agenda. <strong>Iran</strong>'s Foreign Minister <strong>Abbas Araghchi</strong> stated in remarks to reporters that the two sides have reached agreement on the <strong>"guiding principles"</strong> of the negotiations, but more work remains to be done. From a supply chain perspective, the preservation of negotiation grounds suggests that <strong>war risk</strong> premium increases in freight markets could gradually ease. In conclusion, the brief closure of the <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong> stands out as an important case study in which the geopolitical contact points of the global crude oil supply chain are being tested.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Iran</strong> partially closes the <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong> as of February 17.<br>
2. The closure is in effect during the <strong>Revolutionary Guard</strong>'s <strong>"Smart Control"</strong> exercise.<br>
3. In 2025, 13 million barrels of crude pass through the strait daily, representing 31% of global seaborne flows.<br>
4. <strong>BIMCO</strong> expects disruptions to remain minor in scale.<br>
5. <strong>U.S.</strong>-<strong>Iran</strong> negotiations continue in <strong>Geneva</strong> focused on the nuclear program.</p>
