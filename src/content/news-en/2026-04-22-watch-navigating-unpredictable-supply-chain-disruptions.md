<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; <strong>Chris Demetroulis</strong> (<strong>Managing Director</strong>, <strong>Transportation Practice</strong>, <strong>Gallagher</strong>) in an interview with <strong>Robert J. Bowman</strong> (<strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong>); providing expert analysis on some of the biggest risks supply chains face today and how the insurance market is responding. The flood of disruptions currently plaguing global supply chains is forcing businesses and countries into a "wait-and-see mode" as they struggle to respond to unpredictable events—according to <strong>Demetroulis</strong>. Amid economic uncertainty and rising geopolitical tensions, cargo theft is climbing—he notes. Moreover, it is becoming increasingly sophisticated. GPS tracking enables thieves to identify the most advantageous point at which to strike. Simultaneously, cyberattacks are infiltrating companies' information systems. Insurance carriers are responding by investing in former FBI and police personnel to bolster staffing and increase their ability to stay ahead of criminals. "Staying ahead is difficult," says <strong>Demetroulis</strong>. Not surprisingly, companies are filing a growing number of insurance claims—and insurance pricing is reflecting that.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Arthur J. Gallagher &amp; Co</strong>, based in <strong>Rolling Meadows, Illinois, U.S.</strong>, with <strong>J. Patrick Gallagher Jr.</strong> as CEO and founded in 1927, provides insurance brokerage, risk management, and consulting services globally. <strong>Gallagher's Transportation Practice</strong> focuses particularly on the trucking, logistics, freight, ship owners, and <strong>3PL</strong> sectors. Other major global insurance brokerage firms include <strong>Marsh McLennan</strong> (<strong>Marsh</strong>, <strong>Mercer</strong>, <strong>Oliver Wyman</strong>, <strong>Guy Carpenter</strong>, with <strong>John Doyle</strong> as CEO); <strong>Aon</strong> (<strong>Greg Case</strong> CEO, <strong>Dublin/London</strong>); <strong>Willis Towers Watson</strong> (<strong>Carl Hess</strong> CEO); <strong>Lockton Companies</strong> (<strong>Ron Lockton</strong> CEO, <strong>Kansas City</strong>); <strong>Brown &amp; Brown</strong>; <strong>Hub International</strong>; <strong>NFP</strong> (owned by <strong>Aon</strong>); <strong>USI Insurance</strong>; <strong>Truist Insurance Holdings</strong>—all key ecosystem players. <strong>Roanoke Insurance</strong>, <strong>Avalon Risk Management</strong>, <strong>TT Club</strong>, and <strong>UK P&amp;I Club</strong> are leading transportation-specific insurance firms. Leading <strong>marine cargo</strong> and <strong>logistics liability</strong> insurance underwriters include <strong>AIG</strong>, <strong>Chubb</strong>, <strong>Allianz Global Corporate &amp; Specialty</strong> (<strong>AGCS</strong>), <strong>Munich Re</strong>, <strong>Swiss Re</strong>, <strong>Zurich Insurance</strong>, <strong>Lloyd's of London</strong>, <strong>Tokio Marine</strong>, <strong>QBE</strong>, and <strong>Travelers</strong>—the major insurance players.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the main <strong>cargo theft</strong> types are: (1) <strong>strategic cargo theft</strong> (fictitious carrier records—fictitious pickup, identity theft); (2) <strong>opportunistic cargo theft</strong> (unattended vehicles, sleeping drivers); (3) <strong>warehouse theft</strong>; (4) <strong>pilferage</strong> (piecemeal theft); (5) <strong>cyber-enabled theft</strong> (system hacking); (6) <strong>insider theft</strong>—the main theft typologies. <strong>CargoNet</strong> (owned by <strong>Verisk Analytics</strong>, <strong>Keith Lewis</strong> Vice President); <strong>BSI Supply Chain Services and Solutions</strong> (<strong>Tony Pelli</strong> Practice Director); <strong>Sensitech</strong> (owned by <strong>Carrier Global</strong>); <strong>Overhaul</strong>; <strong>Tive</strong>; <strong>Tracfone</strong>—are leading <strong>cargo theft</strong> tracking and prevention providers. In 2024, <strong>BSI</strong> reports a 273% annual increase in the U.S. for <strong>truckload theft</strong>—with <strong>strategic cargo theft</strong> being the primary driver. Major risk zones include <strong>California</strong> (<strong>Los Angeles</strong> and <strong>Inland Empire</strong>), <strong>Texas</strong>, <strong>Florida</strong>, <strong>Illinois</strong> (<strong>Chicago</strong>), <strong>Georgia</strong>, and <strong>New Jersey</strong>—the leading high-theft states.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the main <strong>supply chain cyber risk</strong> types are: (1) <strong>ransomware</strong> (<strong>Maersk NotPetya</strong> 2017; <strong>Toll Group</strong>, <strong>Expeditors</strong> 2022); (2) <strong>business email compromise</strong> (<strong>BEC</strong>); (3) <strong>supply chain attack</strong> (<strong>SolarWinds</strong>, <strong>Kaseya</strong>); (4) <strong>OT/ICS attack</strong> (<strong>Colonial Pipeline</strong> 2021); (5) <strong>third-party vendor breach</strong>—the main cyber risk categories. Leading <strong>supply chain cybersecurity</strong> firms include <strong>CrowdStrike</strong> (<strong>George Kurtz</strong> CEO); <strong>Palo Alto Networks</strong> (<strong>Nikesh Arora</strong> CEO); <strong>Mandiant</strong> (owned by <strong>Google Cloud</strong>); <strong>SecurityScorecard</strong>; <strong>BitSight</strong>; <strong>Black Kite</strong>; <strong>RiskRecon</strong> (owned by <strong>Mastercard</strong>); <strong>Prevalent</strong>; <strong>OneTrust</strong>; <strong>Bishop Fox</strong>—key ecosystem players. <strong>NIST</strong> (<strong>National Institute of Standards and Technology</strong>, <strong>SP 800-161 Supply Chain Risk Management</strong>); <strong>CISA</strong> (<strong>Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency</strong>, <strong>Sean Plankey</strong> Director nominee); <strong>FBI Cyber Division</strong>; <strong>Secret Service</strong>—are the leading U.S. federal cyber authorities. <strong>TIA</strong> (<strong>Transportation Intermediaries Association</strong>) and <strong>FMCSA</strong> (<strong>Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration</strong>) are the main transportation regulatory authorities. In conclusion, <strong>Demetroulis's</strong> disruption and insurance advice suggests that global supply chain risk management and the insurance market are being fundamentally redesigned—for supply chain managers, <strong>cargo theft</strong> prevention, <strong>cyber risk</strong> management, and <strong>captive insurance</strong> appear to be the main strategic priorities.</p>
