<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Neil Shearing</strong>, chief economist at <strong>Capital Economics</strong> and author of <em>The Fractured Age</em>, emphasizes in his latest analysis that globalization has not died, but rather changed direction.</p>
<p><br>
Trade volumes continue to reach historic highs; ports are full, ships are operating.</p>
<p><br>
Yet today, <strong>the fundamental dynamics determining the flow of trade have shifted from economic efficiency to geopolitical strategy</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to Shearing, the period we are in is not the <strong>"end of globalization" but rather the "fracturing of trade"</strong> era.</p>
<p><br>
This transformation creates new opportunities and risks for maritime shipping and supply chains.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Globalization Did Not Die, It Changed Direction</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">Despite proclamations that globalization is dead, <strong>the data tells a different story</strong>:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Global trade volume is tracking near record levels.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Port operations continue at high intensity.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Goods flows are not declining, merely <strong>shifting toward new routes.</strong></p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">Shearing explains this as follows:</p>
<blockquote style="text-align: justify">"Trade is not declining; it is changing direction, fragmenting, and being reshaped."</blockquote>
<p style="text-align: justify">In other words, we are witnessing <strong>geopolitical-driven restructuring</strong>, not "deglobalization."</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Trade Order Fragmented Along the US–China Axis</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">Today, the global trade system is being reshaped around two major economic blocs:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The Western bloc led by the US</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The rising economy network centered on China</strong></p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to Shearing, this means <strong>not a collapse of trade, but a division of trade</strong>.<br>
This division is particularly evident in <strong>strategic sectors</strong>:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Semiconductors</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Pharmaceutical ingredients</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Dual-use goods</strong> – technologies that can be used for both civilian and military purposes.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">Production and supply chains in these sectors are rapidly being <strong>repositioned</strong> due to security concerns.<br>
New manufacturing hubs are rising in <strong>India, Vietnam, Mexico</strong>, and <strong>Southeast Asian</strong> countries.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>From Smartphones to Drones: Emerging Manufacturing Hubs</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">Shearing's example clearly demonstrates the scale of the change:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Five years ago, <strong>70% of smartphones sold in the US were manufactured in China.</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Today, that figure has dropped to <strong>25%.</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">More than half of smartphones imported into the US now come from <strong>India and Vietnam.</strong></p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">Over the same period, <strong>the share of total US imports from China has fallen from 20% to 10%</strong>.<br>
The US now purchases more from <strong>Vietnam, Mexico, and India.</strong></p>
<p><br>
Meanwhile, China has redirected its exports toward <strong>Russia and other emerging economies.</strong></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The result: Trade does not end, only <strong>routes change.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>The Return of Geopolitics: From Economic Decision to Strategic Decision</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">The fundamental reason for the reshaping of trade flows is <strong>economic rationale being replaced by strategic priorities.</strong></p>
<p><br>
Both Washington and Beijing are now setting their policies not only on the basis of cost and efficiency, but on <strong>geopolitical security.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">As part of this transformation, the concept of "friend-shoring" is also gaining traction.</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The US</strong> is moving to establish supply chains through "allied nations."</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>China</strong> is increasing its integration with <strong>Russia and Central Asia</strong> for energy and raw material supply.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">This situation benefits countries like <strong>Vietnam, India, and Mexico</strong>; as the global manufacturing network is now concentrating in these regions.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Implications for Global Maritime Shipping: Routes Are Being Redrawn</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">The most concrete impact of this geopolitical restructuring is seen in <strong>the changing of maritime shipping routes</strong>.<br>
Shearing makes the following observations:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Trade is diversifying rather than declining:</strong><br>
While new lines emerge in strategic products, volume growth generally continues.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>New routes are longer:</strong><br>
As manufacturing shifts from Asia to Mexico or India, shipping times lengthen — which <strong>increases transport demand.</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>While some cargo flows smoothly, volatility in strategic loads is increasing.</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Flexibility becomes a competitive advantage in the freight sector.</strong></p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">In short, <strong>global shipping does not end — it is being reshaped.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Volatility Becomes the New Normal</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">The most distinctive feature of the new era: <strong>volatility</strong>.</p>
<p><br>
Geopolitical competition, sudden sanctions, embargoes, or political crises can <strong>change established trade flows overnight.</strong></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Shearing warns:</p>
<blockquote style="text-align: justify">"New tariffs, sanctions, or political ruptures can overturn established flows in an instant.<br>
Shipowners must invest in fleets that can respond quickly to demand fluctuations and route changes."</blockquote>
<p style="text-align: justify">Consequently, for the maritime sector, <strong>flexible fleet management, route diversification, and geopolitical awareness</strong> are becoming strategic requirements.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Conclusion: Globalization Did Not End, It Took a New Form</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">The era of <strong>borderless, frictionless trade growth has ended.</strong></p>
<p><br>
In its place is emerging <strong>a global trade network redirected among geopolitical blocs and shaped by strategic products</strong>.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In Shearing's words:</p>
<blockquote style="text-align: justify">"Trade did not die – it is changing direction, fragmenting, and being redirected."</blockquote>
<p style="text-align: justify">The task for the maritime sector is clear:<br>
 <strong>To understand not the death of globalization, but its rebirth, and to adapt to this new fragmented world order.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong></h5>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Globalization has not ended; it has entered a process of geopolitical-driven restructuring.</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Two economic blocs</strong> centered on the <strong>US and China</strong> are forming.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Manufacturing is shifting from China to India, Vietnam, and Mexico</strong>; trade flows are changing.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Strategic sectors (chips, pharmaceuticals, defense products)</strong> are being transported via new routes.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Friend-shoring</strong> is strengthening the US strategy of sourcing from allied nations.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Shipowners and freight operators</strong> must shift toward <strong>flexible fleet and route planning</strong> to counter demand volatility.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Conclusion:</strong> Trade has not died; it continues to flow across <strong>a map redrawn by geopolitics.</strong></p></li>
</ul>
