<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain Podcast</strong>; moderated by <strong>Bob Bowman</strong>; examines how the second <strong>Trump</strong> Administration is enforcing anti-forced-labor laws in global supply chains. When it comes to combating forced labor, the government has an array of tools at its disposal. What approach is <strong>U.S. Customs and Border Protection</strong> (<strong>CBP</strong>) taking under the <strong>Trump</strong> Administration? In this episode, the government's enforceable forms, inspections, and the thicket of both civil and criminal investigations are examined. The guide is <strong>Angela Santos</strong>, partner and customs practice leader at <strong>ArentFox Schiff</strong> law firm. She explains what manufacturers, importers, and other entities in the extended supply chain need to do to remain compliant with anti-forced-labor laws. <strong>ArentFox Schiff</strong>'s 2025 <strong>"Guide for Global Businesses: Navigating the Complexities of Today's Forced Labor Laws"</strong> serves as the core reference material for the discussion.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>U.S. Customs and Border Protection</strong> (<strong>CBP</strong>) is a federal agency under the <strong>Department of Homeland Security</strong> (<strong>DHS</strong>) — <strong>Pete Flores</strong>, <strong>Acting CBP Commissioner</strong>, assumes office during the <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> period. The <strong>Forced Labor Division</strong> is responsible for <strong>CBP</strong>'s forced labor enforcement unit. The <strong>Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act</strong> (<strong>UFLPA</strong>) was signed by <strong>Biden</strong> on December 23, 2021 — and went into effect on June 21, 2022 — applying a <strong>rebuttable presumption</strong> for products sourced from the <strong>Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region</strong> (<strong>XUAR</strong>) — requiring importers to prove that products were not produced through forced labor. <strong>Section 307 of the Tariff Act of 1930</strong> (<strong>19 U.S.C. § 1307</strong>) bans the importation into the <strong>U.S.</strong> of products made with forced labor — modernized by the <strong>Trade Facilitation and Trade Enforcement Act</strong> (<strong>TFTEA</strong>) in 2016. The <strong>Withhold Release Order</strong> (<strong>WRO</strong>) is the primary tool <strong>CBP</strong> uses to halt shipments suspected of forced labor — a <strong>Finding</strong> (formal determination) bans products where forced labor is proven entirely.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>UFLPA Entity List</strong> is <strong>CBP</strong>'s list of entities identified as linked to forced labor — as of end-2024, 100+ Chinese companies are on the list — <strong>cotton</strong>, <strong>polysilicon</strong>, <strong>tomato</strong>, <strong>aluminum</strong>, <strong>seafood</strong>, <strong>auto parts</strong>, <strong>solar panels</strong>, <strong>apparel</strong> are the major high-risk sectors. <strong>ArentFox Schiff LLP</strong> is a major <strong>U.S.</strong> law firm with offices in <strong>Washington DC</strong>, <strong>New York</strong>, <strong>Chicago</strong>, <strong>San Francisco</strong>, <strong>Los Angeles</strong>, and <strong>Boston</strong> — <strong>Angela Santos</strong> is <strong>Customs Practice Leader</strong> and <strong>Partner</strong>. Other major <strong>U.S.</strong> customs and trade law firms include <strong>Sandler, Travis & Rosenberg</strong>, <strong>Sidley Austin</strong>, <strong>Crowell & Moring</strong>, <strong>Sharretts, Paley, Carter & Blauvelt</strong>, <strong>Grunfeld, Desiderio, Lebowitz, Silverman & Klestadt</strong>, <strong>Rode & Qualey</strong>, <strong>Husch Blackwell</strong>, <strong>Akin Gump</strong>, <strong>Squire Patton Boggs</strong>, <strong>Hogan Lovells</strong>, and <strong>Mayer Brown</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, concrete steps for forced labor compliance include: (1) <strong>UFLPA Risk Assessment</strong>, supply chain mapping; (2) <strong>supplier audit</strong>, third-party audits — <strong>SGS</strong>, <strong>Bureau Veritas</strong>, <strong>Intertek</strong>, <strong>TUV Rheinland</strong>, <strong>SMETA</strong> (<strong>Sedex Members Ethical Trade Audit</strong>), <strong>BSCI</strong> (<strong>Business Social Compliance Initiative</strong>), <strong>WRAP</strong> (<strong>Worldwide Responsible Accredited Production</strong>); (3) <strong>chain of custody</strong> certification — raw material traceability for <strong>cotton</strong>, <strong>polysilicon</strong>, <strong>aluminum</strong>, <strong>seafood</strong>; (4) <strong>UFLPA Operational Guidance for Importers</strong> (June 2022), <strong>CBP</strong>'s official guidance; (5) coordination with <strong>customs broker</strong>; (6) <strong>Customs Trade Partnership Against Terrorism</strong> (<strong>CTPAT</strong>) and <strong>CTPAT Trade Compliance</strong> programs; (7) <strong>Importer Self-Assessment</strong> (<strong>ISA</strong>); (8) <strong>blockchain</strong> and <strong>DLT</strong> traceability — <strong>IBM Food Trust</strong>, <strong>Everledger</strong>, <strong>Sourcemap</strong>, <strong>TextileGenesis</strong>; (9) <strong>third-party verification</strong> and <strong>traceability service</strong>; (10) <strong>internal audit</strong> and <strong>training program</strong>. The <strong>EU</strong>, through the <strong>Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive</strong> (<strong>CSDDD</strong>) in 2024, has imposed similar obligations — the <strong>EU Forced Labor Regulation</strong> in 2024 bans products made with forced labor. <strong>Germany's Lieferkettengesetz</strong> (<strong>Supply Chain Act</strong>) in 2023, <strong>France's Loi de Vigilance</strong> in 2017, the <strong>UK Modern Slavery Act</strong> in 2015, the <strong>Australia Modern Slavery Act</strong> in 2018, and <strong>Canada's Bill S-211</strong> in 2024 are major national laws. In conclusion, the topic of forced labor compliance that <strong>Angela Santos</strong> addresses with <strong>Bob Bowman</strong> in the podcast is one of the highest priority compliance issues for <strong>U.S.</strong> importers during the <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> period.</p>
