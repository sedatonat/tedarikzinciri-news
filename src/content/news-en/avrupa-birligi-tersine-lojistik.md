<p style="text-align: justify">Europe's leading railway institutions — <strong>CER</strong>, <strong>ERFA</strong>, <strong>UIRR</strong>, <strong>UIP</strong> and <strong>UNIFE</strong> — have issued a strongly-worded joint statement opposing the European Council's approach under the <strong>Weights and Dimensions Directive (WDD)</strong> to grant cross-border transit permits for 44-ton fossil-fuel trucks and mega-trucks known as <strong>European Modular Systems (EMS)</strong> or "<strong>gigaliners</strong>". According to sector representatives, this regulation reverses the EU's long-established <strong>rail–intermodal</strong> policies, raising the risk of "<strong>reverse modal shift</strong>".</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Currently in the EU, cross-border road transport is generally capped at <strong>40 tons</strong>. This limit has been a key parameter making the transport of heavy and long-distance cargo via <strong>rail freight</strong> economically more attractive. The Council's approach eliminates this advantage by permitting 44-ton fossil-fuel trucks. Sector representatives argue this constitutes a policy that <strong>subsidizes roads while weakening railways</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The institutions' statement references a 2024 independent study, noting that this decision will produce three critical consequences:</p>
<ol>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Increased CO₂ Emissions:</strong> Shifting cargo from rail to road will significantly raise emission intensity.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Infrastructure Damage:</strong> Higher axle loads will increase maintenance costs across the EU's road network.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Safety Risk:</strong> The proliferation of gigaliner vehicles poses risks to both highway safety and vulnerable urban areas.</p></li>
</ol>
<p style="text-align: justify">Another factor intensifying the statement's tone is the allegation that the European Commission has backed down on the <strong>Combined Transport Directive</strong>. The railway sector had argued that the EU's <strong>Greening Freight Transport Package</strong> could only balance heavier road transport regulations if paired with parallel measures to incentivize rail investment. However, the Combined Transport Directive is now reportedly being entirely withdrawn or seriously weakened.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The joint statement emphasizes this key passage:<br>
 <strong>"The current Council approach risks achieving the exact opposite: prioritizing cheaper, high-emission road transport over sustainable rail and intermodal chains."</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">For this reason, sector representatives are calling on the <strong>European Parliament</strong> to take a firmer stance once the file completes the Council stage and moves to the <strong>trilogues</strong> process. The Parliament will shape the final text of the WDD through trilateral negotiations among the Council, Commission and Parliament. The railway sector is making three core demands to Parliament:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The 44-ton limit should apply <strong>only to ZEV (Zero-Emission Vehicles)</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>EMSys / gigaliners</strong> vehicles must comply with <strong>intermodal terminal compatibility</strong>, making them loadable onto trains.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Member states must be required to conduct <strong>rigorous impact assessments</strong> before permitting cross-border heavy truck use.</p></li>
</ul>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Sector representatives argue that without these modifications, the EU's <strong>goal of doubling rail freight volume by 2050</strong> will become impossible. At a time when Asia–Europe corridors are diversifying and the EU is tightening its carbon reduction policies, a shift in freight back to roads threatens climate targets, infrastructure budgets and logistics security alike.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Political Balance: The Council – Parliament – Railway Triangle</strong></h3>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>European Council</strong> states it aims with the WDD to "increase logistic flexibility and boost road efficiency".</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>railway sector</strong> believes this approach violates <strong>modal balance</strong> principles and provides "hidden subsidies" to road transport.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>European Parliament</strong> will now have the final say in trilateral negotiations and an opportunity to revisit the decision's environmental, economic and logistics dimensions.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">In conclusion, the WDD file represents a critical test of the EU's vision for the future of freight transport. The sector warns that absent Parliament's intervention, <strong>rail modal share</strong> — a cornerstone of Europe's green logistics policy — may experience a sharp decline in coming years.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>5. Key Points</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Points:</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The Council seeks to open 44-ton fossil trucks and <strong>gigaliner EMS</strong> for cross-border use.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The railway sector says this will trigger <strong>reverse modal shift</strong> and divert cargo to roads.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Withdrawal or dilution of the Combined Transport Directive is viewed as a "betrayal" by the sector.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Three demands from Parliament: 44-ton approval only for ZEV, EMS–intermodal compatibility, mandatory impact assessment.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Otherwise, the EU's <strong>2050 rail freight doubling</strong> goal will become unachievable.</p></li>
</ul>
