<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>supply chain crisis</strong> caused by instability in the Middle East is disrupting <strong>Mediterranean feeder services</strong> and, consequently, <strong>inland transport</strong> in Europe. Although feeder services are maritime, they directly influence when and where containers become available for onward inland movement. They therefore shape rail and road transport indirectly.</p><p style="text-align: justify">Red Sea attacks and security concerns at the Strait of Hormuz have redirected mainline container ships via the Cape of Good Hope, disrupting the orderly arrival of containers at Mediterranean transshipment hubs such as Barcelona, Algeciras, Piraeus, and Valencia. Feeder vessels are forced to wait for containers off mainliners. This uncertainty makes <strong>intermodal bookings</strong> harder to plan.</p><p style="text-align: justify">Rail operators in Europe are encountering unexpected congestion at terminal gates. Existing block train plans are being upended while <strong>delivery dates communicated to cargo owners shift</strong>. Industry analysts said that, assuming the Middle East crisis will not resolve in the short term, intermodal operators need flexible planning capability and investment in <strong>dual feeder hub rotation</strong>. Europe's inland rail operations are losing their advantage of operating independently from ocean volatility.</p>

<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. The Middle East crisis-driven supply chain crunch is disrupting Mediterranean feeder services.<br>
2. It has become uncertain when and where containers will reach inland markets, indirectly affecting rail and road.<br>
3. The arrival rhythm has broken at transshipment hubs such as Barcelona, Algeciras, Piraeus, and Valencia.<br>
4. Unexpected congestion is hitting terminal gates; intermodal bookings cannot be planned.<br>
5. The sector must invest in dual feeder hub rotation and flexible planning.</p>
