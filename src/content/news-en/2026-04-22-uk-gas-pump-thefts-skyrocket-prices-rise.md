<p style="text-align: justify">Petrol station fuel thefts in the <strong>U.K.</strong> are up <strong>62%</strong> compared with a year ago. The figures come from new data compiled by fuel theft recovery firm <strong>Pay My Fuel</strong> for the <strong>BBC</strong> and published on 21 April. The crime wave is being attributed to price spikes driven by the conflict in <strong>Iran</strong>. Fuel debt recovery platform <strong>Forecourt Eye</strong> says theft levels are now worse than 2022, when prices surged sharply following <strong>Russia</strong>'s invasion of <strong>Ukraine</strong>, according to reporting by the <strong>U.K.</strong>'s <strong>Times</strong>. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>downstream petroleum</strong> supply chain creates a value chain across <strong>terminal</strong>, <strong>jobber</strong>, and <strong>retail forecourt</strong> stages via <strong>refinery gate</strong> pricing. The <strong>Argus Northwest Europe</strong> and <strong>Platts CIF NWE</strong> indices provide reference pricing for <strong>EN228</strong> standard petrol and <strong>EN590</strong> diesel.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Thefts are mostly carried out by consumers filling their tanks and driving away without paying. However, there are also reports of criminals arriving at petrol stations with vans full of jerry cans, filling the cans, leaving without paying, and then selling the fuel at impromptu roadside stops. In the <strong>U.K.</strong>, payment for petrol typically involves filling first and then paying inside at a counter or at the pump. While a transition to pay-first systems could theoretically be implemented quickly, it is not attractive because petrol and diesel retailers report that their staff are already experiencing increased abuse due to high fuel prices. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.K.</strong> retail petrol market is divided among <strong>BP</strong>, <strong>Shell</strong>, <strong>Esso (ExxonMobil)</strong>, and supermarket forecourts such as <strong>Tesco</strong>, <strong>Sainsbury's</strong>, <strong>Asda</strong>, and <strong>Morrisons</strong>. <strong>BMM</strong> (<strong>Bulk Modal Movement</strong>)-based fuel distribution operates through refinery and terminal complexes at <strong>Avonmouth</strong>, <strong>Immingham</strong>, <strong>Grangemouth</strong>, and <strong>Stanlow</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The Guardian</strong> reports that in March, the <strong>U.K.</strong> government clashed with the <strong>U.K. Petrol Retailers Association</strong> after allegations emerged that the "inflammatory language" government ministers use when accusing retailers of "price gouging" and "ripping off" drivers may be inciting abuse against forecourt staff. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>retail margin</strong> pressure requires <strong>forecourt</strong> operators to reassess the balance between <strong>self-service</strong> and <strong>cashier-staffed</strong> configurations. Integration of <strong>ANPR</strong> (<strong>Automatic Number Plate Recognition</strong>) and <strong>CCTV</strong> systems serves as a primary control mechanism against <strong>drive-off</strong> incidents. The <strong>BOSS</strong> (<strong>British Oil Security Syndicate</strong>) sector initiative provides a shared crime intelligence pool among member stations.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Petrol for cars and vans is approximately <strong>40%</strong> more expensive in the <strong>U.K.</strong> than in the <strong>U.S.</strong> due to higher taxes. Since the conflict began, the cost of filling a typical family car with petrol has risen by £14 ($18.20), while a typical diesel tank has increased by approximately £27. The <strong>U.K.</strong> government states that fuel thieves "must face the full force of the law" and says police forces are taking a proactive approach to the issue. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>fuel duty</strong>, along with <strong>VAT</strong>, comprises approximately <strong>50%</strong> of the <strong>U.K.</strong> pump price. <strong>fuel duty</strong> adjustments under the <strong>Spring Statement</strong> and <strong>Autumn Budget</strong> are managed by <strong>HMRC</strong> and are reflected directly in consumer prices. Ultimately, the <strong>U.K.</strong>'s fuel theft wave exemplifies how the <strong>Iran</strong> conflict's energy price shock translates into changes in social behaviour, forcing retail fuel operations to reassess their security budgets.</p>
