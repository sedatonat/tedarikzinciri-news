<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>A.P. Moller-Maersk A/S</strong> shares are declining as investors digest a more modest-than-expected improvement in the container shipping giant's full-year profit guidance. <strong>Maersk</strong>'s earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation and amortization (<strong>EBITDA</strong>) are expected to fall in the range of $9 billion to $9.5 billion for 2025—compared with the prior guidance putting the low end at $8 billion—according to the <strong>Copenhagen</strong>-based company's announcement on November 5. Analysts surveyed had forecast an average of $9.11 billion. <strong>Maersk</strong> also raised its guidance for the global container market, now expecting roughly 4% growth this year versus its prior forecast of 2%–4%. Third-quarter earnings surpassed expectations. Still, the stock fell as much as 7.5% in <strong>Copenhagen</strong>—its largest decline since April. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>A.P. Moller-Maersk A/S</strong>, based in <strong>Copenhagen, Denmark</strong> and founded in 1904 by <strong>Arnold Peter Møller</strong>, is the world's second-largest container shipping company.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">"The third quarter is somewhat of a bubble. Yes, it's a good report, but everything points to very tough earnings conditions ahead," <strong>Mikkel Emil Jensen</strong>, senior analyst at <strong>Sydbank A/S</strong>, said by phone. "Investors aren't really finding any solace in that." From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Maersk</strong> CEO <strong>Vincent Clerc</strong> has led the group since 2023. <strong>Maersk Ocean</strong>, <strong>Maersk Logistics &amp; Services</strong>, <strong>APM Terminals</strong>, and <strong>Maersk Towage &amp; Maritime Services</strong> form the main business segments. <strong>Maersk</strong> operates a fleet of approximately 700+ container vessels with capacity of 4.5+ million <strong>TEU</strong> (Twenty-foot Equivalent Unit) globally. <strong>MSC Mediterranean Shipping Company</strong> remains the global leader with 6+ million <strong>TEU</strong>. <strong>CMA CGM</strong> (France), <strong>COSCO Shipping Lines</strong> (China), <strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong> (Germany), <strong>ONE</strong> (Ocean Network Express; Japan; <strong>NYK</strong>+<strong>MOL</strong>+<strong>K-Line</strong> merger), <strong>Evergreen Marine</strong> (Taiwan), <strong>HMM</strong> (South Korea), <strong>Yang Ming</strong> (Taiwan), <strong>ZIM Integrated Shipping Services</strong> (Israel), and <strong>Wan Hai Lines</strong> (Taiwan) round out the global Top 10 container carriers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, global container shipping alliances are being restructured as of February 2025. <strong>Gemini Cooperation</strong> (<strong>Maersk</strong>+<strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong>), <strong>Premier Alliance</strong> (<strong>HMM</strong>+<strong>ONE</strong>+<strong>Yang Ming</strong>), <strong>Ocean Alliance</strong> (<strong>CMA CGM</strong>+<strong>COSCO</strong>+<strong>Evergreen</strong>+<strong>OOCL</strong>), and <strong>MSC</strong> (operating independently and through bilateral partnerships) form the primary alliances for 2025 and beyond. The previous <strong>2M Alliance</strong> (<strong>Maersk</strong>+<strong>MSC</strong>) ended in January 2025. <strong>THE Alliance</strong> (<strong>Hapag</strong>+<strong>HMM</strong>+<strong>ONE</strong>+<strong>Yang Ming</strong>) is also being restructured. The <strong>Red Sea</strong> crisis, triggered by <strong>Houthi</strong> attacks, reduced <strong>Suez Canal</strong> traffic by over 60% in 2024—the <strong>Cape of Good Hope</strong> route adds 10-14 extra days and 30% in fuel costs. <strong>Maersk</strong> is targeting a full return to the <strong>Red Sea</strong> by 2026, though early signs are improving. The <strong>Panama Canal</strong>, constrained by drought in 2024, is expected to normalize in 2025.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, container shipping economics are tracked through key indices including the <strong>SCFI</strong> (Shanghai Containerized Freight Index), <strong>WCI</strong> (Drewry World Container Index), <strong>FBX</strong> (Freightos Baltic Index), <strong>HARPEX</strong>, and <strong>HRCI</strong>. Spot rates, contract rates, fuel surcharge (<strong>BAF</strong>, Bunker Adjustment Factor), congestion surcharges, peak season surcharges, and <strong>GRI</strong> (General Rate Increase) form the main pricing components. <strong>Asia-Europe</strong>, <strong>Trans-Pacific</strong>, <strong>Trans-Atlantic</strong>, and <strong>Intra-Asia</strong> are the primary trade routes. Orderbook, delivery schedule, scrapping, and idle fleet are key metrics for ship supply-demand balance. Global container ship orders are reaching historic highs in 2025—deliveries in 2026-2028 risk creating overcapacity. <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> tariffs, particularly on <strong>U.S.-China</strong> volumes, are driving front-loading, nearshoring, and route diversion as main responses. Ultimately, <strong>Maersk</strong>'s slumping stock price despite raised guidance underscores the container industry's structural supply-demand uncertainty.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Maersk</strong> raised its 2025 <strong>EBITDA</strong> guidance to 9-9.5B dollars on November 5.<br>
2. Stock fell 7.5%—the steepest daily decline since April.<br>
3. Container market growth forecast revised upward from 2%-4% to 4%.<br>
4. <strong>Mikkel Emil Jensen</strong> at <strong>Sydbank</strong> describes the Q3 report as a "bubble quarter."<br>
5. Investors remain concerned about tough earnings conditions ahead.</p>
