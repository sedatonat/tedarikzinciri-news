<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>U.S.</strong> airlines face an "<strong>existential threat</strong>" stemming from a jet fuel surge that could dramatically elevate costs, according to <strong>Deutsche Bank</strong>. From a supply chain perspective, this warning carries direct implications not only for passenger carriers but also for global <strong>airfreight</strong> capacity. Given that <strong>belly cargo</strong> forms the backbone of global fast supply chains alongside the <strong>integrator</strong> networks of <strong>FedEx</strong>, <strong>UPS</strong>, and <strong>DHL</strong>, the jet fuel shock is directly pressuring <strong>e-commerce</strong> express shipping pricing, <strong>pharma cool chain</strong> flows, and <strong>aerospace</strong> spare parts logistics. The pace at which cost passthrough translates into contracts will be the determining factor during this period when <strong>annual airfreight</strong> tariffs are being renewed.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Jet fuel prices in the <strong>U.S.</strong> have more than doubled since the start of the year during the conflict in the <strong>Middle East</strong>, while crude oil has risen roughly 50 percent over the same period. Such a widening of the <strong>crack spread</strong>—the gap between underlying crude prices and jet fuel prices—last occurred in 2005. <strong>Deutsche Bank</strong> shared this assessment in a client note dated March 6. Analyst <strong>Michael Linenberg</strong> stated that "<strong>Some of the industry's financially weakest carriers could halt operations</strong>" and warned that "<strong>Absent near-term relief, airlines around the world could be forced to ground thousands of aircraft</strong>." From a supply chain perspective, capacity reductions by smaller and mid-size carriers are leading to consolidation of the <strong>capacity squeeze</strong> in favor of the large integrator networks.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Airline stocks extended their declines on March 6 alongside other travel and logistics companies. The sector index fell 19 percent over six trading sessions and is heading toward its worst period since April. <strong>Deutsche Bank</strong> is the latest firm to sound alarm about the impact of the escalating conflict in <strong>Iran</strong> on airlines. From a supply chain perspective, this financial pressure is reshaping <strong>fleet</strong> investment decisions, <strong>fuel hedging</strong> strategies, and <strong>route network</strong> rationalization. Delays in retiring the <strong>Boeing 787</strong>, <strong>Airbus A350</strong>, and other fuel-efficient wide-body aircraft are creating room for daily rate softening in the <strong>aircraft leasing</strong> market.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>American Airlines Group Inc.</strong> faced a downgrade of its stock rating by <strong>Rothschild &amp; Co. Redburn</strong> in early March; the firm now expects the carrier to post losses this year as it pays higher fuel costs. From a supply chain perspective, airline weakness paradoxically produces tightness at the <strong>airfreight rate</strong> level. Reduced capacity from weakened carriers tightens availability, yet fuel costs still push prices higher. <strong>BCO</strong> modal choices between air transport and <strong>sea-air</strong>, <strong>express ocean</strong>, and <strong>direct ocean</strong> will be a critical agenda item in <strong>service contract</strong> design in the coming weeks. In sum, <strong>Deutsche Bank</strong>'s warning has become an important indicator clearly exposing the global fast-shipping system's vulnerability to energy crises.</p>
<p><br></p>

1. <strong>Deutsche Bank</strong> warns of an "existential threat" to airlines.<br>

4. Sector index falls 19 percent over six trading sessions.<br>
5. <strong>Rothschild &amp; Co. Redburn</strong> downgrade <strong>American Airlines</strong> rating.</p>
