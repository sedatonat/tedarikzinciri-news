<p style="text-align: justify">U.S. President <strong>Donald Trump</strong> announced that Mexico could face a <strong>5% additional tariff</strong> on imports from the country, claiming that Mexico has failed to meet its obligations under the <strong>1944 Water Treaty</strong> currently in force between the two nations. In a post on <strong>Truth Social</strong> on December 9, Trump asserted that Mexico has created a significant water deficit in the amount owed to the United States and that this situation has been particularly harmful to <strong>U.S. farmers</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">According to Trump, Mexico has created an <strong>800,000 acre-feet</strong> water shortfall over the past five-year cycle under the treaty. The president is demanding that at least <strong>200,000 acre-feet</strong> of water be released immediately by year's end, with the remaining amount transferred in the near term. Trump stated that he has instructed the preparation of documentation to implement the tariffs <strong>IMMEDIATELY</strong> if this call goes unanswered.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>What Does the 1944 Water Treaty Say?</strong></h3>\n<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>1944 U.S.–Mexico Water Treaty</strong> governs water-sharing between the two countries over the <strong>Colorado River</strong> and <strong>Rio Grande</strong> basins. Under the treaty:</p>\n<ul>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The United States leaves Mexico <strong>1.5 million acre-feet</strong> of water annually from the <strong>Colorado River</strong> and its tributaries,</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Mexico is obligated to deliver to the United States <strong>350,000 acre-feet</strong> of water annually from the <strong>Rio Grande</strong> and its tributaries.</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p style="text-align: justify">These obligations are measured in <strong>five-year cycles</strong>. The most recent cycle ended in <strong>October</strong>, and according to data from the <strong>International Boundary and Water Commission (IBWC)</strong>, Mexico delivered to the United States only slightly more than <strong>800,000 acre-feet</strong> during this period. This figure falls well short of the <strong>1.75 million acre-feet</strong> target set for the five-year cycle.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Tariff Threat and Trade Policy Context</strong></h3>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Trump's announcement further complicates an already complex <strong>U.S.–Mexico trade relationship</strong>. The United States currently applies a <strong>25% tariff</strong> on products from Mexico; however, goods that comply with the <strong>USMCA</strong> (United States–Mexico–Canada Agreement) are <strong>temporarily exempt</strong> from this tariff.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">This exemption was scheduled to <strong>expire on November 1</strong>. However, Mexican President <strong>Claudia Sheinbaum</strong> stated in early November that the exemption could be extended due to ongoing discussions between the two countries on <strong>non-tariff trade barriers</strong>. No clear framework has yet been shared regarding how Trump's 5% additional tariff threat will <strong>interact with the existing 25% tariff and USMCA exemptions</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Diplomatic Ground and Timing</strong></h3>\n<p style="text-align: justify">This development comes shortly after Trump met with <strong>Claudia Sheinbaum</strong> and Canadian Prime Minister <strong>Mark Carney</strong> last week. The three leaders held a roughly half-hour meeting at the <strong>Kennedy Center</strong> in Washington ahead of the <strong>2026 FIFA World Cup Final Draw</strong>. Trump indicated that <strong>trade</strong> was a primary agenda item in that discussion.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Simultaneously, the United States, Mexico, and Canada are preparing for the <strong>USMCA review process scheduled for summer 2026</strong>. For this reason, the tariff threat based on the water treaty is not merely an environmental or agricultural dispute but is also being viewed as <strong>a pressure tactic in broader trade negotiations</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Supply Chain and Sectoral Impacts</strong></h3>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Mexico is a critical supply partner for the United States in automotive, white goods, agriculture, and consumer products. Even a 5% additional tariff could:</p>\n<ul>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Increase <strong>cross-border supply chain</strong> costs,</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Create uncertainty in nearshoring strategies,</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Affect U.S. importers' pricing and inventory planning.</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p style="text-align: justify">In particular, in <strong>low-margin, high-volume</strong> sectors such as automotive and agricultural products, the impact of additional tariffs could be felt quickly.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>General Assessment</strong></h3>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Trump's water-based tariff threat to Mexico demonstrates that trade policies are now directly linked not only to economic factors but also to <strong>environmental and infrastructure agreements</strong>. As 2026 approaches in U.S.–Mexico relations, water, agriculture, and trade are increasingly intertwined.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Points:</strong></h3>\n<ul>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Trump announced that a <strong>5% additional tariff</strong> could be applied to Mexico.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Justification: Non-compliance with obligations under the <strong>1944 Water Treaty</strong>.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Mexico has a water shortfall of approximately <strong>800,000 acre-feet</strong> over the past five-year cycle.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The U.S. currently applies a <strong>25% tariff</strong> to Mexico; USMCA-compliant products are temporarily exempt.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">How the additional tariff will integrate with existing tariffs remains unclear.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">This development occurs ahead of the <strong>USMCA 2026 review process</strong>.</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p><br></p>\n<p>----------</p>\n\n<p>--------------------</p>\n<p><strong>Author: </strong><a href="http://sedatonat.com/"><u>SedatOnat.com</u></a></p>\n<p>--------------------</p>\n\n<p>We would be delighted to receive your feedback.</p>\n<p>Wishing you happy reading.</p>\n
