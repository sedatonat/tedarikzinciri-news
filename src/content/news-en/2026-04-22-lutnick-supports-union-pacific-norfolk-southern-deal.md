<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Commerce Secretary</strong> <strong>Howard Lutnick</strong> said he will support consolidation as a tool to make the <strong>U.S.</strong> freight rail industry more efficient—a potential endorsement of <strong>Union Pacific</strong>&rsquo;s proposed 72 billion dollar acquisition of <strong>Norfolk Southern</strong>. The railroad industry&rsquo;s current interchange system at the nation&rsquo;s midpoint is creating bottlenecks that need to be relieved, <strong>Lutnick</strong> said in a <strong>CNBC</strong> interview on August 19. "Whether that needs to happen through a merger or in some other way, I leave to the regulators and the supervisors," <strong>Lutnick</strong> said. "But the concept of more efficient throughput across the country is clearly something we applaud." <strong>Union Pacific</strong> announced in July a deal to acquire <strong>Norfolk Southern</strong> for approximately 72 billion dollars in cash and stock. The combination would merge <strong>Union Pacific</strong>&rsquo;s network across <strong>Western</strong> states with <strong>Norfolk Southern</strong>&rsquo;s <strong>East Coast</strong> tracks—creating the <strong>U.S.</strong>&rsquo;s first truly transcontinental single-operator <strong>Class I</strong> railroad.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Union Pacific Corporation</strong> (<strong>UP</strong>; <strong>UNP</strong>)—based in <strong>Omaha, Nebraska</strong>—is the largest <strong>Class I</strong> railroad in the <strong>U.S.</strong>, operating a 32,000-mile network across 23 western states, with <strong>Jim Vena</strong> as <strong>President &amp; CEO</strong>. <strong>Norfolk Southern Corporation</strong> (<strong>NSC</strong>; <strong>NSC</strong>)—based in <strong>Atlanta, Georgia</strong>—operates a 19,500-mile network in the eastern <strong>U.S.</strong> across 22 eastern states, with <strong>Mark George</strong> as <strong>President &amp; CEO</strong>. <strong>Howard Lutnick</strong> is the <strong>U.S. Secretary of Commerce</strong>, assuming office during the <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> administration and former CEO of <strong>Cantor Fitzgerald</strong>. The <strong>U.S.</strong> has seven <strong>Class I</strong> railroads: <strong>Union Pacific</strong>, <strong>BNSF Railway</strong> (owned by <strong>Berkshire Hathaway</strong>), <strong>CSX Corporation</strong>, <strong>Norfolk Southern</strong>, <strong>Canadian National Railway</strong> (<strong>CN</strong>), <strong>Canadian Pacific Kansas City</strong> (<strong>CPKC</strong>), and <strong>Kansas City Southern</strong> (now part of <strong>CPKC</strong>). The <strong>Surface Transportation Board</strong> (<strong>STB</strong>) is the <strong>U.S.</strong> federal agency that regulates railroad mergers, with <strong>Robert Primus</strong> as <strong>STB</strong> Chairman.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.S.</strong> railroad industry moves more than 2 billion tons of freight annually, representing 28 percent of <strong>U.S.</strong> freight tonnage and 40 percent in ton-miles. Intermodal (containers, trailer-on-flatcar) and carload (coal, grain, automobiles, chemicals, metals, forest products, aggregates) are the primary commodity categories. The <strong>UP</strong> and <strong>NS</strong> merger would bypass bottlenecks at major interchange points in <strong>Chicago</strong>, <strong>Memphis</strong>, <strong>St. Louis</strong>, <strong>New Orleans</strong>, and <strong>Kansas City</strong>—enabling direct transcontinental service under a single operator from <strong>U.S.</strong> <strong>Pacific</strong> ports (Los Angeles, Long Beach, Oakland, Seattle, Tacoma) to <strong>Atlantic</strong> ports (New York-New Jersey, Norfolk, Charleston, Savannah, Jacksonville, Baltimore). The <strong>Association of American Railroads</strong> (<strong>AAR</strong>)—based in <strong>Washington, DC</strong>—is the <strong>U.S.</strong> railroad industry representative organization, with <strong>Ian Jefferies</strong> as <strong>President &amp; CEO</strong>. The <strong>Federal Railroad Administration</strong> (<strong>FRA</strong>) is the federal regulator within the <strong>Department of Transportation</strong>. <strong>Precision Scheduled Railroading</strong> (<strong>PSR</strong>) is an operational model developed by <strong>E. Hunter Harrison</strong> and adopted by <strong>CSX</strong>, <strong>UP</strong>, <strong>NS</strong>, <strong>CN</strong>, and <strong>CPKC</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>UP-NS</strong> merger must pass all public interest tests in the <strong>STB</strong> approval process—with evaluations of competitive impact, rate issues, service impact, employee impact, and environmental impact. No <strong>Class I</strong> merger has been approved by the <strong>STB</strong> since the <strong>UP-Southern Pacific</strong> merger in 1996, except for <strong>Canadian Pacific-Kansas City Southern</strong> (<strong>CPKC</strong>), which was completed in 2023. The 2001 Major Rail Consolidation Procedures established stricter <strong>STB</strong> standards—requiring the merger proponent to demonstrate public benefit. <strong>BNSF</strong> and <strong>CSX</strong> are potential counter-merger candidates. <strong>U.S.</strong> shippers, represented by the <strong>National Industrial Transportation League</strong> (<strong>NITL</strong>), <strong>American Chemistry Council</strong>, <strong>National Grain and Feed Association</strong>, <strong>National Mining Association</strong>, and <strong>American Forest &amp; Paper Association</strong>, are primary carrier-sector organizations likely to oppose railroad consolidation over single-operator pricing power concerns. Consequently, <strong>Lutnick</strong>'s support signal within the <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> administration creates significant political momentum for <strong>STB</strong> approval of the <strong>UP-NS</strong> merger—though final approval depends on the regulatory review process, which historically takes years.</p>
