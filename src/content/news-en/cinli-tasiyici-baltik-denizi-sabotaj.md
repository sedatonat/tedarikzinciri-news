<p>Denmark's Navy has detained the Chinese cargo vessel <strong>Yi Peng 3</strong> in the Baltic Sea on suspicion of sabotaging two submarine fiber-optic data cables. The vessel was observed departing from Russia and transiting through Sweden's exclusive economic zone, passing through areas where fiber cables in the Baltic Sea are located. The first cable, which runs between Germany and Finland and was damaged on November 17, and the second cable, located between Sweden and Lithuania and severed a day later, are both under investigation.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p>According to the <strong>Wall Street Journal</strong>, the <strong>Yi Peng 3</strong> vessel departed from a Russian port on November 15 and was subsequently observed passing through both damaged cable zones. Following these incidents, Denmark's Navy detained the vessel after days of surveillance. Swedish Prime Minister <strong>Ulf Kristersson</strong> stated in a statement released on November 20 that the fiber cables require "careful examination" and that the incident "could be sabotage."</p>\n<p>A Chinese government spokesperson rejected the allegations, asserting that all Chinese vessels must comply with international laws and regulations, particularly those pertaining to "submarine infrastructure protection." The <strong>Yi Peng 3</strong> reportedly departed the port on November 15 under the guidance of a Russian pilot. However, Russian authorities also denied involvement in the incidents.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p>These developments reflect growing tensions in international maritime commerce and cybersecurity. Since submarine fiber-optic cables form the foundation of global internet and communication networks, such sabotage attempts could have serious economic and security consequences. Swedish and Lithuanian governments are emphasizing the importance of international cooperation in clarifying the incidents and preventing similar attacks.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h5><strong>Key Points:</strong></h5>\n<ul>\n  <li><p>The Chinese cargo vessel <strong>Yi Peng 3</strong> was detained by Denmark's Navy on suspicion of interfering with two fiber-optic cables in the Baltic Sea.</p></li>\n  <li><p>The first cable, located between Germany and Finland, was damaged on November 17.</p></li>\n  <li><p>The second cable, situated between Sweden and Lithuania, was severed on November 18.</p></li>\n  <li><p>The <strong>Yi Peng 3</strong> vessel was confirmed to have passed through the incident zones after departing from a Russian port on November 15.</p></li>\n  <li><p>Swedish Prime Minister <strong>Ulf Kristersson</strong> stated that the incidents could constitute sabotage.</p></li>\n  <li><p>The Chinese government rejected the allegations, claiming compliance with international laws.</p></li>\n  <li><p>Russia, despite piloting the vessel out of port, denied involvement in the incidents.</p></li>\n  <li><p>Protection of submarine fiber-optic cables is critical to global communication infrastructure.</p></li>\n  <li><p>The incidents reflect growing concerns regarding international maritime commerce and cybersecurity.</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p><br></p>\n<p>---</p>\n<p><br></p>\n\n<p><br></p>\n<p>-------------------</p>\n\n<p>Wishing you happy reading.</p>
