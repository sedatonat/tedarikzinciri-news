<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain Podcast</strong>; sponsored by <strong>JLL</strong> in a new episode featuring editorial conversation with <strong>Bob Bowman</strong> &mdash; examining how advanced manufacturing in the <strong>U.S.</strong> is transforming <strong>America's Heartland</strong>. <strong>JLL</strong>'s report titled &ldquo;<strong>Adapting to Shifting Trade Policies: 5 Predictions for Industrial Real Estate</strong>&rdquo; serves as the primary reference material for the podcast episode &mdash; analyzing how shifting trade policies in the <strong>U.S.</strong> are shaping the industrial real estate market through five forecasts. <strong>JLL</strong> (<strong>Jones Lang LaSalle Incorporated</strong>); based in <strong>Chicago, Illinois</strong>; is one of the world's largest commercial real estate services and investment management firms &mdash; with <strong>Christian Ulbrich</strong> serving as <strong>President &amp; CEO</strong>. <strong>JLL Industrial &amp; Logistics</strong> is a global leader in logistics facilities; manufacturing facilities; data centers; and cold chain facilities categories &mdash; the second-largest industrial real estate service provider in the <strong>U.S.</strong> after <strong>Prologis</strong>. <strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong>; published by <strong>Keller International Publishing</strong> &mdash; with <strong>Bob Bowman</strong> as <strong>Editor-in-Chief</strong> &mdash; is one of the oldest and most respected publications for the supply chain industry.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.S. Heartland</strong> historically encompasses the <strong>Midwest</strong> and <strong>Great Lakes</strong> regions &mdash; with states including <strong>Ohio</strong>; <strong>Indiana</strong>; <strong>Michigan</strong>; <strong>Illinois</strong>; <strong>Wisconsin</strong>; <strong>Iowa</strong>; <strong>Minnesota</strong>; <strong>Missouri</strong>; <strong>Tennessee</strong>; <strong>Kentucky</strong>; <strong>North Carolina</strong>; <strong>South Carolina</strong>; <strong>Alabama</strong>; and <strong>Georgia</strong> serving as major manufacturing hubs. <strong>Detroit, Michigan</strong> (automotive); <strong>Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania</strong> (steel); <strong>Akron, Ohio</strong> (tires); <strong>Cleveland, Ohio</strong> (heavy machinery); <strong>Cincinnati, Ohio</strong> (Procter &amp; Gamble); <strong>Indianapolis, Indiana</strong> (Eli Lilly; Cummins); <strong>Columbus, Ohio</strong> (Intel new fab); <strong>Phoenix, Arizona</strong> (TSMC fab); <strong>Austin, Texas</strong> (Samsung); <strong>Huntsville, Alabama</strong> (Mazda Toyota); <strong>Chattanooga, Tennessee</strong> (Volkswagen); <strong>Spartanburg, South Carolina</strong> (BMW); <strong>Greenville-Spartanburg</strong> (Michelin); <strong>Greer, South Carolina</strong> (BMW Plant); <strong>Smyrna, Tennessee</strong> (Nissan); <strong>Lordstown, Ohio</strong> (Foxconn-LMC) are major advanced manufacturing hubs. The <strong>CHIPS and Science Act</strong>; signed by <strong>Biden</strong> in 2022 &mdash; allocates 280 billion dollars in investment for semiconductor manufacturing and <strong>R&amp;D</strong>. The <strong>Inflation Reduction Act</strong> (<strong>IRA</strong>); signed in 2022 &mdash; encompasses 369 billion dollars in green energy and climate investment &mdash; incentivizing <strong>EV</strong> production and battery facility investments. The <strong>Bipartisan Infrastructure Law</strong> (<strong>BIL</strong>); signed in 2021 &mdash; provides 1.2 trillion dollars in infrastructure investment &mdash; upgrading bridges; highways; ports; railways; broadband; and electrical grids.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.S.</strong> reshoring trend is being driven by <strong>Trump 1.0</strong> (2017-2021) and <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> (2025-) tariff policies; <strong>COVID-19</strong> supply chain disruptions; <strong>U.S.-China</strong> trade wars; the <strong>Russia-Ukraine</strong> conflict; the <strong>Red Sea Houthi</strong> crisis; and <strong>Taiwan Strait</strong> geopolitical tensions &mdash; creating new industrial geography. The <strong>Reshoring Initiative</strong> reports that 287,000 new jobs were brought back to the <strong>U.S.</strong> in 2024 &mdash; with projections of 350,000+ in the first half of 2025. <strong>Section 232</strong> (steel 50%; aluminum 50%); <strong>Section 301</strong> (China 25-100%); <strong>IEEPA</strong> (new reciprocal); and <strong>USMCA</strong> reform are among the primary <strong>U.S.</strong> trade policy tools. <strong>Intel Ohio One</strong>; constructing a 28 billion dollar semiconductor complex in <strong>New Albany, Ohio</strong> &mdash; with the first fab scheduled for completion in 2025. <strong>TSMC Arizona</strong>; building three fabs in <strong>Phoenix</strong> totaling 65 billion dollars &mdash; with 4nm; 3nm; and 2nm production technologies. <strong>Samsung Texas</strong>; constructing a 17 billion dollar fab in <strong>Taylor, Texas</strong>. <strong>Micron New York</strong>; planning a 100 billion dollar four-fab complex in <strong>Clay, New York</strong> &mdash; with a 20-year timeline. <strong>GlobalFoundries</strong>; expanding a 12 billion dollar fab in <strong>Malta, New York</strong>. <strong>Ford BlueOval City</strong>; building a 5.6 billion dollar <strong>EV</strong> and battery complex in <strong>Stanton, Tennessee</strong>. <strong>GM Ultium Cells</strong>; constructing battery facilities in <strong>Lordstown, Ohio</strong>; <strong>Spring Hill, Tennessee</strong>; and <strong>Lansing, Michigan</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>JLL</strong>'s five predictions frame how the <strong>U.S.</strong> industrial real estate market is adapting to trade policy changes: (1) tariff uncertainty is increasing inventory stockpiling and warehouse demand; (2) reshoring and nearshoring are accelerating build-to-suit demand for advanced manufacturing facilities; (3) nearshoring to <strong>Mexico</strong> with <strong>USMCA</strong> advantages in regions like <strong>Bajio</strong>; <strong>Monterrey</strong>; <strong>Tijuana</strong>; and <strong>Saltillo</strong> is driving logistics park demand; (4) port-proximity logistics demand continues near <strong>Long Beach</strong>; <strong>Los Angeles</strong>; <strong>Savannah</strong>; <strong>Houston</strong>; <strong>Charleston</strong>; <strong>Jacksonville</strong>; <strong>Norfolk</strong>; and <strong>New York-New Jersey</strong> ports; (5) inland port intermodal hub demand is rising in <strong>Dallas</strong>; <strong>Memphis</strong>; <strong>Chicago</strong>; <strong>Kansas City</strong>; <strong>Columbus</strong>; <strong>Atlanta</strong>; and <strong>Indianapolis</strong>. <strong>Prologis</strong> (<strong>San Francisco, California</strong>); <strong>Duke Realty</strong> (being acquired by <strong>Prologis</strong>); <strong>Rexford Industrial</strong>; <strong>EastGroup Properties</strong>; <strong>Terreno Realty</strong>; <strong>First Industrial Realty Trust</strong>; <strong>STAG Industrial</strong>; <strong>Americold</strong> (cold chain); and <strong>Lineage Logistics</strong> (cold chain) are among the major <strong>U.S.</strong> industrial <strong>REITs</strong>. <strong>Cushman &amp; Wakefield</strong>; <strong>CBRE Group</strong>; <strong>Colliers International</strong>; <strong>Newmark</strong>; <strong>Kidder Mathews</strong>; <strong>Lee &amp; Associates</strong>; and <strong>Avison Young</strong> are among <strong>JLL</strong>'s primary competitors. In conclusion, <strong>JLL</strong>'s podcast episode presents a clear framework for industry professionals on how the ongoing manufacturing renaissance in the <strong>U.S.</strong> is shaping the industrial real estate market &mdash; with policy uncertainty; incentive tools; and logistics infrastructure decisions influencing all strategic decisions.</p>
