<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Bloomberg News</strong> reports that Chinese analysts expect the <strong>US to relax its recently enacted comprehensive export restrictions</strong>. The projections have intensified following trade talks scheduled ahead of a <strong>summit in South Korea this week</strong> between <strong>Chinese President Xi Jinping</strong> and <strong>US President Donald Trump</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Last weekend, <strong>US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent</strong> announced during the <strong>ASEAN Summit</strong> in Malaysia that a <strong>preliminary agreement draft</strong> has been completed for submission to the two countries' leaders. The draft covers subjects such as <strong>tariffs</strong>, <strong>shipping fees</strong>, <strong>fentanyl trade</strong>, and <strong>export restrictions</strong>. Bessent also stated that he believed China would <strong>"delay for one year"</strong> the <strong>export restrictions on rare earth elements</strong> it recently implemented.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Expectations of "mutual concessions"</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">Beijing sources assess that the US could suspend the <strong>new export control rules announced on September 29</strong> in exchange for this delay. The regulation in question subjects <strong>Chinese companies on Washington's "entity list"</strong> as well as their <strong>affiliates</strong> to the same restrictions. This rule particularly prevents <strong>Chinese tech giants like Huawei Technologies Co.</strong> from having <strong>indirect access to US-origin semiconductor products</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Beijing-based consulting firm <strong>Hutong Research</strong> stated in its analysis that "In Kuala Lumpur, the Beijing administration wants to create a <strong>quid pro quo mechanism</strong> whereby the suspension of the 'affiliates rule' is exchanged for a delay in rare earth restrictions." According to the analysis, the <strong>Trump administration will first pause implementation, then relax the regulation</strong>, while China will use this delay as <strong>"insurance" against the risk of the US backing down</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Noteworthy commentary from academics</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Wu Xinbo, Director of the Fudan University Center for American Studies</strong>, stated that these talks will not be limited to tariff reductions alone, and <strong>progress can be made on export and technology restrictions</strong>. Wu said, "In this process, China expects progress not only on tariff exemptions, but also on <strong>technology transfer, the BIS 50% rule</strong>, and export controls."</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Analysts point out that if Xi's talks with Trump only yield a limited "tariff ceasefire," disappointment could ensue. This is because <strong>rare earth elements</strong> are viewed as <strong>China's strongest commercial leverage in the global supply chain</strong>. For the US to demonstrate flexibility in this area, <strong>significant counter-concessions</strong> would need to be on the table.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Background and strategic importance</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>BIS rule that went into effect on September 29</strong> brought companies that are at least 50% owned by those on the blacklist under <strong>the same sanctions framework</strong>. This situation seriously complicated the indirect export to China of products such as <strong>artificial intelligence chips, semiconductor manufacturing equipment, and advanced technology products</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Beijing assessed this move as a step that damaged the balance created during <strong>US-China trade talks held in Madrid</strong>, and in response, it activated <strong>global control regulations on all products containing rare earth elements</strong>. These metals are <strong>at the heart of US manufacturing, from chips to electric vehicles</strong>. Following China's supply stoppage, <strong>some US factories were forced to halt production earlier in the year</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Previous precedents and political dimension</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">A similar situation occurred previously in <strong>June 2025</strong>. After China <strong>agreed to resume rare earth shipments</strong>, the Trump administration <strong>withdrew threats of restrictions on semiconductor design software, jet engine parts, and nuclear material sales</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">However, according to experts, using such trade regulations <strong>as negotiation tools</strong> is a controversial approach, particularly among <strong>national security circles</strong>. Nonetheless, it is noted that <strong>Congress showed strong resistance to such measures during the Trump era</strong>, with <strong>intra-party opposition remaining at low levels</strong>, especially ahead of the election year and budget discussions.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In this context, the Xi-Trump meeting is not merely a <strong>trade negotiation</strong>, but also could represent a <strong>temporary truce in the technology war</strong>. Considering <strong>both sides' mutual dependence on energy, rare earth, and high-tech supply chains</strong>, any possible easing that emerges could provide <strong>temporary relief in global production chains</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong></h5>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Chinese analysts expect the <strong>US to ease export restrictions</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>US-China leaders will meet in South Korea this week</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Possible compromise: China will <strong>delay rare earth export restrictions by 1 year</strong>; the US will <strong>stop implementation of the "affiliates rule."</strong></p></li>
