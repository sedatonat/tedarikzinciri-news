<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports — <strong>Helen Atkinson</strong> (<strong>Managing Editor</strong>): "The future is already here; it's just not evenly distributed" — <strong>William Gibson</strong> wrote in his 1984 novel <strong>Neuromancer</strong>. Supply chain and procurement managers need to listen carefully to ensure this future reaches them — said <strong>Jay Koganti</strong>, Vice President of Supply Chain at Estée Lauder's Center of Excellence, during his presentation on "AI Trends Transforming Supply Chain and How Leaders Respond" at the <strong>DPW New York</strong> conference on June 11. Because what artificial intelligence promises for supply chain operations is enormous. It can accelerate the supply chain's journey from the back office to the boardroom more than any other change. The reason is that the supply chain itself is enormous — <strong>Koganti</strong> argues. In any country's economy, it represents approximately 20 to 30 percent of <strong>GDP</strong>. Globally it employs 450 million people — and contributes 1.45 trillion dollars to U.S. <strong>GDP</strong> alone. Of course, <strong>AI</strong> is already being used in supply chains — adding its capabilities to countless other efforts to optimize operational efficiency. But there is a shift underway — and you need to come to terms with it — <strong>Koganti</strong> warns. <strong>AI</strong> starts as algorithms — now it's showing its semantic dimension (self-learning, analyzing, even making decisions) — and there is an emerging incredible promise in terms of spatial computing and extended reality (<strong>XR</strong>). "Human agency continues to steer the <strong>AI</strong> age" — <strong>Koganti</strong> says — augmenting human decision-making in increasingly useful (and probably concerning) ways. <strong>Koganti</strong> calls on the procurement community to look for what is called the "foresight function" in planning tools — which can enhance data analysis, scenario generation and trend identification — enabling more informed and proactive decision-making — together with human oversight.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>DPW</strong> (<strong>Digital Procurement World</strong>), founded by <strong>Matthias Gutzmann</strong> Founder &amp; CEO, based in <strong>Berlin, Germany</strong>, is the largest digital transformation conference series globally for procurement and supply chain leaders. <strong>DPW Amsterdam</strong>, <strong>DPW New York</strong>, and <strong>DPW Singapore</strong> are the main events. <strong>Estée Lauder Companies</strong> (<strong>EL</strong>; <strong>New York</strong>; <strong>Stephane de la Faverie</strong> CEO) is one of the world's largest luxury beauty and skincare companies — <strong>Estée Lauder</strong>, <strong>MAC</strong>, <strong>Clinique</strong>, <strong>La Mer</strong>, <strong>Bobbi Brown</strong>, <strong>Aveda</strong>, <strong>Tom Ford Beauty</strong>, <strong>Jo Malone London</strong>, and <strong>Le Labo</strong> are its flagship brands. <strong>Jay Koganti</strong>, <strong>Vice President of Supply Chain at Center of Excellence</strong>, leads supply chain innovation and digital transformation. Other leading global <strong>CPG</strong> and beauty <strong>AI</strong> pioneers include <strong>L'Oréal</strong> (<strong>Nicolas Hieronimus</strong> CEO), <strong>Procter &amp; Gamble</strong>, <strong>Unilever</strong>, <strong>Beiersdorf</strong>, <strong>LVMH</strong>, <strong>Kéring</strong>, <strong>Coty</strong>, <strong>Shiseido</strong>, <strong>Amorepacific</strong>, <strong>Natura</strong>, and <strong>Avon</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>spatial computing</strong> and <strong>extended reality</strong> (<strong>XR</strong>) ecosystem comprises: (1) <strong>virtual reality</strong> (<strong>VR</strong>): <strong>Meta Quest</strong> (<strong>Mark Zuckerberg</strong> CEO), <strong>Sony PlayStation VR2</strong>, <strong>HTC Vive</strong>, <strong>Pico</strong> (<strong>ByteDance</strong>), and <strong>Varjo</strong> are main providers; (2) <strong>augmented reality</strong> (<strong>AR</strong>): <strong>Microsoft HoloLens</strong>, <strong>Magic Leap</strong>, <strong>RealWear</strong>, <strong>Vuzix</strong>, and <strong>Snap Spectacles</strong> are main providers; (3) <strong>mixed reality</strong> (<strong>MR</strong>): <strong>Apple Vision Pro</strong> (<strong>Tim Cook</strong> CEO; February 2024 launch) and <strong>Meta Quest 3</strong> are main providers; (4) <strong>industrial metaverse</strong>: <strong>NVIDIA Omniverse</strong> (<strong>Jensen Huang</strong> CEO), <strong>Siemens Xcelerator</strong> (<strong>Roland Busch</strong> CEO), <strong>Microsoft Mesh</strong>, <strong>PTC ThingWorx</strong>, and <strong>Dassault Systemes 3DEXPERIENCE</strong> are main platforms. <strong>Generative AI</strong> and <strong>foundation models</strong> include <strong>OpenAI GPT</strong>, <strong>Anthropic Claude</strong>, <strong>Google Gemini</strong>, <strong>Meta Llama</strong>, <strong>Mistral AI</strong>, <strong>xAI Grok</strong>, <strong>DeepSeek</strong>, <strong>Cohere</strong>, <strong>NVIDIA NeMo</strong>, and <strong>IBM Granite</strong> as main providers. <strong>Agentic AI</strong> frameworks include <strong>Salesforce Agentforce</strong>, <strong>Microsoft Copilot Studio</strong>, <strong>Google Vertex AI Agents</strong>, <strong>AWS Bedrock</strong>, <strong>LangChain</strong>, <strong>LlamaIndex</strong>, <strong>AutoGen</strong>, and <strong>CrewAI</strong> as main frameworks.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the "foresight function" that <strong>Koganti</strong> referenced is becoming an increasingly standard capability in supply chain planning software. Main providers include <strong>Blue Yonder</strong> (formerly <strong>JDA</strong>; <strong>Panasonic</strong>), <strong>Kinaxis Maestro</strong>, <strong>SAP IBP</strong>, <strong>Oracle Demand Planning</strong>, <strong>o9 Solutions</strong>, <strong>OMP Plus</strong>, <strong>RELEX Solutions</strong>, <strong>ToolsGroup</strong>, <strong>John Galt Solutions</strong>, <strong>Anaplan</strong>, <strong>Coupa Supply Chain Design</strong>, <strong>Logility</strong>, <strong>Ikigai Labs</strong>, <strong>Causality Link</strong>, and <strong>QAD Dynasys</strong>. On the procurement side, <strong>SAP Ariba</strong>, <strong>Coupa</strong>, <strong>GEP Smart</strong>, <strong>Ivalua</strong>, <strong>Jaggaer</strong>, <strong>Zycus</strong>, <strong>Workday Strategic Sourcing</strong>, <strong>Oracle Procurement Cloud</strong>, <strong>Keelvar</strong>, <strong>Globality</strong>, <strong>Suplari</strong>, and <strong>Sievo</strong> are main providers. <strong>Procurement intelligence</strong> platforms include <strong>Beroe</strong>, <strong>Spend Matters</strong>, <strong>Ardent Partners</strong>, and <strong>Hackett Group</strong> as main consulting platforms. The <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> tariff regime makes foresight, scenario modeling, and rapid response capabilities vital — structurally strengthening demand for <strong>AI</strong>-powered planning platforms. As a result, the evolution of <strong>AI</strong> that <strong>Koganti</strong> highlighted — from algorithm to semantic to spatial computing — appears to be the main driver of structural change across the global supply chain sector over the next decade.</p>
