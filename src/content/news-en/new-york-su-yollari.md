<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>New York City</strong> has initiated a fundamental pilot project in its logistics infrastructure to address rising delivery volumes, congested truck traffic, and urban air pollution. The city administration has launched a new pilot program that uses <strong>waterways as an active freight network</strong> to reduce road-based transport dependency for cargo movement. This initiative is being implemented under the <strong>Blue Highways</strong> program and aims to reunite New York's historical port identity with modern urban logistics.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The pilot project creates a <strong>marine freight route</strong> between <strong>Pier 79 in Manhattan</strong> and <strong>Brooklyn Marine Terminal in Red Hook, Brooklyn</strong>. Cargo is transported across New York Harbor via <strong>ferry</strong> and then transferred to <strong>electric cargo bikes</strong> for last-mile delivery upon reaching Manhattan. This distribution model covers the dense commercial and residential zones between <strong>23rd Street and 70th Street</strong> in Manhattan.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">New York City Department of Transportation (NYC DOT) Commissioner <strong>Ydanis Rodriguez</strong> expressed the program's vision in these words:<br>
 <strong>"Waterways are the new highways in New York City."</strong><br>
According to Rodriguez, Blue Highways represents New York's transition from a <strong>20th-century truck-focused freight system</strong> to a <strong>cleaner, smarter, and more resilient</strong> logistics structure for the 21st century.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Operational Structure and Initial Volumes</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">The pilot operation is being conducted in partnership with logistics firm <strong>DutchX</strong> and passenger and cargo transportation company <strong>NY Waterway</strong>. In its initial phase, the system is expected to transport <strong>300–400 packages per day</strong>. Cargo categories include:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>cosmetics</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>fashion items</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>lifestyle goods</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">small-volume <strong>household products</strong></p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">City officials indicate that volume will be increased gradually as the pilot progresses, and performance will be regularly measured using metrics such as <strong>capacity utilization</strong>, <strong>truck miles traveled reduction</strong>, and <strong>emissions impact</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Why Waterways?</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">In New York City, <strong>approximately 90% of cargo entering and circulating within the city is transported by trucks</strong>. This situation, particularly in dense areas like Manhattan, creates:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">chronic traffic congestion,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">delivery delays,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">air and noise pollution,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">pedestrian and bicycle safety risks</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">City officials aim to reduce this pressure by using waterways for freight and provide logistics flows with <strong>an alternative backbone</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">NY Waterway CEO <strong>Armand Pohan</strong> emphasized the importance of this approach, stating:<br>
 <strong>"Our city's waterways are key to getting trucks off Manhattan streets while getting freight delivered more quickly and efficiently."</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Cargo Bike Integration and Last-Mile Delivery</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">One of the pilot's distinguishing features is the completion of waterway transport with <strong>electric cargo bikes</strong>. Compared to traditional truck–van distribution, this approach offers:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">near-zero exhaust emissions,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">greater maneuverability on narrow streets,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">reduced parking and stopping issues</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">Cargo bikes stand out as a <strong>hyper-local distribution</strong> model, particularly for small to medium-sized e-commerce orders.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>DutchX</strong> Co-Founder <strong>Marcus Hoed</strong> described the pilot as a "beginning," stating:<br>
 <strong>"It proves that better options exist, and it is only the beginning of what this system will unlock."</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Future Plans: A Broader Waterway Network</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">New York City does not plan to limit the Blue Highways model to a single route. Authorities are evaluating similar freight operations at three additional locations:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Pier 11</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>34th Street Pier</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Battery Maritime Building</strong></p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">The long-term goal is to establish a <strong>multi-node water-based freight network</strong> across New York Harbor.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">As this network expands, the city will be able to accommodate rising <strong>online order volumes</strong> without adding more trucks and will strengthen its logistics infrastructure in terms of <strong>climate resilience</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Strategic Assessment</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">The Blue Highways pilot simultaneously addresses three fundamental urban logistics challenges:</p>
<ol>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Congestion reduction</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Emissions &amp; air quality improvement</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Supply chain resilience</strong></p></li>
</ol>
<p style="text-align: justify">New York's approach establishes an important reference point for how <strong>multimodal last-mile logistics</strong> models can be shaped in dense metropolitan areas.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong></h3>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">NYC has launched a water-based freight pilot under the <strong>Blue Highways</strong> program.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Route: <strong>Pier 79 (Manhattan) – Brooklyn Marine Terminal (Red Hook)</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Cargo is transported by ferry and distributed via <strong>electric cargo bikes</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Initial volume: <strong>300–400 packages per day</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Objective: reduce truck traffic and emissions, improve delivery reliability.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The city is evaluating expansion of the model to <strong>at least three new locations</strong>.</p></li>
</ul>
