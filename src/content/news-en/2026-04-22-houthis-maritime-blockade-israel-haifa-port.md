<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports that <strong>Yemen</strong>'s rebel Houthi movement has declared a "maritime blockade" on <strong>Israel</strong>'s <strong>Haifa</strong> port — warning commercial shipping companies to stay clear or risk being targeted — according to <strong>Breakbulk News</strong>. As reported by <strong>Reuters</strong> on May 19, in a televised statement, the group's military spokesperson <strong>Yahya Saree</strong> said that <strong>Haifa</strong> port has been officially added to the group's list of maritime targets. He called on all shipping companies operating vessels destined for or docked at <strong>Haifa</strong> to change course immediately. "From this moment onward, all companies that own vessels at this port or heading to this port have been informed that the port is included in the list of targets," <strong>Saree</strong> said. So far, despite continued attacks, the <strong>Iron Dome</strong> and other defense systems have intercepted most Houthi missiles aimed at <strong>Israel</strong>. However, over recent months, the Houthis' ability to project force over hundreds of miles has kept shipping companies on edge — forcing significant rerouting of global trade. Earlier this year, after <strong>Washington</strong> ramped up retaliatory strikes, the Houthis agreed to halt attacks on U.S.-flagged vessels. But vessels destined for U.S. allied or <strong>Israeli</strong> ports remain vulnerable.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Port of Haifa</strong> is <strong>Israel</strong>'s largest port, with annual capacity of 1.5 million TEU, purchased in 2023 for $1.18 billion by a consortium of <strong>Adani Group</strong> (founder <strong>Gautam Adani</strong>; <strong>India</strong>) and <strong>Gadot Group</strong> (<strong>Israel</strong>) — and is operated by <strong>Adani Ports and Special Economic Zone</strong> (<strong>APSEZ</strong>; CEO <strong>Karan Adani</strong>). Other major <strong>Israeli</strong> ports include the <strong>Port of Ashdod</strong> (<strong>TIL</strong>; owned by <strong>MSC</strong>; container terminal), the <strong>Port of Eilat</strong> (<strong>Gulf of Aqaba</strong>), and other key national gateways. The <strong>Houthi movement</strong> (officially known as <strong>Ansar Allah</strong>), based in <strong>Sana'a, Yemen</strong>, led by <strong>Abdul-Malik al-Houthi</strong>, is backed by <strong>Iran</strong>'s <strong>Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps</strong> (<strong>IRGC</strong>) Quds Force — forming the "Axis of Resistance" with <strong>Hamas</strong>, <strong>Hezbollah</strong>, and <strong>Iraqi Shi'ite militias</strong>. The <strong>U.S. Department of State</strong> has relisted the Houthis as a <strong>Specially Designated Global Terrorist</strong> (<strong>SDGT</strong>) organization under the <strong>Trump</strong> administration (March 2025).</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Red Sea</strong> trade disruption began in November 2023 with the seizure of the <strong>Galaxy Leader</strong> — since then, more than 100 commercial vessels have been attacked, with 4 sunk, including the <strong>Galaxy Leader</strong>, <strong>Tutor</strong>, <strong>Rubymar</strong>, and <strong>Sounion</strong>. Traffic through the <strong>Bab al-Mandeb Strait</strong> and <strong>Suez Canal</strong> (led by Chairman <strong>Osama Rabie</strong> of the <strong>Suez Canal Authority</strong>) has declined by over 50 percent, from 1.1 billion tons annually in 2023 to 2024. The alternative route around the <strong>Cape of Good Hope</strong> (<strong>South Africa</strong>) adds 10-14 days to shipping time between <strong>Asia</strong> and <strong>Europe</strong>, increases daily vessel operating costs by $30,000-$50,000, and has driven global container freight rates up 3-5 fold. <strong>Mediterranean Shipping Co.</strong> (<strong>MSC</strong>; President <strong>Diego Aponte</strong>; <strong>Geneva, Switzerland</strong>), <strong>Maersk</strong> (CEO <strong>Vincent Clerc</strong>; <strong>Copenhagen, Denmark</strong>), <strong>CMA CGM</strong> (CEO <strong>Rodolphe Saadé</strong>; <strong>Marseille, France</strong>), <strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong> (CEO <strong>Rolf Habben Jansen</strong>; <strong>Hamburg, Germany</strong>), and <strong>ZIM Integrated Shipping Services</strong> (<strong>Israel</strong>) are among the major container carriers affected. <strong>Operation Aspides</strong> (<strong>EU NAVFOR</strong>; Force Commander <strong>Vasilios Gryparis</strong>) and <strong>Operation Prosperity Guardian</strong> (<strong>U.S. Combined Task Force 153</strong>) are the principal protective operations.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the impact on <strong>marine insurance</strong> is significant: <strong>Lloyd's of London</strong>'s <strong>Joint War Committee</strong> (<strong>JWC</strong>) has designated the <strong>Red Sea</strong>, <strong>Bab al-Mandeb</strong>, <strong>Gulf of Aden</strong>, <strong>Yemen</strong>, <strong>Israel</strong>, <strong>Lebanon</strong>, <strong>Iran</strong>, and <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong> as principal "Listed Areas" — with additional war risk premiums per vessel climbing to 0.5-2 percent of vessel value. <strong>BIMCO</strong> (<strong>Baltic and International Maritime Council</strong>; Secretary General <strong>David Loosley</strong>), the <strong>International Chamber of Shipping</strong> (<strong>ICS</strong>; Secretary General <strong>Guy Platten</strong>), and the <strong>International Maritime Organization</strong> (<strong>IMO</strong>; Secretary General <strong>Arsenio Dominguez</strong>) are the principal sector bodies — each commenting on global trade norms in light of the Houthi crisis. Houthi blockade declarations have escalated in stages: (1) November 2023, vessels under <strong>Israeli</strong> flag; (2) December 2023, all vessels heading to <strong>Israel</strong>; (3) March 2024, U.S. and <strong>British</strong> vessels; (4) May 2024, ownership assessment; and (5) May 2025, blockade of <strong>Haifa</strong> port. Consequently, <strong>Saree</strong>'s declaration of the <strong>Haifa</strong> blockade represents a significant escalation — with global maritime trade security and reassessment of <strong>Eastern Mediterranean</strong> routes seemingly forcing supply chain managers to prioritize war risk insurance and route flexibility as core strategic imperatives.</p>
