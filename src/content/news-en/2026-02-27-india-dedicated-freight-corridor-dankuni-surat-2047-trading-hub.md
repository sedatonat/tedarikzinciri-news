<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>India</strong> is pushing forward with domestic rail freight corridors after the 1 February budget. The government accelerated its ambitious plans for the country to become <strong>a key trading hub by 2047</strong>. In this context, <strong>Indian Railways</strong> has initiated fast-track action for the new <strong>Dankuni-Surat Dedicated Freight Corridor (DFC)</strong>.</p><p style="text-align: justify">The new DFC will link <strong>Dankuni</strong> in West Bengal with <strong>Surat</strong> in Gujarat, connecting India's eastern and western coasts. The corridor could move freight between the country's two major port regions (Kolkata/Haldia and Surat/Mumbai) via inland routes. India's existing DFC network already runs the <strong>Eastern DFC</strong> (Ludhiana-Dankuni) and <strong>Western DFC</strong> (Dadri-JNPT Mumbai) operationally.</p><p style="text-align: justify">The new corridor will accommodate double-stack container trains running at 100 km/h. Being entirely dedicated to freight — unlike conventional lines — it offers high productivity without passenger train conflicts. India's goal is to raise rail's share of freight transport from 27 per cent to <strong>45 per cent by 2030</strong>. The target seeks both to reduce road-based carbon emissions and to enhance manufacturing competitiveness.</p>

<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. India initiated fast-track action for the new Dankuni-Surat Dedicated Freight Corridor.<br>
2. The corridor will link West Bengal and Gujarat, connecting the east and west coasts.<br>
3. The government aims to make the country a key trading hub by 2047.<br>
4. The existing Eastern and Western DFCs are already operational.<br>
5. India targets rail's freight share rising to 45 per cent by 2030.</p>
