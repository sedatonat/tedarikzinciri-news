<p style="text-align: justify">Investments in <strong>automation</strong>, <strong>robotics</strong> and <strong>artificial intelligence (AI)</strong> in warehouse operations are accelerating. While this transformation reinforces the perception that the human factor is being increasingly sidelined, industry data and expert opinion point to a more complex picture. An analysis published by <strong>Modern Materials Handling</strong> shows that in the automation age, the role of humans in warehouses is not disappearing but is undergoing fundamental change.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to <strong>U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS)</strong> data, the number of people employed in U.S. warehouses reached <strong>1.83 million</strong> as of August 2025. This figure represents a significant increase compared to <strong>628,000</strong> in 2010 and <strong>510,000</strong> in 2000. By contrast, manufacturing employment declined sharply during the same period. Manufacturing employment, which was approximately <strong>20 million</strong> in the 1990s, fell to <strong>12.7 million</strong> as of 2025. This inverse trend demonstrates that warehouse operations are assuming an increasingly central role in the economy.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Despite the increase in overall headcount, warehouse managers are struggling to find skilled labour. The primary reason is that the warehouse workforce profile is undergoing fundamental transformation. <strong>Chris Steiner</strong>, a senior executive at <strong>Dematic</strong>, notes that the average tenure of an employee at a single warehouse has dropped to under two years. Around 2005, this tenure was said to be <strong>over 10 years</strong>. This change is linked to working conditions, shift patterns, flexibility requirements, and limited career advancement opportunities. A survey by <strong>Gallup</strong> ranks warehouse work at the bottom in terms of perception as a "quality job".</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Nevertheless, human labour remains critical for warehouse operations to continue. <strong>Bryan Jensen</strong> from <strong>St. Onge</strong> emphasizes that while the number of warehouse employees may decline in the future, overall <strong>throughput</strong> will increase exponentially. According to Jensen, even if staffing is cut in half within a few years, operational volume quadruples. This picture demonstrates that automation is not completely replacing people but rather multiplying productivity.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to experts, the main axis of transformation is the shift of workers away from <strong>manual</strong>, <strong>repetitive</strong> and <strong>physically demanding</strong> work towards planning, supervision and technical roles. <strong>Andres Boullosa</strong> from <strong>Zebra Technologies</strong> states that warehouse workers are transitioning to higher-value-added roles and that this is a positive development in the long run. <strong>Lee Rector</strong>, CEO of <strong>LaborAI</strong>, notes that <strong>WES</strong>, <strong>WCS</strong> and <strong>WMS</strong> systems are being integrated with <strong>AI</strong> to rebalance the workforce on a time and space basis.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>ROI</strong> calculations play a decisive role in this transformation. <strong>Bill Atherton</strong> from <strong>FORTNA</strong> notes that automation investments are evaluated at every stage in comparison with labour. Transitions between stages such as <strong>manual cart</strong>, <strong>pick-to-light</strong>, <strong>autonomous mobile robots (AMRs)</strong> and <strong>cobots</strong> are shaped each time by business case analyses. For this reason, there is no one-size-fits-all automation-balance formula for each warehouse.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The article treats <strong>Amazon</strong> as an extreme scenario. According to <strong>The New York Times</strong>, Amazon aims to automate <strong>75%</strong> of its operations. This target means the non-hiring of hundreds of thousands of workers. However, experts emphasize that Amazon holds an exceptional position due to its scale and capital resources.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In the near term, certain roles are losing significance. <strong>Trailer loading/unloading</strong>, <strong>palletizing</strong>, <strong>cycle counting</strong> and floor-level supervisory roles are declining. Conversely, new roles such as <strong>robot trainers</strong>, <strong>maintenance technicians</strong>, <strong>AI engineers</strong> and <strong>digital system supervisors</strong> are emerging. Experts agree that this transformation is occurring in a <strong>gradual</strong> manner, not suddenly.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In conclusion, humans in automated warehouses are not losing importance; rather, they are evolving into more skilled, knowledge-based and value-focused roles. This transformation carries the potential to make warehouse work more sustainable and attractive in the long run.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Takeaways</strong></h3>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">U.S. warehouse employment is increasing while manufacturing employment is declining</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Average tenure of warehouse workers has fallen significantly</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Automation</strong> and <strong>AI</strong> reduce manual work while elevating planning and technical roles</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>AMRs</strong> and <strong>cobots</strong> take on supporting roles for the workforce</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">While some operational roles decline, maintenance, automation and <strong>AI</strong>-focused roles are increasing</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The transformation is proceeding gradually, not abruptly</p></li>
</ul>
