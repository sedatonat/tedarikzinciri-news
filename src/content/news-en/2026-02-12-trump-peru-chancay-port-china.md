<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The Trump</strong> administration has warned <strong>Peru</strong> that it is losing sovereignty over a <strong>Chinese</strong>-owned port near the country's capital after a local judge ruled that the <strong>Chancay</strong> port is exempt from certain regulatory oversight. The large-scale <strong>Chancay</strong> port, located on <strong>Peru</strong>'s <strong>Pacific</strong> coast, was designed as a critical new trade link between <strong>Asia</strong> and <strong>Latin America</strong>. But as <strong>Donald Trump</strong> seeks to reassert <strong>U.S.</strong> power across the <strong>Americas</strong>, the port has re-emerged as a flashpoint of tension between <strong>Washington</strong> and <strong>Beijing</strong>. From a supply chain perspective, this competition directly affects long-term contract pricing and regulatory risk profiles for transit freight routes between <strong>Asia-Pacific</strong> and <strong>South America</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>U.S. State Department</strong>'s <strong>Bureau of Western Hemisphere Affairs</strong> posted on <strong>X</strong> on February 11: "<strong>Concerned about latest reports that Peru could be powerless to oversee Chancay, one of its largest ports, which is under jurisdiction of predatory Chinese owners</strong>," and added the warning: "<strong>Let this be a cautionary tale for the region and the world: cheap Chinese money costs sovereignty</strong>." The warning marks the Trump administration's most direct criticism yet of <strong>Peru</strong>'s close ties with <strong>China</strong>. <strong>China</strong> is <strong>Peru</strong>'s largest trade partner, followed by the <strong>U.S.</strong> The flashpoint is the <strong>Chancay</strong> port, built at a cost of $1.3 billion and inaugurated in 2024 by <strong>Chinese President Xi Jinping</strong>. The port accelerates shipments from <strong>South America</strong> to <strong>China</strong> and is operated by <strong>Chinese</strong>-owned <strong>Cosco Shipping Ports</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Gonzalo Rios, deputy general manager of the port and a retired admiral, told <strong>Bloomberg</strong>: "<strong>Chancay is not an enclave. It's not a place where the Peruvian state has no sovereignty</strong>." <strong>Rios</strong> noted that a number of state agencies, including customs, regulate port activities and that the dispute highlighted by the <strong>U.S.</strong> is limited only to the infrastructure regulator. The local court ruling found that the infrastructure regulator <strong>Ositran</strong> has no regulatory authority over <strong>Chancay</strong>. <strong>Ositran</strong> regulates <strong>Peru</strong>'s other major ports, which consist of concessions on public land, while <strong>Chancay</strong> is structured as a privately owned port. This structural difference forms the technical basis for the court ruling.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Chancay</strong> holds an important position in <strong>Cosco</strong>'s global terminal portfolio and adds value to transshipment flows running through <strong>Brazil</strong>, <strong>Chile</strong>, and <strong>Argentina</strong> on <strong>Asia-Pacific</strong> routes. The average 25-30-day transit advantage the port offers to exporters of copper, fish meal, and agricultural commodities structurally improves the logistics cost base of <strong>South American</strong> producers. On the other hand, <strong>Peru</strong>'s regulatory adjustments under <strong>U.S.</strong> pressure affect <strong>Cosco</strong>'s terminal investment planning and the port selection decisions of <strong>Chinese</strong> line operators (<strong>OOCL</strong>, <strong>COSCO Shipping Lines</strong>). Ultimately, the <strong>Chancay</strong> dispute marks an important turning point indicating that control of <strong>Latin America</strong> ports is now central to the global geopolitical competition.</p>
