<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; analyst insight; President <strong>Trump</strong>'s latest round of tariffs — whether implemented or threatened — should not be expected to trigger a boom in domestic manufacturing in the U.S. — or to bring an end to the growth of "nearshored" production capacity in <strong>Mexico</strong>. This is the view of <strong>Ivan Hernandez</strong>, <strong>Latin America Managing Director</strong> at <strong>QIMA</strong> — <strong>QIMA</strong> is a software and service provider enabling supply chain compliance in the consumer products, food, and life sciences sectors. Among the stated objectives of <strong>Trump</strong> tariffs is to force American manufacturers to return production to the U.S. However, this anticipated "reshoring" surge faces multiple hurdles and practical challenges — according to <strong>Hernandez</strong>. One of these is a shortage of skilled manufacturing labor in the U.S. — <strong>Mexico</strong>'s primary relative advantage.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>QIMA</strong>, headed by CEO <strong>Sébastien Breteau</strong>, is <strong>Hong Kong</strong>-based and was founded in 2005; it provides supply chain compliance, industrial inspection, laboratory testing, ethical audits, and certification services globally. <strong>QIMA</strong>'s main competitors are <strong>SGS</strong> (<strong>Géraldine Picaud</strong> CEO; <strong>Geneva, Switzerland</strong>); <strong>Bureau Veritas</strong> (<strong>Hinda Gharbi</strong> CEO; <strong>Paris</strong>); <strong>Intertek</strong> (<strong>Andre Lacroix</strong> CEO; <strong>London</strong>); <strong>TÜV Rheinland</strong>; <strong>TÜV SÜD</strong>; <strong>DEKRA</strong>; <strong>UL Solutions</strong>; <strong>Eurofins Scientific</strong>; <strong>Element Materials Technology</strong>; <strong>ALS Limited</strong>; and other major <strong>TIC</strong> (<strong>Testing Inspection Certification</strong>) sector players. The main nearshoring regions in <strong>Mexico</strong> are: (1) <strong>Monterrey</strong> (<strong>Nuevo León</strong>; automotive; electronics; heavy equipment); (2) <strong>Saltillo</strong> (<strong>Coahuila</strong>; automotive); (3) <strong>Tijuana</strong> (<strong>Baja California</strong>; medical devices; electronics); (4) <strong>Ciudad Juárez</strong> (<strong>Chihuahua</strong>; automotive; medical devices); (5) <strong>Querétaro</strong> (aerospace; automotive); (6) <strong>Guadalajara</strong> (<strong>Jalisco</strong>; electronics — <strong>Mexico</strong>'s "Silicon Valley"); (7) <strong>León</strong>/<strong>Bajío</strong> (automotive); (8) <strong>Aguascalientes</strong> (automotive); (9) <strong>San Luis Potosí</strong> (automotive); (10) <strong>Yucatán</strong>; are the main manufacturing hubs.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the main maquiladora and <strong>IMMEX</strong> program framework in <strong>Mexico</strong> is the <strong>Industria Manufacturera Maquiladora y de Servicios de Exportación</strong> — <strong>Mexico</strong>'s primary preferential export manufacturing framework. The <strong>Index Industria Maquiladora</strong>; <strong>Asociación Mexicana de la Industria Automotriz</strong> (<strong>AMIA</strong>); <strong>National Association of Manufacturers</strong> (<strong>NAM</strong>); are the main <strong>Mexico</strong> sector organizations. Major companies from the U.S. investing heavily in nearshoring to <strong>Mexico</strong> include <strong>Tesla</strong> (<strong>Nuevo León</strong> Gigafactory plan); <strong>BMW</strong>; <strong>Mercedes-Benz</strong>; <strong>Audi</strong>; <strong>Volkswagen</strong>; <strong>Stellantis</strong>; <strong>Ford</strong>; <strong>GM</strong>; <strong>Foxconn</strong> (server production); <strong>Lego</strong>; <strong>Mattel</strong>; <strong>Samsung</strong>; <strong>LG</strong>; <strong>Kimberly-Clark</strong>; <strong>3M</strong>; <strong>Caterpillar</strong>; <strong>John Deere</strong>; <strong>Honeywell</strong>; <strong>Whirlpool</strong>; <strong>Honda</strong>; <strong>Toyota</strong>; <strong>Mazda</strong>; <strong>Nissan</strong>; are among the major investor companies in <strong>Mexico</strong>. <strong>Mexico</strong>'s foreign direct investment (<strong>FDI</strong>) reached 36 billion dollars in 2023 — a record level. <strong>Plan México</strong> is the <strong>Sheinbaum</strong> administration's economic independence framework.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the main reshoring and nearshoring tracking organizations and data sources include the <strong>Reshoring Initiative</strong> (<strong>Harry Moser</strong> Founder); the primary U.S. reshoring tracking organization. The <strong>Kearney Reshoring Index</strong>; <strong>BCG Industrial Renaissance Report</strong>; <strong>McKinsey Reshoring Survey</strong>; are major consulting firm reshoring reports. The <strong>U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics</strong>; U.S. manufacturing employment data; <strong>Federal Reserve Industrial Production Index</strong>; are primary U.S. manufacturing data sources. The main manufacturing labor challenges in the U.S. include: (1) skilled worker shortage (<strong>Manufacturing Institute</strong> 2023 survey: 2.1 million unfilled positions by 2030); (2) aging workforce; (3) underqualified candidates; (4) STEM education gap; (5) high labor costs; are the primary U.S. manufacturing challenges. <strong>Mexico</strong>'s advantages include: (1) labor costs approximately 50%-70% lower than the U.S.; (2) young and growing workforce; (3) strong engineering base; (4) <strong>USMCA</strong> preferential access; (5) geographic proximity; are the main advantages. The <strong>Trump</strong> 2.0 <strong>USMCA</strong> review in 2026 is a primary source of uncertainty. In conclusion, <strong>QIMA</strong>'s advocacy for <strong>Mexico</strong> nearshoring remains fundamentally contested globally amid post-<strong>Trump</strong> tariff supply chain reconfigurations — <strong>Mexico</strong> and <strong>USMCA</strong> strategy appears to be a key strategic priority for supply chain managers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Ivan Hernandez</strong> (<strong>QIMA</strong> Latin America) sees <strong>Mexico</strong> nearshoring continuing.<br>
2. <strong>Trump</strong> tariffs will not create a U.S. reshoring boom.<br>
3. <strong>Monterrey</strong>; <strong>Tijuana</strong>; <strong>Querétaro</strong>; are the main <strong>Mexico</strong> manufacturing hubs.<br>
4. <strong>Mexico</strong> has a labor cost advantage of approximately 50%-70% lower than the U.S.<br>
5. <strong>Tesla</strong>; <strong>Foxconn</strong>; <strong>BMW</strong>; are among the major investor companies in <strong>Mexico</strong>.</p>
