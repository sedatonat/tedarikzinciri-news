<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>New U.S. port fees targeting China's maritime industry</strong> have imposed <strong>additional costs exceeding $42 million on Cosco Shipping and its affiliate OOCL (Orient Overseas Container Line) in the first week of implementation</strong>.</p>
<p><br>
<strong>According to vessel tracking data</strong>, <strong>post-Panamax</strong>-class ships are among the most heavily impacted by these fees.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Post-Panamax Vessels Face $3–4 Million Fees</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">Under the new regulations, <strong>each large container vessel (post-Panamax class) operated by Cosco and OOCL calling at U.S. ports</strong> is subject to a <strong>port fee ranging between $3 and $4 million</strong>.<br>
This creates a significant additional cost burden for vessel operators, particularly those active in high-volume ports such as <strong>Los Angeles, Long Beach, and New York-New Jersey</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Apart from Cosco and OOCL, only <strong>one small China-based liner operator</strong> and <strong>five China-built container vessels</strong> have been impacted by these fees. However, the total cost for these vessels is estimated to be only <strong>several million dollars</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Annual Costs Could Reach $2 Billion</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to experts, if current trends continue, <strong>the combined annual fee burden for Cosco and OOCL could reach as high as $2 billion</strong>.</p>
<p><br>
This projection strengthens claims that the U.S. government is implementing port fees as <strong>an extension of trade restrictions targeting China-based shipbuilding and maritime operations</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The goal of the new fees is to <strong>reduce the competitive advantage of Chinese-made or Chinese-controlled vessels at U.S. ports.</strong></p>
<p><br>
However, global shipping experts warn that this measure could <strong>increase cargo transport costs, adversely affecting U.S. importers as well</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Scope and Initial Results</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">The port fees implemented by the U.S. administration in October primarily target <strong>vessels built in China or operated by state-affiliated Chinese operators</strong>.<br>
Specifically:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Cosco Shipping Lines</strong> (state-controlled) and</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>OOCL (Orient Overseas Container Line)</strong> (a subsidiary of Cosco Group)<br>
are the principal carriers directly affected.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to vessel tracking data, in the first week alone these two operators made <strong>a total of 12 calls at U.S. ports</strong>, with fees paid for these calls totaling <strong>over $42 million</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Global Impact and Industry Reaction</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">Shipping industry representatives warn that these port fees could <strong>create new cost pressures in global freight supply chains</strong>.</p>
<p><br>
Many analysts predict that these fees will <strong>drive up freight rates on trans-Pacific routes</strong> and <strong>may ultimately be reflected in consumer prices</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Additionally, the U.S. has effectively made this measure <strong>a new component of its ongoing tariff war with China</strong>.</p>
<p><br>
This development could create <strong>new barriers to market access</strong>, particularly for <strong>China-based shipbuilders and maritime operators</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Potential Chinese Countermeasures</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">The Beijing government is expected to respond with <strong>reciprocal port fees or administrative restrictions</strong>.</p>
<p><br>
Indeed, on October 14, China announced sanctions against <strong>five U.S.-linked subsidiaries of Hanwha Ocean</strong>, with observers characterizing this decision as <strong>a response to U.S. port fees</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Thus, <strong>a new trade front in maritime shipping has opened between the U.S. and China</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Conclusion: U.S.-China Shipping Tension Enters New Phase</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">The port fees implemented by the U.S. are significant enough to affect not only Chinese carriers but also <strong>the broader global container shipping market</strong>.</p>
<p><br>
<strong>Major shipping lines like Cosco and OOCL</strong> are expected to pass these costs on to <strong>freight rates in the long term.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">If trade tensions between the two countries continue to escalate, <strong>new route changes in global supply chains</strong>, <strong>cargo rerouting</strong>, and <strong>regional capacity imbalances</strong> will become inevitable.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong></h5>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The U.S. new port fees</strong> specifically target China's maritime sector.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Cosco and OOCL</strong> incurred <strong>additional costs totaling $42 million</strong> in the first week of implementation.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Fees for <strong>post-Panamax</strong> vessels range from <strong>$3–4 million</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Annual costs are projected to reach <strong>as much as $2 billion</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The U.S. objective is to limit the competitive power of Chinese-built or state-affiliated vessels.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">According to experts, this measure could drive up <strong>trans-Pacific freight rates</strong> and <strong>global shipping costs</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">China is expected to respond with <strong>reciprocal sanctions and port regulations</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The escalation signals <strong>a new trade war in the maritime and logistics sectors</strong>.</p></li>
</ul>
