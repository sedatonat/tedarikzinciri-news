<p style="text-align: justify">Is agentic artificial intelligence <strong>running loose</strong> in your supply chain? In recent months, we have witnessed major advances in agentic AI—a technology designed to autonomously carry out multiple individual business functions. One area where agentic AI can deliver significant value is the supply chain, where global reach, high data volumes, and complexity from numerous business partners can overwhelm human operators. But does agentic AI, as currently deployed in supply chains, have sufficient <strong>guardrails</strong> to prevent it from going off the rails? From a supply chain perspective, the concept of <strong>guardrails</strong> encompasses <strong>governance</strong>, <strong>risk assessment</strong>, <strong>monitoring</strong>, and <strong>incident response</strong> mechanisms under frameworks such as the <strong>NIST AI Risk Management Framework</strong>, the <strong>EU AI Act</strong>, and standards like <strong>ISO/IEC 42001</strong> (<strong>AI Management System</strong>).</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Leaving it <strong>loose</strong> without proper monitoring and control exposes businesses to every kind of potential problem. In this episode, we get advice directly on how to address this from <strong>Justin Rice</strong>, Chief Product and Technology Officer at <strong>CBTS</strong>, an IT service management and consulting firm. We address concerns about security, accuracy, and the potential for <strong>poisoned</strong> data. What if a rogue agentic AI system refuses to shut down? From a supply chain perspective, <strong>data poisoning</strong> attacks aim to distort model behavior by injecting malicious examples into <strong>training data</strong>. The <strong>MITRE ATLAS</strong> (<strong>Adversarial Threat Landscape for AI Systems</strong>) framework provides catalogs of <strong>tactics, techniques and procedures</strong> (<strong>TTPs</strong>). <strong>Prompt injection</strong>, <strong>jailbreaking</strong>, <strong>model extraction</strong>, <strong>membership inference</strong>, and <strong>backdoor attack</strong> are among the primary attack vectors.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>CBTS</strong> (<strong>Cincinnati Bell Technology Solutions</strong>), a subsidiary of <strong>altafiber</strong>, provides services in <strong>managed services</strong>, <strong>cloud</strong>, <strong>cybersecurity</strong>, and <strong>communications</strong>. <strong>Justin Rice</strong>'s role as <strong>CPTO</strong> gives him responsibility for designing enterprise adoption strategies for agentic <strong>AI</strong>. The scenario of AI agents refusing to shut down is a practical manifestation of research areas in <strong>AI alignment</strong> and <strong>corrigibility</strong>. <strong>Anthropic</strong>'s <strong>Constitutional AI</strong>, <strong>OpenAI</strong>'s <strong>RLHF</strong> (<strong>Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback</strong>), and <strong>Google DeepMind</strong>'s <strong>Sparrow</strong> projects aim to align agentic <strong>AI</strong> with human values. <strong>Kill switch</strong>, <strong>circuit breaker</strong>, <strong>hard stop</strong>, and <strong>fallback policy</strong> mechanisms are mandatory in production systems.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>AI agent observability</strong> requires <strong>tool use logging</strong>, <strong>chain of thought tracking</strong>, <strong>token usage monitoring</strong>, and <strong>anomaly detection</strong> implementations. <strong>LangSmith</strong>, <strong>Arize</strong>, <strong>Datadog LLM Observability</strong>, <strong>Weights &amp; Biases</strong>, and <strong>Honeycomb</strong> are leading market providers in this space. Compliance with <strong>SOC 2</strong>, <strong>ISO 27001</strong>, <strong>HIPAA</strong>, and <strong>PCI DSS</strong> is mandatory when AI agents interact with corporate data. The <strong>least privilege</strong> principle, along with <strong>RBAC</strong> (<strong>Role-Based Access Control</strong>) and <strong>ABAC</strong> (<strong>Attribute-Based Access Control</strong>) models, are used to define the boundaries of AI agent authority. The podcast is hosted by <strong>Bob Bowman</strong>, Editor-in-Chief of <strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong>. In summary, <strong>Rice</strong>'s insights clearly demonstrate the security and governance challenges that come with the adoption of agentic <strong>AI</strong> in the supply chain.</p>
