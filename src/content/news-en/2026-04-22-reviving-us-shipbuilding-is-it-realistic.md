<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; <strong>Helen Atkinson</strong> (<strong>Managing Editor</strong>, <strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong>); analyst insight; America; was great at building commercial oceangoing ships. Can it be great again? The current administration appears willing to try to find out. President <strong>Biden</strong> first floated the idea of pushing back against <strong>China's</strong> dominance in shipbuilding. Now President <strong>Trump</strong> threatens to fine ships built in <strong>China</strong> up to 1.5 million dollars to enter U.S. ports. Additionally, in the first two years of a plan put forth by the Office of the <strong>U.S. Trade Representative</strong> (<strong>USTR</strong>), 1 percent of U.S. exports will be carried on U.S.-built ships &mdash; rising to 15 percent over 7 years. Pictured is the guided missile destroyer <strong>Ted Stevens</strong> (<strong>DDG 128</strong>) at <strong>Ingalls Shipbuilding</strong> in <strong>Pascagoula, Mississippi</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the historical peak of the U.S. shipbuilding industry occurred during World War II &mdash; the <strong>Liberty Ships</strong> and <strong>Victory Ships</strong> programs (Henry J. Kaiser); producing over 2,700 commercial ships annually in 1944. Today, U.S. commercial shipbuilding lags far behind <strong>China</strong>, <strong>South Korea</strong>, and <strong>Japan</strong> &mdash; accounting for approximately 0.1 percent of global new construction orders (on a CGT basis). In 2024, <strong>China</strong> captured over 50 percent of global new construction orders, <strong>South Korea</strong> 30 percent, <strong>Japan</strong> 15 percent, and <strong>Europe</strong> and other countries the remainder, constituting the primary structure. Major commercial and military shipbuilding firms in the U.S. include <strong>HII</strong> (<strong>Huntington Ingalls Industries</strong>; <strong>Chris Kastner</strong> CEO; <strong>Newport News</strong> and <strong>Pascagoula</strong>), <strong>General Dynamics</strong> (<strong>NASSCO</strong>, <strong>Bath Iron Works</strong>, <strong>Electric Boat</strong>; <strong>Phebe Novakovic</strong> CEO), <strong>Bollinger Shipyards</strong> (<strong>Ben Bordelon</strong> CEO), <strong>Eastern Shipbuilding Group</strong>, <strong>Fincantieri Marinette Marine</strong>, <strong>VT Halter Marine</strong>, <strong>Marinette Marine</strong>, <strong>Vigor Industrial</strong>, <strong>Conrad Shipyard</strong>, representing the core ecosystem players.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the major <strong>Chinese</strong> shipbuilding firms are <strong>China State Shipbuilding Corporation</strong> (<strong>CSSC</strong>), <strong>Jiangnan Shipyard</strong>, <strong>Hudong-Zhonghua Shipbuilding</strong>, <strong>Dalian Shipbuilding Industry</strong>, <strong>Shanghai Waigaoqiao Shipbuilding</strong>, representing the primary <strong>Chinese</strong> players &mdash; with <strong>CSSC</strong> capturing approximately 25-30 percent of global CGT. <strong>South Korea's</strong> major firms include <strong>HD Hyundai Heavy Industries</strong> (<strong>HHI</strong>; <strong>Chung Ki-sun</strong> Vice Chairman), <strong>Samsung Heavy Industries</strong> (<strong>SHI</strong>), <strong>Hanwha Ocean</strong> (formerly <strong>Daewoo Shipbuilding</strong>), representing the primary <strong>South Korean</strong> players. <strong>Japan's</strong> major firms include <strong>Imabari Shipbuilding</strong>, <strong>Japan Marine United</strong> (<strong>JMU</strong>), <strong>Mitsui E&S</strong>, <strong>Tsuneishi</strong>, representing the primary <strong>Japanese</strong> players. <strong>Hanwha Ocean</strong> acquired <strong>Philly Shipyard</strong> in 2024 &mdash; marking the first major <strong>South Korean</strong> shipbuilding firm presence in the U.S. U.S. federal authorities include <strong>U.S. Maritime Administration</strong> (<strong>MARAD</strong>; <strong>U.S. Department of Transportation</strong>), <strong>Office of the U.S. Trade Representative</strong> (<strong>USTR</strong>; <strong>Jamieson Greer</strong>), <strong>U.S. Navy NAVSEA</strong> (<strong>Naval Sea Systems Command</strong>), <strong>U.S. Coast Guard</strong>, representing the primary federal maritime authorities.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Jones Act</strong> (1920 <strong>Merchant Marine Act</strong>) mandates that vessels built in the U.S., flagged under the U.S., crewed by U.S. citizens, and owned by U.S. interests be used in trade between U.S. ports &mdash; representing the primary protective legislation for U.S. shipbuilding. The <strong>Cargo Preference Act</strong> (1954) mandates that a portion of U.S. government cargo be carried on U.S.-flagged vessels. The <strong>Maritime Security Program</strong> (<strong>MSP</strong>), <strong>National Defense Reserve Fleet</strong> (<strong>NDRF</strong>), represent the primary U.S. maritime security programs. The <strong>USTR Section 301</strong> investigation (into <strong>Chinese</strong> shipbuilding, maritime, and logistics sectors) was initiated by the <strong>Biden</strong> administration in 2024 and expanded by the <strong>Trump</strong> administration &mdash; with the fee for U.S. port entry by Chinese-built vessels reaching up to 1.5 million dollars. The reaction from the U.S. transportation and shipping sector includes <strong>NRF</strong>, <strong>U.S. Chamber of Commerce</strong>, <strong>World Shipping Council</strong> (<strong>Joe Kramek</strong> CEO), representing the primary opposition groups &mdash; asserting that the fees will ultimately be passed on to consumers. <strong>SCA</strong> (<strong>Shipbuilders Council of America</strong>), <strong>American Maritime Partnership</strong> (<strong>AMP</strong>), represent the primary domestic shipbuilding advocacy groups. In conclusion, <strong>Atkinson's</strong> analysis of U.S. shipbuilding suggests that global maritime shipping geography is undergoing fundamental redesign &mdash; for supply chain managers, <strong>Chinese</strong>-built vessel fees and alternative East <strong>Asian</strong> supply sources appear to represent a primary strategic priority.</p>
