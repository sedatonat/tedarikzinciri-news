<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports that the <strong>Port of Los Angeles</strong> does not expect a significant surge in cargo following the 90-day pause in reciprocal tariffs between the <strong>U.S.</strong> and <strong>China</strong> — though it does anticipate volumes from <strong>China</strong> to move higher. During a May 19 briefing, <strong>Gene Seroka</strong>, Executive Director of the <strong>Port of Los Angeles</strong>, stated that he expects vessel volumes to remain well below what the port experienced during the pandemic peak — while also forecasting lower inventory across "various retail sectors" and for parts supply to <strong>U.S.</strong> factories. "This leaves us with fewer product selections and presumably higher prices," he said. "For now, uncertainty persists in every business meeting I have — and finding the best decisions possible for companies remains difficult." <strong>Seroka</strong> also confirmed that many <strong>U.S.</strong> companies have begun resuming shipments from <strong>China</strong> after pausing orders prior to the Trump administration's announcement of the tariff pause in early May. However, the sudden surge in demand for cargo space has driven an increase in shipping rates — pushing retailers and manufacturers to "plan carefully."</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Port of Los Angeles</strong> (<strong>POLA</strong>), based in <strong>San Pedro, California, U.S.</strong>, with <strong>Gene Seroka</strong> as Executive Director since 2014, is the largest container port in the <strong>U.S.</strong>, handling 8.6 million TEU annually in 2023, and serves as the primary U.S. gateway for over 40% of <strong>Asia-Pacific</strong> container traffic. The <strong>Port of Long Beach</strong> (<strong>POLB</strong>; <strong>Mario Cordero</strong> CEO), located adjacent to <strong>POLA</strong> in <strong>San Pedro Bay</strong>, handled 8.0 million TEU annually in 2023. Together, the two ports handle 35-40% of all U.S. container traffic — serving as the primary axis for <strong>Asia-Pacific</strong> connectivity. <strong>POLA</strong>'s principal carrier partners include <strong>MSC</strong>, <strong>Maersk</strong>, <strong>CMA CGM</strong>, <strong>COSCO Shipping</strong>, <strong>ONE</strong> (<strong>Ocean Network Express</strong>), <strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong>, <strong>Evergreen</strong>, <strong>Yang Ming</strong>, <strong>HMM</strong>, and <strong>Wan Hai</strong>. <strong>POLA</strong>'s major terminals are operated by <strong>APM Terminals Pier 400</strong>, <strong>Yusen Terminals</strong>, <strong>Fenix Marine Services</strong>, <strong>TraPac</strong>, and <strong>Everport Terminal Services</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.S.-China</strong> tariff war timeline includes: (1) February 2025, <strong>Trump</strong> imposes 10% tariffs on <strong>China</strong>; (2) March 2025, increases to 20%; (3) April 2025, escalates to 145% as reciprocal tariffs — with <strong>China</strong> responding at 125%; (4) May 2025, following negotiations in <strong>Geneva, Switzerland</strong>, a 90-day pause — with the U.S. reducing tariffs from 145% to 30% and <strong>China</strong> cutting from 125% to 10%; (5) additional negotiations in <strong>London, UK</strong> in June 2025. The <strong>U.S. Trade Representative</strong> (<strong>USTR</strong>; <strong>Jamieson Greer</strong>), <strong>Treasury Secretary</strong> <strong>Scott Bessent</strong>, and <strong>Commerce Secretary</strong> <strong>Howard Lutnick</strong> form the principal U.S. negotiating team. On the <strong>China</strong> side, <strong>He Lifeng</strong> (Vice Premier) and <strong>Wang Wentao</strong> (<strong>Ministry of Commerce</strong>) are the lead negotiating officials. <strong>POLA</strong> volume trends show May 2025 volumes down 10% year-over-year — declining from months exceeding 1 million TEU before tariffs to 750,000-800,000 TEU afterward. The <strong>National Retail Federation</strong> (<strong>NRF</strong>; <strong>Matt Shay</strong> CEO) <strong>Global Port Tracker</strong> (in partnership with <strong>Hackett Associates</strong>) forecasts U.S. imports could decline 15-25% year-over-year in the second half of 2025.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, container freight rates — tracked by the <strong>Drewry World Container Index</strong> for <strong>Asia-U.S. West Coast</strong> 40-foot containers — have risen to $4,500-$5,500 in May 2025 following the sudden demand surge after the tariff pause — below pandemic peaks ($12,000+) but well above normal levels ($1,500-$2,500). The <strong>Shanghai Containerized Freight Index</strong> (<strong>SCFI</strong>), <strong>Baltic Exchange</strong>, and <strong>Freightos Baltic Index</strong> (<strong>FBX</strong>) are primary price indices. <strong>Asia-Pacific</strong> carrier alliances include <strong>2M</strong> (Maersk + MSC; dissolving in 2025), <strong>Gemini Cooperation</strong> (Maersk + Hapag-Lloyd; February 2025), <strong>Premier Alliance</strong> (ONE + HMM + Yang Ming), and <strong>Ocean Alliance</strong> (CMA CGM + COSCO + OOCL + Evergreen). In conclusion, <strong>Seroka</strong>'s assessment of uncertainty suggests that globally, the U.S.-China trade war and <strong>Trans-Pacific</strong> container trade remain fundamentally unstable — with tariff buffer stock and flexible planning appearing to be primary strategic priorities for supply chain managers.</p>

3. <strong>U.S.</strong> companies are resuming orders — shipping rates are increasing.<br>
