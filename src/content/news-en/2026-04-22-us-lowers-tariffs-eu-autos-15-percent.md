<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The U.S.</strong> is reducing tariffs on automotive imports from the <strong>European Union</strong> to 15% retroactively from August 1, cementing the terms of a framework trade agreement that the two sides struck approximately two months ago. The <strong>Department of Commerce</strong> and the <strong>Office of the U.S. Trade Representative</strong> released a document online on September 24 detailing the changes, reducing customs duties on a range of goods. The decision will help further ease tensions between <strong>Washington</strong> and <strong>Brussels</strong>, as the two sides move to implement the details of a trade agreement announced at one of <strong>President Donald Trump</strong>'s golf courses in <strong>Scotland</strong>. The formality of the <strong>Federal Register</strong> filing comes as lower tariffs have been anticipated for weeks, making it particularly welcome news for <strong>Europe</strong>'s automotive industry. Shares of German automakers <strong>Volkswagen AG</strong>, <strong>Porsche AG</strong>, and <strong>Mercedes-Benz Group AG</strong> rose on the news. <strong>Porsche</strong>, being among the hardest hit since it primarily sells imports into the <strong>U.S.</strong>, climbed 3.8% in <strong>Frankfurt</strong> on September 24. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.S.-EU framework agreement</strong> was struck in July 2025 at <strong>Trump</strong>'s golf facility in <strong>Turnberry, Scotland</strong>, with <strong>European Commission President</strong> <strong>Ursula von der Leyen</strong>. <strong>Howard Lutnick</strong> serves as <strong>U.S. Secretary of Commerce</strong>. <strong>Jamieson Greer</strong> is the <strong>USTR</strong> Trade Representative. <strong>Maroš Šefčovič</strong>, who is <strong>Slovak</strong>, serves as <strong>European Commissioner for Trade</strong> and is the primary negotiator on the <strong>Brussels</strong> side.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the changes outlined in the filing include an exemption list for sectors including aircraft, aircraft parts, and generic pharmaceutical drugs plus components—as well as "non-existent natural resources" such as mushrooms and certain metals and ores—effective from September 1. These goods will retain lower so-called "<strong>most favored nation</strong>" or <strong>MFN</strong> tariffs. The <strong>WTO</strong> (<strong>World Trade Organization</strong>), <strong>GATT</strong> (<strong>General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade</strong>), and the <strong>MFN</strong> principle are the primary multilateral trade frameworks. The <strong>HTS</strong> (<strong>Harmonized Tariff Schedule</strong>) and <strong>HS code</strong> are the primary trade coding standards. <strong>Section 232</strong> (<strong>U.S. Trade Expansion Act of 1962</strong>; <strong>national security</strong>), <strong>Section 301</strong> (<strong>Trade Act of 1974</strong>; <strong>unfair foreign trade</strong>), <strong>Section 201</strong> (<strong>safeguard</strong>), and <strong>IEEPA</strong> (<strong>International Emergency Economic Powers Act</strong>) are the primary <strong>U.S.</strong> tariff mechanisms. <strong>USMCA</strong> (<strong>U.S.-Mexico-Canada Agreement</strong>; formerly <strong>NAFTA</strong>), <strong>U.S.-Korea FTA</strong> (<strong>KORUS</strong>), <strong>U.S.-Japan Trade Agreement</strong>, and <strong>U.S.-EU framework</strong> are the primary <strong>U.S.</strong> free trade and framework agreements.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>European</strong> automotive industry produces 18-20 million units annually on a global basis and employs 12% of all sectors. The <strong>ACEA</strong> (<strong>European Automobile Manufacturers' Association</strong>) is the primary <strong>European</strong> automotive industry organization. The <strong>Volkswagen Group</strong> (<strong>VW</strong>; <strong>Audi</strong>; <strong>Porsche</strong>; <strong>SEAT</strong>; <strong>Cupra</strong>; <strong>Skoda</strong>; <strong>Lamborghini</strong>; <strong>Bentley</strong>; <strong>Ducati</strong>; <strong>Scania</strong>; <strong>MAN</strong>; <strong>Bugatti Rimac</strong> joint venture), <strong>Stellantis</strong> (<strong>Fiat</strong>; <strong>Chrysler</strong>; <strong>Jeep</strong>; <strong>Ram</strong>; <strong>Dodge</strong>; <strong>Peugeot</strong>; <strong>Citroën</strong>; <strong>Opel</strong>; <strong>Vauxhall</strong>; <strong>DS</strong>; <strong>Alfa Romeo</strong>; <strong>Lancia</strong>; <strong>Maserati</strong>; <strong>Abarth</strong>), <strong>BMW Group</strong> (<strong>BMW</strong>; <strong>Mini</strong>; <strong>Rolls-Royce</strong>), <strong>Mercedes-Benz Group</strong>, <strong>Daimler Truck</strong>, <strong>Renault Group</strong> (<strong>Renault</strong>; <strong>Dacia</strong>; <strong>Alpine</strong>; <strong>Mobilize</strong>), <strong>Volvo Cars</strong> (owned by <strong>Geely</strong>), <strong>Polestar</strong>, <strong>Ferrari</strong>, <strong>Aston Martin</strong>, <strong>Jaguar Land Rover</strong> (owned by <strong>Tata Motors</strong>), and <strong>McLaren</strong> are the primary <strong>European</strong> automotive brands. <strong>Robert Bosch</strong>, <strong>Continental</strong>, <strong>ZF Friedrichshafen</strong>, <strong>Schaeffler</strong>, <strong>Mahle</strong>, <strong>Brembo</strong>, <strong>Valeo</strong>, <strong>Faurecia</strong> (<strong>Forvia</strong>), <strong>Plastic Omnium</strong>, and <strong>Magneti Marelli</strong> (<strong>Marelli</strong>) are the primary <strong>European</strong> Tier 1 suppliers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.S.-EU</strong> automotive trade balance sees the <strong>U.S.</strong> importing 38-40 billion dollars annually in automobiles from the <strong>EU</strong>, while exporting only 8-10 billion dollars to the <strong>EU</strong>. <strong>Volkswagen Group</strong>, <strong>Porsche</strong>, and <strong>Audi</strong> are the largest <strong>European</strong> exporters to the <strong>U.S.</strong> market. <strong>BMW Group</strong> operates the globally largest <strong>BMW</strong> facility in <strong>Spartanburg, South Carolina</strong>, exporting the <strong>X3</strong>, <strong>X5</strong>, and <strong>X7</strong>. <strong>Mercedes-Benz</strong> produces <strong>SUVs</strong> in <strong>Tuscaloosa, Alabama</strong>. <strong>Volkswagen</strong> manufactures the <strong>Atlas</strong> and <strong>ID.4</strong> in <strong>Chattanooga, Tennessee</strong>. <strong>Stellantis</strong> produces <strong>Jeep</strong>, <strong>Ram</strong>, <strong>Chrysler</strong>, and <strong>Dodge</strong> in <strong>Detroit</strong>, <strong>Toledo, Ohio</strong>, <strong>Sterling Heights, Michigan</strong>, and <strong>Belvidere, Illinois</strong>. <strong>Volvo Cars</strong> manufactures the <strong>EX90</strong> and <strong>S60</strong> in <strong>Charleston, South Carolina</strong>. <strong>Polestar</strong> produces the <strong>Polestar 3</strong> in <strong>Charleston</strong>. <strong>Audi</strong> and <strong>Porsche</strong> do not produce domestically in the <strong>U.S.</strong>, making them the brands most heavily impacted by tariff effects. Consequently, the <strong>U.S.-EU</strong> 15% auto tariff lock-in represents a concrete signal that the transatlantic automotive trade environment is reaching near-term clarity, though structural imbalances persist.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. The <strong>U.S.</strong> is reducing <strong>EU</strong> auto tariffs to 15% retroactively from August 1.<br>
2. <strong>Commerce</strong> and <strong>USTR</strong> file with the <strong>Federal Register</strong> on September 24.<br>
3. <strong>VW</strong>, <strong>Porsche</strong>, and <strong>Mercedes-Benz</strong> shares respond positively.<br>
4. <strong>Porsche</strong> climbs 3.8% in <strong>Frankfurt</strong>.<br>
5. Aircraft, generic pharma, and mushroom/metal exemptions take effect from September 1.</p>
