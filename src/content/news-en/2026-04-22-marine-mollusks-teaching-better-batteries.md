<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong>; in the race to make advanced technologies cheaper, cleaner, and more efficient, scientists have turned to an unlikely teacher: a tiny marine mollusk. Researchers at <strong>UC Irvine</strong>, working with partners in <strong>Japan</strong>, are discovering how <strong>chitons</strong> (small sea creatures living in intertidal zones worldwide) build their ultra-hard teeth layer by layer. This insight is now directing efforts to develop cleaner, more precise methods for synthesizing critical materials that power everything from fuel cells to next-generation electronics. "Chitons grow new teeth every few days—superior teeth made from materials used in industrial cutting tools, grinding media, dental implants, surgical implants, and protective coatings—yet made at room temperature and nanoscale precision," says <strong>David Kisailus</strong>, a materials and engineering professor at <strong>UC Irvine</strong> and director of the school's <strong>Biomimetic and Nanostructured Materials Laboratory</strong>. "We can learn a great deal from these biological designs and processes."</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, biomimetics is an engineering design discipline that mimics biological systems in nature—popularized by <strong>Janine Benyus</strong>'s 1997 book "<strong>Biomimicry: Innovation Inspired by Nature</strong>." <strong>Velcro</strong> (imitating burdock plant), <strong>Shinkansen</strong> bullet train (imitating kingfisher beak), <strong>Eastgate Centre Harare</strong> (termite nest ventilation), <strong>Speedo Fastskin</strong> (shark skin), <strong>Lotusan</strong> paint (lotus effect), <strong>Mercedes-Benz Bionic</strong> (boxfish hydrodynamics), and <strong>Whalepower turbines</strong> (humpback whale flipper) are primary examples of biomimetic applications. <strong>UC Irvine</strong> (<strong>University of California Irvine</strong>) is based in <strong>Irvine, California</strong>; <strong>Howard Gillman</strong> is <strong>Chancellor</strong>. <strong>Henry Samueli School of Engineering</strong> is <strong>UC Irvine</strong>'s engineering school—<strong>Magnus Egerstedt</strong> is <strong>Dean</strong>. <strong>David Kisailus</strong> previously served at <strong>UC Riverside</strong> before joining <strong>UC Irvine</strong>—a pioneer in the field of biomimetic materials science.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the global battery industry has primary application areas in <strong>EV</strong> (<strong>Electric Vehicle</strong>), grid storage (<strong>BESS</strong>), and consumer electronics. <strong>CATL</strong> (<strong>Contemporary Amperex Technology Co. Limited</strong>; <strong>Ningde, China</strong>) is the world's largest battery manufacturer—<strong>Robin Zeng</strong> is <strong>Founder &amp; Chairman</strong>—commanding more than 35% of the global <strong>EV</strong> battery share. <strong>BYD</strong> (<strong>Build Your Dreams</strong>; <strong>Shenzhen, China</strong>) is the second largest—<strong>Wang Chuanfu</strong> is <strong>Chairman</strong>. <strong>LG Energy Solution</strong> (<strong>Seoul, South Korea</strong>), <strong>Samsung SDI</strong>, <strong>SK On</strong>, <strong>Panasonic Energy</strong> (<strong>Tesla</strong> partner), <strong>Northvolt</strong> (<strong>Sweden</strong>; bankrupt), <strong>EVE Energy</strong>, <strong>Gotion High-Tech</strong>, <strong>SVOLT</strong>, <strong>CALB</strong>, <strong>Farasis Energy</strong>, <strong>Verkor</strong> (<strong>France</strong>), <strong>ACC</strong> (<strong>Stellantis</strong>; <strong>Mercedes</strong>; <strong>TotalEnergies</strong>), <strong>PowerCo</strong> (<strong>VW</strong>), <strong>Envision AESC</strong>, <strong>Ultium Cells</strong> (<strong>GM-LG</strong>), <strong>BlueOval SK</strong> (<strong>Ford-SK</strong>), and <strong>Toyota Battery Manufacturing</strong> are primary <strong>EV</strong> battery manufacturers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, battery chemistries and technologies include <strong>NMC</strong> (<strong>Nickel Manganese Cobalt</strong>), <strong>NCA</strong> (<strong>Nickel Cobalt Aluminum</strong>), <strong>LFP</strong> (<strong>Lithium Iron Phosphate</strong>), <strong>LMFP</strong> (<strong>Lithium Manganese Iron Phosphate</strong>), <strong>solid-state battery</strong> (<strong>SSB</strong>), <strong>lithium-sulfur</strong>, <strong>sodium-ion</strong>, <strong>silicon anode</strong>, <strong>lithium metal anode</strong>, and <strong>dry electrode</strong> as primary battery technologies. <strong>QuantumScape</strong>, <strong>Solid Power</strong>, <strong>Factorial Energy</strong>, <strong>SES AI</strong>, <strong>Sila Nanotechnologies</strong>, <strong>Group14</strong>, <strong>Amprius Technologies</strong>, <strong>StoreDot</strong>, <strong>Enovix</strong>, <strong>Natron Energy</strong>, <strong>Form Energy</strong> (long-duration storage), and <strong>ESS Inc.</strong> and <strong>Eos Energy</strong> are primary next-generation battery startups. <strong>Argonne National Laboratory</strong>, <strong>Oak Ridge National Laboratory</strong>, <strong>SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory</strong>, <strong>Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory</strong>, and <strong>NREL</strong> (<strong>National Renewable Energy Laboratory</strong>) are primary <strong>U.S.</strong> battery research laboratories. <strong>Battery 500 Consortium</strong>, <strong>DOE Joint Center for Energy Storage Research</strong> (<strong>JCESR</strong>), <strong>European Battery Alliance</strong>, and <strong>Faraday Institution</strong> are primary battery research consortia. Ultimately, the biomimetic study of chiton teeth enables material synthesis at room temperature and nanoscale precision—potentially representing a new manufacturing paradigm for the global battery industry.</p>
