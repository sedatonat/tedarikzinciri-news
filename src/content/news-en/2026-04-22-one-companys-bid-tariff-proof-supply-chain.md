<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports that <strong>Josh Carr</strong>, CEO of <strong>Echo</strong>, says the hydrogen water flask and filter company has successfully built a "tariff-proof" supply chain—a notable achievement at a time when many companies are discussing the need to raise consumer prices to cover the additional costs imposed by new tariffs. According to an April 22 press release from <strong>Echo</strong>, the company made the decision in 2024 to shift production from <strong>China</strong> to the <strong>Philippines</strong> as a precaution against the tariffs that the incoming <strong>Trump</strong> administration had been hinting at for months. Today, the company is "operating at full speed," according to CEO <strong>Josh Carr</strong>, and has managed to maintain both its supply and pricing despite absorbing the "significant costs" associated with moving away from <strong>China</strong>. This includes tooling and setup fees that were previously covered by state subsidies from the Chinese government, as well as investments in new supplier relationships and equipment. Nevertheless, <strong>Echo</strong> estimates it will save approximately 10 million dollars by 2025 through its transition to the <strong>Philippines</strong>. "This is not just about delivery times—it's about insulation," says <strong>Carr</strong>. "Because we invested in flexibility before it became urgent, we can absorb price volatility."</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Echo</strong> (<strong>Echo Hydrogen</strong>) is a U.S.-based direct-to-consumer hydrogen water flask and filter manufacturer. Under CEO <strong>Josh Carr</strong>, production was conducted in <strong>China</strong> until 2024, when it was relocated to the <strong>Philippines</strong>. Other major consumer electronics and hardware manufacturers exiting <strong>China</strong> include <strong>Apple</strong> (shifting <strong>iPhone</strong> production to <strong>India</strong> with <strong>Tata Electronics</strong> and <strong>Foxconn</strong>, and <strong>MacBooks</strong> to <strong>Vietnam</strong>); <strong>Dell</strong>; <strong>HP</strong>; <strong>Lenovo</strong>; <strong>Logitech</strong>; <strong>GoPro</strong>; <strong>Anker</strong>; <strong>Nike</strong> (<strong>Vietnam</strong>, <strong>Indonesia</strong>); <strong>Adidas</strong>; <strong>Crocs</strong>; <strong>Hasbro</strong>; <strong>Mattel</strong>; <strong>Funko</strong>; and <strong>iRobot</strong>—all implementing core <strong>China+1</strong> strategies. Primary manufacturing exit destinations include: (1) <strong>Vietnam</strong> (<strong>Hanoi</strong>, <strong>Ho Chi Minh City</strong>, with <strong>VinFast</strong> and <strong>Foxconn</strong> facilities); (2) <strong>India</strong> (<strong>Tata</strong>, <strong>Foxconn India</strong>, <strong>Pegatron India</strong>, <strong>Wistron India</strong>); (3) <strong>Mexico</strong> (<strong>USMCA</strong> advantage, nearshoring); (4) <strong>Thailand</strong>; (5) <strong>Indonesia</strong>; (6) <strong>Malaysia</strong>; (7) <strong>Philippines</strong>; (8) <strong>Bangladesh</strong> (for apparel); (9) <strong>Poland</strong>/<strong>Czech Republic</strong> (for <strong>Europe</strong>); and (10) <strong>Turkey</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Philippines</strong>' manufacturing ecosystem involves key investment promotion agencies: the <strong>Department of Trade and Industry</strong> (<strong>DTI</strong>, with <strong>Cristina Roque</strong> as Secretary); the <strong>Philippine Economic Zone Authority</strong> (<strong>PEZA</strong>); and the <strong>Board of Investments</strong> (<strong>BOI</strong>). Major manufacturing hubs in the <strong>Philippines</strong> include <strong>Subic Bay Freeport</strong>, <strong>Clark Freeport</strong>, <strong>Cavite Economic Zone</strong>, <strong>Laguna Technopark</strong>, and <strong>Cebu Light Industrial Park</strong>. The <strong>Philippines</strong>' 2025 GSP+ status provides tariff advantages for exports to the <strong>European Union</strong>. Additionally, trade relations between the <strong>U.S.</strong> and the <strong>Philippines</strong> operate under the <strong>FY2024 Trade and Investment Framework Agreement</strong> (<strong>TIFA</strong>). On Trump's "Liberation Day" (April 2, 2025), the <strong>Philippines</strong> faces a 17% tariff—still a significant advantage compared to <strong>China</strong>'s 145%+ tariff. Other low-tariff destinations include: <strong>Vietnam</strong> at 46% (suspended); <strong>Thailand</strong> at 36%; <strong>Indonesia</strong> at 32%; <strong>Malaysia</strong> at 24%; and <strong>India</strong> at 26%.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, tariff-proofing strategies include: (1) nearshoring (sourcing from nearby countries); (2) friendshoring (sourcing from allied nations); (3) reshoring (bringing production back); (4) China+1/China+N (production diversification); (5) tariff engineering (product classification optimization); (6) foreign trade zone (<strong>FTZ</strong>) utilization; (7) bonded warehouse operations; (8) de minimis usage (being phased out); (9) first sale doctrine; and (10) HTS (<strong>Harmonized Tariff Schedule</strong>) reclassification. Key nearshoring and tariff strategy consulting firms include <strong>BCG</strong>, <strong>Bain</strong>, <strong>McKinsey</strong>, <strong>AlixPartners</strong>, <strong>Maine Pointe</strong>, <strong>Korn Ferry</strong>, <strong>Argon & Co</strong>, <strong>Inverto</strong>, <strong>Roland Berger</strong>, and <strong>Kearney</strong>. Principal U.S. federal tariff authorities include the <strong>U.S. Trade Representative</strong> (<strong>USTR</strong>, <strong>Jamieson Greer</strong>); <strong>U.S. Customs and Border Protection</strong> (<strong>CBP</strong>); and the <strong>Commerce Department</strong> (<strong>Howard Lutnick</strong>). In conclusion, <strong>Echo</strong> CEO <strong>Carr</strong>'s supply chain restructuring experience suggests that the exit strategy from <strong>China</strong> is being fundamentally redesigned globally—with early action, scenario planning, and a multi-country supply base appearing as core strategic priorities for supply chain managers.</p>

2. In 2025, <strong>Echo</strong> will realize approximately 10 million dollars in tariff cost savings.<br>
3. On Trump's Liberation Day, the <strong>Philippines</strong> faces a 17% tariff compared to 145%+ for <strong>China</strong>.<br>
