<p style="text-align: justify">One of the world's largest nuclear facilities is returning to operation as of January 21. According to reports carried by <strong>The New York Times</strong>, <strong>Tokyo Electric Power (TEPCO)</strong>, which also operates the <strong>Fukushima</strong> facility, is bringing Unit 6, the first reactor at the <strong>Kashiwazaki-Kariwa</strong> complex located in the main southern island of <strong>Niigata Prefecture</strong>, back online. The restart has been delayed by one day due to a safety alarm failing to sound during a test conducted over the weekend. This move signals a new phase in <strong>Japan</strong>'s nuclear policy since 2011 and reignites debate over electricity supply security for the country's core industrial base.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Fifteen years ago, an 8.9-magnitude earthquake sent a tsunami to <strong>Japan</strong>'s coastline, cutting power and cooling to three reactors at the <strong>Fukushima Daiichi</strong> facility. The disaster led to a nuclear accident in which three reactor cores largely melted within the first three days. Following the 2011 disaster, <strong>Japan</strong> shut down all 54 operating reactors. Today, the country stands out among major economies turning back to nuclear to feed chip factories that are carbon neutral and power data centers required for artificial intelligence that operate 24 hours continuously to meet growing electricity demand. This situation illustrates how the world's fourth-largest economy is accelerating the pace of restarting reactors that have been mothballed for more than a decade.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The Times</strong> assessment directly links <strong>Japan</strong>'s shift to the sustained baseline power demand brought by artificial intelligence and data center growth. Carbon-free, continuous, and large-scale power requirements enable nuclear to regain prominence in the country's energy portfolio. The restart of <strong>Kashiwazaki-Kariwa</strong> serves as a balancing factor, strengthening electricity supply security for the <strong>Tokyo Metropolitan</strong> region while simultaneously reducing dependence on <strong>liquefied natural gas (LNG)</strong> imports. From a supply chain perspective, this restart carries with it a view that revalues uranium fuel value chains, cooling equipment manufacturing, and nuclear safety equipment suppliers over the medium and long term.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Before the <strong>Fukushima</strong> disaster, <strong>Japan</strong> had one of the world's most comprehensive nuclear programs. After the disaster, only 15 of the 33 operating reactors have been restarted, a situation stemming largely from the rigorous approval process conducted by local and state governments. The new era brings back into focus how public opinion and regulatory approval processes are determining factors on the pace of the nuclear program. The safety testing regime that <strong>TEPCO</strong> is conducting at <strong>Kashiwazaki-Kariwa</strong> will serve as a benchmark for future restarts. As a result, <strong>Japan</strong>'s step has become one of the important links in a global transformation in which baseline power electricity supply is being reshaped around nuclear in the age of artificial intelligence.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>\n1. <strong>TEPCO</strong> brings <strong>Kashiwazaki-Kariwa Unit 6</strong> back online on January 21.<br>\n2.
