<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Stena Bulk</strong> announced the sale of the oil tanker <strong>Stena Immaculate</strong>, which suffered severe damage following a tragic collision in the North Sea in March. According to the company's statement, the vessel was transferred to a buyer for <strong>repair and preparation to return to service</strong> and will be repaired at <strong>Desan Shipyard</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">With the sale, the vessel was re-flagged from <strong>the United States flag to the Malta flag</strong>. In its statement, Stena Bulk thanked all parties involved in the salvage and cargo transfer operations for their <strong>professional support and cooperation</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This sale took place <strong>seven months after the collision, which resulted in one fatality and caused a significant aviation fuel spill</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Collision details</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">The incident occurred on <strong>March 10, 2025</strong>, near the Humber Estuary off the English coast, <strong>approximately 14 nautical miles northeast of Spurn Head</strong>. The <strong>Portuguese-flagged container ship Solong</strong> struck the anchored <strong>Stena Immaculate</strong> tanker at <strong>09:47</strong> while <strong>sailing at 16 knots</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The collision ruptured the tanker's <strong>No. 7 starboard fuel tank</strong>, spilling approximately <strong>220,000 barrels (220,204.5 barrels)</strong> of aviation fuel into the sea. As a result of the impact, fuel also splashed onto <strong>the Solong's bow</strong>, and <strong>a fire broke out quickly</strong>. The flames spread to cargo containers on the upper deck of the container ship.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The collision claimed the life of <strong>Mark Pernia, a Filipino deck seaman aboard the Solong</strong>, who went missing and was not found despite search efforts. <strong>UK authorities</strong> concluded that <strong>Pernia had died</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Investigation and legal proceedings</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">In the preliminary report released by the <strong>UK Maritime Accidents Investigation Branch (MAIB)</strong>, it was noted that visibility in the incident area <strong>ranged from 0.25 to 2 nautical miles</strong>. The report also revealed that <strong>neither vessel had a dedicated lookout on the bridge at the time</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Following these findings, <strong>Solong's captain Vladimir Motin</strong> was arrested to stand trial on charges of <strong>gross negligence manslaughter</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In MAIB's final report:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>navigation and watchkeeping</strong> practices,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>crew fatigue management</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>vessel maintenance condition</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>safety of the offshore area used as an anchorage at the Humber entrance</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">and <strong>the impact of environmental factors on the collision</strong> will be evaluated.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">Investigations into the collision were not limited to the UK; authorities from the <strong>United States</strong> and <strong>Portugal</strong> are also conducting their own <strong>independent investigations</strong> due to the international nature of the incident.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Stena Immaculate's new status</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">Following the collision, the decision was made to <strong>withdraw Stena Immaculate from the Stena Bulk fleet</strong>. In August 2025, the <strong>U.S. Maritime Administration (MARAD)</strong> approved the <strong>CS Anthem</strong> tanker as a replacement under the <strong>Tanker Security Program (TSP)</strong>. As a result, CS Anthem, together with other tankers managed by Crowley named <strong>Stena Imperative</strong> and <strong>Stena Impeccable</strong>, joined the <strong>Department of Defense's strategic fuel fleet</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The sale of Stena Immaculate</strong> allows the company to end the technical and financial burden resulting from the collision, while creating <strong>a repair and return-to-service opportunity</strong> for the new buyer. The vessel will undergo comprehensive technical renovation at <strong>Desan Shipyard in Istanbul Tuzla</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Industrial implications</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to experts, the incident has brought into focus <strong>the security protocols of ship anchorage areas</strong>, <strong>navigational watchkeeping standards</strong>, and <strong>tanker traffic waiting in the Humber approaches</strong>. Particularly, the absence of a lookout <strong>in conditions of heavy fog and poor visibility</strong> is assessed as a critical vulnerability in such collisions.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Should Stena Immaculate return to service, the vessel is expected to be <strong>modernized in compliance with international environmental and safety standards</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
