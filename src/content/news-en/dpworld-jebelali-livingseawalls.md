<p style="text-align: justify">Global port and logistics operator <strong>DP World</strong> has taken a significant step forward in its commitment to marine ecosystem conservation and enhancing the environmental resilience of port infrastructure at <strong>Jebel Ali Port</strong>. According to the company's announcement, <strong>1,000 eco-engineered Living Seawalls panels</strong> have been successfully installed at the port facility, completing the <strong>first phase</strong> of the program. The project aims to ensure that port infrastructure not only supports commercial operations but also creates <strong>functional marine habitats</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The Living Seawalls panels are based on the <strong>Living Seawalls</strong> program developed by the <strong>Sydney Institute of Marine Science</strong>. These modular panels are designed with <strong>shapes, grooves and surface textures</strong> that mimic natural rock formations, enabling marine organisms such as mussels, corals, algae and small crustaceans to attach to the surfaces and develop <strong>complex micro-habitats</strong> over time.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Target for Region's Longest Continuous Structure</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">DP World plans to expand the Living Seawalls installation at Jebel Ali Port to <strong>6,000 panels by 2028</strong>. Once this scale is reached, the project will be positioned as <strong>the longest continuous ecological coastal structure in the Middle East region</strong>. The company views this structure not merely as a local environmental initiative but as <strong>a scalable global model</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Abdulla Bin Damithan, DP World GCC CEO and Managing Director</strong>, emphasizes that ports play a critical role in coastal resilience. According to Bin Damithan, <strong>science-based designs</strong> enable continuous trade while simultaneously allowing natural life to flourish. This approach transcends traditional port development thinking and highlights the concept of <strong>nature-positive infrastructure</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Biodiversity and Scientific Monitoring</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>biological monitoring process for the Living Seawalls panels at Jebel Ali Port will commence in 2026</strong>. This work aims to provide data to <strong>global scientific research on ecological resilience in port environments</strong>. The monitoring will include regular analysis of species diversity, colonization rates and ecosystem functions.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">DP World notes that this scientific approach will not only measure environmental performance but also contribute to developing port planning and maintenance strategies in a <strong>data-driven</strong> manner.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Findings from the Callao Pilot</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">The Jebel Ali implementation is based on a pilot project previously conducted at the <strong>Port of Callao in Peru</strong>. During a <strong>12-month monitoring period</strong> on the Callao Living Seawalls panels, <strong>66 species belonging to 12 different marine organism groups</strong> were identified. Among these, <strong>seven species were observed only on Living Seawalls surfaces</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The pilot study also found <strong>no invasive species</strong>, while <strong>algal diversity and ecological functions increased</strong>. These results demonstrate that Living Seawalls panels improve not only species numbers but also ecosystem <strong>functionality</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Strategic Framework and Global Objectives</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">The Jebel Ali project is being implemented as part of <strong>DP World's Ocean Strategy</strong> and aligns with the global biodiversity targets established under the <strong>Kunming–Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework</strong>. This framework aims to reverse the negative impacts of human activities on nature and <strong>halt nature loss by 2030</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">DP World views adapting port infrastructure to be compatible with biodiversity as a strategic investment in terms of long-term <strong>climate adaptation</strong>, <strong>coastal protection</strong> and <strong>social license</strong>. The dense concrete and steel structures surrounding ports create challenges for natural life, while Living Seawalls transform these hard surfaces into <strong>ecologically active areas</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Overall Assessment</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">The first 1,000 Living Seawalls panels installed by DP World at Jebel Ali Port demonstrate that ports can be more than just trade centers; they can also function as <strong>ecosystem-supporting infrastructure</strong>. Should the project reach 6,000 panels by 2028, Jebel Ali could become a global example of <strong>an integrated port showcasing science, sustainability and logistics</strong>. This approach provides <strong>an applicable and scalable reference model</strong> for other major ports worldwide.</p>
<p><br></p>
