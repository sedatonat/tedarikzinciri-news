<p style="text-align: justify">One of the most critical commercial relationships in the U.S. parcel delivery sector, the <strong>USPS–Amazon contract</strong>, is mired in serious uncertainty as of December 2025. The current agreement expires in <strong>October 2026</strong>; however, reports that USPS Postmaster General <strong>David Steiner</strong> wants to conduct a <strong>reverse auction</strong> early in 2026 have galvanized both Amazon and the broader shipping industry. According to Washington Post reporting, USPS is considering a model that would grant <strong>access to postal facilities to the companies bidding highest</strong>, replacing Amazon's current privileged access. If implemented, Amazon would need to compete with <strong>national retail brands</strong> and new regional carriers, despite being USPS's largest customer.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The move comes as a surprise, as Amazon noted it has been in contract renewal discussions with USPS for nearly <strong>a year</strong>, and the change in direction adds uncertainty to its distribution network. Amazon's statement reads:</p>
<p><br>
"<strong>We were surprised to hear they want to run an auction… we are evaluating all options.</strong>"<br>
Amazon generates an annual <strong>6 billion USD</strong> in volume and accounts for <strong>7.5%</strong> of USPS sales. Therefore, a potential split could create significant operational costs for both parties.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This development is directly tied to USPS's long-standing financial pressures. The agency reported a <strong>9 billion USD loss in FY2025</strong>. Although USPS has achieved substantial capacity growth in package volume (60 million → 88 million packages/day), excess capacity during off-season periods and long-term volume declines in <strong>First-Class Mail</strong> are worsening revenue loss.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to <strong>ShipMatrix</strong>'s Robert Persuit, the crisis stems from three fundamental historical factors:</p>
<ol>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>UPS–USPS relationship collapsed in 2025:</strong> USPS's attempt to sharply increase final-mile pricing for UPS led to UPS pulling its <strong>Surepost</strong> and <strong>Mail Innovations</strong> operations. This operational split caused UPS hundreds of millions of dollars in additional costs.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The same move resulted in revenue loss for USPS and contributed to the <strong>9 billion dollar loss</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">As a result, USPS has had to attempt to demand higher prices from Amazon while simultaneously being forced to discuss <strong>re-rapprochement with UPS</strong> — a two-way squeeze.</p></li>
</ol>
<p style="text-align: justify">Amazon's complete withdrawal from USPS is not an easy option either. Persuit summarizes it this way:<br>
"<strong>Amazon holds the leverage here, but is unlikely to make abrupt moves.</strong>"<br>
Amazon carries significant infrastructure risk for both USPS and its own operations. The "<strong>glide down</strong>" it experienced with UPS (a 1.9 million package-per-day average decline between 2024Q3–2025Q3) remains fresh in memory. An Amazon departure from USPS could produce a similar operational shock.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to Persuit's analysis, the risk USPS faces today is not only Amazon's potential departure; it is also the rise of new micro-carriers such as <strong>DoorDash, Veho, Jitsu, Better Trucks</strong>. As major U.S. retailers increasingly shift to <strong>in-house delivery</strong> models, the risk of erosion in USPS demand grows.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>LPF Spend Management</strong> founder Nate Skiver takes a cautious view of the reverse auction commentary. In his view, USPS trying to "squeeze" Amazon makes no sense, because USPS gains massive benefit from Amazon volume in both <strong>network density</strong> and <strong>margin contribution</strong>. Skiver says:</p>
<p><br>
"<strong>There is no way the USPS is trying to squeeze Amazon… the idea of pushing Amazon out makes no sense.</strong>"</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Skiver also notes that Amazon is attempting to insource all its parcel volume in the long term, but this will not happen before 2026.</p>
<p><br>
Because the Amazon volume USPS carries is <strong>4–5 times</strong> the volume UPS carries, shifting this volume to other operators or Amazon Logistics could take years.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Therefore, as 2026 approaches, the picture remains unclear:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">USPS, due to financial pressures, is considering higher rates and a competitive reverse auction.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Amazon, while having alternatives on the table, knows it cannot balance its system in the short term without USPS.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Potential "reconciliation" talks between UPS and USPS could shift bargaining dynamics.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Both parties are seeking to achieve a <strong>stronger contract position</strong> without losing the other side.</p></li>
</ul>
<h2 style="text-align: justify"><br></h2>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong></h3>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">USPS's reverse auction plan for the 2026 contract is generating discussion.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Amazon, with <strong>6 billion USD</strong> in annual volume, is USPS's largest customer.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">USPS reported a <strong>9 billion USD loss in 2025</strong>; excess capacity and the UPS split are creating pressure.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Amazon knows a complete departure from USPS would be costly, but is "evaluating all options."</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">USPS is entering a period where it faces new competitors like DoorDash, Veho, and Jitsu.</p></li>
</ul>
