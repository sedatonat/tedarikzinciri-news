<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; <strong>U.S.</strong> agricultural industry; <strong>Coca-Cola</strong>'s rumored transition from high-fructose corn syrup (<strong>HFCS</strong>; <strong>High-Fructose Corn Syrup</strong>) to real cane sugar for beverages sold in the <strong>U.S.</strong>; is warning could jeopardize the nation's corn farmers. In a statement dated July 16; <strong>John Bode</strong>; President of the <strong>Corn Refiners Association</strong>; took a critical approach to <strong>Coca-Cola</strong>'s potential switch to cane sugar — claiming it "doesn't make sense." "Replacing high-fructose corn syrup with cane sugar would cause thousands of American food manufacturing jobs to be lost; reduce farm income and increase foreign sugar imports with no nutritional benefit" <strong>Bode</strong> said. The reaction came in response to a post shared by President <strong>Donald Trump</strong> on July 16 to the <strong>Truth Social</strong> platform — saying he was in talks with <strong>Coca-Cola</strong> about switching to cane sugar — and stating "they have agreed to do it." <strong>Coca-Cola</strong>; initially said it appreciated the president's enthusiasm — then in a follow-up statement on July 17 — defended its use of <strong>HFCS</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective <strong>The Coca-Cola Company</strong>; based in <strong>Atlanta Georgia</strong>; is the world's largest beverage company — <strong>James Quincey</strong>; is <strong>Chairman &amp; CEO</strong>. <strong>Coca-Cola</strong> operates <strong>Diet Coke</strong>; <strong>Coke Zero Sugar</strong>; <strong>Sprite</strong>; <strong>Fanta</strong>; <strong>Dasani</strong>; <strong>Smartwater</strong>; <strong>Powerade</strong>; <strong>Minute Maid</strong>; <strong>Simply</strong>; <strong>Fairlife</strong>; <strong>Costa Coffee</strong>; <strong>Topo Chico</strong>; <strong>Honest Tea</strong>; among its core brands. In the <strong>U.S.</strong>; <strong>Coca-Cola</strong> has traditionally used <strong>HFCS</strong> — using cane sugar in <strong>Mexico</strong> and the <strong>UK</strong> — which became known as "<strong>Mexican Coke</strong>." The <strong>Corn Refiners Association</strong> (<strong>CRA</strong>); based in <strong>Washington DC</strong>; represents the <strong>U.S.</strong> corn refining sector — <strong>John Bode</strong> is <strong>President &amp; CEO</strong>. <strong>Archer Daniels Midland</strong> (<strong>ADM</strong>; <strong>Chicago Illinois</strong>); <strong>Cargill</strong> (<strong>Minneapolis Minnesota</strong>); <strong>Ingredion</strong> (<strong>Westchester Illinois</strong>); <strong>Tate &amp; Lyle</strong> (<strong>London UK</strong>); are primary <strong>HFCS</strong> producers. <strong>Pepsi</strong> (<strong>PepsiCo</strong>; <strong>Purchase NY</strong> — <strong>Ramon Laguarta</strong> CEO); <strong>Dr Pepper Snapple</strong> (<strong>Keurig Dr Pepper</strong>; <strong>Burlington MA</strong>/<strong>Plano TX</strong>); are primary competitors.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective the <strong>U.S.</strong> corn sector is the world's largest with annual production of 14-15 billion bushels — <strong>Iowa</strong>; <strong>Illinois</strong>; <strong>Nebraska</strong>; <strong>Minnesota</strong>; <strong>Indiana</strong>; <strong>South Dakota</strong>; <strong>Kansas</strong>; <strong>Ohio</strong>; <strong>Missouri</strong>; <strong>Wisconsin</strong>; are the primary <strong>Corn Belt</strong> states. <strong>HFCS</strong> was discovered in the <strong>1970s</strong> by <strong>Japanese</strong> scientists (<strong>Yoshiyuki Takasaki</strong>; <strong>Agricultural Research Institute</strong>; <strong>Tokyo</strong>) using enzyme technology — it is a liquid sweetener derived from corn — with primary uses in beverages; bread; sauce; jam; and candy. <strong>U.S.</strong> sugar production is based on sugar beets (<strong>sugar beet</strong>; <strong>Minnesota</strong>; <strong>North Dakota</strong>; <strong>Idaho</strong>; <strong>Michigan</strong>) and sugar cane (<strong>sugar cane</strong>; <strong>Florida</strong>; <strong>Louisiana</strong>; <strong>Hawaii</strong>; <strong>Texas</strong>) — but does not meet domestic demand — imports from <strong>Brazil</strong>; <strong>Mexico</strong>; <strong>Australia</strong>; <strong>Thailand</strong>; <strong>India</strong>; <strong>Philippines</strong>; <strong>Guatemala</strong>; are made through the <strong>Tariff Rate Quota</strong> (<strong>TRQ</strong>) system. The <strong>USDA</strong> (<strong>U.S. Department of Agriculture</strong>); with <strong>Brooke Rollins</strong> as Secretary; and the <strong>FDA</strong> (<strong>Food and Drug Administration</strong>); with <strong>Marty Makary</strong> as Commissioner; are the primary regulators. <strong>HFCS-55</strong> (55% fructose; for beverages) and <strong>HFCS-42</strong> (42% fructose; for food) are the primary commercial types.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective the <strong>Make America Healthy Again</strong> (<strong>MAHA</strong>) movement; led by <strong>HHS</strong> Secretary <strong>Robert F. Kennedy Jr.</strong>; is calling for conservative reforms on <strong>HFCS</strong>; ultra-processed foods; food dyes; pesticides; and vaccines — creating pressure for structural change in the food industry. <strong>Coca-Cola</strong>'s potential transition of its <strong>U.S.</strong> beverage line from <strong>HFCS</strong> to cane sugar would carry: (1) an economic blow to corn farmers in <strong>Iowa</strong>; <strong>Illinois</strong>; <strong>Nebraska</strong>; (2) an opportunity for cane sugar exporters in <strong>Brazil</strong>; <strong>Mexico</strong>; <strong>India</strong>; (3) upward pressure on global sugar prices; (4) certain support for the <strong>U.S.</strong> sugar beet (<strong>sugar beet</strong>) sector; (5) reforms in <strong>HFCS</strong> food labeling; as major consequences. The <strong>American Sugar Alliance</strong>; <strong>U.S. Sugar Corporation</strong>; <strong>American Sugar Refining</strong> (<strong>Domino Sugar</strong>); <strong>Imperial Sugar</strong>; are primary representatives of the <strong>U.S.</strong> sugar sector. The <strong>Sugar Association</strong> has long campaigned against <strong>HFCS</strong> — with <strong>Gluconolactone</strong>; <strong>Stevia</strong>; <strong>Allulose</strong>; <strong>Erythritol</strong>; <strong>Monk Fruit</strong>; among the primary natural low-calorie sweetener alternatives. <strong>Aspartame</strong>; <strong>Sucralose</strong>; <strong>Acesulfame K</strong>; <strong>Saccharin</strong>; are the primary artificial sweeteners. In conclusion the <strong>Trump-Coca-Cola-RFK Jr.</strong> sweetener debate signals structural change pressure in <strong>U.S.</strong> agricultural and food supply chain policy — its impact appears to be decisive in the medium and long term on farm income; beverage pricing and import balances.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. <strong>John Bode</strong> (<strong>Corn Refiners Association</strong>) objects to <strong>Coca-Cola</strong>'s switch to cane sugar.<br>
2. <strong>Trump</strong> announced on July 16 on <strong>Truth Social</strong> that <strong>Coca-Cola</strong> agreed to the switch.<br>
3. <strong>Coca-Cola</strong> released a statement on July 17 defending its use of <strong>HFCS</strong>.<br>
4. <strong>RFK Jr.</strong> (<strong>HHS</strong> Secretary) has long opposed <strong>HFCS</strong>.<br>
5. "<strong>Mexican Coke</strong>" is the version of <strong>Coca-Cola</strong> that uses cane sugar.</p>
