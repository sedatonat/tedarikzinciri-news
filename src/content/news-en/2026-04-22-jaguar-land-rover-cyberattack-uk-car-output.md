<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Jaguar Land Rover Automotive Plc</strong> has been paralyzed by a cyberattack, causing British automobile production to plummet 27% in September—adding further pressure on the country's troubled automotive sector. According to data released by the <strong>Society of Motor Manufacturers &amp; Traders</strong> (<strong>SMMT</strong>) on October 24, automobile production fell to 51,090 units in September. The total decline including commercial vehicles was even more dramatic at 36%—following <strong>Stellantis NV</strong>'s earlier closure of its <strong>Vauxhall</strong> van plant in <strong>Luton</strong> this year. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Jaguar Land Rover</strong>, headquartered in <strong>Coventry, U.K.</strong>, is owned by <strong>Tata Motors</strong> (<strong>India</strong>), which acquired it from <strong>Ford Motor Company</strong> for $2.3 billion in 2008. <strong>Solihull</strong>, <strong>Halewood</strong>, and <strong>Castle Bromwich</strong> (being closed) are the primary <strong>U.K.</strong> manufacturing facilities. <strong>Range Rover</strong>, <strong>Range Rover Sport</strong>, <strong>Range Rover Velar</strong>, <strong>Range Rover Evoque</strong>, <strong>Discovery</strong>, <strong>Discovery Sport</strong>, <strong>Defender</strong>, <strong>Jaguar F-Pace</strong>, <strong>Jaguar XF</strong>, and <strong>Jaguar I-Pace</strong> (<strong>EV</strong>) comprise the main product line. <strong>Adrian Mardell</strong> is CEO of <strong>JLR</strong>. The photograph was taken by <strong>Chris Ratcliffe</strong> of <strong>Bloomberg</strong> at the <strong>JLR</strong> facility in <strong>Solihull, U.K.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>JLR cyberattack</strong> began in September 2025—as a result of the attack, <strong>JLR</strong>'s global manufacturing systems (<strong>MES</strong>, <strong>ERP</strong>, <strong>supplier portals</strong>, <strong>parts ordering</strong>) went offline. <strong>Scattered Spider</strong> (<strong>UNC3944</strong>, <strong>Octo Tempest</strong>), <strong>BlackCat/ALPHV</strong>, <strong>LockBit</strong>, <strong>Cl0p</strong>, and <strong>RansomHub</strong> are among the leading modern ransomware groups. The group behind the attack on <strong>JLR</strong> was initially unclear—but investigations point to <strong>Scattered Spider</strong>. <strong>JLR</strong>'s primary <strong>U.K.</strong> suppliers, including <strong>Evtec Aluminium</strong>, <strong>UK Speedy Welding</strong>, <strong>Nifco UK</strong>, <strong>Stadco</strong>, <strong>GKN Automotive</strong>, <strong>Penso</strong>, <strong>Magnesium Elektron</strong>, <strong>Bromford Iron &amp; Steel</strong>, <strong>Sertec</strong>, <strong>BBS</strong>, and <strong>Llanwern</strong>, have been impacted. The <strong>U.K. government</strong> has provided <strong>JLR</strong> with £1.5 billion in credit guarantees to enable suppliers to receive payment. <strong>Tata Motors</strong> is providing additional financing from <strong>Mumbai</strong> to inject cash into <strong>JLR</strong>. <strong>JLR</strong> production came to a complete halt throughout September-October 2025, with a gradual resumption beginning in November.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.K.</strong> automotive sector is in a declining trend with global annual production of 800,000 units—down more than 50% from the 2016 peak of 1.7 million units. <strong>Brexit</strong>, uncertainty around <strong>EV</strong> transition investments, and tariff/local content rules are the primary structural challenges. <strong>Nissan Sunderland</strong>, <strong>Toyota Burnaston</strong>, <strong>BMW Mini Oxford</strong>, <strong>Ford Bridgend</strong> (closing), <strong>Honda Swindon</strong> (closed 2021), <strong>Stellantis Vauxhall Luton</strong> (closing 2025), <strong>Stellantis Vauxhall Ellesmere Port</strong>, <strong>Bentley Crewe</strong>, <strong>Rolls-Royce Goodwood</strong>, <strong>Aston Martin Gaydon</strong>, <strong>McLaren Woking</strong>, <strong>Lotus Hethel</strong> (<strong>Geely</strong>), <strong>Morgan Malvern</strong>, and <strong>Caterham</strong> are the major automotive facilities in the <strong>U.K.</strong> The <strong>U.K. ZEV mandate</strong> requires 22% of vehicles sold in 2025 to be <strong>EV</strong>—80% by 2030 and 100% by 2035. <strong>Mike Hawes</strong>, CEO of <strong>SMMT</strong>, regularly comments on the weak production figures.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, automotive cyberattack threats are growing structurally. <strong>NotPetya</strong> 2017 (<strong>Maersk</strong>, <strong>Merck</strong>, <strong>FedEx</strong>; total damage over $10 billion), <strong>SolarWinds</strong> 2020 (<strong>Orion</strong>), <strong>Colonial Pipeline</strong> 2021 (<strong>DarkSide</strong>), <strong>JBS Foods</strong> 2021 (<strong>REvil</strong>), <strong>MGM Resorts</strong> 2023 (<strong>Scattered Spider</strong>), <strong>Caesars</strong> 2023 (<strong>Scattered Spider</strong>), <strong>Change Healthcare</strong> 2024 (<strong>BlackCat</strong>, <strong>UnitedHealth</strong>), <strong>CDK Global</strong> 2024 (affecting 15,000+ car dealerships), and <strong>JLR</strong> 2025 represent some of the largest industrial and supply chain cyberattacks in history. <strong>OT</strong> (<strong>Operational Technology</strong>), <strong>ICS</strong> (<strong>Industrial Control System</strong>), <strong>SCADA</strong>, and <strong>PLC</strong> are core components of manufacturing systems—traditional <strong>IT</strong> security tools are insufficient. <strong>Claroty</strong>, <strong>Dragos</strong>, <strong>Nozomi Networks</strong>, <strong>SCADAfence</strong> (<strong>Honeywell</strong>), and <strong>Armis</strong> are leading <strong>OT/ICS</strong> security firms. <strong>NIS2</strong> (<strong>Network and Information Security Directive 2</strong>, <strong>EU</strong>), <strong>CRA</strong> (<strong>Cyber Resilience Act</strong>, <strong>EU</strong>), <strong>UK NCSC</strong> (<strong>National Cyber Security Centre</strong>), <strong>NIST CSF</strong> (<strong>U.S.</strong>), <strong>ISO 27001</strong>, and <strong>IEC 62443</strong> are key cybersecurity standards. In conclusion, <strong>JLR</strong>'s 27% production collapse is a clear indicator of the automotive supply chain's structural vulnerability to cyberattacks.</p>

<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. <strong>JLR cyberattack</strong> caused a 27% drop in <strong>U.K.</strong> September automobile production.<br>
2. Total (including commercial vehicles) declined 36%—compounded by <strong>Stellantis Luton</strong> closure.<br>
3. <strong>SMMT</strong> data reported September at 51,090 units.<br>
4. The <strong>British</strong> automotive sector faces multiple structural pressures.<br>
5. The <strong>U.K. government</strong> provided <strong>JLR</strong> suppliers with £1.5 billion in credit guarantees.</p>
