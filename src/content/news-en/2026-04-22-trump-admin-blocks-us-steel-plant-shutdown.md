<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The Trump</strong> administration is reported to be preventing <strong>U.S. Steel</strong> from halting operations at its facility in <strong>Granite City, Illinois</strong> — leveraging newly acquired control in the company as part of an agreement allowing the <strong>White House</strong> to approve the company's merger with <strong>Japan's Nippon Steel</strong>. <strong>U.S. Steel</strong> informed approximately 800 workers at the facility in early September that it was planning to suspend steel slab processing through November. <strong>The Wall Street Journal</strong> reported that days later, <strong>Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick</strong> intervened, telling the company that the administration would not permit the closure to proceed. <strong>U.S. Steel</strong> subsequently announced on September 19 that the facility would remain open. "The <strong>Trump</strong> administration has been a great friend to <strong>American</strong> steel industry — and we have ongoing positive and productive discussions with them on various matters — but we do not plan to discuss the details of these discussions," the company said in a press statement. <strong>President Donald Trump</strong> agreed to allow the $14 billion merger between <strong>Nippon Steel</strong> and <strong>U.S. Steel</strong> to proceed in June — conditional on the companies granting the <strong>U.S.</strong> government a so-called "golden share" in <strong>U.S. Steel's</strong> operations. Exactly what power this authority grants the <strong>White House</strong> was not immediately clear; however, <strong>CNBC</strong> reported in mid-June that the federal government is believed to be receiving control over some seats on <strong>U.S. Steel's</strong> board of directors as part of the merger.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, according to an interview with <strong>Todd Tucker</strong>, director of industrial policy and trade at the <strong>Roosevelt Institute</strong>, conducted with <strong>NPR</strong>, a "golden share" is a mechanism used by governments worldwide to ensure national ownership and control of strategic companies. <strong>United States Steel Corporation</strong> (<strong>U.S. Steel</strong>; <strong>Big Steel</strong>) was founded in 1901 by <strong>J.P. Morgan</strong>, <strong>Andrew Carnegie</strong>, and <strong>Charles M. Schwab</strong> — based in <strong>Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania</strong> — and was historically one of the largest companies globally. <strong>David Burritt</strong> is <strong>U.S. Steel's</strong> CEO. <strong>Nippon Steel Corporation</strong> (<strong>NSC</strong>; <strong>Nippon Steel</strong>), based in <strong>Tokyo, Japan</strong>, is one of the world's largest steel producers — producing over 60 million tons of steel annually. <strong>Eiji Hashimoto</strong> is <strong>Nippon Steel's</strong> President & CEO. <strong>Granite City Works</strong> is <strong>U.S. Steel's</strong> integrated steel production facility in <strong>Madison County, Illinois</strong> — located near <strong>St. Louis, Missouri</strong> — and ranks among the major <strong>U.S. Steel</strong> facilities alongside <strong>Mon Valley Works</strong> and <strong>Gary Works</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.S.</strong> steel industry produces 80-90 million tons of steel annually globally — ranking as a leading steel producer after <strong>China</strong> (1 billion+ tons), <strong>India</strong>, <strong>Japan</strong>, <strong>South Korea</strong>, <strong>Russia</strong>, <strong>Germany</strong>, and <strong>Turkey</strong>. <strong>Nucor Corporation</strong>, based in <strong>Charlotte, North Carolina</strong>, is the largest <strong>U.S.</strong> steel producer globally — particularly a leader in <strong>EAF</strong> (<strong>Electric Arc Furnace</strong>) and <strong>mini-mill</strong> technology. <strong>Cleveland-Cliffs Inc.</strong>, based in <strong>Cleveland, Ohio</strong>, is another major <strong>U.S.</strong> steel producer — having acquired <strong>AK Steel</strong>, <strong>Severstal North America</strong>, and <strong>ArcelorMittal USA</strong> facilities — with <strong>Lourenco Goncalves</strong> as CEO. <strong>Steel Dynamics</strong>, <strong>Commercial Metals Company</strong> (<strong>CMC</strong>), <strong>SSAB Americas</strong>, <strong>Algoma Steel</strong>, <strong>Stelco</strong> (<strong>Cleveland-Cliffs</strong>), <strong>EVRAZ North America</strong>, <strong>NLMK USA</strong>, <strong>Big River Steel</strong> (<strong>U.S. Steel</strong>), <strong>JSW Steel USA</strong>, <strong>Nippon Steel North America</strong>, <strong>Mitsui Steel</strong>, and <strong>Optimus Steel</strong> are among the leading <strong>U.S.</strong> steel producers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the global steel industry's primary organizations include the <strong>WSA</strong> (<strong>World Steel Association</strong>), <strong>OECD Steel Committee</strong>, <strong>Eurofer</strong> (<strong>European Steel Association</strong>), <strong>AISI</strong> (<strong>American Iron and Steel Institute</strong>), <strong>Steel Manufacturers Association</strong> (<strong>SMA</strong>), and <strong>Specialty Steel Industry of North America</strong> (<strong>SSINA</strong>). Primary steel production technologies include <strong>BOF</strong> (<strong>Basic Oxygen Furnace</strong>), <strong>EAF</strong> (<strong>Electric Arc Furnace</strong>; over 70% of U.S. production), <strong>DRI</strong> (<strong>Direct Reduced Iron</strong>), <strong>HBI</strong> (<strong>Hot Briquetted Iron</strong>), <strong>blast furnace</strong>, <strong>integrated mill</strong>, and <strong>mini-mill</strong>. Major steel product categories include <strong>HRC</strong> (<strong>Hot Rolled Coil</strong>), <strong>CRC</strong> (<strong>Cold Rolled Coil</strong>), <strong>HDG</strong> (<strong>Hot Dipped Galvanized</strong>), <strong>EG</strong> (<strong>Electrogalvanized</strong>), <strong>plate</strong>, <strong>rebar</strong>, <strong>structural</strong>, and <strong>wire rod</strong>. Leading green steel initiatives include <strong>green steel</strong>, <strong>hydrogen steel</strong>, <strong>HYBRIT</strong> (<strong>SSAB-LKAB-Vattenfall</strong>), <strong>H2 Green Steel</strong> (<strong>Stegra</strong>), <strong>ArcelorMittal Sestao</strong>, <strong>Salzgitter SALCOS</strong>, <strong>thyssenkrupp Steel tkH2Steel</strong>, <strong>Boston Metal</strong>, and <strong>Electra</strong>. <strong>Section 232 steel tariffs</strong> were initiated at 25% by <strong>Trump 1.0</strong> in 2018 — raised to 50% by <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> in 2025. Consequently, <strong>Trump's</strong> blocking of the <strong>U.S. Steel Granite City</strong> closure represents a concrete indicator that state industrial policy intervention in the <strong>U.S.</strong> has entered a new phase — particularly through the deployment of the "golden share" mechanism.</p>
