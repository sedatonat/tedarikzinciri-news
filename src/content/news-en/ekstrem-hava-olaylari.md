<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Extreme weather events</strong> are increasingly disrupting <strong>supply chains and logistics networks</strong> on a global scale. The impact of climate events such as flooding, storms, fires, and extreme temperatures affects not only transportation but also manufacturing, storage, and supply planning directly. This situation is driving companies to reshape their <strong>climate resilience</strong> strategies.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Within the risk mitigation framework, organizations are using <strong>climate risk modeling</strong> tools to analyze the vulnerability of infrastructure and supply points. These models identify which regions are exposed to which risks, enabling the preparation of <strong>prioritized intervention plans</strong>. Additionally, <strong>multi-sourcing</strong> and <strong>buffer inventory</strong> practices soften the impact of supply disruptions and enhance operational flexibility.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>AI forecasting</strong> and <strong>early warning systems</strong> extend the preparation time for logistics networks by simulating the potential impacts of weather events in advance. This allows shipments to be rerouted, critical material flows to be redirected, and operations in at-risk regions to be temporarily suspended.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">At the operational level, <strong>alternate routing</strong> planning, <strong>yard/dock scheduling</strong>, and site safety protocols are being deployed. These measures ensure the continuity of shipments while prioritizing personnel safety. Additionally, infrastructure strengthening efforts are accelerating at ports, warehouses, and distribution centers.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">On the financial and legal side, <strong>force majeure</strong> and <strong>business continuity</strong> provisions are being updated. Companies are adding specific clauses to their contracts addressing <strong>climate-related disruptions</strong>, clarifying both insurance coverage and compensation processes. This approach minimizes post-event financial losses and maintains cash flow stability.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In conclusion, minimizing the impact of extreme weather events on supply chains requires <strong>integrated preparedness</strong> across the <strong>meteorology–operations–finance</strong> triangle. Climate-focused data analytics, flexible supply strategies, and robust contract management have become essential components of sustainable operations.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li><p>Climate modeling raises risk awareness.</p></li>
  <li><p>Multi-sourcing/buffer stocks cushion shocks.</p></li>
  <li><p>AI forecasting simulates impact.</p></li>
  <li><p>Alternate routing maintains operations.</p></li>
  <li><p>Contract and insurance provisions are updated.</p></li>
</ul>
