<p style="text-align: justify">The container shipping industry is rapidly shifting toward cleaner fuels — more than half of all new ships on order are being prepared to run on alternatives to conventional heavy fuel oil. As of the end of August 2025, shipowners have on order 534 container ships capable of using alternative fuels — according to data released on September 18 by the maritime organization <strong>BIMCO</strong>. These vessels account for 53% of all container ships currently on order and 77% of the total cargo capacity on order — measured in twenty-foot equivalent units (<strong>TEU</strong>). <strong>BIMCO</strong> projects that more than a quarter of the global container fleet will operate on alternative fuels by 2030. <strong>BIMCO</strong> notes that larger vessels are leading the transition — vessels with alternative fuels account for 81% of all orders for ships with 8,000 <strong>TEU</strong> or greater capacity. Conversely, other major maritime sectors are following more slowly — alternative fuel vessels account for only 8%, 9%, and 17% of bulkers, product tankers, and crude oil tankers on order, respectively. <strong>BIMCO</strong> suggests this may reflect structural differences — the container sector is dominated by a few major companies, while the bulker and tanker markets are more fragmented and comprised of smaller operators. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>BIMCO</strong> (<strong>Baltic and International Maritime Council</strong>), headquartered in <strong>Bagsværd, Denmark</strong>, was founded in 1905 and is the world's largest international maritime organization — representing more than 60% of global commercial ton supply. <strong>David Loosley</strong> is <strong>BIMCO</strong> Secretary General &amp; CEO. <strong>Niels Rasmussen</strong> is <strong>BIMCO</strong>'s chief maritime analyst.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>LNG</strong> (<strong>Liquefied Natural Gas</strong>) is currently the most popular alternative fuel — preferred to power approximately two-thirds of all alternative-fuel vessels on order — with methanol fuels accounting for 31%. <strong>Methanol</strong> emerged in 2023 — but <strong>LNG</strong> has since surpassed it — likely due to greater global availability and established infrastructure for fuel supply. <strong>HFO</strong> (<strong>Heavy Fuel Oil</strong>), <strong>VLSFO</strong> (<strong>Very Low Sulphur Fuel Oil</strong>), <strong>MGO</strong> (<strong>Marine Gas Oil</strong>), and <strong>MDO</strong> (<strong>Marine Diesel Oil</strong>) are the main conventional marine fuels — with <strong>VLSFO</strong> becoming dominant following the <strong>IMO 2020</strong> sulfur cap. <strong>LNG</strong>, <strong>methanol</strong>, <strong>ammonia</strong>, <strong>hydrogen</strong>, <strong>biofuel</strong>, <strong>e-fuel</strong> (<strong>e-methanol</strong>, <strong>e-ammonia</strong>, <strong>e-LNG</strong>), and <strong>nuclear</strong> (<strong>SMR</strong>, <strong>Small Modular Reactor</strong>) are the main alternative marine fuels. <strong>Dual-fuel engine</strong> technologies include <strong>WinGD X-DF</strong>, <strong>MAN Energy Solutions ME-GA</strong>, <strong>ME-LGIM</strong> (<strong>methanol</strong>), and <strong>ME-LGIA</strong> (<strong>ammonia</strong>). <strong>WinGD</strong> (<strong>Winterthur Gas &amp; Diesel</strong>, owned by <strong>CSSC</strong>), <strong>MAN Energy Solutions</strong> (<strong>Volkswagen Group</strong>), <strong>Wärtsilä</strong>, <strong>Mitsubishi Heavy Industries</strong>, <strong>Hyundai Heavy Industries</strong>, and <strong>Doosan Engine</strong> are the major ship engine manufacturers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, global container shipping is led by <strong>MSC</strong> (<strong>Mediterranean Shipping Company</strong>, the world's largest with 6+ million <strong>TEU</strong> capacity), <strong>Maersk</strong> (<strong>A.P. Moller-Maersk</strong>, second largest), <strong>CMA CGM</strong> (third, including <strong>APL</strong>, <strong>ANL</strong>, <strong>Mercosul Line</strong>, <strong>Containerships</strong>, <strong>Sofrana ANL</strong>, <strong>CEVA Logistics</strong>, and <strong>Bolloré Logistics</strong>), <strong>COSCO Shipping Lines</strong> (including <strong>OOCL</strong>), <strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong> (merging with <strong>UASC</strong> and <strong>CSAV</strong>), <strong>ONE</strong> (<strong>Ocean Network Express</strong>, a merger of <strong>NYK</strong>, <strong>MOL</strong>, and <strong>K Line</strong>), <strong>Evergreen Marine</strong>, <strong>HMM</strong> (formerly <strong>Hyundai Merchant Marine</strong>), <strong>Yang Ming</strong>, <strong>ZIM</strong>, <strong>Wan Hai Lines</strong>, <strong>PIL</strong> (<strong>Pacific International Lines</strong>), <strong>SITC International</strong>, <strong>Zhonggu Logistics</strong>, <strong>X-Press Feeders</strong>, <strong>Sinokor</strong>, and <strong>KMTC Line</strong>. The major global carrier alliances include <strong>2M Alliance</strong> (<strong>MSC</strong> + <strong>Maersk</strong>, ending in 2025), <strong>Gemini Cooperation</strong> (<strong>Maersk</strong> + <strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong>, February 2025), <strong>Premier Alliance</strong> (<strong>HMM</strong> + <strong>ONE</strong> + <strong>Yang Ming</strong>), and <strong>Ocean Alliance</strong> (<strong>CMA CGM</strong> + <strong>COSCO</strong> + <strong>Evergreen</strong> + <strong>OOCL</strong>).</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>IMO</strong> (<strong>International Maritime Organization</strong>) sets the global maritime decarbonization roadmap. The <strong>IMO 2023 GHG Strategy</strong> sets a net-zero <strong>GHG</strong> target by 2050 — including interim targets of 20-30% reduction by 2030 and 70-80% reduction by 2040. <strong>EEXI</strong> (<strong>Energy Efficiency Existing Ship Index</strong>), <strong>CII</strong> (<strong>Carbon Intensity Indicator</strong>), <strong>SEEMP</strong> (<strong>Ship Energy Efficiency Management Plan</strong>), and <strong>EEDI</strong> (<strong>Energy Efficiency Design Index</strong>) are the main <strong>IMO</strong> efficiency requirements. <strong>FuelEU Maritime</strong> (effective January 2025), <strong>EU ETS</strong> (<strong>Emissions Trading System</strong>, covering shipping from 2024), and <strong>EUA</strong> (<strong>EU Allowances</strong>) are the main European maritime climate regulations. The <strong>IMO Net-Zero Framework</strong> is expected to be approved at <strong>MEPC 83</strong> in October 2025 — including a global fuel standard (<strong>GFI</strong>, <strong>Global Fuel Standard</strong>) and a carbon pricing mechanism (<strong>net-zero contribution</strong>, <strong>NZC</strong>). <strong>Maersk</strong> took delivery of its first large methanol-fueled container ship, <strong>Laura Maersk</strong>, in 2024. <strong>HMM</strong>, <strong>CMA CGM</strong>, <strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong>, <strong>MSC</strong>, <strong>Evergreen</strong>, and <strong>OOCL</strong> are the major container carriers ordering methanol/<strong>LNG</strong> dual-fuel vessels. In conclusion, <strong>BIMCO</strong>'s 2025 data represents a measurable and concrete indicator of container shipping's leadership in maritime decarbonization.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. 534 container ships on order are equipped with alternative fuel capacity.<br>
2. 53% of total orders (vessels) and 77% (<strong>TEU</strong>) are alternative-fuel powered.<br>
3. 81% of large vessels of 8,000+ <strong>TEU</strong> are alternative-fuel powered.<br>
4. <strong>LNG</strong> accounts for 2/3 of alternative orders; methanol for 31%.<br>
5. By 2030, a quarter of the global container fleet is projected to operate on alternative fuel.</p>
