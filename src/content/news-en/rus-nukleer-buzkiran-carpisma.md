<p style="text-align: justify">On the night of January 26, 2025, the <strong>50 Let Pobedy</strong>, a Russian nuclear icebreaker, collided with a cargo vessel along the Northern Sea Route, sustaining significant hull damage. The incident occurred in the <strong>Kara Sea</strong> while the icebreaker was escorting the cargo ship. Following the collision, a deep crack formed on the starboard side near the bow.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Atomflot</strong>, the operator of Russia's nuclear icebreakers, stated that the <strong>50 Let Pobedy</strong> remained seaworthy and continued operations in the <strong>Ob Gulf</strong>. It was confirmed that the damaged area is not near the nuclear reactor and the power unit is operating normally. However, uncertainty remains as to whether the vessel will complete its planned winter ice-breaking operations or return early to <strong>Murmansk</strong> for repairs.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>50 Let Pobedy</strong> is one of eight nuclear icebreakers currently in service with Russia. Construction began in 1989, but following an extended halt due to the collapse of the Soviet Union, the vessel entered service in 2007. Until the commissioning of the new <strong>Arktika</strong>-class nuclear icebreakers in 2020, the <strong>50 Let Pobedy</strong> held the title of the world's largest nuclear icebreaker.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This incident is not the first collision between nuclear icebreakers and cargo vessels. In 2017, the nuclear icebreaker <strong>Vaygach</strong> collided with the <strong>handymax</strong>-class bulk carrier <strong>Nordic Barents</strong> in the <strong>Kara Sea</strong>. Both vessels were able to continue their voyages at that time. Additionally, in July 2010, two fully laden Russian tankers, <strong>Indiga</strong> and <strong>Varzuga</strong>, collided in moderate ice conditions with low visibility. However, both vessels maintained their seaworthiness and no oil spillage occurred. This was possible due to their heavy ice class rating of <strong>1A Super</strong> and double-hull construction.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Recently, Russia has begun sending oil tankers and cargo vessels without ice class through the Northern Sea Route. This frequently violates the <strong>IMO</strong>'s <strong>Polar Code</strong> and the country's own Arctic shipping regulations.</p>
<p><br></p>

  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>50 Let Pobedy</strong> collided with a cargo vessel in the <strong>Kara Sea</strong> on January 26, 2025, sustaining a deep crack in its hull.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Atomflot</strong> stated that the vessel remains seaworthy and continues operations.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>50 Let Pobedy</strong> entered service in 2007 and was the world's largest nuclear icebreaker until 2020.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">A similar collision previously occurred in 2017 between the <strong>Vaygach</strong> icebreaker and the <strong>Nordic Barents</strong> cargo vessel.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Russia's use of non-ice-class vessels on the Northern Sea Route violates international and national regulations.</p></li>
</ul>
