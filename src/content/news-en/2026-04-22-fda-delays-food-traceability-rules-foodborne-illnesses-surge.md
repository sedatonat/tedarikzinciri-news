<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; analyst insight; the <strong>Food and Drug Administration</strong> (<strong>FDA</strong>) is delaying implementation of new food traceability rules by 30 months to give companies additional time to bring their supply chains into compliance — announced March 20. The rules, first proposed during President <strong>Donald Trump</strong>'s first term, require improved record-keeping and visibility across food supply chains, enabling companies to identify and remove contaminated products from circulation before they reach restaurants or grocery shelves. Originally scheduled to begin in January 2026, the rules have now been extended to January 2028. This falls under <strong>FSMA</strong> (<strong>Food Safety Modernization Act</strong>) <strong>Section 204</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, key players at the <strong>U.S. Food and Drug Administration</strong> (<strong>FDA</strong>) include <strong>Marty Makary</strong>, Commissioner (Trump 2.0); the <strong>U.S. Department of Health and Human Services</strong> (<strong>HHS</strong>; <strong>Robert F. Kennedy Jr.</strong>, Secretary); and <strong>Frank Yiannas</strong> (former <strong>FDA Deputy Commissioner for Food Policy</strong> and principal architect of <strong>FSMA Section 204</strong>). <strong>FSMA</strong> was signed into law by <strong>Obama</strong> in 2011, representing the most comprehensive U.S. food safety reform. The principal <strong>FSMA</strong> rules include: (1) <strong>Preventive Controls for Human Food</strong>; (2) <strong>Preventive Controls for Animal Food</strong>; (3) <strong>Foreign Supplier Verification Program</strong> (<strong>FSVP</strong>); (4) <strong>Produce Safety Rule</strong>; (5) <strong>Sanitary Transportation</strong>; (6) <strong>Mitigation Strategies to Protect Food Against Intentional Adulteration</strong>; and (7) <strong>Section 204 Food Traceability Rule</strong> (<strong>Food Traceability List</strong> encompassing soft fruits, salads, cheese, deli meat, fish, shellfish, and eggs). The <strong>Section 204 Food Traceability Final Rule</strong> employs <strong>Key Data Elements</strong> (<strong>KDEs</strong>) and <strong>Critical Tracking Events</strong> (<strong>CTEs</strong>) as core traceability mechanisms.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, leading <strong>food traceability</strong> technology platforms include <strong>IBM Food Trust</strong> (<strong>blockchain</strong>; members include <strong>Walmart</strong>, <strong>Carrefour</strong>, <strong>Nestlé</strong>, <strong>Tyson Foods</strong>, and <strong>Kroger</strong>); <strong>FoodLogiQ</strong> (owned by <strong>Trustwell</strong>); <strong>iTradeNetwork</strong>; <strong>HarvestMark</strong> (owned by <strong>Trimble</strong>); <strong>ReposiTrak</strong> (<strong>Park City Group</strong>); <strong>FreshPoint</strong> (<strong>Sysco</strong>); <strong>Provenance</strong>; <strong>OriginTrail</strong>; <strong>Wholechain</strong>; <strong>TE-FOOD</strong>; <strong>Connecting Food</strong>; <strong>Worldsync</strong>; and <strong>GS1 GTIN</strong>/<strong>GLN</strong>/<strong>GTIN-14</strong>; <strong>EPCIS</strong>/<strong>Verified by GS1</strong> as core ecosystem players. <strong>Walmart</strong>'s 2018 <strong>blockchain</strong> mandate for leafy greens represents a landmark early adoption example. Primary <strong>foodborne illness</strong> pathogens in the U.S. include <strong>Salmonella</strong>, <strong>Listeria monocytogenes</strong>, <strong>E. coli O157:H7</strong>, <strong>Campylobacter</strong>, <strong>Norovirus</strong>, and <strong>Clostridium perfringens</strong>. Notable 2024 U.S. foodborne outbreaks include <strong>Boar's Head Listeria</strong> (deli meat — 10+ deaths), <strong>McDonald's E. coli</strong> (<strong>Quarter Pounder</strong> — 1+ death), and <strong>Carrots Listeria</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, principal U.S. federal food safety authorities include the <strong>FDA</strong> (<strong>Center for Food Safety and Applied Nutrition</strong>; <strong>CFSAN</strong>), the <strong>USDA Food Safety and Inspection Service</strong> (<strong>FSIS</strong>; overseeing meat, poultry, and egg products), and the <strong>Centers for Disease Control and Prevention</strong> (<strong>CDC</strong>; <strong>FoodNet</strong>; <strong>PulseNet</strong>). Major U.S. food industry advocacy groups include the <strong>National Restaurant Association</strong> (<strong>NRA</strong>), <strong>Consumer Brands Association</strong> (<strong>CBA</strong>), <strong>Food Marketing Institute</strong> (<strong>FMI</strong>), <strong>International Foodservice Distributors Association</strong> (<strong>IFDA</strong>), <strong>Produce Marketing Association</strong> (<strong>PMA</strong>), and <strong>International Fresh Produce Association</strong> (<strong>IFPA</strong>) — all of which have requested the <strong>FSMA Section 204</strong> delay. Additional sector organizations include the <strong>Food Industry Association</strong> and <strong>National Fisheries Institute</strong>. The <strong>Codex Alimentarius</strong> (<strong>FAO</strong>/<strong>WHO</strong>) serves as the principal global food standards framework, while the <strong>EU General Food Law Regulation</strong> (178/2002) and <strong>EU Farm to Fork Strategy</strong> form the core EU food safety frameworks. In conclusion, the <strong>FDA</strong>'s decision to delay the traceability rule appears to be prompting a fundamental redesign of global food supply chain visibility investments — with <strong>blockchain</strong>, <strong>GS1</strong> standards, and <strong>KDE</strong>/<strong>CTE</strong> readiness emerging as principal strategic priorities for supply chain managers.</p>

3. <strong>KDE</strong>/<strong>CTE</strong> represent principal traceability data mechanisms.<br>
