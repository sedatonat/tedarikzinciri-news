<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> video interview reporting shows that when it comes to digitalizing supply chain planning, the world is fundamentally different from just a few years ago — according to <strong>Philip Vervloesem</strong>, Chief Commercial Officer at <strong>OMP</strong>. Compared to just two or three years ago, top-tier organizations are demanding rapid "time to value" in their supply chain planning digital transformation initiatives — as <strong>Vervloesem</strong> notes. Executives cannot afford to wait months or years to see return on investment from automated systems — they need to start working with digitalized information immediately. In some cases, this means targeting "six to eight weeks for first value delivery." Companies seeking to digitalize planning must have the capability to evaluate and sustain improvements demonstrated through <strong>ROI</strong> in inventory management, improved service levels, and sustainability. This last category is important — <strong>Vervloesem</strong> notes — but must be balanced with considerations of "cost, cash and customer." Moving forward, planners must ensure their digitalized processes continue to generate value and are "maximized over time." In the push for faster <strong>ROI</strong> — planning organizations are increasingly focusing on running quick pilots — emphasizing core platform development that can be complemented with "spot solutions."</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>OMP</strong>, based in <strong>Antwerp, Belgium</strong>, with <strong>Paul Vanvuchelen</strong> as <strong>CEO</strong> and <strong>Philip Vervloesem</strong> as Chief Commercial Officer, is a global supply chain planning software provider. <strong>OMP Plus</strong> and <strong>Unison Planning</strong> are its flagship products — with core competencies in <strong>S&OP</strong>, <strong>tactical planning</strong>, <strong>scheduling</strong>, <strong>distribution planning</strong>, and <strong>inventory optimization</strong>. <strong>OMP</strong> is particularly strong in process industries including <strong>chemicals</strong>, <strong>life sciences</strong>, <strong>consumer goods</strong>, <strong>metals</strong>, and <strong>paper</strong>. Major customers include <strong>Bayer</strong>, <strong>BASF</strong>, <strong>Procter &amp; Gamble</strong>, <strong>Pfizer</strong>, <strong>3M</strong>, <strong>SABIC</strong>, <strong>Wacker</strong>, <strong>Henkel</strong>, <strong>Estee Lauder</strong>, <strong>Heineken</strong>, <strong>Tata Steel</strong>, and <strong>JBS Foods</strong>. <strong>Gartner Magic Quadrant for Supply Chain Planning Solutions</strong> positioned <strong>OMP</strong> as a <strong>Leader</strong> in 2024 — alongside <strong>Blue Yonder</strong>, <strong>Kinaxis</strong>, <strong>SAP IBP</strong>, and <strong>o9 Solutions</strong>. Other major competitors include <strong>Oracle</strong>, <strong>Logility</strong>, <strong>Anaplan</strong>, <strong>RELEX Solutions</strong>, <strong>ToolsGroup</strong>, <strong>John Galt Solutions</strong>, <strong>QAD Dynasys</strong>, <strong>Coupa Supply Chain Design</strong>, and <strong>Adexa</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, key modern paradigm shifts in digital supply chain planning include: (1) <strong>composable</strong> and <strong>modular</strong> architecture; (2) <strong>API-first</strong> integration approach; (3) <strong>cloud-native</strong> deployment; (4) <strong>real-time</strong> and <strong>event-driven</strong> processing; (5) <strong>AI/ML</strong>-based forecasting and optimization; (6) <strong>generative AI</strong>-enabled user interface and decision support; (7) <strong>digital twin</strong> and <strong>simulation</strong>; (8) <strong>scenario planning</strong> and <strong>what-if</strong> analysis; (9) <strong>supply chain control tower</strong>; (10) <strong>collaborative planning</strong> and <strong>multi-enterprise</strong> integration; (11) <strong>sustainability metrics</strong> and <strong>carbon footprint</strong> integration; and (12) <strong>cost-to-serve</strong> and <strong>profit optimization</strong>. Key integration technologies include <strong>iPaaS</strong> (Integration Platform as a Service), <strong>API gateway</strong>, <strong>EDI</strong>, and <strong>EAI</strong>. Leading <strong>iPaaS</strong> providers include <strong>Boomi</strong>, <strong>MuleSoft</strong>, <strong>Workato</strong>, <strong>SnapLogic</strong>, <strong>Informatica</strong>, <strong>Tibco</strong>, <strong>SAP Integration Suite</strong>, <strong>Oracle Integration Cloud</strong>, and <strong>Microsoft Logic Apps</strong>. Key stream processing platforms include <strong>Apache Kafka</strong>, <strong>Confluent</strong>, <strong>AWS Kinesis</strong>, <strong>Azure Event Hubs</strong>, and <strong>Google Pub/Sub</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>consumer packaged goods</strong> (<strong>CPG</strong>) sector represents the most digitally mature industry, as <strong>Vervloesem</strong> has indicated — the drivers include: (1) high <strong>SKU</strong> variety; (2) rapid consumer preference shifts; (3) <strong>omnichannel</strong> demand variability; (4) <strong>perishable</strong> product management; (5) <strong>promotion</strong> impact; (6) <strong>direct-to-consumer</strong> (<strong>D2C</strong>) trends; and (7) <strong>private label</strong> competition. Leading global <strong>CPG</strong> companies include <strong>Procter &amp; Gamble</strong>, <strong>Unilever</strong>, <strong>Nestlé</strong>, <strong>Coca-Cola</strong>, <strong>PepsiCo</strong>, <strong>Mondelez International</strong>, <strong>Kraft Heinz</strong>, <strong>General Mills</strong>, <strong>Kellanova</strong> (formerly <strong>Kellogg</strong>), <strong>Mars</strong>, <strong>Colgate-Palmolive</strong>, <strong>Reckitt Benckiser</strong>, <strong>Henkel</strong>, <strong>Beiersdorf</strong>, <strong>Estee Lauder</strong>, <strong>L'Oréal</strong>, and <strong>LVMH</strong>. The <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> tariff regime requires advanced planning capabilities for <strong>front-loading</strong>, <strong>nearshoring</strong>, and <strong>multi-source</strong> strategies — strengthening the value proposition of providers like <strong>OMP</strong>. Ultimately, the "six to eight week" rapid pilot approach mentioned by <strong>Philip Vervloesem</strong> reflects a structural acceleration in the global supply chain planning software market — the speed advantages enabled by <strong>SaaS</strong> and <strong>cloud-native</strong> architecture appear to be the primary differentiator.</p>

<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Philip Vervloesem</strong> (<strong>OMP</strong>) emphasizes the demand for rapid "time to value."<br>
2. The target is six to eight weeks for first value delivery.<br>
3. Inventory, service level, and sustainability represent <strong>ROI</strong> metrics.<br>
4. <strong>Spot solutions</strong> plus <strong>core platform</strong> — the primary integration approach.<br>
5. The <strong>consumer goods</strong> sector leads in digital maturity.</p>
