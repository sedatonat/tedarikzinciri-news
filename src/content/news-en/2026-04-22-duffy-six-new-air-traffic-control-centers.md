<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports that <strong>U.S. Transportation Secretary</strong> <strong>Sean Duffy</strong> has unveiled a comprehensive proposal to overhaul the aging system managing the nation's approximately 45,000 daily flights, calling for the construction of six new air traffic control centers and the replacement of more than 600 radars. More than a dozen airport towers will be renovated—the agency's telecommunications network will be upgraded with new fiber, wireless, and satellite connections. The plan was unveiled at a press conference at the <strong>Transportation Department</strong> headquarters in <strong>Washington</strong> on May 8. <strong>President Donald Trump</strong> joined via phone to discuss aspects of the plan—including his desire to have the effort carried out through a single contract. "I want to give one big beautiful contract responsible for everything," <strong>Trump</strong> said while <strong>Duffy</strong> held a telephone to the podium microphone. The proposal marks an alarming sign of strain and represents the broadest initiative in years to modernize the nation's air traffic control system. Controllers last week lost radio and radar at a <strong>Philadelphia</strong> facility directing aircraft to <strong>Newark Liberty International Airport</strong> in <strong>New Jersey</strong>. The outage left them unable to see or communicate with aircraft transiting busy airspace for approximately 90 seconds—according to <strong>Bloomberg News</strong>. <strong>Duffy</strong> was joined on stage by CEOs of <strong>Delta Air Lines Inc.</strong>, <strong>United Airlines</strong>, <strong>American Airlines</strong>, <strong>Southwest Airlines</strong>, and <strong>JetBlue</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Sean Duffy</strong>, <strong>U.S. Department of Transportation</strong> (<strong>USDOT</strong>) Secretary, is a former <strong>Wisconsin</strong> Republican congressman and former <strong>Fox News</strong> anchor appointed by the <strong>Trump</strong> administration in January 2025. The <strong>Federal Aviation Administration</strong> (<strong>FAA</strong>), a subordinate agency of <strong>USDOT</strong>, has <strong>Bryan Bedford</strong> nominated as Administrator (former <strong>Republic Airways</strong> CEO) with <strong>Chris Rocheleau</strong> serving as Acting Administrator. The FAA's primary systems include <strong>NextGen</strong> (Next Generation Air Traffic Control), <strong>NOTAM</strong> (Notice to Airmen), <strong>STARS</strong> (Standard Terminal Automation Replacement System), and <strong>ERAM</strong> (En Route Automation Modernization). Major U.S. carriers include <strong>Delta Air Lines</strong> (<strong>Ed Bastian</strong> CEO; <strong>Atlanta</strong>), <strong>United Airlines Holdings</strong> (<strong>Scott Kirby</strong> CEO; <strong>Chicago</strong>), <strong>American Airlines Group</strong> (<strong>Robert Isom</strong> CEO; <strong>Fort Worth</strong>), <strong>Southwest Airlines</strong> (<strong>Bob Jordan</strong> CEO; <strong>Dallas</strong>), <strong>JetBlue Airways</strong> (<strong>Joanna Geraghty</strong> CEO; <strong>New York</strong>), <strong>Alaska Airlines</strong>, <strong>Spirit Airlines</strong>, <strong>Frontier Airlines</strong>, and <strong>Hawaiian Airlines</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, major U.S. cargo carriers include <strong>FedEx Express</strong> (<strong>Memphis</strong>), <strong>UPS Airlines</strong> (<strong>Louisville</strong>), <strong>Atlas Air</strong> (owned by <strong>Apollo</strong>), <strong>Amazon Air</strong> (<strong>Hebron, Kentucky</strong>), <strong>Kalitta Air</strong>, <strong>Western Global Airlines</strong>, and <strong>Ameriflight</strong>. <strong>Newark Liberty International Airport</strong> (<strong>EWR</strong>) is operated by the <strong>Port Authority of New York and New Jersey</strong> (<strong>PANYNJ</strong>; <strong>Rick Cotton</strong> Executive Director)—a major hub for <strong>United Airlines</strong>—handling approximately 49 million passengers annually. The <strong>Philadelphia TRACON</strong> (Terminal Radar Approach Control) is operated by the <strong>FAA</strong> and serves multiple airports in the <strong>New York</strong> region, including <strong>EWR</strong>. Air traffic controller shortages have been a longstanding issue—the <strong>FAA</strong> operates with approximately 3,000 vacant positions out of a 14,000-person certified controller workforce. The <strong>National Air Traffic Controllers Association</strong> (<strong>NATCA</strong>; <strong>Nick Daniels</strong> President) is the primary union and has called for accelerated training and extended retirement ages.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, major air traffic control modernization technologies include (1) <strong>ADS-B</strong> (Automatic Dependent Surveillance-Broadcast), (2) <strong>Data Comm</strong>, (3) <strong>SWIM</strong> (System Wide Information Management), (4) <strong>NAS Voice System</strong>, (5) <strong>TFM</strong> (Traffic Flow Management), (6) <strong>PBN</strong> (Performance-Based Navigation), (7) <strong>Time-Based Flow Management</strong>, and (8) satellite-based surveillance. Major air traffic technology providers include <strong>Raytheon</strong> (owned by <strong>RTX</strong>), <strong>Lockheed Martin</strong>, <strong>Leidos</strong>, <strong>Saab Sensis</strong>, <strong>Indra Sistemas</strong>, <strong>Thales Group</strong>, <strong>L3Harris Technologies</strong>, <strong>Frequentis</strong>, <strong>Honeywell Aerospace</strong>, and <strong>Collins Aerospace</strong>. Key industry organizations include the <strong>Air Line Pilots Association</strong> (<strong>ALPA</strong>; <strong>Jason Ambrosi</strong> President), <strong>Airlines for America</strong> (<strong>A4A</strong>; <strong>Nick Calio</strong> CEO), and the <strong>Aerospace Industries Association</strong> (<strong>AIA</strong>). In essence, <strong>Duffy</strong>'s proposal for six new centers appears to reflect a fundamental reassessment of U.S. air traffic control infrastructure modernization and resilience requirements globally—on-time delivery and flight safety appear to be paramount strategic priorities for airline cargo operations and supply chain managers.</p>
