<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Bloomberg</strong> data shows that China continues to import Russian liquefied natural gas (LNG) subject to U.S. sanctions.</p>
<p><br>
Despite U.S. President <strong>Donald Trump</strong> blacklisting Russian energy giants such as <strong>Rosneft</strong> and <strong>Lukoil</strong>, the White House did not introduce <strong>new restrictions on LNG trade</strong> in its latest sanctions package.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This situation has enabled <strong>shipments from Russia's Arctic LNG 2 project</strong> to reach China.<br>
 <strong>The LNG tanker Iris</strong> <strong>docked at China's Beihai terminal in the south on October 22, 2025.</strong></p>
<p><br>
This shipment was China's <strong>11th sanctioned Russian LNG cargo since late August.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>The LNG Loophole in U.S. Sanctions</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">The latest U.S. sanctions wave targeted Russia's state-controlled oil producers.<br>
However, the White House has not yet expanded <strong>sanctions on companies conducting LNG trade indirectly.</strong></p>
<p><br>
This gap creates <strong>breathing room for Russia's energy revenues.</strong><br>
Moscow aims to <strong>triple LNG exports</strong> by 2030 and has placed Asian markets at the center of this strategy.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">By contrast, <strong>the United Kingdom imposed sanctions on the Beihai terminal last week.</strong></p>
<p><br>
Additionally, <strong>the European Union</strong> approved a new sanctions package against Russia, targeting <strong>45 organizations.</strong><br>
</p>
<p>The list includes <strong>12 Chinese and Hong Kong-based companies.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Beihai: Gateway for Russian LNG into China</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">The Chinese government has designated <strong>the Beihai terminal as a special entry point for shipments from the Arctic LNG 2 projects.</strong></p>
<p><br>
 <strong>Arctic LNG 2</strong> was sanctioned by the U.S. in 2023.</p>
<p><br>
However, China formally began imports through this terminal following <strong>Russian President Vladimir Putin's visit to Beijing</strong> in August 2025.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This move demonstrates that <strong>China maintains strategic solidarity with Russia despite Western sanctions</strong> in its energy security policy.</p>
<p><br>
Beihai has become one of Beijing's most critical LNG hubs in terms of both <strong>energy supply security</strong> and <strong>geopolitical cooperation.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>The Iris and Other Vessels: Flow Continues Despite Sanctions</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The Iris vessel</strong> loaded LNG in early October from <strong>a floating storage unit (FSU) in eastern Russia.</strong></p>
<p><br>
The fuel stored at this facility came directly from the <strong>Arctic LNG 2</strong> project.</p>
<p><br>
The U.S. Treasury Department had previously placed both this storage facility and <strong>the Iris</strong> on its sanctions list.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Satellite imagery and vessel tracking data</strong> show that at least <strong>three additional LNG tankers are heading to the Beihai terminal.</strong></p>
<p><br>
One of them was identified as <strong>an LNG vessel belonging to a Hong Kong-based company</strong> that <strong>fueled from a sanctioned Russian tanker off the Malaysian coast.</strong></p>
<p><br>
This indicates that <strong>"shadow fleet"</strong> tactics are rapidly expanding into LNG trade as well.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>New Tensions in Global Energy Balances</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">While U.S. <strong>sanctions on the Russian oil and LNG sector</strong> restrict Moscow's access to Western markets, they enhance the <strong>strategic role of countries like China and India in energy imports.</strong></p>
<p><br>
Beijing's persistence in this area carries not only economic but also <strong>geopolitical significance.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Although China is seeking diversification in LNG supplies, through this relationship with Russia it aims to ensure <strong>long-term supply security.</strong></p>
<p><br>
As long as U.S. sanctions do not cover LNG transportation, <strong>Russia's energy flow to Asian markets is expected to continue.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Conclusion: China-Russia Energy Cooperation Deepening</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">Russia's strategy to protect energy revenues under sanctions pressure is gaining new direction with China's support.<br>
The Beihai terminal has become a symbol of this new <strong>"pivot to Asia"</strong> strategy.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This development demonstrates that the <strong>global impact of Western energy sanctions remains limited</strong> and that Asia-centered alternative energy networks are strengthening.</p>
<p><br>
The U.S. failure to expand LNG sanctions provides <strong>indirect relief to Moscow</strong> and <strong>geopolitical advantage to Beijing.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Points:</strong></h5>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>According to Bloomberg data</strong>, China accepted its <strong>11th Russian LNG shipment</strong> as of October 2025.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The Iris vessel</strong> delivered LNG received from the sanctioned <strong>Arctic LNG 2</strong> facility to <strong>the Beihai terminal.</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The U.S.</strong> blacklisted oil giants <strong>Rosneft</strong> and <strong>Lukoil</strong>, but <strong>did not impose additional restrictions on LNG trade.</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The United Kingdom</strong> added the Beihai terminal to its sanctions list; the <strong>EU</strong> targeted <strong>45 organizations and 12 Chinese/Hong Kong companies.</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">At least <strong>three new vessels</strong> are proceeding to <strong>Beihai with sanctioned Russian LNG cargoes.</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Through this move, China is <strong>strengthening energy solidarity with Russia</strong> and <strong>expanding Asia-centered energy networks.</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The exclusion of LNG transportation from U.S. sanctions</strong> is creating <strong>strategic breathing room</strong> for Russia.</p></li>
</ul>
