<p style="text-align: justify">Warehouse robotics is in serious growth mode. According to <strong>Next Move Strategy Consulting</strong>, more than 51,000 autonomous mobile robots (<strong>AMR</strong>) were shipped globally in 2023—a figure projected to reach 180,000 units by 2030. Warehouse robotics improves efficiency and performance, particularly in the face of labor challenges. However, at a certain point, adding more robots to solve a problem yields diminishing returns. At the same time, it becomes rapidly expensive. So what happens when modern technology combining advanced cloud and edge computing can optimize the efficiency of <strong>AMR</strong> deployment? This sponsored article is presented by <strong>Zebra Technologies</strong>. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Zebra Technologies Corporation</strong>, based in Lincolnshire, Illinois, was founded in 1969 and is the global leader in barcode scanners, mobile computers, <strong>RFID</strong>, printers, and warehouse automation solutions. In 2021, <strong>Zebra</strong> acquired <strong>Fetch Robotics</strong> for $305 million—a key move into the <strong>AMR</strong> portfolio.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the global <strong>AMR</strong> and <strong>AGV</strong> (<strong>Automated Guided Vehicle</strong>) market was valued at approximately $5 billion in 2024 and is expected to grow to over $15 billion by 2030. Unlike <strong>AGV</strong>s, <strong>AMR</strong>s do not require fixed paths or magnetic tape—key navigation technologies include <strong>SLAM</strong> (<strong>Simultaneous Localization and Mapping</strong>), <strong>LIDAR</strong> (<strong>Light Detection and Ranging</strong>), <strong>computer vision</strong>, <strong>3D camera</strong>, and <strong>ToF sensor</strong> (<strong>Time of Flight</strong>). Major players in the global <strong>AMR</strong> and warehouse robotics sector include <strong>Locus Robotics</strong>, <strong>6 River Systems</strong> (<strong>Ocado</strong>+<strong>Shopify</strong> divestment), <strong>Geek+</strong> (<strong>China</strong>), <strong>Quicktron</strong> (<strong>China</strong>), <strong>HAI Robotics</strong> (<strong>China</strong>), <strong>AutoStore</strong> (<strong>Norway</strong>), <strong>Exotec</strong> (<strong>France</strong>; <strong>Skypod</strong>), <strong>Berkshire Grey</strong> (<strong>SoftBank</strong>), <strong>RightHand Robotics</strong>, <strong>Symbotic</strong> (<strong>Walmart</strong> partner), <strong>Attabotics</strong> (bankrupt), <strong>Vecna Robotics</strong>, <strong>Mobile Industrial Robots</strong> (<strong>MiR</strong>; <strong>Teradyne</strong>), <strong>OTTO Motors</strong> (<strong>Rockwell Automation</strong>), and <strong>Seegrid</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, warehouse robotics encompasses <strong>goods-to-person</strong> (<strong>G2P</strong>; <strong>AutoStore</strong>, <strong>Exotec</strong>, <strong>Symbotic</strong>, <strong>Geek+</strong>), <strong>person-to-goods</strong> (<strong>P2G</strong>; <strong>Locus</strong>, <strong>6 River</strong>; collaborative AMRs), <strong>goods-to-robot</strong> (full automation), <strong>cube storage</strong> (<strong>AutoStore</strong>), <strong>shuttle systems</strong> (<strong>SSI Schaefer</strong>, <strong>Dematic</strong>, <strong>Knapp</strong>), <strong>vertical lift module</strong> (<strong>VLM</strong>), <strong>horizontal carousel</strong>, <strong>mini-load AS/RS</strong>, <strong>pallet AS/RS</strong>, <strong>conveyor systems</strong>, and <strong>robotic arms</strong> (<strong>Berkshire Grey</strong>, <strong>RightHand</strong>, <strong>Covariant</strong>) as primary automation categories. <strong>Cloud computing</strong>, <strong>edge computing</strong>, <strong>fog computing</strong>, <strong>5G</strong>, <strong>Wi-Fi 6/7</strong>, <strong>private cellular</strong>, <strong>fleet orchestration</strong>, <strong>dynamic slotting</strong>, <strong>real-time inventory</strong>, <strong>traffic management</strong>, <strong>collision avoidance</strong>, <strong>battery management</strong>, and <strong>opportunity charging</strong> are the core technology components of modern warehouse automation. <strong>Zebra</strong>'s key sector products include <strong>Symmetry Fleet Management</strong>, <strong>SmartLens RFID</strong>, <strong>SmartSight Inventory Robot</strong>, and <strong>FulfillmentEdge</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, warehouse automation <strong>ROI</strong> calculations rely on key financial metrics: <strong>capital expenditure</strong> (<strong>CapEx</strong>), <strong>operational expenditure</strong> (<strong>OpEx</strong>), <strong>total cost of ownership</strong> (<strong>TCO</strong>), <strong>payback period</strong>, <strong>net present value</strong> (<strong>NPV</strong>), and <strong>internal rate of return</strong> (<strong>IRR</strong>). Key operational metrics include <strong>throughput</strong> (units/lines/orders/pallets per hour), <strong>pick rate</strong>, <strong>order accuracy</strong>, <strong>perfect order rate</strong>, <strong>cycle time</strong>, <strong>dock-to-stock time</strong>, <strong>inventory turns</strong>, <strong>storage density</strong>, and <strong>cubic utilization</strong>. <strong>RaaS</strong> (<strong>Robotics-as-a-Service</strong>), a capex-light subscription model, is pioneered by <strong>Locus Robotics</strong>, <strong>6 River</strong>, and <strong>inVia</strong>. Key software layers include <strong>WMS</strong> (<strong>Warehouse Management System</strong>; <strong>Manhattan Active Warehouse</strong>, <strong>SAP EWM</strong>, <strong>Blue Yonder Luminate</strong>, <strong>Oracle WMS</strong>, <strong>Körber HighJump</strong>, <strong>Tecsys</strong>), <strong>WCS</strong> (<strong>Warehouse Control System</strong>), and <strong>WES</strong> (<strong>Warehouse Execution System</strong>). The <strong>orchestration layer</strong> represents the modern approach to managing multiple robot fleets (heterogeneous manufacturers/brands) from a single control plane. In conclusion, <strong>Zebra</strong>'s message of "minimizing robots to maximize throughput" is a clear indicator of the trend in which software-based optimization is outpacing hardware investment in warehouse automation.</p>

3. Cloud plus edge computing optimize <strong>AMR</strong> fleet efficiency.<br>
