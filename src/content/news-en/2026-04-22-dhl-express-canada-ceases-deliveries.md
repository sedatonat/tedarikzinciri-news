<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports that <strong>DHL Express Canada</strong> is suspending delivery service nationwide on June 20, 2025 — as workers continue strike action and new federal legislation banning replacement workers takes effect. According to <strong>CBC</strong>, more than 2,000 <strong>DHL</strong> employees represented by <strong>Unifor</strong> — including truck drivers, couriers, warehouse and call center staff — have been on strike since June 8 following a lockout by the German-owned delivery company after negotiations broke down. Stalled negotiations have intensified with new federal legislation that prohibits replacement workers from filling roles during federally regulated strikes or lockouts. <strong>DHL</strong> continued operations through the first twelve days of the work stoppage, but is now suspending operations as the legislation takes effect. <strong>Unifor</strong> says its bargaining priorities with <strong>DHL</strong> center on wages, working conditions, and workplace surveillance and automation. <strong>DHL</strong>'s Canadian customers include <strong>Lululemon</strong>, <strong>Shein</strong>, and <strong>Siemens</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Deutsche Post DHL Group</strong>, based in <strong>Bonn, Germany</strong>, with CEO <strong>Tobias Meyer</strong>, operates as the world's largest logistics company — generating over 80 billion euros in annual revenue with 600,000+ employees. <strong>DHL Express</strong>, <strong>DHL Supply Chain</strong>, <strong>DHL Global Forwarding</strong>, <strong>DHL eCommerce</strong>, and <strong>Deutsche Post</strong> are its primary operating divisions. <strong>DHL Express Canada</strong>, based in <strong>Brampton, Ontario</strong>, with CEO <strong>Andrew Williams</strong>, operates an end-to-end express delivery network across the country. <strong>Unifor</strong>, led by National President <strong>Lana Payne</strong>, is Canada's largest private-sector union — encompassing 315,000+ members across transportation, automotive, communications, energy, healthcare, retail, agriculture, food processing, forest products, and mining. The <strong>Canada Industrial Relations Board</strong> oversees collective bargaining in federally regulated sectors. <strong>Bill C-58</strong> (an amendment to the <strong>Canada Labour Code</strong>) takes effect on June 20, 2025 — banning the use of replacement workers during lawful strikes or lockouts in federally regulated workplaces — marking a historic shift in worker rights in <strong>Canada</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the global express delivery and last-mile market is dominated by <strong>FedEx Corporation</strong> (<strong>Memphis, Tennessee</strong>; CEO <strong>Raj Subramaniam</strong>); <strong>United Parcel Service</strong> (<strong>UPS</strong>; <strong>Atlanta, Georgia</strong>; CEO <strong>Carol Tomé</strong>); <strong>DHL Express</strong>; <strong>SF Express</strong> (<strong>Shenzhen, China</strong>); <strong>JD Logistics</strong>; <strong>Cainiao Network</strong> (<strong>Alibaba</strong>); <strong>YTO Express</strong>; <strong>STO Express</strong>; <strong>ZTO Express</strong>; <strong>Yunda Express</strong>; <strong>Aramex</strong> (<strong>Dubai, UAE</strong>); <strong>TNT</strong> (FedEx-owned); <strong>La Poste</strong> (<strong>France</strong>); <strong>Royal Mail</strong> (<strong>UK</strong>); <strong>Australia Post</strong>; <strong>Japan Post</strong>; and <strong>Korea Post</strong>. In <strong>Canada</strong>, key local and international providers include <strong>Canada Post</strong>, <strong>Purolator</strong> (TFI International subsidiary), <strong>FedEx Ground Canada</strong>, <strong>UPS Canada</strong>, <strong>Loomis Express</strong> (<strong>TFI International</strong>), and <strong>Day &amp; Ross</strong>. The service disruption at <strong>DHL Express Canada</strong> impacts North American supply chain flows for major customers such as <strong>Lululemon Athletica</strong> (<strong>Vancouver, British Columbia</strong>; CEO <strong>Calvin McDonald</strong>); <strong>Shein</strong> (<strong>Singapore</strong>; CEO <strong>Sky Xu</strong>); and <strong>Siemens</strong> (<strong>Munich, Germany</strong>; CEO <strong>Roland Busch</strong>) — with rapid pivot to alternative providers, inventory position management, and communication strategy emerging as key operational response measures.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, recent global labor union activism and logistics impacts include: (1) <strong>UPS Teamsters</strong> 2023 agreement (<strong>Sean O'Brien</strong>, Teamsters General President); (2) <strong>ILA</strong> (<strong>International Longshoremen's Association</strong>; President <strong>Harold Daggett</strong>) port strikes with <strong>USMX</strong> on the <strong>U.S. East Coast</strong> and <strong>Gulf Coast</strong> (October 2024–January 2025); (3) <strong>ILWU</strong> (<strong>International Longshore and Warehouse Union</strong>) strike threats on the <strong>U.S. West Coast</strong>; (4) <strong>Boeing IAM District 751</strong> strike (September–November 2024); (5) <strong>Volkswagen IG Metall</strong> strikes (December 2024); (6) <strong>Amazon Teamsters</strong> campaigns; (7) <strong>UAW</strong> (<strong>United Auto Workers</strong>; President <strong>Shawn Fain</strong>) <strong>Detroit Big Three</strong> 2023 strikes. In <strong>Canada</strong>, recent disruptions include the <strong>Canada Post</strong> strike (November–December 2024), port strikes in <strong>Vancouver</strong> and <strong>Montreal</strong> (October 2024), and strike threats from railroad workers at <strong>Canadian National Railway</strong> (<strong>CN</strong>) and <strong>Canadian Pacific Kansas City</strong> (<strong>CPKC</strong>) (2024). The <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> tariff regime, combined with tariffs targeting <strong>Canada</strong> and <strong>USMCA</strong> (<strong>United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement</strong>) negotiations, creates layered challenges for North American supply chains alongside logistics disruptions. Ultimately, the <strong>DHL Express Canada</strong> service suspension exemplifies the structural impact of labor movements and federal legislation on logistics globally — making supplier diversification and contract resilience appear to be paramount strategic priorities for supply chain managers.</p>
