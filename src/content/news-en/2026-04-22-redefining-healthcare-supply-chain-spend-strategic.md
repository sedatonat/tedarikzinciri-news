<p style="text-align: justify">For much of modern healthcare history, supply chain operations—procurement, inventory management, and keeping clinical shelves stocked—have been treated as a necessary back-office function. Today, that model is no longer viable. Hospitals operate in an environment characterized by thin margins, persistent supply disruptions, and mounting cost scrutiny. Given this reality, supply chain systems in modern healthcare are increasingly viewed as a mission-critical lever with direct impact on financial stability, clinical operations, and organizational resilience. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>healthcare supply chain</strong> encompasses categories including <strong>med/surg supplies</strong>, <strong>pharmaceuticals</strong>, <strong>medical devices</strong>, <strong>capital equipment</strong>, <strong>purchased services</strong>, <strong>food &amp; nutrition</strong>, <strong>linen</strong>, and <strong>EVS</strong> (<strong>Environmental Services</strong>), representing annual spending of $3 trillion-plus in the <strong>U.S.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>University Hospitals</strong> VP of Supply Chain <strong>Chad Fleischer</strong> states: "In the current environment we're operating in, we have to review every product, piece of equipment, and service coming into our organization." What's driving this shift isn't simply chasing lower prices. There's growing recognition that supply chain operations influence nearly every facet of a health system—from operating rooms to pharmacies, from capital planning to executive-level decision making. <strong>University Hospitals</strong> is an academic medical center based in <strong>Cleveland, Ohio</strong>, operating 23 hospitals across the <strong>Northeast Ohio</strong> region. Regional competitors include <strong>Cleveland Clinic</strong>, <strong>MetroHealth</strong>, <strong>OhioHealth</strong>, and <strong>Mercy Health</strong>. Professional organizations include the <strong>HFMA</strong> (<strong>Healthcare Financial Management Association</strong>), <strong>AHRMM</strong> (<strong>Association for Health Care Resource &amp; Materials Management</strong>), and <strong>SMI</strong> (<strong>Strategic Marketplace Initiative</strong>).</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Today's strongest hospital supply chains are those that have developed enterprise-level governance structures—places where "<strong>expense cabinets</strong>" are used to identify, prioritize, and execute cost-savings initiatives across a system's budget. This shift reflects the growing realization that supply chain decisions are inseparable from a hospital's broader daily operations. Product selections affect clinical workflows, inventory practices influence waste and availability, and contracting decisions can have far-reaching long-term consequences. From a supply chain perspective, key players in the healthcare purchasing ecosystem include <strong>GPOs</strong> (<strong>Group Purchasing Organizations</strong>) such as <strong>Vizient</strong>, <strong>Premier Inc.</strong>, <strong>HealthTrust</strong>, and <strong>Intalere</strong> (acquired by <strong>Vizient</strong>), as well as the <strong>Healthcare Supply Chain Association</strong>. <strong>UDI</strong> (<strong>Unique Device Identifier</strong>) is mandated by the <strong>FDA</strong> and tracked in the <strong>GUDID</strong> (<strong>Global UDI Database</strong>). Standards from <strong>HIBCC</strong> and <strong>GS1</strong> Healthcare support medical device traceability.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In the <strong>healthcare ERP</strong>/<strong>SCM</strong> space, the market is dominated by <strong>Workday</strong>, <strong>Oracle Health</strong> (<strong>Cerner</strong>), <strong>Infor CloudSuite Healthcare</strong>, <strong>Epic</strong> (dominant in <strong>EHR</strong>), <strong>Premier Stockd</strong>, and <strong>GHX</strong> (<strong>Global Healthcare Exchange</strong>). Modern hospital inventory strategies include <strong>par level management</strong>, <strong>just-in-time delivery</strong>, <strong>vendor managed inventory</strong> (<strong>VMI</strong>), <strong>consignment inventory</strong>, and <strong>RFID asset tracking</strong>. The <strong>340B Drug Pricing Program</strong> provides drug cost advantages for <strong>safety-net hospitals</strong>. <strong>Physician preference items</strong> (<strong>PPI</strong>) account for 40-60 percent of total spending in categories such as <strong>orthopedics</strong>, <strong>cardiology</strong>, and <strong>spine</strong>. <strong>Clinical integration</strong>, <strong>value analysis committees</strong>, and the <strong>cost-benefit-quality</strong> triangle are fundamental disciplines in modern hospital procurement management. Ultimately, the <strong>University Hospitals</strong> example clearly demonstrates how healthcare supply chain has evolved from a back-office function to a strategic value lever.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. Healthcare supply chain is evolving from a back-office function to a mission-critical lever.<br>
2. <strong>Chad Fleischer</strong>, VP of Supply Chain at <strong>University Hospitals</strong>, emphasizes comprehensive review of all incoming products and services.<br>
3. <strong>Expense cabinet</strong> governance structures are emerging as critical tools.<br>
4. Product selection directly impacts clinical workflows.<br>
5. Thin margins and supply disruptions define the current environment.</p>
