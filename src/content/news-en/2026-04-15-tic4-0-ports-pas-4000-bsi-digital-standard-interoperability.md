<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>Terminal Industry Committee 4.0 (TIC4.0)</strong> is urging ports and terminals to adopt a new information model to improve <strong>interoperability</strong> and reduce supply chain costs. The call follows BSI's publication of <strong>PAS 4000</strong>. TIC4.0 said the specification establishes a <strong>common semantic framework</strong> to harmonise how operational data is structured and exchanged across ports and logistics hubs.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The standard has been sponsored by TIC4.0 and published through <strong>BSI's PAS process</strong>. The organisation noted that global trade has long depended on technical standards. For example, <strong>ISO 668</strong>, first issued in 1968, defines the dimensions and specifications for Series 1 freight containers, enabling global stackability and transferability. Other standards, including <strong>ISO 23412</strong> for temperature-controlled delivery services in land transport, support consistency in specialised logistics segments.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to TIC4.0, increasing reliance on digital systems makes common data structures essential for the wider adoption of <strong>APIs</strong>, <strong>data flows</strong>, and <strong>automated decision-support mechanisms</strong>. PAS 4000 has the potential to reduce integration costs in port operations, accelerate cross-system data exchange, and scale digital twin applications. The standard aims to bring a broad spectrum of stakeholders — from terminal operators to technology vendors — together under a common language.</p>

3. The standard provides a common semantic framework for structuring operational data.<br>
4. The goal is to improve interoperability across ports and lower supply chain costs.<br>
5. The call recalls global trade's historic reliance on standards like ISO 668.</p>
