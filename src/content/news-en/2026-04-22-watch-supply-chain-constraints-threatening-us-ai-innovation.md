<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; <strong>David Carter</strong> (<strong>Industrials Senior Analyst</strong> at <strong>RSM US</strong>) in an interview with <strong>Robert J. Bowman</strong> (<strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong>); analyst insight from <strong>David Carter</strong>, senior industrial analyst at <strong>RSM US</strong>, explains the major supply chain constraints stifling major U.S. data center efforts advancing innovation in artificial intelligence. Over the last several quarters, U.S. data center developers known as "hyperscalers" have begun citing in earnings calls problems acquiring sufficient microchips and land to drive continuing AI innovation efforts, says <strong>Carter</strong>. At the heart of the issue is the need for heavy capital investment in constructing chip manufacturing facilities in the U.S. The <strong>CHIPS and Science Act</strong>, passed in 2022 for this purpose, is a long-term process—it will take years before meaningful change is visible. "There's a really long, long runway to actually build this," <strong>Carter</strong> says. Meanwhile, data centers are finding their supply chains and supporting infrastructure increasingly inadequate for the task at hand. The most complex form of semiconductor manufacturing is power-hungry and land-hungry.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>RSM US LLP</strong>, based in <strong>Chicago, Illinois, U.S.</strong>, led by CEO <strong>Brian Becker</strong>, is the U.S. member of the global <strong>RSM International</strong> network, providing mid-market audit, tax and consulting services. Other major mid-market audit and consulting firms include <strong>BDO USA</strong>, <strong>Grant Thornton</strong>, <strong>Crowe</strong>, <strong>Baker Tilly</strong>, <strong>CliftonLarsonAllen</strong>, <strong>Plante Moran</strong>, <strong>EisnerAmper</strong> and <strong>Withum</strong> as major ecosystem players. The major U.S. <strong>hyperscaler</strong> data center developers are <strong>Amazon Web Services</strong> (<strong>AWS</strong>; CEO <strong>Matt Garman</strong>), <strong>Microsoft Azure</strong> (CEO <strong>Satya Nadella</strong>), <strong>Google Cloud</strong> (CEO <strong>Thomas Kurian</strong>), <strong>Meta Platforms</strong> (CEO <strong>Mark Zuckerberg</strong>), <strong>Oracle Cloud Infrastructure</strong> (CEO <strong>Safra Catz</strong>), <strong>Apple</strong> (with its own data centers), and <strong>IBM Cloud</strong>, making up the seven major hyperscalers. Next-generation <strong>AI-first hyperscaler</strong> firms include <strong>CoreWeave</strong> (CEO <strong>Michael Intrator</strong>; backed by <strong>NVIDIA</strong>), <strong>Lambda Labs</strong>, <strong>Crusoe Energy</strong>, <strong>Nebius</strong>, <strong>Together AI</strong>, and <strong>xAI</strong> (<strong>Elon Musk</strong>; <strong>Memphis</strong> Colossus), as major next-generation players.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the major global advanced semiconductor foundries are: (1) <strong>TSMC Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing</strong> (CEO <strong>C.C. Wei</strong>; <strong>Taiwan</strong>; global #1; <strong>Arizona TSMC AZ</strong> fab construction); (2) <strong>Samsung Foundry</strong> (<strong>Han Jin-man</strong>, Foundry Head; <strong>Texas Taylor</strong> fab); (3) <strong>Intel Foundry</strong> (CEO <strong>Lip-Bu Tan</strong>; <strong>Arizona</strong>, <strong>Ohio</strong>, <strong>Germany</strong> fabs); (4) <strong>GlobalFoundries</strong> (CEO <strong>Tim Breen</strong>; <strong>Malta, New York</strong>); (5) <strong>UMC United Microelectronics</strong> (<strong>Taiwan</strong>); (6) <strong>SMIC Semiconductor Manufacturing International Corp</strong> (<strong>China</strong>), as major foundry players. <strong>NVIDIA</strong> (CEO <strong>Jensen Huang</strong>) is the primary <strong>AI accelerator GPU</strong> designer—with <strong>H100</strong>, <strong>H200</strong>, <strong>B200 Blackwell</strong>, and <strong>GB200 Grace Blackwell</strong> as main product lines. <strong>AMD</strong> (CEO <strong>Lisa Su</strong>; <strong>MI300X</strong>, <strong>MI325X</strong>), <strong>Intel</strong> (<strong>Gaudi 3</strong>), <strong>Cerebras</strong>, <strong>Groq</strong>, and <strong>Tenstorrent</strong> are major <strong>AI silicon</strong> competitors. <strong>ASML</strong> (CEO <strong>Christophe Fouquet</strong>; <strong>Veldhoven, Netherlands</strong>) is the sole supplier of <strong>EUV lithography</strong>—a critical bottleneck point.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, data center supply chain constraints include: (1) <strong>HBM</strong> (<strong>High Bandwidth Memory</strong>; major producers <strong>SK Hynix</strong>, <strong>Samsung</strong>, <strong>Micron</strong>); (2) <strong>CoWoS</strong> (<strong>Chip on Wafer on Substrate</strong>; <strong>TSMC</strong> sole supplier of advanced packaging); (3) electrical power (grid capacity); (4) cooling systems (liquid cooling, immersion cooling); (5) land; (6) optical cables (optical transceiver); (7) power distribution units (<strong>PDU</strong>); (8) server racks; (9) <strong>UPS</strong> and generators; (10) construction materials and labor, as the ten major bottlenecks. The <strong>CHIPS and Science Act</strong> (signed in August 2022 by <strong>Joe Biden</strong>) provides 52.7 billion dollars in semiconductor manufacturing and R&D incentives. The <strong>Trump</strong> administration in 2025 has shut down the CHIPS Act implementation office—<strong>Howard Lutnick</strong> (<strong>Commerce Secretary</strong>) prefers a tariff-based approach. <strong>SEMI</strong> (<strong>Semiconductor Equipment and Materials International</strong>; CEO <strong>Ajit Manocha</strong>) is the major industry organization. In summary, <strong>Carter</strong>'s analysis of AI supply chain constraints suggests that U.S. AI innovation globally depends on semiconductor, energy, and land supply—and for supply chain managers, <strong>onshoring</strong>, alternative foundry partnerships, and energy infrastructure planning appear to be major strategic priorities.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. <strong>David Carter</strong> at <strong>RSM US</strong> notes that hyperscalers are citing microchip and land shortages in earnings calls.<br>
2. The <strong>CHIPS and Science Act</strong> of 2022 is a long-term process—meaningful change will not be visible for years.<br>
3. <strong>TSMC</strong>, <strong>Samsung</strong>, and <strong>Intel</strong> are the major advanced foundry players.<br>
4. <strong>NVIDIA</strong>, <strong>HBM</strong>, and <strong>CoWoS</strong> represent major structural AI bottlenecks.<br>
5. <strong>ASML EUV lithography</strong> is the sole supplier—a critical bottleneck point.</p>
