<p style="text-align: justify">An investigation spanning several months, led by U.S. Trade Representative <strong>Katherine Tai</strong>, has found that China achieved its dominant position in the global shipbuilding sector through unfair practices. These findings could pave the way for measures such as imposing tariffs or port charges on ships built in China.</p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">The investigation was initiated in April 2024 following a request filed by <strong>United Steelworkers</strong> and four other U.S. unions under Section 301 of the 1974 Trade Act. This section allows the United States to take action against foreign countries engaged in practices that unjustly impede American commerce. The investigation concluded that <strong>China provided financial support to its shipbuilding and maritime sectors, erected barriers against foreign companies, and gained advantages through forced technology transfer and intellectual property theft. It also found that China artificially maintained low labor costs.</strong></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>According to the data, China's global shipbuilding market share rose from 5 percent in 2000 to over 50 percent in 2023.</strong> This increase was largely achieved through state subsidies. During the same period, the U.S. share of the shipbuilding market fell below 1 percent. This situation has resulted in a severe decline in the American shipbuilding sector.</p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">The investigation's findings could lay the groundwork for implementing measures such as tariffs or port charges on ships built in China. Such measures will be evaluated after public comment is gathered. The Biden administration has continued the tariffs implemented during the Trump era to counter China's dominance and introduced new tariffs and export controls.</p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Experts note that reviving the U.S. shipbuilding and maritime sectors will take decades and require billions of dollars in investment. Tariffs alone will not be sufficient, and more comprehensive strategies are needed. The number of active shipyards in the United States has declined significantly compared to previous years. As a result, the U.S. could face dependence on allied nations for the construction of vessels required by its naval forces.</p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">China has rejected criticism of its shipbuilding practices. A Chinese Ministry of Commerce spokesman in Beijing stated at the time the investigation was announced, "It is not factual and runs counter to economic logic for the U.S. to blame China for its own industrial problems."</p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">These developments have intensified calls for revitalizing the shipbuilding sector in the United States. In particular, <strong>Scott Paul</strong>, president of the <a href="https://www.americanmanufacturing.org/"><u><strong>American Alliance for Manufacturing</strong></u></a>, noted that the findings are compelling, stating, "A process will be initiated to halt the erosion of our shipbuilding industry and enable its renewed growth."</p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Additionally, efforts by the U.S. to increase maritime capacity through cooperation with allied nations are on the agenda. For example, South Korea's <strong>Hanwha</strong> group acquired the American <strong>Philly Shipyard</strong>. Canada's <strong>Davie Shipbuilding</strong> company also announced it will invest in an American shipyard.</p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Similar concerns have been voiced in Europe. <strong>Christophe Tytgat</strong>, secretary general of <strong>SEA Europe</strong>, noted that Europe regaining shipbuilding capacity is critical for strategic autonomy, stating, "This strategy should strengthen technological leadership, facilitate investment, and support a skilled workforce."</p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">In conclusion, the U.S. investigation into China's shipbuilding practices has resonated significantly throughout the global maritime industry. This situation is fueling debate about how the U.S. and other countries will restructure their own shipbuilding sectors and respond to China's dominant position.</p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>\n<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Points:</strong></h5>\n<ul>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The U.S. investigation revealed China's unfair practices in the shipbuilding sector.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">China's global shipbuilding market share rose from 5 percent in 2000 to over 50 percent in 2023.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The U.S. shipbuilding market share fell below 1 percent.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The investigation found that China gained advantages through financial support, forced technology transfer, and intellectual property theft.</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p><br></p>\n<p>---</p>\n<p><strong>News Link: </strong><a href="https://splash247.com/washington-probe-lashes-chinas-unfair-shipbuilding-practices/"><u>https://splash247.com/washington-probe-lashes-chinas-unfair-shipbuilding-practices/</u></a></p>\n<p>-------------------</p>\n<p><strong>!!!
