<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Eric Rimkeit</strong>, marketing director at <strong>Supplyframe</strong>, explains the reasons behind an anticipated spike in capacitor pricing and what it means for electronics supply chains. Capacitors, which perform fundamental functions in electronic products such as power stabilization and noise filtering, have traditionally been regarded as <strong>popcorn parts</strong> — small devices costing approximately three cents per unit. However, like the famous missing <strong>golden screw</strong> example that can halt an automotive production line, capacitors are critical to a product's operation. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>passive component</strong> category comprises subcategories including <strong>MLCC</strong> (<strong>Multi-Layer Ceramic Capacitor</strong>), <strong>tantalum</strong>, <strong>aluminum electrolytic</strong>, and <strong>film capacitor</strong>. <strong>Murata</strong>, <strong>Samsung Electro-Mechanics</strong>, <strong>TDK</strong>, <strong>Yageo</strong>, <strong>Taiyo Yuden</strong>, <strong>KEMET</strong>, and <strong>AVX</strong> are the principal manufacturers in the global capacitor market.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Equally important to the broader electronics supply chain is their pricing. A new report from <strong>Supplyframe</strong> projects that capacitor demand will increase 14% quarter-over-quarter in the second quarter of this year. As a result, buyers of electronic products can expect to pay more as the additional costs are passed on to them. <strong>Rimkeit</strong>, however, states that the primary driver of price increases is artificial intelligence — particularly the new wave of data centers supporting it. Other areas affected by shifts in price and demand include aerospace and defense, medical devices, and industrial <strong>edge</strong> <strong>AI</strong> embedded in machinery within factory walls. From a supply chain perspective, the capacitor density in <strong>AI server</strong> cards is three to five times higher than in standard servers. <strong>NVIDIA H100</strong>, <strong>H200</strong>, and <strong>B200</strong>-based <strong>HGX</strong> systems require <strong>VRM</strong> (<strong>Voltage Regulator Module</strong>) and <strong>power delivery network (PDN)</strong> designs with hundreds of high-value <strong>polymer tantalum</strong> and <strong>MLCC</strong> components.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Over the past year, <strong>Rimkeit</strong> notes that capacitor pricing has declined an average of 31%. This is the result of excess inventories accumulated during the <strong>COVID-19</strong> pandemic and design variations. However, for <strong>AI</strong> data centers, as demand for specific component types they require increases, expectations shift toward higher prices. From a supply chain perspective, a textbook example of the <strong>bullwhip effect</strong> has been observed in the capacitor market during the 2021-2024 period. Pandemic <strong>panic buying</strong> inflated <strong>safety stock</strong> levels between <strong>EMS</strong> (<strong>Electronics Manufacturing Service</strong>) and <strong>OEM</strong> providers to 18 months of consumption, followed by an <strong>inventory correction</strong> period during 2024-2025 creating price pressure. The proliferation of <strong>NCNR</strong> (<strong>Non-Cancellable Non-Returnable</strong>) contracts complicates risk management on the buyer side.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Capacitor manufacturers are attempting to diversify supply chains by shifting operations away from <strong>China</strong>, but according to <strong>Rimkeit</strong>, supply remains concentrated among a limited number of producers. From a supply chain perspective, capacitor manufacturing bases are distributed among <strong>Japan</strong>, <strong>South Korea</strong>, <strong>Taiwan</strong>, and <strong>China</strong>. <strong>Murata</strong> is opening new fabs in the <strong>Philippines</strong> and <strong>Thailand</strong>; <strong>TDK</strong> is expanding capacity in <strong>Vietnam</strong>; and <strong>Yageo</strong> is growing production in <strong>Mexico</strong>. Supply of <strong>BaTiO3</strong> (<strong>Barium Titanate</strong>) dielectric powder and internal electrode materials containing <strong>palladium</strong> represent additional bottlenecks from a <strong>tier-2</strong> raw material supplier perspective. <strong>Tantalum</strong> raw material is concentrated in the <strong>DRC</strong> and <strong>Rwanda</strong> under the <strong>3TG</strong> (<strong>Tin-Tantalum-Tungsten-Gold</strong>) <strong>conflict mineral</strong> category and is subject to <strong>Dodd-Frank Section 1502</strong> traceability requirements. As a result, <strong>Rimkeit</strong>'s analysis is clear evidence that the <strong>AI</strong> wave is cascading throughout the lowest layers of the electronics supply chain.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Supplyframe</strong> projects that capacitor demand will increase 14% quarter-over-quarter in Q2 2026.<br>
2. The primary driver is <strong>AI</strong> data centers.<br>
3. Capacitor prices have declined an average of 31% over the past year; the trend is reversing.<br>
4. Other affected sectors include aerospace-defense, medical, and industrial edge AI.<br>
5. Capacitor manufacturers are diversifying away from <strong>China</strong>, but concentration continues.</p>
