<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; analyst view; with the implementation of punitive tariffs on U.S. imports from <strong>Mexico</strong> and <strong>Canada</strong> set to take effect on March 4 at 12:01am, threats of price increases loom for popular consumer products. Hours before the deadline, President <strong>Trump</strong> said on March 3 that neither country had any chance of striking a last-minute deal to avert the tariffs — according to <strong>The New York Times</strong>. "Everything is set," he said. "It takes effect tomorrow." According to <strong>BBC News</strong>, items most likely to be affected immediately include automobiles, fuel, lumber (and consequently new homes), avocados, and maple syrup. Additionally, <strong>Modelo</strong> beer — the top-selling beer in the U.S. — is among the main affected products.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Trump</strong>'s March 2025 <strong>Mexico</strong>/<strong>Canada</strong> tariff action timeline includes: (1) February 1, 2025; announcement of 25% <strong>Mexico</strong>/<strong>Canada</strong> tariffs and 10% <strong>China</strong> tariffs under the <strong>IEEPA</strong> (<strong>International Emergency Economic Powers Act</strong>); (2) February 3, 2025; 30-day delay; (3) March 4, 2025; tariffs take effect; (4) March 6, 2025; additional 30-day delay for products covered under <strong>USMCA</strong>; (5) March 12, 2025; 25% global steel and aluminum tariffs; (6) April 2, 2025; Liberation Day reciprocal tariffs — key action phases. The <strong>USMCA</strong> (<strong>United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement</strong>; effective 2020; successor to <strong>NAFTA</strong>) is in its mandatory review period starting in 2026 — renegotiation is expected under <strong>Trump</strong>'s 2.0 administration. <strong>Modelo Especial</strong>, manufactured by <strong>Constellation Brands</strong> (CEO <strong>Bill Newlands</strong>; Mexico-based origin; purchased U.S. licensing rights from <strong>Anheuser-Busch InBev</strong> in 2013), and <strong>Corona Extra</strong> are major <strong>Constellation Brands</strong> products — leaders in the U.S. beer market.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, key U.S. import categories from <strong>Mexico</strong>/<strong>Canada</strong> and tariff impact include: (1) <strong>Automotive</strong> (<strong>Mexico</strong> accounts for 35-40% of U.S. auto imports; <strong>Ford</strong>, <strong>GM</strong>, <strong>Stellantis</strong>, <strong>Toyota</strong>, <strong>Honda</strong>, <strong>Nissan</strong>, <strong>Volkswagen</strong>, <strong>BMW</strong>, <strong>Mercedes-Benz</strong> manufacturing in Mexico); (2) <strong>Oil and energy</strong> (<strong>Canada</strong> represents ~60% of U.S. crude oil imports — <strong>Suncor Energy</strong>, <strong>Cenovus</strong>, <strong>Imperial Oil</strong>, <strong>Enbridge</strong>); (3) <strong>Lumber</strong> (<strong>Canada</strong> supplies 85% of U.S. softwood lumber imports); (4) <strong>Avocados</strong> (<strong>Mexico</strong> supplies ~90% of U.S. avocado imports — <strong>Mission Produce</strong>, <strong>Calavo Growers</strong>, <strong>Avocados From Mexico</strong>); (5) <strong>Maple syrup</strong> (<strong>Quebec Maple Syrup Producers</strong>; 71% of global supply); (6) <strong>Beer and beverages</strong> (<strong>Modelo</strong>, <strong>Corona</strong>, <strong>Tequila</strong>); (7) <strong>Agricultural products</strong> (vegetables, fruits, beef); (8) <strong>Medical devices</strong> (<strong>Tijuana</strong> cluster); (9) <strong>Electronics</strong>; (10) <strong>Steel and aluminum</strong> — major import categories. The <strong>American Automotive Policy Council</strong> (<strong>AAPC</strong>) and <strong>Alliance for Automotive Innovation</strong> (<strong>AAI</strong>; CEO <strong>John Bozzella</strong>) are key U.S. automotive sector lobbying groups.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Mexico</strong>'s likely countermeasures against the U.S. include actions under President <strong>Claudia Sheinbaum</strong>, Economy Minister <strong>Marcelo Ebrard</strong>, and the <strong>Plan México</strong> economic independence framework. <strong>Canada</strong>'s countermeasures against the U.S. include those under <strong>Mark Carney</strong>, expected as Prime Minister in March 2025 following <strong>Justin Trudeau</strong>, along with <strong>Ontario</strong> Premier <strong>Doug Ford</strong> and leadership in <strong>Quebec</strong> and <strong>British Columbia</strong> — including a synchronized 25% reciprocal tariff on <strong>China</strong>-origin goods and threats to cut <strong>Quebec Hydro</strong> electricity exports as key <strong>Canada</strong> countermeasures. Key U.S. lobbying groups opposing tariffs include the <strong>U.S. Chamber of Commerce</strong> (CEO <strong>Suzanne Clark</strong>), <strong>Business Roundtable</strong> (CEO <strong>Joshua Bolten</strong>), <strong>National Association of Manufacturers</strong> (<strong>NAM</strong>; CEO <strong>Jay Timmons</strong>), <strong>Retail Industry Leaders Association</strong> (<strong>RILA</strong>; CEO <strong>Brian Dodge</strong>), and <strong>National Retail Federation</strong> (<strong>NRF</strong>). In sum, the <strong>Mexico</strong>/<strong>Canada</strong> tariffs pose a fundamental threat to globally integrated <strong>USMCA</strong> supply chains — supply chain managers appear to be making re-evaluation of <strong>nearshoring</strong> strategy and countermeasure tariff hedging key strategic priorities.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Trump</strong> is implementing 25% tariffs on <strong>Mexico</strong>/<strong>Canada</strong> on March 4, 2025.<br>
2. Automobiles, fuel, lumber, avocados, and beer are among the main affected products.<br>
3. <strong>Modelo</strong>, a <strong>Constellation Brands</strong> product, is the U.S. beer market leader.<br>
4. There is a 30-day additional delay for products covered under <strong>USMCA</strong>.<br>
5. <strong>Mexico</strong>'s <strong>Sheinbaum</strong> and <strong>Canada</strong>'s <strong>Carney</strong> lead the main countermeasure efforts.</p>
