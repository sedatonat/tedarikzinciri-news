<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Trump</strong> administration is planning to provide a $1 billion loan to help bring part of the <strong>Pennsylvania</strong> nuclear facility at <strong>Three Mile Island</strong> back online. <strong>The New York Times</strong> reports that the credit will be given to <strong>Constellation Energy</strong> — a company working with <strong>Microsoft</strong> to restore and restart the <strong>Unit 1</strong> reactor at <strong>Three Mile Island</strong>. The project is expected to generate enough electricity to power 800,000 homes — and allow <strong>Microsoft</strong> to purchase a portion of the facility's output to power the company's artificial intelligence data centers. The <strong>Trump</strong> administration's loan covers more than half of the project's estimated $1.6 billion price tag — though past nuclear energy projects in the <strong>U.S.</strong> have frequently exceeded their original budgets. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Constellation Energy Corporation</strong>, based in <strong>Baltimore, Maryland</strong>, became independent through a spinoff from <strong>Exelon</strong> in 2022 — making it the <strong>U.S.</strong>'s largest competitive nuclear operator. <strong>Constellation</strong> operates 21 nuclear reactors with a combined capacity of 22 GW.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Neither <strong>Constellation</strong> nor the Energy Department has provided details on the loan or its terms. <strong>Three Mile Island</strong> was shut down in 1979 — following the <strong>Unit 2</strong> reactor's partial meltdown and release of small amounts of radioactive material into the surrounding region. The non-operational <strong>Unit 1</strong> reactor at <strong>Three Mile Island</strong> had its restart plan announced by <strong>Constellation</strong> in 2024 — financed with a 20-year power purchase agreement (PPA) with <strong>Microsoft</strong>. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.S. DOE</strong> (<strong>Department of Energy</strong>) <strong>Loan Programs Office</strong> (<strong>LPO</strong>) manages the <strong>ATVM</strong> (<strong>Advanced Technology Vehicles Manufacturing</strong>), <strong>Title 17</strong>, and <strong>Tribal Energy Loan Guarantee</strong> programs to finance large-scale clean energy projects. <strong>Chris Wright</strong> is the <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> Energy Secretary — with fossil fuel experience as former CEO of <strong>Liberty Energy</strong> but also supporting nuclear energy. <strong>Three Mile Island Unit 1</strong> is an 837 MW capacity <strong>Babcock &amp; Wilcox</strong>-designed <strong>PWR</strong> (<strong>Pressurized Water Reactor</strong>) — which was shut down in 2019. The restart is officially named the <strong>Crane Clean Energy Center</strong> — named in honor of <strong>Christopher Crane</strong>, known as the mentor to <strong>Constellation</strong>'s CEO <strong>Joseph Dominguez</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the global nuclear energy renaissance is being triggered by the explosive electricity demand of <strong>AI</strong> data centers. <strong>Microsoft</strong>, <strong>Google</strong> (<strong>Alphabet</strong>), <strong>Amazon</strong> (<strong>AWS</strong>), <strong>Meta</strong>, and <strong>Oracle</strong> are the major <strong>hyperscaler</strong>s — leading the way in nuclear and other 24/7 clean energy PPAs. <strong>Google</strong> has signed with <strong>Kairos Power</strong> (<strong>SMR</strong>); <strong>Amazon</strong> with <strong>X-energy</strong> (<strong>SMR</strong>) and <strong>Talen Energy</strong> (<strong>Susquehanna</strong> nuclear); <strong>Meta</strong>, <strong>Constellation</strong>, and <strong>Oracle</strong> have signed multiple nuclear agreements with <strong>Sama Resources</strong> and <strong>Centrus</strong>. <strong>Westinghouse Electric</strong>, <strong>GE Vernova Hitachi Nuclear Energy</strong>, <strong>BWXT</strong>, <strong>NuScale Power</strong>, <strong>TerraPower</strong> (<strong>Bill Gates</strong>), <strong>X-energy</strong>, <strong>Holtec International</strong>, <strong>Oklo</strong>, <strong>Last Energy</strong>, and <strong>Aalo Atomics</strong> are the main <strong>SMR</strong> (<strong>Small Modular Reactor</strong>) and nuclear technology players. <strong>EDF</strong> (<strong>Électricité de France</strong>), <strong>Rosatom</strong> (<strong>Russia</strong>), <strong>CNNC</strong> (<strong>China</strong>), and <strong>KEPCO</strong> (<strong>South Korea</strong>) are the major players in large-scale reactor exports globally.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the nuclear fuel supply chain consists of stages including uranium mining (<strong>Cameco</strong>, <strong>Kazatomprom</strong>, <strong>Orano</strong>, <strong>Uranium One</strong>), conversion (<strong>Cameco</strong>, <strong>Orano</strong>, <strong>Rosatom</strong>), enrichment (<strong>Urenco</strong>, <strong>Orano</strong>, <strong>Rosatom</strong>, <strong>Centrus</strong>), and fuel fabrication (<strong>Westinghouse</strong>, <strong>Framatome</strong>, <strong>Global Nuclear Fuel</strong>, <strong>Mitsubishi Nuclear Fuel</strong>). <strong>HALEU</strong> (<strong>High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium</strong>) is critical for <strong>SMR</strong>s and advanced reactors — but the <strong>U.S.</strong> faces a supply gap due to dependence on <strong>Russia</strong>. <strong>Kazakhstan</strong>, <strong>Canada</strong>, <strong>Australia</strong>, <strong>Namibia</strong>, <strong>Niger</strong>, <strong>Russia</strong>, <strong>Uzbekistan</strong>, and the <strong>U.S.</strong> are the leading countries in global uranium production. The <strong>NRC</strong> (<strong>Nuclear Regulatory Commission</strong>) is the <strong>U.S.</strong> nuclear safety regulator. <strong>Data center power</strong>, <strong>colocated nuclear</strong>, <strong>behind-the-meter</strong>, and <strong>front-of-the-meter</strong> are the main frameworks of AI-era nuclear sales models. In conclusion, the <strong>Trump</strong> administration's <strong>Three Mile Island</strong> loan is a concrete indication of the public-private financing model of the <strong>U.S.</strong> nuclear renaissance.</p>
