<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; <strong>SCB Think Tank</strong>; analyst insight; innovation; the lifeblood of manufacturing. As a result, manufacturers must conduct research and develop new products, improvements, and production efficiencies on a regular basis to keep up—or stay ahead of—their competitors. Commonly referred to as the <strong>R&D Credit</strong>, the <strong>Federal Credit for Increasing Research Expenses</strong> can help offset some of the costs associated with these efforts. While most manufacturers are quite familiar with the <strong>R&D Credit</strong>, there are several important updates manufacturers need to keep in mind for 2025 and beyond. In January 2025, the <strong>Internal Revenue Service</strong> (<strong>IRS</strong>) released the final version of <strong>Form 6765</strong> (<strong>Credit for Increasing Research Activities</strong>)—which includes additional disclosure requirements.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.S. Internal Revenue Service</strong> (<strong>IRS</strong>), under Commissioner <strong>Daniel Werfel</strong> (with <strong>Billy Long</strong> nominated for the Trump 2.0 transition), headquartered in <strong>Washington DC</strong>, is the primary U.S. federal tax authority. The <strong>R&D Tax Credit</strong> falls under <strong>Internal Revenue Code Section 41</strong>—established by the <strong>Reagan</strong> administration in 1981 and made permanent through the 2015 <strong>PATH Act</strong>. The primary calculation methods for the <strong>R&D Credit</strong> are (1) the <strong>Regular Research Credit</strong> (<strong>RRC</strong>) and (2) the <strong>Alternative Simplified Credit</strong> (<strong>ASC</strong>). The main categories of <strong>Qualified Research Expenses</strong> (<strong>QREs</strong>) include (1) wages, (2) materials, (3) contract research, and (4) computer leasing. <strong>Section 174</strong>, modified by the <strong>Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017</strong> (<strong>TCJA</strong>), which mandates amortization of <strong>R&E</strong> expenses—five years in the U.S. and 15 years for foreign—represents the major post-2022 tax change. The 2025 <strong>Form 6765</strong> update includes additional <strong>Section G</strong> disclosure requirements—specifically details on business components, quantified <strong>QREs</strong>, and other new requirements.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the primary U.S. manufacturing sectors that invest in <strong>R&D</strong> include (1) <strong>Pharmaceutical</strong> (<strong>Big Pharma</strong>—<strong>Pfizer</strong>, <strong>J&J</strong>, <strong>Merck</strong>, <strong>AbbVie</strong>, <strong>Eli Lilly</strong>); (2) <strong>Aerospace and Defense</strong> (<strong>Lockheed Martin</strong>, <strong>RTX</strong>, <strong>Boeing</strong>, <strong>Northrop Grumman</strong>); (3) <strong>Semiconductors</strong> (<strong>Intel</strong>, <strong>TSMC</strong>, <strong>NVIDIA</strong>, <strong>AMD</strong>, <strong>Qualcomm</strong>, <strong>Micron</strong>); (4) <strong>Automotive</strong> (<strong>Ford</strong>, <strong>GM</strong>, <strong>Stellantis</strong>, <strong>Tesla</strong>, <strong>Rivian</strong>); (5) <strong>Software and Tech</strong> (<strong>Microsoft</strong>, <strong>Google</strong>, <strong>Apple</strong>, <strong>Meta</strong>, <strong>Amazon</strong>); (6) <strong>Medical Devices</strong> (<strong>Medtronic</strong>, <strong>Abbott Laboratories</strong>, <strong>Stryker</strong>, <strong>Boston Scientific</strong>); (7) <strong>Chemicals and Materials</strong> (<strong>Dow</strong>, <strong>DuPont</strong>, <strong>3M</strong>); (8) <strong>Industrial Manufacturing</strong>; (9) <strong>Energy</strong>; and (10) <strong>Food and Beverage</strong>. Annual <strong>R&D</strong> spending in the U.S. totals approximately $700 billion—representing roughly 30% of global <strong>R&D</strong> expenditures. The <strong>National Science Foundation</strong> (<strong>NSF</strong>; <strong>Sethuraman Panchanathan</strong>, Director); the <strong>Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency</strong> (<strong>DARPA</strong>); and the <strong>National Institutes of Health</strong> (<strong>NIH</strong>) are the primary U.S. federal <strong>R&D</strong> funding agencies.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the primary U.S. manufacturing sector advocacy groups and organizations include the <strong>National Association of Manufacturers</strong> (<strong>NAM</strong>; <strong>Jay Timmons</strong>, CEO); <strong>Manufacturers Alliance</strong> (<strong>Steve Schoeny</strong>, CEO); the <strong>National Tooling and Machining Association</strong> (<strong>NTMA</strong>); the <strong>Precision Metalforming Association</strong> (<strong>PMA</strong>); the <strong>Aerospace Industries Association</strong> (<strong>AIA</strong>; <strong>Eric Fanning</strong>, CEO); the <strong>Semiconductor Industry Association</strong> (<strong>SIA</strong>; <strong>John Neuffer</strong>, CEO); the <strong>Advanced Medical Technology Association</strong> (<strong>AdvaMed</strong>; <strong>Scott Whitaker</strong>); and the <strong>Pharmaceutical Research and Manufacturers of America</strong> (<strong>PhRMA</strong>; <strong>Stephen Ubl</strong>). The primary <strong>R&D</strong> tax advisory firms in the U.S. include the <strong>Big 4</strong> (<strong>Deloitte</strong>, <strong>PwC</strong>, <strong>EY</strong>, <strong>KPMG</strong>); <strong>Grant Thornton</strong>; <strong>BDO USA</strong>; <strong>RSM US</strong>; <strong>Crowe</strong>; <strong>BKD</strong>; <strong>Moss Adams</strong>; <strong>alliantgroup</strong>; <strong>Source Advisors</strong>; and <strong>Tri-Merit</strong>. The <strong>Tax Foundation</strong>, <strong>Tax Policy Center</strong>, and <strong>Bipartisan Policy Center</strong> are the primary U.S. tax policy research organizations. As a result, the 2025 <strong>R&D Credit</strong> update represents a fundamental reinforcement of U.S. manufacturing <strong>R&D</strong> incentives globally—making preparation for <strong>Section 174</strong> amortization and <strong>Form 6765</strong> disclosure requirements a key strategic priority for supply chain managers.</p>
