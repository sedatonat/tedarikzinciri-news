<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports that <strong>Pawel Z. Chadzynski</strong>, <strong>Senior Director of Strategic Research</strong> at <strong>Aras</strong>, offers analyst insight into how industrial companies must overcome three interconnected challenges to support their sustainability efforts: driving sustainability, ensuring compliance, and fostering innovation. The digital thread—a consistent, seamless flow of information throughout the product lifecycle—can enable companies to understand the impact of their decision-making on green initiatives and empower them to use this data for better decisions. Data sits at the heart of sustainability—how it is captured, connected, and used. Sustainability is evolving from a corporate responsibility initiative to a business imperative, a value recognized by industrial companies worldwide. In fact, some consumers are prioritizing sustainability in their purchasing decisions, with many willing to pay a premium for environmentally responsible products. For companies, sustainability is not just about meeting consumer expectations; it is also about driving profitability, efficiency, and long-term resilience. In a 2025 research report titled "<strong>The Future of Product Lifecycle Management and Digital Engineering</strong>," an overwhelming majority of respondents—92 percent—agree that progress on sustainability is key to their organizations' economic success. However, achieving sustainability goals requires more than good intentions. Businesses must determine the right sustainability priorities based on their data, track progress in real time and adapt their strategies, and foster collaboration with suppliers and partners.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Aras Corporation</strong>, headquartered in <strong>Andover, Massachusetts, U.S.</strong>, with CEO <strong>Roque Martin</strong>, was founded in 2000 and is a portfolio company of <strong>GI Partners</strong>. It provides a global <strong>PLM</strong> (<strong>Product Lifecycle Management</strong>) and <strong>digital thread</strong> platform. Core products include <strong>Aras Innovator</strong>, <strong>Aras Cloud</strong>, and <strong>Aras Open Innovator</strong>, which serve as the main platform components. Principal <strong>Aras</strong> customers include <strong>Airbus</strong>, <strong>Honda</strong>, <strong>General Motors</strong>, <strong>Hitachi</strong>, <strong>Microsoft</strong>, <strong>Nissan</strong>, <strong>Mitsubishi Electric</strong>, <strong>Carrier</strong>, <strong>Schaeffler</strong>, and <strong>Kawasaki Heavy Industries</strong>—major industrial firms. Other leading <strong>PLM</strong> providers include <strong>Dassault Systèmes</strong> (<strong>3DEXPERIENCE</strong>; CEO <strong>Pascal Daloz</strong>; <strong>France</strong>), <strong>Siemens Digital Industries Software</strong> (<strong>Teamcenter</strong>; CEO <strong>Tony Hemmelgarn</strong>), <strong>PTC</strong> (<strong>Windchill</strong>; CEO <strong>Neil Barua</strong>; <strong>Boston</strong>), <strong>Autodesk Fusion Lifecycle</strong>, <strong>SAP PLM</strong>, <strong>Oracle Agile PLM</strong>, <strong>Bentley Systems</strong>, <strong>Hexagon Manufacturing Intelligence</strong>, and <strong>Centric Software</strong>—key ecosystem players.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>digital thread</strong> enables the flow of information across the product lifecycle—from product design through manufacturing, maintenance, and service, to eventual disposal or recycling. Its core components include: (1) <strong>CAD</strong> (<strong>Computer-Aided Design</strong>); (2) <strong>CAM</strong> (<strong>Computer-Aided Manufacturing</strong>); (3) <strong>PLM</strong> (<strong>Product Lifecycle Management</strong>); (4) <strong>ERP</strong> (<strong>Enterprise Resource Planning</strong>); (5) <strong>MES</strong> (<strong>Manufacturing Execution System</strong>); (6) <strong>SCM</strong> (<strong>Supply Chain Management</strong>); (7) <strong>QMS</strong> (<strong>Quality Management System</strong>); (8) <strong>IoT</strong> and <strong>digital twin</strong>; (9) <strong>BOM</strong> (<strong>Bill of Materials</strong>) and <strong>EBOM</strong>/<strong>MBOM</strong>/<strong>SBOM</strong>; and (10) <strong>PDM</strong> (<strong>Product Data Management</strong>). Leading <strong>digital twin</strong> providers include <strong>Siemens Industrial Turbomachinery</strong>, <strong>GE Digital Predix</strong>, <strong>IBM Maximo</strong>, <strong>SAP Digital Twin</strong>, <strong>Microsoft Azure Digital Twins</strong>, <strong>AWS IoT TwinMaker</strong>, and <strong>NVIDIA Omniverse</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, key sustainability regulatory frameworks include: (1) <strong>EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive</strong> (<strong>CSRD</strong>; starting with 2024 data); (2) <strong>EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive</strong> (<strong>CSDDD</strong>; 2024); (3) <strong>EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism</strong> (<strong>CBAM</strong>; full implementation in 2026); (4) <strong>U.S. SEC Climate Disclosure Rule</strong> (approved March 2024; stayed in 2024); (5) <strong>California Climate Corporate Data Accountability Act</strong> (<strong>SB 253</strong>); (6) <strong>UK Sustainability Disclosure Standards</strong> (<strong>SDS</strong>); and (7) <strong>ISSB</strong> (<strong>International Sustainability Standards Board</strong>)—leading regulatory and standards frameworks. The <strong>Digital Product Passport</strong> (<strong>DPP</strong>; <strong>EU</strong>) will be mandatory for batteries, textiles, and electronics by 2027—a key traceability tool. Leading reporting frameworks include <strong>Carbon Disclosure Project</strong> (<strong>CDP</strong>), <strong>Science Based Targets initiative</strong> (<strong>SBTi</strong>), <strong>Sustainability Accounting Standards Board</strong> (<strong>SASB</strong>), and <strong>Global Reporting Initiative</strong> (<strong>GRI</strong>). In conclusion, Chadzynski's recommendation on the digital thread suggests that global industrial sustainability and <strong>PLM</strong> integration are being fundamentally redesigned—for supply chain managers, product lifecycle data flow and carbon footprint tracking appear to be emerging as a top strategic priority.</p>
