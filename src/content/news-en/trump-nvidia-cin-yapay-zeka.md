<p style="text-align: justify">U.S. President <strong>Donald Trump</strong> announced that U.S.-based semiconductor manufacturer <strong>Nvidia</strong> will be permitted to sell <strong>advanced artificial intelligence (AI) chips</strong> to select customers in China under specific conditions. According to <strong>BBC News</strong>, under the decision Nvidia can sell its <strong>H200 AI chips</strong> model only to a limited number of Chinese buyers approved by Washington. In return, the U.S. government will receive <strong>25%</strong> of the revenue generated from these sales directly.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In a post on <strong>Truth Social</strong>, Trump characterized this policy as a step that "<strong>supports American jobs, strengthens U.S. manufacturing, and benefits American taxpayers</strong>." Administration sources indicate that similar agreements with other major U.S. chip manufacturers such as <strong>AMD</strong> and <strong>Intel</strong> are expected to be reached in the coming days.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This decision signals a notable adjustment in the Trump administration's long-standing policy of <strong>stringent export controls</strong>. In recent years, the U.S. had imposed comprehensive restrictions on the export of advanced semiconductors to limit China's capacity to develop <strong>cutting-edge AI</strong> and <strong>military applications</strong>. The new model, however, adopts a "<strong>controlled access plus revenue sharing</strong>" approach rather than an outright ban.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Nvidia's Position and Response</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">In a statement to BBC, Nvidia described the decision as "<strong>a good, balanced, and well-considered solution for America</strong>." Previously, in August, the company had signed a separate agreement with the U.S. government alongside <strong>AMD</strong>, committing to transfer <strong>15%</strong> of revenue from high-performance AI chips sold to China to Washington. The increase to 25% in the new arrangement demonstrates the Trump administration's intent to use technology trade directly as a <strong>fiscal gain</strong> tool.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Nvidia CEO <strong>Jensen Huang</strong> met with Trump in early December. This meeting is regarded as part of the company's intensive lobbying efforts to reopen controlled access to the Chinese market. China remains one of the world's largest AI hardware markets for Nvidia and has a decisive impact on the company's revenues.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Export Controls and Security Dimension</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">Despite this flexibility, the Trump administration has not overlooked security risks. On <strong>December 8</strong>, a Texas-based federal task force announced the dismantling of a <strong>smuggling network</strong> with Chinese ties. According to authorities, this network <strong>illegally exported</strong> <strong>Nvidia H100 and H200 chips</strong> valued at <strong>at least 160 million USD</strong> to China and Hong Kong.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to the investigation, the network used forged <strong>shipping documents</strong> to reroute chips through different countries and received <strong>over 50 million USD in wire transfers</strong> from Chinese sources to finance operations. This incident clearly demonstrates why the U.S. prefers to manage export controls through tight oversight rather than removing them entirely.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>A New Era in U.S.–China Technology Trade</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">This decision reflects that the U.S. is shifting toward a model of <strong>conditional engagement</strong> rather than complete decoupling with China in technology trade. From the administration's perspective, this model simultaneously pursues three core objectives:</p>
<ol>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Preserve the global competitiveness of U.S. companies</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Limit China's uncontrolled access to advanced technology</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Generate direct revenue for the federal budget</strong>.</p></li>
</ol>
<p style="text-align: justify">However, experts warn that this approach may produce complex consequences in the long term. Chinese firms sourcing chips through approved lists could complicate oversight; on the other hand, complete closure of the Chinese market for U.S. companies has been prevented.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Supply Chain and Global Impacts</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">AI chips are a critical input for global <strong>advanced manufacturing</strong>, <strong>data centers</strong>, <strong>cloud computing</strong>, and <strong>defense-linked technologies</strong>. Nvidia's resumption of limited sales to China:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Could ease global AI hardware supply,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Could increase revenues for U.S. manufacturers,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Could prevent a complete halt to China's AI investments.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">Conversely, the decision demonstrates that the technology competition between the U.S. and China is now shaped not only by security considerations but also along the axis of <strong>commercial revenue sharing</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong></h3>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Trump approved Nvidia's sale of <strong>H200 AI chips</strong> to limited Chinese customers.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The U.S. government will receive <strong>25%</strong> of sales revenue.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Similar agreements with <strong>AMD</strong> and <strong>Intel</strong> are expected.</p></li>

  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The policy signals a shift toward <strong>controlled access plus revenue sharing</strong> rather than an outright ban.</p></li>
</ul>
