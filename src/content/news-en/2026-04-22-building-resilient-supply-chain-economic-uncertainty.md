<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; <strong>Amy Dean</strong> (<strong>SCB Contributor</strong>); analyst insight; as supply chains in 2025 focus on boosting continuity and efficiency; they are looking to warehouse management systems (<strong>WMS</strong>) and other cutting-edge applications to cope with demand fluctuations; potential disruptions and rising costs. <strong>WMS</strong> data can help companies diversify; predict and enable flexibility within the supply chain. Organizations can better anticipate shifts in consumer spending — and proactively adjust inventory strategies to navigate inflationary pressures and financial downturns. Beyond this; advanced analytics; real-time visibility and <strong>AI</strong>-enabled predictive capabilities are key supply chain resilience tools.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective; the <strong>SupplyChainBrain Think Tank</strong> is <strong>SCB</strong>'s opinion and contribution blog platform — publishing articles written by industry professionals. Leading <strong>WMS</strong> providers include <strong>Manhattan Associates</strong> (<strong>Manhattan Active Warehouse Management</strong>; <strong>Eddie Capel</strong> CEO; <strong>Atlanta</strong>); <strong>Blue Yonder</strong> (formerly <strong>JDA</strong>; <strong>Panasonic</strong>-owned); <strong>Oracle WMS Cloud</strong>; <strong>SAP EWM</strong> (<strong>Extended Warehouse Management</strong>); <strong>Infios</strong> (formerly <strong>Körber Supply Chain Software</strong>; <strong>Chad Collins</strong> CEO); <strong>Microlistics</strong>; <strong>Tecsys</strong>; <strong>HighJump</strong> (formerly <strong>Körber</strong>); <strong>Made4net</strong>; <strong>Softeon</strong>; <strong>Logiwa</strong>; <strong>3PL Central</strong>; <strong>Mecalux Easy WMS</strong>; <strong>Reply Click WMS</strong>; among the core ecosystem players. The <strong>WMS</strong> market size is estimated at 4.5+ billion USD (2024) — growing at a <strong>CAGR</strong> of 15%+ — with the <strong>Gartner Magic Quadrant for Warehouse Management Systems</strong> serving as a key industry ranking reference.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective; key <strong>supply chain resilience</strong> technology categories include: (1) <strong>Control Tower</strong> (<strong>Blue Yonder Luminate</strong>; <strong>One Network</strong>; <strong>Kinaxis</strong>; <strong>o9 Solutions</strong>); (2) <strong>Demand Sensing</strong> (<strong>Demand Solutions</strong>; <strong>RELEX</strong>; <strong>ToolsGroup</strong>); (3) <strong>Inventory Optimization</strong> (<strong>Logility</strong>; <strong>Coupa</strong>); (4) <strong>Multi-Echelon Inventory Optimization</strong> (<strong>MEIO</strong>); (5) <strong>Network Design</strong> (<strong>Coupa Llamasoft</strong>; <strong>RIVER LOGIC</strong>); (6) <strong>Supply Chain Visibility</strong> (<strong>FourKites</strong>; <strong>project44</strong>; <strong>Shippeo</strong>; <strong>Sixfold</strong>; <strong>Wakeo</strong>); (7) <strong>Risk Management</strong> (<strong>Resilinc</strong>; <strong>Everstream Analytics</strong>; <strong>riskmethods</strong>); (8) <strong>Digital Twin</strong> (<strong>AnyLogistix</strong>; <strong>Coupa</strong>; <strong>SAP Digital Supply Chain</strong>); (9) <strong>S&amp;OP</strong>/<strong>IBP</strong> (<strong>Integrated Business Planning</strong>); (10) <strong>Sustainability and ESG monitoring</strong>; among the core technology stack. <strong>Kinaxis</strong> (<strong>John Sicard</strong> CEO; <strong>Ottawa</strong>); <strong>o9 Solutions</strong> (<strong>Chakri Gottemukkala</strong> CEO; <strong>Dallas</strong>); <strong>Anaplan</strong> (<strong>Frank Calderoni</strong> CEO; <strong>Thoma Bravo</strong>-owned); <strong>RELEX Solutions</strong> (<strong>Mikko Kärkkäinen</strong> CEO; <strong>Helsinki</strong>); are among the core ecosystem players.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective; key <strong>supply chain resilience</strong> strategies include: (1) <strong>multi-sourcing</strong> (supplier diversification); (2) <strong>nearshoring</strong>/<strong>friend-shoring</strong>; (3) increased safety stock; (4) <strong>postponement</strong> (late differentiation); (5) <strong>dual-sourcing</strong> (critical components); (6) <strong>regionalization</strong> (regional supply chain networks); (7) buffer capacity; (8) contract flexibility; (9) <strong>scenario planning</strong>; (10) <strong>tariff hedging</strong>; among the key strategic tools. <strong>MIT Center for Transportation &amp; Logistics</strong> (<strong>Yossi Sheffi</strong>); <strong>Stanford Global Supply Chain Management Forum</strong>; <strong>Penn State Center for Supply Chain Research</strong>; <strong>Michigan State University</strong> (<strong>Steven A. Melnyk</strong>); <strong>Arizona State University</strong>; are among leading U.S. academic supply chain centers. <strong>Council of Supply Chain Management Professionals</strong> (<strong>CSCMP</strong>; <strong>Mark Baxa</strong> President); <strong>Institute for Supply Management</strong> (<strong>ISM</strong>; <strong>Tom Derry</strong> CEO); <strong>APICS</strong> (now <strong>ASCM</strong>); are among leading U.S. supply chain professional organizations. The <strong>Gartner Top 25 Supply Chain</strong> serves as a key annual industry ranking. In conclusion; <strong>Dean</strong>'s supply chain resilience advocacy suggests that planning is being fundamentally redesigned under global economic uncertainty — <strong>WMS</strong>; <strong>visibility</strong>; and <strong>scenario planning</strong> appear to be core strategic priorities for supply chain executives.</p>
