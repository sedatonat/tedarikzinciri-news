<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Port of Long Beach</strong> has demonstrated strong performance throughout 2025, maintaining high cargo volumes despite ongoing tariff uncertainties in U.S.–China trade. According to port management, retailers and importers' practice of <strong>anticipating tariff increases and filling inventories early (frontloading)</strong> supported volumes throughout the year.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>End of October: 8.2 million TEU — 4.1% increase</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">The port processed <strong>8.2 million TEU in January–October</strong>, achieving a <strong>4.1% increase</strong> compared to the same period in 2024. 2024 was Long Beach's busiest year on record (9.6 million TEU).</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">CEO <strong>Mario Cordero</strong> told media briefing:</p>
<blockquote style="text-align: justify">"We are quite close to matching, or even exceeding, last year's record of 9.6 million TEU by year-end."</blockquote>
<p style="text-align: justify">Cordero also emphasized that <strong>throughout 2025, the port has operated with zero congestion and seamless operations</strong>, in contrast to the bottlenecks experienced in the post-pandemic period.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Frontloading artificially inflated volumes</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">COO <strong>Noel Hacegaba</strong> noted that tariff-driven <strong>artificial peaks</strong> occurred in 2025:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The busiest months were <strong>January 2025</strong> (Trump's inauguration)</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">And <strong>July 2025</strong> (just before the 90-day reprieve from country-specific reciprocal tariffs expired)</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to Hacegaba:</p>
<blockquote style="text-align: justify">"The frontloading trend continued this year and was the primary driver of record volumes."</blockquote>
<p style="text-align: justify">This behavior led retailers in particular to <strong>bring in holiday merchandise much earlier</strong> to avoid high cost risks.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Volume decline inevitable in final two months</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to the National Retail Federation (NRF) and Hackett Associates data:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Volume declines are expected at U.S. ports in November–December 2025</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The reason: holiday season merchandise was largely imported in the first half of the year.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">NRF Vice President <strong>Jonathan Gold</strong> stated on November 7:</p>
<blockquote style="text-align: justify">"Store shelves are full and price pressures have been largely minimized. This is because retailers pulled in imports early to avoid tariffs."</blockquote>
<p style="text-align: justify">The NRF projects 2025 will close with a total <strong>2.3% volume decline</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>2026: First-quarter trough, mid-year recovery</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">Looking into 2026, forecasts are mixed:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Larger volume declines are expected nationally in Q1 2026</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Tariff impacts will be more visible to retailer and consumer pricing.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to Cordero:</p>
<blockquote style="text-align: justify">"Consumers will feel the tariff impact more acutely in 2026. Despite this, we expect <strong>modest volume growth</strong> for 2026."</blockquote>
<p style="text-align: justify">This outlook is supported by a <strong>temporary accord reached between the U.S. and China in October</strong>:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Some tariffs were reduced,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Reciprocal penalty tariffs were suspended.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">Cordero:</p>
<blockquote style="text-align: justify">"We expect increased volume from China. However, the real question in coming years will be how much global production shifts from China to Southeast Asia."</blockquote>
<blockquote><br></blockquote>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Hackett Associates: Uncertainty at highest levels</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">Founder <strong>Ben Hackett</strong> noted in his November 7 report:</p>
<blockquote style="text-align: justify">"Market forecasting is extremely difficult due to tariff dynamics. We expect a modest decline in 2025 and a more significant decline in Q1 2026."</blockquote>
<blockquote><br></blockquote>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong></h5>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Long Beach processed <strong>8.2 million TEU in January–October 2025</strong>, maintaining strong momentum.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Primary driver of volume growth: <strong>excessive frontloading due to tariff uncertainty</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Volume declines are expected in the final two months of the year.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">NRF: Forecasts a <strong>2.3% decline</strong> for 2025 overall.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">2026: Sharp first-quarter decline, <strong>modest recovery</strong> expected for the year.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">U.S.–China accord may provide some volume support.</p></li>
</ul>
