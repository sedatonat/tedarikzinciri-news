<p style="text-align: justify">As ZIM Integrated Shipping Services evaluates potential acquisition offers in December 2025, Israeli media has reported that <strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong> has submitted a formal takeover bid for ZIM. However, upon examining the bid's details and ZIM's current shareholder structure, it appears that a potential sale faces multiple layers of <strong>geopolitical, legal, and national security</strong> obstacles.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to information reported by <strong>Globes</strong>, ZIM's board of directors is conducting a comprehensive review to evaluate strategic options for the company, and has placed Hapag-Lloyd's offer on the table as part of this process. However, the same report notes that ZIM CEO <strong>Eli Glickman</strong> has also submitted an independent acquisition proposal for the company, indicating that the process has become a multi-party competitive environment.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The harshest pushback has come from the <strong>workers' committee</strong> representing ZIM employees. The committee points out that <strong>Qatar Investment Authority (QIA)</strong> and <strong>Saudi Public Investment Fund (PIF)</strong>—funds holding stakes in Hapag-Lloyd's shareholder structure—together hold approximately <strong>22–23 percent</strong> of the company, arguing that such an acquisition would create a <strong>national security risk</strong> for Israel.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Committee head <strong>Oren Ksafim</strong> stated, "<strong>If ZIM is in Qatari-Saudi hands, in the next war we shall be cut off by sea</strong>," arguing that ZIM's military–logistical contributions, particularly during the "Swords of Iron" conflict, would be lost in the future. This view is grounded in the understanding that ZIM has long been a strategic asset that the Israeli state has relied on during periods of crisis.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The most critical element in this regard is the <strong>"Special State Share" (Golden Share)</strong> mechanism held by the Israeli government. This special share grants the government <strong>veto rights</strong> over any sale or merger that could affect the company's identity, flag, strategic operations, or national security functions. According to experts, given Hapag-Lloyd's shareholder structure, it is deemed "virtually certain" that the Israeli government would reject the offer.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Analysts note that the Hapag-Lloyd bid is <strong>commercially sensible</strong>, yet <strong>politically and securitywise nearly impossible</strong>. The Germany-based carrier could gain access to ZIM's strong Asia–Mediterranean routes connected to Palestine, Turkey, India, and China through the acquisition. However, it is not expected that the Israeli government would permit such a sale by disregarding its security concerns.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Meanwhile, ZIM employees' opposition is not merely symbolic; active mobilization against the sale process has been reported within the company. Both unions and employee groups have previously generated public pressure against foreign state funds indirectly controlling Israeli-based companies that contribute to defense infrastructure.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Due to these factors, experts assess that the strongest candidates in the acquisition process are likely to be <strong>Israeli investors</strong>, <strong>management buyout</strong> proposals originating from ZIM's senior management, or Western companies perceived as politically <strong>neutral</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In conclusion, while Hapag-Lloyd's offer will be considered in ZIM's ongoing strategic review process, it is assessed as having an extremely low probability of success due to <strong>the Israeli government's veto authority, employees' national security objections, and the sensitivity created by Gulf state funds in the shareholder structure</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Points:</strong></h3>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Hapag-Lloyd's shareholders include <strong>QIA (approximately 12 percent)</strong> and <strong>PIF (approximately 10 percent)</strong>; this situation is viewed as a security risk by Israel.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">ZIM employees oppose the offer, arguing that the company would be unable to support Israel in future wartime scenarios.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The Israeli government's <strong>Golden Share</strong> grants it <strong>veto authority</strong> over any sale transaction that it deems a strategic risk.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Analysts agree that while Hapag-Lloyd's bid is commercially rational, it has <strong>very low political chances</strong> of success.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Alternative proposals, including <strong>management buyouts</strong> and investors close to the Israeli government, are viewed as more likely.</p></li>
</ul>
