<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports that <strong>Robert J. Bowman</strong>, analyst at <strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong>, offers this insight: policymakers assessing the impact of President <strong>Trump</strong>'s immigration policies on U.S. agriculture would do well to examine the labor shortage that hit farms in the United Kingdom with the arrival of <strong>COVID-19</strong>. In 2020, the combination of pandemic lockdowns and the United Kingdom's withdrawal from the <strong>European Union</strong> left the country without sufficient workers to harvest crops. Foreign labor, primarily from Eastern Europe, accounted for roughly 95 percent of the workforce — and that supply was largely cut off. Crops rotted in fields without pickers. The Brexit lesson holds profound implications for the <strong>H-2A</strong> visa program.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the U.S. agricultural workforce comprises approximately 2.4 million farm workers according to <strong>USDA Economic Research Service</strong> data — roughly 50 to 70 percent of whom are undocumented, per the <strong>USDA Farm Labor Survey</strong> and the <strong>National Agricultural Workers Survey</strong> (<strong>NAWS</strong>). Principal source countries include <strong>Mexico</strong>, <strong>Guatemala</strong>, <strong>Honduras</strong>, and <strong>El Salvador</strong>, which also serve as primary <strong>H-2A</strong> supplier nations. The <strong>H-2A Temporary Agricultural Worker Program</strong>, jointly administered by the <strong>U.S. Department of Labor</strong>, <strong>U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services</strong> (<strong>USCIS</strong>), and the <strong>U.S. Department of State</strong>, represents the primary legal seasonal agricultural worker visa program — approximately 380,000+ <strong>H-2A</strong> visas were issued in 2024. Trump 2.0 immigration policy, led by key officials including <strong>Tom Homan</strong> as Border Czar, <strong>Stephen Miller</strong> as Deputy Chief of Staff for Policy, <strong>Kristi Noem</strong> as Secretary of Homeland Security, and <strong>Pete Hegseth</strong> as Secretary of Defense, has raised concerns about mass deportation plans and potential labor shortages in agriculture and construction.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, primary labor-intensive agricultural sectors in the U.S. include: (1) fresh produce in <strong>California</strong>, <strong>Florida</strong>, <strong>Oregon</strong>, <strong>Washington</strong>, and <strong>Arizona</strong>; (2) wine grapes in <strong>California</strong>; (3) peaches and watermelons in <strong>Texas</strong>, <strong>Georgia</strong>, and <strong>South Carolina</strong>; (4) fruit in <strong>Michigan</strong>; (5) potatoes in <strong>Idaho</strong>; and (6) dairy operations in <strong>Wisconsin</strong>, <strong>California</strong>, and <strong>Idaho</strong>. Key agricultural labor unions include the <strong>United Farm Workers</strong> (<strong>UFW</strong>, led by President <strong>Teresa Romero</strong>), the <strong>Farm Labor Organizing Committee</strong> (<strong>FLOC</strong>), and the <strong>National Farm Workers Service Center</strong>. Major farmer and employer organizations include the <strong>American Farm Bureau Federation</strong> (<strong>AFBF</strong>, with President <strong>Zippy Duvall</strong>), the <strong>Western Growers Association</strong>, and the <strong>National Council of Agricultural Employers</strong> (<strong>NCAE</strong>). The <strong>UK Seasonal Worker Scheme</strong> represents Britain's primary seasonal agricultural visa program, with a 45,000-visa ceiling in 2024; principal scheme operators include <strong>Concordia</strong>, <strong>AG Recruitment</strong>, <strong>Fruitful Jobs</strong>, <strong>HOPS Labour Solutions</strong>, and <strong>Pro-Force</strong>. The <strong>National Farmers' Union</strong> (<strong>NFU</strong>, UK, led by President <strong>Tom Bradshaw</strong>) is the primary British farming organization.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, leading agricultural automation technologies that offer alternatives to manual labor include: (1) robotic strawberry picking by <strong>Octinion</strong>, <strong>Tortuga AgTech</strong>, and <strong>Agrobot</strong>; (2) tomato picking systems from <strong>Root AI</strong> and <strong>Tevel Aerobotics</strong>; (3) apple picking robots from <strong>Abundant Robotics</strong>, <strong>Advanced Farm Technologies</strong>, and <strong>FFRobotics</strong>; (4) asparagus harvesting by <strong>Cerescon</strong>; (5) weed control solutions from <strong>FarmWise</strong>, <strong>Carbon Robotics</strong>, and <strong>Naio Technologies</strong>; (6) autonomous tractors from <strong>John Deere</strong>, <strong>AGCO Fendt</strong>, and <strong>Monarch Tractor</strong>; and (7) vertical farming operations such as <strong>AeroFarms</strong>, <strong>Plenty</strong>, <strong>Bowery Farming</strong>, and <strong>BrightFarms</strong>. Major agricultural equipment manufacturers include <strong>John Deere</strong> (CEO <strong>John May</strong>, based in <strong>Moline, Illinois</strong>), <strong>AGCO</strong>, <strong>CNH Industrial</strong>, and <strong>Kubota</strong>. Key research and development authorities include the <strong>USDA Agricultural Research Service</strong> (<strong>ARS</strong>) and the <strong>NSF National AI Institute for Sustainable Agriculture</strong>. In conclusion, <strong>Bowman</strong>'s comparative analysis of the U.K. and U.S. agricultural landscapes suggests that global agricultural labor sourcing dynamics are being fundamentally redesigned — making <strong>H-2A</strong> visa capacity, agricultural automation investments, and alternative nearshore agricultural sourcing critical strategic priorities for supply chain managers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Robert J. Bowman</strong> of <strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> argues that the Brexit and <strong>COVID-19</strong> agricultural labor crisis in the U.K. offers critical lessons for the U.S.<br>
2. Approximately 50 to 70 percent of U.S. farm workers are estimated to be undocumented.<br>
3. The <strong>H-2A</strong> visa program issued over 380,000 visas in 2024.<br>
4. Trump 2.0 mass deportation policies pose risks to agricultural and construction sectors.<br>
5. <strong>John Deere</strong>, <strong>FFRobotics</strong>, and <strong>FarmWise</strong> are among leading agricultural automation players.</p>
