<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain Think Tank</strong> addresses a hidden dimension of the global supply chain crisis—one that few executives fully grasp: the energy supply chains fueling industrial operations worldwide remain heavily carbon-intensive, creating unprecedented risks for companies tied to <strong>ESG</strong> targets. With annual <strong>CO2</strong> emissions of 37.4 billion tons and the power sector accounting for 40% of energy-related emissions, thermal power generation presents the largest supply chain and <strong>ESG</strong>-related challenge. Developing economies remain heavily dependent on thermal power generation for energy security and economic growth. In India alone, coal-fired power plants generate more than 60-70% of electricity—supporting industries that employ millions and deliver essential services to 1.4 billion people. Similar dependencies exist across other developing nations, where thermal power remains the backbone of industrial development and poverty reduction.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, global thermal power generation is sourced from <strong>coal</strong>, <strong>natural gas</strong>, <strong>fuel oil</strong>, <strong>nuclear</strong>, <strong>biomass</strong>, and <strong>geothermal</strong> resources. The <strong>IEA</strong> (<strong>International Energy Agency</strong>) reports that in 2024, 59% of global electricity generation comes from fossil fuels, 30% from renewables (including hydro), and 9% from nuclear. <strong>China</strong> (1,530 GW coal; 318 GW gas), <strong>India</strong> (220 GW coal), <strong>USA</strong> (208 GW coal; 481 GW gas), <strong>Indonesia</strong>, <strong>South Africa</strong>, <strong>Australia</strong>, <strong>Germany</strong>, <strong>Poland</strong>, <strong>Turkey</strong>, and <strong>Vietnam</strong> are major thermal power nations. <strong>Coal India Limited</strong>, <strong>National Thermal Power Corporation</strong> (<strong>NTPC</strong>), <strong>Adani Power</strong>, <strong>Tata Power</strong>, <strong>Reliance Power</strong>, and <strong>JSW Energy</strong> are India's leading coal and thermal power companies. <strong>China Huaneng Group</strong>, <strong>China Datang</strong>, <strong>China Huadian</strong>, <strong>State Power Investment Corporation</strong>, and <strong>China Energy Investment Corporation</strong> are China's major thermal power companies. <strong>Duke Energy</strong>, <strong>Southern Company</strong>, <strong>American Electric Power</strong>, <strong>Dominion Energy</strong>, <strong>Berkshire Hathaway Energy</strong>, <strong>Vistra Corp</strong>, and <strong>NRG Energy</strong> are leading U.S. thermal power producers. <strong>RWE</strong>, <strong>EnBW</strong>, <strong>Uniper</strong>, <strong>Vattenfall</strong>, <strong>Drax Group</strong>, and <strong>EPH</strong> are Europe's major thermal power companies.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>ESG</strong> (<strong>Environmental Social Governance</strong>) and decarbonization frameworks include <strong>SBTi</strong> (<strong>Science Based Targets initiative</strong>), <strong>RE100</strong>, <strong>EP100</strong>, <strong>EV100</strong>, <strong>TCFD</strong> (<strong>Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures</strong>), <strong>CDP</strong> (<strong>Carbon Disclosure Project</strong>), <strong>GRI</strong> (<strong>Global Reporting Initiative</strong>), <strong>ISSB</strong> (<strong>International Sustainability Standards Board</strong>), <strong>SASB</strong> (<strong>Sustainability Accounting Standards Board</strong>), <strong>CSRD</strong> (<strong>Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive</strong>), <strong>EU Taxonomy</strong>, <strong>SEC Climate Disclosure Rule</strong>, and <strong>California SB 253/261</strong>. The <strong>GHG Protocol</strong> defines three emissions scopes: <strong>Scope 1</strong> (direct emissions), <strong>Scope 2</strong> (purchased electricity emissions), and <strong>Scope 3</strong> (value chain emissions). The <strong>Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism</strong> (<strong>CBAM</strong>) is the EU's border carbon adjustment mechanism applied to carbon-intensive imported products—steel, aluminum, cement, fertilizer, hydrogen, and electricity—with full implementation beginning in 2026. <strong>Power Purchase Agreement</strong> (<strong>PPA</strong>), <strong>Virtual PPA</strong>, <strong>Renewable Energy Certificates</strong> (<strong>REC</strong>), <strong>Guarantee of Origin</strong> (<strong>GO</strong>), and <strong>I-REC</strong> are key renewable energy procurement tools.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the four-pillar solution encompasses: (1) <strong>efficiency enhancement</strong>, improving thermal plant efficiency through ultra-supercritical (<strong>USC</strong>), advanced ultra-supercritical (<strong>AUSC</strong>), and combined-cycle gas turbine (<strong>CCGT</strong>) technologies; (2) <strong>carbon capture, utilization and storage</strong> (<strong>CCUS</strong>)—<strong>Northern Lights</strong> (<strong>Norway</strong>), <strong>Quest CCS</strong> (<strong>Canada</strong>), <strong>Petra Nova</strong> (<strong>USA</strong>), and <strong>Boundary Dam</strong> (<strong>Canada</strong>) are major operational <strong>CCUS</strong> projects; (3) <strong>fuel transition</strong>, phased shift from coal to natural gas, natural gas to green hydrogen, <strong>ammonia co-firing</strong>, and <strong>biomass co-firing</strong>; (4) <strong>renewable integration</strong>, balancing grids with <strong>solar</strong>, <strong>wind</strong>, <strong>hydro</strong>, <strong>geothermal</strong>, <strong>BESS</strong> (<strong>Battery Energy Storage System</strong>), <strong>pumped hydro storage</strong>, and <strong>green hydrogen</strong>. <strong>Mitsubishi Power</strong>, <strong>Siemens Energy</strong>, <strong>GE Vernova</strong>, <strong>Doosan Enerbility</strong>, <strong>Toshiba</strong>, and <strong>Hitachi Energy</strong> are leading power equipment manufacturers. <strong>Just Energy Transition Partnership</strong> (<strong>JETP</strong>) comprises financing agreements between the <strong>G7</strong> and <strong>South Africa</strong>, <strong>Indonesia</strong>, <strong>Vietnam</strong>, and <strong>Senegal</strong>. Ultimately, the thermal power paradox elevates the question of how developing economies manage the balance between energy security and decarbonization as a critical supply chain and <strong>ESG</strong> issue—with the four-pillar approach providing a pragmatic and phased transition roadmap.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. The power sector accounts for 40% of global annual 37.4 billion tons of <strong>CO2</strong> emissions.<br>
2. Coal plants in <strong>India</strong> generate 60-70% of electricity.<br>
3. Developing economies depend on thermal power for millions of jobs and a population of 1.4 billion+.<br>
4. The four-pillar solution encompasses efficiency, <strong>CCUS</strong>, fuel transition, and renewable integration.<br>
5. <strong>JETP</strong> comprises <strong>G7</strong> financing partnerships with <strong>South Africa</strong>, <strong>Indonesia</strong>, <strong>Vietnam</strong>, and <strong>Senegal</strong>.</p>
