<p style="text-align: justify">Fashion retail faces a rapidly expanding <strong>shrink</strong> problem in recent years. According to an analysis in <strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong>, inventory losses are no longer limited to in-store theft; they have become a systemic issue across the entire supply chain, spanning from production to warehouse, from store backrooms to e-commerce. Data from the <strong>National Retail Federation (NRF)</strong> reveals that shoplifting cases increased by <strong>93% during 2019–2023</strong>, with associated financial losses rising by <strong>90%</strong>. Total annual shrink losses are estimated to reach approximately <strong>$100 billion USD</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to a recent survey by <strong>Avery Dennison</strong> involving 250 retailers, more than <strong>64% of participants</strong> identified <strong>"inaccurate or incomplete shipping information"</strong> as one of the root causes of shrink. <strong>Uwe Hennig</strong>, senior executive responsible for the company's global retail segment, summarizes the issue as follows:<br>
 <strong>"If you don't know what's missing, you can't restock it, and that's a double loss."</strong><br>
</p>
<p>This approach underscores that shrink represents not just lost product but also <strong>missed sales opportunities</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to Hennig, the nature of shrink has also changed. Losses that were historically driven primarily by <strong>in-store individual theft</strong> now emerge at <strong>manufacturing locations (such as China and Bangladesh)</strong> and in the early stages of the supply chain. The statement "<strong>On the whole journey, there are a lot of opportunities for product getting lost. It happens everywhere.</strong>" demonstrates that loss is now an end-to-end problem.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>The Core Issue: Lack of Visibility</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">For fashion retailers, the fundamental problem is identified not as theft but as <strong>information gaps</strong>. Systems may indicate a product is in the store; however, it may not physically be on the shelf. The product could be:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">left forgotten in the store backroom,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">transferred to another store but not recorded,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">rapidly depleted following an intensive campaign</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">This situation becomes even more critical for <strong>omnichannel</strong> retailers, as online customers quickly turn to competing sellers when they cannot find their desired <strong>size, color, and style</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Many retailers have sought to improve inventory tracking by providing store employees with <strong>mobile devices</strong>. However, according to Hennig, manual data entry typically produces <strong>incomplete or outdated information</strong> in practice, compounding the error.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>RFID: An Old Yet Rediscovered Solution</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to Hennig, the solution rests on a technology that has long existed but was <strong>late to be adopted in retail</strong>: <strong>Radio Frequency Identification (RFID)</strong>. With roots tracing back to World War II, RFID received its first patent 40 years ago, yet its widespread adoption for inventory accuracy has accelerated in recent years.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">With traditional <strong>barcode</strong> systems, retailers typically conduct physical inventory counts once a year; this process is expensive, time-consuming, and produces results that quickly become obsolete. With RFID, brands such as H&amp;M and Adidas can perform inventory counts <strong>every two weeks or every 10 days</strong>.</p>
<p><br>
 <strong>"That means you're updating the system with real data,"</strong> says Hennig.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">RFID not only improves stock accuracy but also plays an effective role in <strong>antitheft</strong> applications. When used alongside cameras and ceiling readers:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">products removed from the store without payment,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">fraudulent return attempts,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">fitting room–checkout discrepancies</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">can be detected. Hennig notes that machine learning algorithms enable analysis of <strong>products entering the fitting room but never reaching checkout</strong>, providing valuable insights for both loss prevention and understanding customer behavior.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Cost Versus Loss?</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">Although RFID tag costs have declined over the years, they remain a significant investment. However, for retailers, the real risk lies in <strong>lost sales</strong> and <strong>customer dissatisfaction</strong>. According to Hennig, a five-cent RFID tag may seem unjustifiable for an inexpensive item; however, the cost loses its meaning if it ensures the customer finds what they are looking for.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Today, RFID adoption rates in fashion retail in the US and Europe have reached <strong>70%</strong>. Technology originally used solely for inventory management has now become a core component of innovative retail models such as <strong>shrink control</strong>, <strong>self-checkout</strong>, and <strong>checkout-free stores</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Strategic Conclusion</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">Rising shrink rates are compelling fashion retailers to place technological solutions at the center of operations. In this context, RFID is positioned not merely as a tracking tool but as a <strong>strategic infrastructure element</strong> that delivers <strong>visibility, speed, security, and data quality</strong>.</p>
<h2 style="text-align: justify"><br></h2>
<h3><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong></h3>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Shoplifting cases increased by <strong>93%</strong> between 2019–2023; annual shrink losses amount to approximately <strong>$100 billion USD</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Shrink now originates not just in stores but <strong>across the supply chain</strong>.</p></li>
