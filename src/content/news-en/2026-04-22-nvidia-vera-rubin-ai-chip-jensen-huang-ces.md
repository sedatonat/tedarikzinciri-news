<p style="text-align: justify">Nvidia CEO <strong>Jensen Huang</strong> said the chipmaker will begin shipping a new <strong>AI</strong> chip in the coming months this year — a processor that delivers more compute power than its predecessors while consuming less electricity. <strong>The New York Times</strong> reports that the chip, known as <strong>Vera Rubin</strong>, has been in development for three years and is designed to meet <strong>AI</strong> demands faster and more cost-effectively than its predecessors. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Nvidia</strong> is based in <strong>Santa Clara, California</strong> and was founded in 1993 by <strong>Jensen Huang</strong>, <strong>Chris Malachowsky</strong>, and <strong>Curtis Priem</strong>. In the <strong>GPU</strong> market, it competes against <strong>AMD</strong>, <strong>Intel</strong>, <strong>Imagination Technologies</strong>, <strong>ARM Mali</strong>, and <strong>Qualcomm Adreno</strong>. In the <strong>data center AI accelerator</strong> market, it holds over 80% share as of 2026.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Huang</strong> will make the announcement on January 5 at <strong>CES</strong>, his annual keynote conference in <strong>Las Vegas</strong>, adding another announcement about <strong>Nvidia</strong>'s work on autonomous vehicles in partnership with <strong>Mercedes-Benz</strong>. <strong>Huang</strong> said the automotive manufacturer will begin shipping cars equipped with <strong>Nvidia</strong>'s self-driving technology, which is comparable to <strong>Tesla</strong>'s <strong>Autopilot</strong>. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Nvidia DRIVE</strong>, <strong>Thor</strong>, and <strong>Hyperion</strong> platforms are used by <strong>BMW</strong>, <strong>Volvo</strong>, <strong>Polestar</strong>, <strong>Lucid</strong>, <strong>Rivian</strong>, <strong>NIO</strong>, <strong>XPeng</strong>, <strong>Li Auto</strong>, <strong>Zeekr</strong>, and <strong>JLR</strong>. <strong>Mobileye</strong> (<strong>Intel</strong> spinoff), <strong>Qualcomm Snapdragon Ride</strong>, <strong>Tesla FSD</strong>, and <strong>Nvidia DRIVE</strong> are the primary competitors in the <strong>ADAS</strong>/<strong>AD</strong> space.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Other buyers of the new <strong>Rubin</strong> chips include <strong>Microsoft</strong> and <strong>Amazon</strong>, fulfilling a promise <strong>Huang</strong> made when he first introduced the chip at the company's annual conference in <strong>San Jose, California</strong> in March 2025. <strong>The Times</strong> notes that if the new chips deliver on their promises, they could enable companies to develop <strong>AI</strong> at lower cost and begin to at least ease the rising power demands of data centers being built around the world. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Nvidia</strong>'s roadmap includes generations of <strong>Hopper</strong> (<strong>H100</strong>, <strong>H200</strong>), <strong>Blackwell</strong> (<strong>B100</strong>, <strong>B200</strong>, <strong>GB200 NVL72</strong>), <strong>Rubin</strong> (2026), <strong>Rubin Ultra</strong> (2027), and <strong>Feynman</strong> (2028). <strong>TSMC</strong>'s <strong>4N</strong>/<strong>3N</strong>/<strong>2N</strong> processes, <strong>CoWoS</strong> (<strong>Chip-on-Wafer-on-Substrate</strong>) advanced packaging, and <strong>HBM3e</strong>/<strong>HBM4</strong> (<strong>SK Hynix</strong>, <strong>Micron</strong>, <strong>Samsung</strong>) form the technical foundation of <strong>Nvidia</strong>'s supply chain.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">U.S.-based <strong>Nvidia</strong> has emerged as one of the leading providers of critical <strong>AI</strong> technology, placing its supply chain under government scrutiny. In December, President <strong>Donald Trump</strong> said the chipmaker would be permitted to sell <strong>H200</strong> <strong>AI</strong> chips to an approved short list of buyers in <strong>China</strong>, provided it turns over 25% of the revenue from such sales to the U.S. government. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>BIS</strong> (<strong>Bureau of Industry and Security</strong>) <strong>EAR</strong> (<strong>Export Administration Regulations</strong>) advanced computing export controls have restricted exports of <strong>A100</strong>, <strong>H100</strong>, <strong>H800</strong>, and <strong>A800</strong> to <strong>China</strong> since 2022. The <strong>AI Diffusion Rule</strong> divides countries into three tiers. <strong>Trump</strong>'s revenue-sharing arrangement represents a new interpretation of the U.S. export control regime. <strong>Vera Rubin</strong> is named in honor of the renowned <strong>U.S.</strong> astronomer <strong>Vera Rubin</strong>. In conclusion, <strong>Nvidia</strong>'s <strong>Rubin</strong> chip promises to shape a new era of <strong>AI</strong> compute economics.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Nvidia</strong> will begin shipping the <strong>Vera Rubin</strong> <strong>AI</strong> chip this year.<br>
2. <strong>Jensen Huang</strong> will make the announcement at <strong>CES Las Vegas</strong> on January 5.<br>
3. <strong>Mercedes-Benz</strong> autonomous vehicle partnership is an additional announcement.<br>
4. <strong>Microsoft</strong> and <strong>Amazon</strong> emerge as <strong>Rubin</strong> buyers.<br>
5. <strong>Trump</strong> permits <strong>H200</strong> China sales in exchange for 25% revenue share.</p>
