<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Kawasaki Heavy Industries Ltd.</strong> says it will be forced to raise prices on its high-end motorcycles to offset the impact of U.S. tariffs. The company's motorcycles rely on motors and other components shipped from <strong>Japan</strong> — making them subject to President <strong>Donald Trump</strong>'s import duties — according to CEO <strong>Yoshinori Kanehana</strong> in an interview with <strong>Bloomberg TV</strong> on December 1. The tariffs could also affect the company's plans to hire approximately 4,000 workers in <strong>Nebraska</strong>, he said. Passing the cost of tariffs on to customers means roughly a 17 percent price increase, <strong>Kanehana</strong> added. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Kawasaki Heavy Industries</strong>, a <strong>Tokyo, Japan</strong>-based conglomerate founded by <strong>Shozo Kawasaki</strong> in 1896, is a globally diversified industrial company. Beyond motorcycles, the <strong>Kawasaki Group</strong> operates in aerospace, maritime, rail, energy, robotics, and healthcare sectors.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Despite the potential drag on sales, <strong>Kanehana</strong> noted that the price increases will largely affect larger, more expensive models. "The wealthy will buy them," he said. <strong>Kawasaki Heavy</strong> is widely known for its popular motorcycles, but its business spans from marine frigate engines to metro rail cars. The aerospace division is a bright spot — helping boost second-quarter earnings. The company manufactures fuselages for <strong>Boeing Co.</strong>'s <strong>787</strong> and <strong>737</strong> passenger aircraft. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Kawasaki</strong>'s motorcycle division produces UTVs (Utility Task Vehicles), ATVs (All-Terrain Vehicles), Jet Skis, and rail cars for the U.S. market at its facility in <strong>Lincoln, Nebraska</strong>. <strong>Honda</strong>, <strong>Yamaha</strong>, <strong>Suzuki</strong>, and <strong>Kawasaki</strong> are <strong>Japan</strong>'s "Big Four" producers in the global motorcycle market. <strong>Harley-Davidson</strong>, <strong>BMW Motorrad</strong>, <strong>Triumph</strong>, <strong>Ducati</strong>, <strong>KTM</strong> (Pierer Mobility), <strong>Royal Enfield</strong> (Eicher Motors), <strong>Bajaj Auto</strong>, <strong>Hero MotoCorp</strong>, and <strong>TVS</strong> are other leading players in the global motorcycle market.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> tariff policy is set to cause significant disruption in global trade beginning February 2025 — with the <strong>International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA)</strong>, <strong>Section 232</strong>, and <strong>Section 301</strong> as principal legal tools. <strong>Japan</strong> initially faced a 24 percent reciprocal tariff, later reduced to 15 percent through negotiations. Auto and auto parts under <strong>Section 232</strong> face a 25 percent tariff. <strong>Toyota</strong>, <strong>Honda</strong>, <strong>Nissan</strong>, <strong>Mazda</strong>, <strong>Subaru</strong>, <strong>Mitsubishi</strong>, <strong>Suzuki</strong>, <strong>Daihatsu</strong>, <strong>Isuzu</strong>, and <strong>Hino</strong> are the principal players in <strong>Japan</strong>'s automotive exports to the <strong>U.S.</strong> <strong>Kawasaki Heavy</strong> estimates net tariff impact of 90 billion yen (approximately 580 million dollars) for the first half of the current fiscal year — reported in consolidated financial results. <strong>Mitsubishi Heavy Industries</strong>, <strong>IHI Corporation</strong>, and <strong>Kawasaki Heavy Industries</strong> are <strong>Japan</strong>'s three largest heavy industrial conglomerates — significant structural component suppliers in the <strong>Boeing</strong> and <strong>Airbus</strong> supply chain.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the global motorcycle market, with annual sales volume around 60 million units, has emerging markets — particularly <strong>India</strong>, <strong>China</strong>, <strong>Indonesia</strong>, <strong>Vietnam</strong>, <strong>Thailand</strong>, and the <strong>Philippines</strong> — as principal growth drivers. Large displacement motorcycles (600cc+) such as the <strong>Kawasaki Ninja</strong>, <strong>Kawasaki Z</strong>, <strong>Kawasaki Versys</strong>, <strong>Kawasaki Vulcan</strong>, and <strong>Kawasaki H2</strong> are primary product lines. The <strong>U.S.</strong> motorcycle market sees approximately 500,000 new unit sales annually, with <strong>Harley-Davidson</strong>, <strong>Honda</strong>, <strong>Yamaha</strong>, <strong>Kawasaki</strong>, and <strong>Indian Motorcycle</strong> (Polaris) as principal players. <strong>EV motorcycles</strong> — <strong>Zero Motorcycles</strong>, <strong>LiveWire</strong> (a Harley-Davidson spinoff), <strong>Energica</strong>, <strong>Damon</strong>, and <strong>Verge</strong> — are leading brands in the emerging electric motorcycle segment. The <strong>Boeing 787 Dreamliner</strong> features global work-sharing, with <strong>Mitsubishi Heavy Industries</strong> producing wings, <strong>Kawasaki Heavy Industries</strong> the mid-fuselage, <strong>Subaru Corporation</strong> the wing center-carry-through panel, <strong>Spirit AeroSystems</strong> Section 41 (nose), and <strong>Vought Aircraft</strong> the aft fuselage. In conclusion, <strong>Kawasaki</strong>'s 17 percent price increase signal stands as concrete evidence of the direct and visible impact of <strong>Trump</strong>'s tariffs on global product pricing.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Kawasaki</strong> is planning a 17 percent price increase to offset tariffs.<br>
2. CEO <strong>Yoshinori Kanehana</strong> disclosed this to <strong>Bloomberg TV</strong> on December 1.<br>
3. <strong>Nebraska</strong>'s 4,000 hiring plan could be affected by tariffs.<br>
4. <strong>Kawasaki</strong> also manufactures <strong>Boeing 787</strong>/<strong>737</strong> fuselages.<br>
