<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports that federal regulators in <strong>Germany</strong> have warned <strong>Amazon</strong> that it may be violating the nation's competition laws through pricing controls that limit the visibility of certain sellers' products. In a preliminary assessment released on June 2 by <strong>Germany</strong>'s <strong>Federal Cartel Office</strong> (<strong>FCO</strong>; <strong>Bundeskartellamt</strong>), the authority found that <strong>Amazon</strong>'s algorithms deprioritize sellers in search results—and exclude them from advertising—when product prices are marked as either too high or "not competitive." The <strong>FCO</strong> argues that <strong>Amazon</strong>'s pricing practices are fundamentally questionable from a competition perspective because the company competes directly with other marketplace retailers on its own platform. <strong>FCO</strong> President <strong>Andreas Mundt</strong> states: "Because <strong>Amazon</strong> competes directly with other marketplace retailers on its platform, influencing competitors' pricing—even in the form of a price cap—is fundamentally questionable from a competition perspective." In a statement to <strong>CNBC</strong>, an <strong>Amazon</strong> spokesperson strongly disagrees with the <strong>FCO</strong>'s findings, saying that changing the retailer's pricing controls would be "bad for customers and selling partners."</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Bundeskartellamt</strong> (<strong>Federal Cartel Office</strong>; <strong>FCO</strong>), based in <strong>Bonn, Germany</strong>, is <strong>Germany</strong>'s independent competition authority—investigating monopolies and competition violations across all sectors, including <strong>Big Tech</strong>, retail, automotive, and financial services. <strong>Amazon</strong>, based in <strong>Seattle, WA</strong>, with CEO <strong>Andy Jassy</strong>, operates <strong>Amazon Marketplace</strong> with 9.7 million third-party sellers globally; third-party sellers account for 62% of marketplace sales—with approximately $638 billion in 2024 net revenue. The <strong>Amazon Buy Box</strong> algorithm is driven by competitive pricing, <strong>Prime</strong> eligibility, seller performance scores, and inventory status as key factors—82% of sales go to the <strong>Buy Box</strong> winning seller. <strong>Amazon Brand Registry</strong>, <strong>Amazon Vendor Central</strong>, and <strong>Amazon Seller Central</strong> are the primary seller interfaces. <strong>Amazon Advertising</strong> offers <strong>Sponsored Products</strong>, <strong>Sponsored Brands</strong>, and <strong>Sponsored Display</strong> as primary advertising products—with 2024 advertising revenue reaching $56 billion.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Amazon</strong>'s pricing and data practices have been under regulatory scrutiny on both the <strong>EU</strong> and <strong>U.S.</strong> sides for years. In 2022, the company reached a settlement with the <strong>EU</strong> to address allegations that <strong>Amazon</strong> gave preferential treatment to its own retail business over independent sellers. A year later, 17 states, together with the <strong>U.S. Federal Trade Commission</strong> (<strong>FTC</strong>; former Chair <strong>Lina Khan</strong>; currently <strong>Andrew Ferguson</strong>), filed suit accusing <strong>Amazon</strong> of unlawfully inflating prices and blocking sellers from offering lower prices than the company's own internal retail brands. The <strong>EU Digital Markets Act</strong> (<strong>DMA</strong>) took effect in 2024—designating <strong>Amazon</strong> as a <strong>gatekeeper</strong> alongside <strong>Google</strong>, <strong>Apple</strong>, <strong>Meta</strong>, <strong>Microsoft</strong>, <strong>ByteDance</strong> (TikTok), and <strong>Booking.com</strong>—with aggressive self-preferencing, data portability, third-party access, and app store rules as key obligations. Governments worldwide are responding to power-balance issues with marketplaces and large platforms—with the <strong>UK Competition and Markets Authority</strong> (<strong>CMA</strong>), <strong>French Autorité de la concurrence</strong>, and <strong>Italian Autorità Garante della Concorrenza</strong> (<strong>AGCM</strong>) as primary parallel regulators.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Amazon</strong>'s key regulatory proceedings include: (1) 2022 <strong>EU Commission Buy Box</strong> settlement; (2) 2023 <strong>FTC v. Amazon</strong> antitrust lawsuit (ongoing); (3) 2024 <strong>Italian Competition Authority</strong> €1.13 billion fine; (4) 2024 <strong>UK CMA</strong> Marketplace investigation; (5) 2025 <strong>EU DMA</strong> compliance monitoring; (6) 2025 <strong>India CCI</strong> Flipkart-Amazon joint investigation; (7) 2024 <strong>Mexico COFECE</strong> digital marketplace study; (8) 2024 <strong>Japan JFTC</strong> supplier contract review; (9) 2024 <strong>South Korea KFTC</strong> compliance work; (10) 2024 <strong>Brazil CADE</strong> e-commerce assessment as key parallel cases. Other significant marketplace competition cases include <strong>Apple App Store</strong> (<strong>EU DMA</strong>; <strong>Epic Games v. Apple</strong>), <strong>Google Play</strong> (<strong>Epic Games v. Google</strong>; <strong>U.S. DOJ v. Google</strong>), <strong>Meta Marketplace</strong> (<strong>EU Commission</strong>), and <strong>Booking.com</strong> and <strong>Expedia</strong> (parity clauses) as major sector players. In conclusion, <strong>Bundeskartellamt</strong>'s <strong>Amazon</strong> pricing cap investigation suggests that <strong>Big Tech</strong> marketplace control and seller ecosystem governance are being fundamentally reassessed globally—multi-channel marketplace strategy appears to be a key strategic priority for supply chain managers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Notes:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Bundeskartellamt</strong> (<strong>Andreas Mundt</strong>) evaluates <strong>Amazon</strong> price cap algorithm as a violation.<br>
2. <strong>Amazon</strong> algorithms deprioritize products marked as "too high" or "not competitive" from search and advertising.<br>
3. <strong>Amazon</strong> strongly disputes the findings—saying changes would harm customers and partners.<br>
