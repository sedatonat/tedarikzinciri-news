<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Colin McAleenan</strong>, vice president of marketing and business development at <strong>HAI Robotics</strong>, notes that how supply chain leaders invest in automation has fundamentally changed over the past five years. Five years ago, <strong>VC</strong> (<strong>Venture Capital</strong>) appetite was robust and capital flowed into the supply chain automation sector. But now, in an environment where interest rates have risen and capital costs have increased, automation decisions are being made far more cautiously, met with skepticism from <strong>CFO</strong>s. <strong>McAleenan</strong> observes that careers of automation project leaders are at risk; a failed project can mean not just capital loss but personal reputation damage as well. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>HAI Robotics</strong> is a pioneer in the <strong>ACR</strong> (<strong>Autonomous Case-handling Robot</strong>) category and competes in the <strong>goods-to-person</strong> market with rivals such as <strong>Geek+</strong>, <strong>AutoStore</strong>, <strong>Exotec</strong>, <strong>Symbotic</strong>, and <strong>Locus Robotics</strong>. The <strong>HaiPick</strong> system can handle cases and totes in racks 5-10 meters high and increases <strong>cube utilization</strong> 4-5x compared to traditional racking.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>McAleenan</strong> emphasizes that supply chain leaders must move away from a single distribution center mindset and adopt a broader network, planning, and delivery perspective. An automation decision at one <strong>DC</strong> has a domino effect on <strong>upstream</strong> processes (planning, inventory, procurement) and <strong>downstream</strong> processes (delivery, <strong>last-mile</strong>, customer experience). <strong>McAleenan</strong> notes that data science and processes—including the automation supplier—require simultaneous investment. He uses the phrase <strong>"mutually assured success"</strong>; the supplier's success and the customer's success are interdependent. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>network design</strong> decisions, <strong>greenfield</strong> versus <strong>brownfield</strong> facility choices, geographic placement of <strong>DC</strong>s, capacity allocation by <strong>customer cluster</strong>, and <strong>WMS</strong>, <strong>OMS</strong>, <strong>TMS</strong> integration are critical decision parameters. <strong>Llamasoft</strong> (now <strong>Coupa</strong>), <strong>Optilogic</strong>, <strong>AnyLogic</strong>, and <strong>Kinaxis</strong> are key players in the <strong>network optimization</strong> and <strong>simulation</strong> domains.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">According to <strong>McAleenan</strong>, the pain points of automation projects are a long journey in sustaining the supplier-customer relationship. <strong>McAleenan</strong> uses the phrase <strong>"If you can see what the data is telling you right in front of you on the shop floor, chances are you've got the right inputs and the right processes"</strong>. Data visibility, real-time operator dashboards on the shop floor, <strong>andon board</strong> and <strong>visual factory management</strong> applications are concrete indicators of automation success. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>OEE</strong> (<strong>Overall Equipment Effectiveness</strong>) is the product of three dimensions—<strong>availability</strong>, <strong>performance</strong>, and <strong>quality</strong>—and world-class standard is 85% or higher. <strong>MTBF</strong> (<strong>Mean Time Between Failures</strong>) and <strong>MTTR</strong> (<strong>Mean Time To Repair</strong>) metrics directly impact the <strong>ROI</strong> of automation investment. Application of <strong>Six Sigma</strong> and <strong>lean manufacturing</strong> principles in the automated warehouse is documented within <strong>ISO 9001</strong> and <strong>ISO 13485</strong> quality management systems frameworks.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>McAleenan</strong>'s observations clearly summarize today's automation investment climate. Five years ago, in an environment of abundant capital, failed projects could easily be absorbed until the next round. Today, every dollar must pass through <strong>capex</strong> committees and <strong>investment review board</strong>s. From a supply chain perspective, as <strong>WACC</strong> (<strong>Weighted Average Cost of Capital</strong>) has risen to the 8-12% range, calculations of <strong>NPV</strong> (<strong>Net Present Value</strong>), <strong>IRR</strong> (<strong>Internal Rate of Return</strong>), and <strong>payback period</strong> must be performed more rigorously. <strong>Total Cost of Ownership</strong> (<strong>TCO</strong>) models include not just hardware cost but also <strong>integration cost</strong>, <strong>change management</strong>, <strong>training</strong>, <strong>spare parts</strong>, <strong>energy consumption</strong>, and <strong>decommissioning</strong> line items. The <strong>RaaS</strong> (<strong>Robotics-as-a-Service</strong>) model is becoming increasingly prevalent in response to demand to shift from <strong>capex</strong> to <strong>opex</strong>. In conclusion, <strong>McAleenan</strong>'s views clearly demonstrate that warehouse automation is an engineering and data investment requiring financial discipline.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>\n1. <strong>McAleenan</strong> notes that <strong>VC</strong> appetite was robust 5 years ago.<br>\n2. Data science and processes require investment alongside the automation supplier.<br>\n5.
