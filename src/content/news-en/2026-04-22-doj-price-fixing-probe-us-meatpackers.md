<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ)</strong> is opening an antitrust investigation into the <strong>U.S.</strong> meat packing industry over allegations that companies are manipulating beef prices. According to <strong>The Wall Street Journal</strong>, the <strong>DOJ</strong> investigation will focus on <strong>National Beef Inc.</strong>, <strong>Cargill Inc.</strong>, <strong>Tyson Foods</strong>, and <strong>JBS NV</strong>, examining allegations that the four companies are colluding on what they pay cattle ranchers for live cattle and then setting consumer beef prices to boost profits. <strong>U.S.</strong> cattle herds have fallen to their lowest levels since 1951, which means that under basic supply-and-demand arithmetic, beef and carcass prices should be rising. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>protein</strong> supply chain has a vertical structure comprising cow-calf operation, backgrounding, feedlot, and packing plant stages, with daily prices reported by the <strong>USDA Agricultural Marketing Service (AMS)</strong> via the 5-area daily weighted average.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">In reality, retail beef prices have climbed to record levels over the past year, yet cattle ranchers have failed to benefit fully. Together, <strong>National Beef</strong>, <strong>Cargill</strong>, <strong>Tyson Foods</strong>, and <strong>JBS NV</strong> control approximately 85% of the <strong>U.S.</strong> beef processing market and are frequently scrutinized for their alleged role in artificially inflating consumer beef prices. In January 2026, <strong>Tyson Foods</strong> and <strong>Cargill</strong> agreed to pay an $87.5 million class-action settlement related to price-fixing allegations across more than two dozen states. From a supply chain perspective, packer concentration is a historical concern regulated under the <strong>Packers and Stockyards Act of 1921</strong> and the <strong>Sherman Antitrust Act</strong>. The <strong>USDA Grain Inspection, Packers and Stockyards Administration (GIPSA)</strong> and the <strong>FTC</strong> conduct joint monitoring for <strong>HHI</strong> (<strong>Herfindahl-Hirschman Index</strong>) concentration metrics in the meat packing industry.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">President <strong>Donald Trump</strong> also opened an antitrust investigation into the four companies in 2020 during his first term, but that investigation did not result in any major enforcement action or penalties. Otherwise, <strong>U.S.</strong> cattle ranchers are facing drought conditions over the past year, higher prices for grain and feed, rising interest rates, and concerns about the resurgence of the <strong>New World screwworm</strong>, a parasitic fly reemerging for the first time in over 20 years just south of the <strong>U.S.</strong>/<strong>Mexico</strong> border. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>NWS</strong> (<strong>New World Screwworm</strong>) outbreak requires the reactivation of the sterile insect technique (SIT) program being run by the <strong>USDA APHIS</strong> and <strong>SENASICA</strong> (<strong>Mexico</strong>). The weekly capacity of 100 million sterile flies produced at the <strong>COPEG</strong> (<strong>Comisión Panamá-Estados Unidos</strong>) facility is critical to slowing the spread of the outbreak.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>JBS</strong> is of <strong>Brazilian</strong> origin, is listed on the <strong>NYSE</strong>, and controls <strong>Pilgrim's Pride</strong>. <strong>Cargill</strong> is privately held and one of the world's largest agricultural trading companies. <strong>Tyson Foods</strong> is headquartered in <strong>Arkansas</strong> and is the <strong>U.S.</strong> market leader in the chicken, beef, and pork trinity. <strong>National Beef</strong> is controlled by <strong>Marfrig Global Foods</strong>. The cattle futures market trades through live cattle and feeder cattle contracts within <strong>CME Group</strong>, and these positions are used by packers for basis hedging. <strong>Captive supply</strong> and <strong>formula pricing</strong> practices are a core subject of allegations that they reduce cash market transparency. Ultimately, the <strong>DOJ</strong> investigation brings the structural concentration problem in the <strong>U.S.</strong> protein supply chain back to the forefront and signals a harbinger of the Trump administration's second-term approach to agricultural antitrust policy.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. <strong>DOJ</strong> is opening a price-fixing antitrust investigation into <strong>National Beef</strong>, <strong>Cargill</strong>, <strong>Tyson</strong>, and <strong>JBS</strong>.<br>
2. The four companies control ~85% of the <strong>U.S.</strong> beef processing market.<br>
3. <strong>U.S.</strong> cattle herds are at their lowest level since 1951.<br>
4. <strong>Tyson</strong> and <strong>Cargill</strong> agreed to pay an $87.5 million class-action settlement in January 2026.<br>
5. <strong>New World screwworm</strong> is reemerging, creating additional pressure on cattle ranchers.</p>
