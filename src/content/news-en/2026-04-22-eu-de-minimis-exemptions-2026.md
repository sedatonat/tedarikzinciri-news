<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The European Union</strong>, seeking to manage a flood of duty-free shipments from <strong>Chinese</strong> retailers like <strong>Shein</strong> and <strong>Temu</strong>, is voting to end <strong>de minimis</strong> exemptions for low-value packages as early as 2026. The <strong>EU</strong> had initially agreed in 2023 to phase out duty-free exemptions for shipments below 150 euros ($175) by 2028. However, on November 13, <strong>EU</strong> finance ministers voted to accelerate the timeline by two years in response to repeated calls from European retailers to "avoid delay" in addressing <strong>de minimis</strong> rule concerns — <strong>European Trade Commissioner</strong> <strong>Maros Sefcovic</strong> told <strong>Reuters</strong>. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>de minimis</strong> rule permits duty-free, undeclared imports for low-value shipments globally as a customs simplification measure — set at $800 in the <strong>U.S.</strong> (<strong>Section 321</strong>), 150 euros in the <strong>EU</strong>, £135 in the <strong>UK</strong>, 20 CAD in <strong>Canada</strong>, 1,000 AUD in <strong>Australia</strong>, and 10,000 yen in <strong>Japan</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>De minimis</strong> policies have enabled <strong>Shein</strong>, <strong>Temu</strong>, and others to ship products directly from <strong>Chinese</strong> factories to consumers at prices significantly below market value globally. In 2024, the number of low-value e-commerce packages sent to the <strong>EU</strong> doubled year-over-year to 4.6 billion, with more than 90 percent originating directly from <strong>China</strong>. In a written statement, <strong>EU</strong> member <strong>Dirk Gotink</strong> said this move was necessary. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Shein Group</strong>, originally based in <strong>Nanjing, China</strong> (now <strong>Singapore</strong>-headquartered), was founded in 2008 by <strong>Chris Xu</strong> and has become the global <strong>fast fashion</strong> leader with a <strong>real-time fashion</strong> business model. <strong>Shein</strong>'s 2024 annual revenue is estimated at approximately 50 billion dollars. <strong>Temu</strong>, launched in the <strong>U.S.</strong> in September 2022 by <strong>PDD Holdings</strong> (formerly <strong>Pinduoduo</strong>), is a subsidiary of the <strong>Shanghai, China</strong>-based parent company. <strong>Temu</strong> has rapidly gained market share in the <strong>U.S.</strong> and as of 2024 is operational in 80+ countries.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.S.</strong> suspended its <strong>de minimis</strong> rule under <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> in February 2025 for packages from <strong>China</strong>/<strong>Hong Kong</strong> — then temporarily reinstated it due to operational challenges — and ultimately ended <strong>de minimis</strong> globally from all countries effective May 2, 2025. <strong>U.S. Customs and Border Protection</strong> (<strong>CBP</strong>) processes 4 million <strong>de minimis</strong> shipments daily — inspecting this volume is challenging given the risks of <strong>fentanyl</strong>, <strong>counterfeit</strong> goods, and <strong>forced labor</strong>. The <strong>European Commission</strong> submitted a customs reform proposal in 2023 — with <strong>EU Single Window Environment for Customs</strong>, <strong>EU Customs Authority</strong>, and <strong>EU Customs Data Hub</strong> as key building blocks. <strong>UFLPA</strong> (<strong>Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act</strong>), <strong>EU CSDDD</strong>, and the <strong>UK Modern Slavery Act</strong> are supply chain due diligence frameworks. <strong>Cainiao</strong> (<strong>Alibaba</strong>), <strong>JD Logistics</strong>, <strong>YunExpress</strong>, <strong>4PX</strong>, <strong>SF Express</strong>, <strong>SHEIN logistics</strong>, and <strong>Temu logistics</strong> are among the leading global logistics providers for <strong>cross-border e-commerce</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the end of <strong>de minimis</strong> will significantly alter <strong>cross-border e-commerce</strong> business models. Following the U.S. <strong>de minimis</strong> phase-out, <strong>Shein</strong> and <strong>Temu</strong> have raised prices and opened local <strong>fulfillment center</strong>s in the <strong>U.S.</strong> — with <strong>Indianapolis</strong>, <strong>Whitestown, Indiana</strong>, <strong>Marina, California</strong>, and <strong>Riverside, California</strong> serving as major <strong>Shein</strong>/<strong>Temu U.S.</strong> distribution hubs. The global <strong>fast fashion</strong> market is dominated by <strong>Inditex</strong> (<strong>Zara</strong>, <strong>Pull&amp;Bear</strong>, <strong>Massimo Dutti</strong>, <strong>Bershka</strong>), <strong>H&amp;M Group</strong> (<strong>H&amp;M</strong>, <strong>COS</strong>, <strong>&amp;Other Stories</strong>), <strong>Fast Retailing</strong> (<strong>UNIQLO</strong>, <strong>GU</strong>, <strong>Theory</strong>), <strong>Shein</strong>, <strong>Boohoo Group</strong> (<strong>Boohoo</strong>, <strong>PrettyLittleThing</strong>, <strong>Nasty Gal</strong>), <strong>ASOS</strong>, <strong>PVH</strong>, and <strong>Tapestry</strong>. <strong>H&amp;M</strong>, <strong>Inditex</strong>, <strong>Mango</strong>, <strong>Primark</strong>, and <strong>Next</strong> are major regional competitors in the <strong>EU</strong>. Postal customs flows are processed under the <strong>UPU</strong> (<strong>Universal Postal Union</strong>) framework — which is also central to terminal dues discussions. Ultimately, the <strong>EU</strong>'s vote to advance the <strong>de minimis</strong> phase-out date from 2028 to 2026 is accelerating a fundamental transformation of the cost and operational structure of global <strong>cross-border e-commerce</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. The <strong>EU</strong> voted to advance the <strong>de minimis</strong> phase-out from 2028 to 2026.<br>
2. <strong>Maros Sefcovic</strong>, Trade Commissioner, is conveying retailer pressure.<br>
3. In 2024, 4.6 billion low-value packages were shipped — over 90 percent from <strong>China</strong>.<br>
4. <strong>Shein</strong> and <strong>Temu</strong> are the primary target business models.<br>
5. The <strong>U.S.</strong> ended <strong>de minimis</strong> globally from all countries effective May 2, 2025.</p>
