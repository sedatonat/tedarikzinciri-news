<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; <strong>Descartes</strong> sponsored content; analyst insight; <strong>US Foods</strong> operates one of the largest private fleets in the country — delivering to more than 250,000 customers ranging from independent restaurants to hospitals and hotels. Now, it is leading a major initiative to modernize daily route planning across a decentralized network of 70+ distribution centers. In this session, you will hear how <strong>US Foods</strong> simplified its operations, reduced complexity, and built a smarter, more agile routing model. Learn how they replaced legacy systems, captured critical tribal knowledge, and leveraged technology to improve service reliability and drive efficiency.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>US Foods Holding Corp.</strong>, based in <strong>Rosemont, Illinois U.S.</strong>, with <strong>Dave Flitman</strong> as CEO, founded in 1989 (formerly a merger of <strong>Sara Lee</strong> and <strong>JR Simplot</strong>), is one of the largest foodservice distributors in the United States. <strong>US Foods</strong>' primary competitors in the foodservice distribution market are <strong>Sysco</strong> (<strong>Kevin Hourican</strong> CEO; <strong>Houston, Texas</strong>; market leader), <strong>Performance Food Group</strong> (<strong>PFG</strong>; <strong>George Holm</strong> CEO; <strong>Richmond, Virginia</strong>), <strong>Gordon Food Service</strong> (<strong>GFS</strong>; <strong>Rich Wolowski</strong> CEO; <strong>Wyoming, Michigan</strong>), <strong>Reinhart Foodservice</strong> (owned by <strong>PFG</strong>), <strong>Shamrock Foods</strong>, <strong>Ben E. Keith Foods</strong>, and <strong>Cheney Brothers</strong>, among the major U.S. foodservice distributors. Globally, <strong>Bidfood</strong>, <strong>Bidcorp</strong>, <strong>Brakes Group</strong> (owned by <strong>Sysco</strong>; UK), <strong>Metro AG</strong>, <strong>Aramark</strong>, <strong>Compass Group</strong>, and <strong>Sodexo</strong> are among the major global foodservice players.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Descartes Systems Group</strong>, based in <strong>Waterloo, Ontario, Canada</strong>, with <strong>Edward Ryan</strong> as CEO, founded in 1981, has <strong>Logistics Technology Platform</strong> as its primary product family — with <strong>Descartes Route Planner</strong> (formerly <strong>Roadnet</strong>), <strong>Descartes Route Optimization</strong>, and <strong>Descartes Mobile</strong> as its main product lines. Other major <strong>route optimization</strong> and <strong>fleet management</strong> providers include <strong>Trimble Transportation</strong> (formerly <strong>PeopleNet</strong> and <strong>TMW Systems</strong>), <strong>Samsara</strong> (<strong>Sanjit Biswas</strong> CEO; <strong>San Francisco</strong>), <strong>Geotab</strong> (<strong>Neil Cawse</strong> CEO; <strong>Oakville, Ontario</strong>), <strong>Verizon Connect</strong> (formerly <strong>Fleetmatics</strong> and <strong>Telogis</strong>), <strong>Omnitracs</strong> (owned by <strong>Solera</strong>), <strong>Workwave</strong>, <strong>Routific</strong>, <strong>Onfleet</strong>, <strong>Routeware</strong>, <strong>Bringg</strong>, <strong>Kuebix</strong>, <strong>Manhattan Active TM</strong>, <strong>Oracle Transportation Management</strong>, <strong>SAP Transportation Management</strong>, <strong>Blue Yonder Luminate Logistics</strong>, <strong>e2open</strong>, <strong>MercuryGate</strong>, and <strong>Alpega Group</strong>, among the key ecosystem players.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>foodservice last-mile delivery</strong> characteristics include: (1) time windows (early morning; prior to restaurant opening); (2) temperature-controlled <strong>multi-temp</strong> transportation (dry, refrigerated, frozen); (3) <strong>HACCP</strong> and <strong>FSMA</strong> compliance; (4) <strong>POD</strong> (<strong>Proof of Delivery</strong>); (5) <strong>tribal knowledge</strong> (driver-customer relationships); (6) <strong>multi-stop</strong> routing (50+ stops); (7) <strong>backhaul</strong> optimization; (8) <strong>Hours of Service</strong> (<strong>HOS</strong>) compliance; (9) <strong>ELD</strong> (<strong>Electronic Logging Device</strong>) integration; (10) vendor compliance. <strong>FMCSA</strong> (<strong>Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration</strong>) and <strong>FDA</strong> (<strong>Food and Drug Administration</strong> — <strong>FSMA</strong>) are the primary U.S. federal regulatory authorities. <strong>National Restaurant Association</strong> (<strong>NRA</strong>; <strong>Michelle Korsmo</strong> CEO) and <strong>International Foodservice Distributors Association</strong> (<strong>IFDA</strong>; <strong>Mark Allen</strong> President) are the major U.S. foodservice industry organizations. In conclusion, <strong>US Foods</strong>' route modernization initiative appears to be part of a broader global redesign of the <strong>foodservice last-mile</strong> ecosystem — with <strong>route optimization</strong>, <strong>multi-temp</strong> transportation, and digitalization of <strong>tribal knowledge</strong> emerging as key strategic priorities for supply chain managers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. <strong>US Foods</strong> operates with 250,000+ customers and 70+ distribution centers.<br>

3. <strong>Tribal knowledge</strong> capture is a core modernization objective.<br>
