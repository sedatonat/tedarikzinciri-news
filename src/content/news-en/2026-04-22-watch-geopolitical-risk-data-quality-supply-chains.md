<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> video interview report; industry expert and adviser <strong>Bart De Muynck</strong> details findings from a new survey on supply chain resilience during periods of uncertainty conducted by <strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> in partnership with <strong>Seagull Software</strong>. The survey asks supply chain managers about current geopolitical and socioeconomic disruptions. The aim is to assess the impact of tariffs—as well as to evaluate how organizations are using data to ensure better visibility of products across their supply chains, <strong>De Muynck</strong> says. Data availability and quality are major concerns. Obtaining the right intelligence at the right time is critical for preventing disruptions and forecasting demand trends, <strong>De Muynck</strong> states. "If I'm placing such a big bet on data, how good is the data? How much data do I have? Is this high-quality data?" he asks. Today's supply chains face a level of risk comparable to the COVID-19 pandemic from five years ago. But even before this, companies faced increasing difficulty with high-volume data and determining what deserves focus.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Bart De Muynck</strong> is founder and CEO of <strong>OnPoint Intelligence</strong>—based in Atlanta, Georgia—and a former VP of Research at <strong>Gartner</strong>. He is a globally recognized supply chain analytics and consulting expert. <strong>Seagull Software</strong> (formerly <strong>BarTender</strong>), based in Bellevue, Washington, with CEO <strong>Roger Lawless</strong>, is a market leader in labeling, tracking, and compliance through <strong>BarTender</strong> label design and print management software. <strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> (<strong>SCB</strong>), with Editor-in-Chief <strong>Bob Bowman</strong>, is one of the world's leading publications in supply chain news. Global uncertainty created by the <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> tariff regime has led to three main categories—(1) fast responders redesigning supply chain structures; (2) those adopting a wait-and-see stance, leaving themselves at risk; (3) those remaining undecided and reactive. <strong>De Muynck</strong> characterizes data as the "unsung hero" of supply chain management—data quality, data governance, and data standardization are key strategic issues.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, leading global supply chain visibility platform providers include <strong>FourKites</strong> (Chicago, Illinois; CEO <strong>Mathew Elenjickal</strong>); <strong>project44</strong> (Chicago, Illinois; CEO <strong>Jett McCandless</strong>); <strong>Shippeo</strong> (Paris, France); <strong>Tive</strong> (Boston, Massachusetts); <strong>Overhaul</strong> (Austin, Texas); <strong>Ten-X</strong>; <strong>Sixfold</strong> (Austrian; owned by <strong>Transporeon</strong>); <strong>Transporeon</strong> (owned by <strong>Trimble</strong>); <strong>FreightWaves SONAR</strong>; <strong>RXO Last Mile</strong>; <strong>Loadsmart</strong>; <strong>Convoy</strong>; and <strong>Uber Freight</strong>. In the control tower category, leading providers include <strong>Blue Yonder Luminate Control Tower</strong>; <strong>Kinaxis Maestro</strong> (formerly <strong>RapidResponse</strong>); <strong>o9 Solutions</strong>; <strong>Coupa Supply Chain Design</strong> (<strong>LLamasoft</strong>); <strong>Anaplan</strong>; <strong>Logility</strong>; <strong>SAP IBP</strong>; <strong>Oracle Supply Chain Planning</strong>; <strong>OMP Plus</strong>; <strong>ToolsGroup</strong>; <strong>RELEX Solutions</strong>; <strong>John Galt Solutions</strong>; and <strong>QAD Dynasys</strong>. Key modern data architecture approaches include <strong>master data management</strong> (<strong>MDM</strong>); <strong>data observability</strong>; <strong>data fabric</strong>; <strong>data mesh</strong>; and <strong>data lakehouse</strong>—with leading platform providers including <strong>Snowflake</strong>; <strong>Databricks</strong>; <strong>Google BigQuery</strong>; <strong>AWS Redshift</strong>; and <strong>Microsoft Fabric</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, data quality issues fall into main categories: (1) missing data; (2) incorrect data; (3) inconsistent data; (4) late data; (5) duplicate data; (6) inaccessible data; (7) decontextualized data. Data quality dimensions (<strong>DQ</strong>) include accuracy; completeness; consistency; timeliness; uniqueness; validity; and integrity. Standard organizations include <strong>DAMA</strong> (<strong>Data Management Association</strong>) and <strong>EDM Council</strong> (<strong>Enterprise Data Management Council</strong>). The <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> tariff regime has surfaced key data requirements: (1) deep classification accuracy at the <strong>HS Code</strong> level; (2) <strong>country of origin</strong> certification; (3) <strong>tier-N</strong> supplier mapping; (4) <strong>Bill of Materials</strong> transparency; (5) traceability for <strong>UFLPA</strong> compliance; and (6) <strong>scenario modeling</strong> and <strong>what-if</strong> analysis. The <strong>SupplyChainBrain-Seagull Software</strong> survey reveals how this structural shift is reflected among supply chain managers—with key modern capability categories including <strong>S&amp;OP</strong>; <strong>S&amp;OE</strong>; <strong>IBP</strong>; <strong>digital twin</strong>; and AI/ML-based demand forecasting. In conclusion, the data quality crisis highlighted by <strong>De Muynck</strong> has become a strategic imperative for supply chain leaders in the <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> period of uncertainty—investment in data appears to be a primary determinant of resilience.</p>
