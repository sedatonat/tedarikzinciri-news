<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports that <strong>Glenn Koepke</strong> (<strong>SCB Contributor</strong>) identifies supply chain theft at all-time high levels. Broker fraud, shipment interception, and other forms of cargo theft are costing supply chains an estimated $35 billion USD annually—according to data cited by supply chain leaders during a February Senate Subcommittee hearing by the <strong>Homeland Security Investigations</strong> agency. Making matters worse, as businesses reconfigure their supply chains in response to tariffs, they are inadvertently exposing themselves to greater risk. Trucking companies, railways, and carriers are justified in pressing selected leaders and institutions to take greater action in combating this growing industry scourge. However, a less-publicized reality deserves attention: too many chief supply chain officers (<strong>CSCOs</strong>) are unknowingly leaving their companies' doors not merely unlocked, but wide open for thieves to walk through. Supply chain theft is increasingly being perpetrated by sophisticated organized crime rings. Bad actors are tailing truckers for hundreds of miles—waiting for opportunities to pilfer entire truckloads—a form of theft that has surged 273 percent in 2024, according to new research and analysis from <strong>BSI Consulting</strong>—then holding them for ransom. Criminals are cutting air brake hoses on freight trains passing through remote regions—and escaping with roughly half a million dollars' worth of <strong>Nike</strong> shoes. They are purchasing DOT numbers and fake safety ratings on <strong>Facebook</strong>—prompting <strong>Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration</strong> (<strong>FMCSA</strong>) to take action to combat this fraud.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Glenn Koepke</strong> is General Manager and Vice President of Customer Success at <strong>FourKites</strong> (based in Chicago, Illinois, U.S.; <strong>Mathew Elenjickal</strong>, Founder/CEO)—<strong>FourKites</strong> is a global leader in real-time transportation visibility (<strong>RTTV</strong>) platforms—tracking over 1,000 customers across 200+ countries. Other major cargo visibility and theft prevention providers include <strong>project44</strong> (<strong>Jett McCandless</strong>, Founder; Chicago), <strong>Shippeo</strong> (<strong>Pierre Khoury</strong>, Founder; France), <strong>Trax Technologies</strong>, <strong>Overhaul</strong>, <strong>CargoNet</strong> (owned by <strong>Verisk Analytics</strong>), <strong>SensiGuard</strong> (owned by <strong>Sensitech</strong>; <strong>Carrier Global</strong>), <strong>SafeRack</strong>, and <strong>BSI Consulting</strong> (<strong>British Standards Institution</strong>; <strong>Susan Taylor Martin</strong>, CEO)—all key ecosystem players. The <strong>Cargo Theft Task Force</strong>, the <strong>Special Investigations Group</strong> at <strong>Travelers Insurance</strong>, and <strong>Verisk Analytics</strong>' <strong>CargoNet Threat Bulletin</strong> are primary industry intelligence platforms. <strong>Homeland Security Investigations</strong> (<strong>HSI</strong>; under <strong>U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement</strong>), the <strong>Federal Bureau of Investigation</strong> (<strong>FBI</strong>), and <strong>U.S. Customs and Border Protection</strong> (<strong>CBP</strong>) are the primary federal law enforcement agencies.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the main categories of cargo theft include: (1) strategic theft (using fraudulent carrier identity), (2) fictitious pickup, (3) identity theft, (4) double brokering scam, (5) cyber-enabled theft, (6) direct hijacking, (7) warehouse burglary, (8) pilferage, (9) insider theft, and (10) cyber-physical attack. <strong>BSI Consulting</strong>'s 2024 report identifies California, Texas, Florida, Georgia, Illinois, New Jersey, Tennessee, and Pennsylvania as the highest-theft regions in the United States. The most-targeted product categories are: (1) electronics (smartphones, laptops), (2) food and beverages, (3) personal care products, (4) household goods, (5) automotive parts, (6) health/pharmaceutical products, (7) apparel and footwear, and (8) jewelry and precious metals. The <strong>Cargo Defense Council</strong>, <strong>Transported Asset Protection Association</strong> (<strong>TAPA</strong>; <strong>Thorsten Neumann</strong>, CEO), and <strong>National Cargo Security Council</strong> (<strong>NCSC</strong>) are primary industry organizations.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, leading theft-prevention technologies include: (1) GPS tracking and geofencing, (2) tamper-evident seal, (3) biometric driver authentication, (4) video surveillance, (5) RFID and IoT sensors, (6) blockchain chain-of-custody, (7) predictive analytics and AI anomaly detection, (8) secure parking program, (9) convoy escort, and (10) cyber-vetted broker network. The <strong>Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration</strong> (<strong>FMCSA</strong>), with <strong>Robin Hutcheson</strong> as Acting Administrator, is a sub-unit of the <strong>Department of Transportation</strong> (<strong>DOT</strong>)—providing DOT number registration and operator safety score management. <strong>Operation Boiling Point</strong> is a national investigative operation by <strong>HSI</strong> targeting cargo theft rings. The <strong>National Insurance Crime Bureau</strong> (<strong>NICB</strong>; <strong>David Glawe</strong>, CEO) is the primary insurance industry intelligence organization. In conclusion, <strong>Koepke</strong>'s warning to <strong>CSCOs</strong> suggests that supply chain security, broker verification, and cargo visibility are being fundamentally redesigned globally—and the integration of RTTV with organized crime intelligence appears to be the key strategic priority for supply chain managers.</p>
