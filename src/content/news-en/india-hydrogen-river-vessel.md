<p style="text-align: justify">India has reached a significant milestone in its pursuit of <strong>zero-emission transport</strong> on inland waterways. The country's <strong>first fully indigenous hydrogen fuel cell passenger vessel</strong> has been commissioned for commercial service on the <strong>Ganges River</strong> in <strong>Varanasi</strong>. According to an announcement by <strong>pib.gov.in</strong>, the vessel is owned by the <strong>Inland Waterways Authority of India (IWAI)</strong> and was constructed by <strong>Cochin Shipyard Limited (CSL)</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The vessel was formally commissioned at a ceremony held at <strong>Namo Ghat</strong> by <strong>Union Minister for Ports, Shipping and Waterways Sarbananda Sonowal</strong>. On its inaugural voyage, accompanied by ministers, government officials, and local representatives, the vessel traversed a route of approximately <strong>five kilometers</strong> between <strong>Namo Ghat and Lalita Ghat</strong>. With this service launch, <strong>Varanasi</strong> has become the first city in India to host <strong>hydrogen-powered passenger transport</strong> on a river.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Technical Specifications and Design</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">The commissioned vessel has been designed as a <strong>24-meter-long</strong> <strong>catamaran</strong> and is certified by the <strong>Indian Register of Shipping</strong>. With a capacity of approximately <strong>50 passengers</strong> and an air-conditioned cabin, the vessel operates at a service speed of approximately <strong>6.5 knots</strong>, suited for inland waterway conditions.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The propulsion system is based on <strong>low-temperature proton exchange membrane (PEM) hydrogen fuel cell</strong> technology. This system is supported by <strong>battery packs</strong> and <strong>solar panels integrated into the roof</strong>. Thanks to this hybrid configuration, the vessel can operate continuously for approximately <strong>eight hours</strong> on a single hydrogen refueling.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Officials note that the hydrogen fuel cell system produces <strong>significantly lower noise and vibration compared to diesel vessels</strong>, thereby improving both passenger comfort and reducing environmental impacts on the river ecosystem.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Hydrogen Infrastructure: A Pilot Model</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">A notable aspect of the project is the simultaneous commissioning of <strong>hydrogen refueling infrastructure</strong>. A <strong>hydrogen refueling facility</strong> was specifically established at the multimodal terminal in <strong>Ramnagar, Ralhupur</strong> for the vessel. This facility is considered a pilot implementation for <strong>hydrogen bunkering networks</strong> planned to be established across India's inland waterways in the future.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to officials, this infrastructure presents a scalable model capable of supporting not only a single vessel but also a <strong>broader hydrogen-powered fleet</strong> that may be commissioned in the coming years.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Indigenous Technology and R&amp;D Dimension</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">The hydrogen fuel cell system used in the vessel is based on technology developed by <strong>KPIT Technologies</strong> in collaboration with <strong>CSIR (Council of Scientific &amp; Industrial Research)</strong> laboratories. This work was undertaken under the <strong>New Millennium Indian Technology Leadership Initiative</strong>. The developed technology was adapted for maritime applications through collaboration between <strong>CSL</strong> and <strong>IWAI</strong> and made ready for commercial deployment.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In this respect, the project represents more than a transport investment; it is a strategic step that combines India's objectives in <strong>indigenous technology development</strong>, <strong>energy transition</strong>, and <strong>maritime innovation</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Inland Waterways and Sustainability Strategy</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>Inland Waterways Authority of India (IWAI)</strong>, established in 1986, is a statutory authority responsible for the development and operation of India's national inland waterways. In recent years, IWAI has been undertaking comprehensive projects to make inland waterways more efficient for both <strong>freight and passenger transport</strong> and to <strong>reduce road transport dependency</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This hydrogen fuel cell passenger vessel aligns directly with India's <strong>National Green Hydrogen Mission</strong> and <strong>decarbonisation</strong> objectives. The widespread adoption of zero-emission solutions on inland waterways is critical, particularly in densely populated and historically significant cities, for <strong>reducing air pollution</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>General Assessment</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">The first indigenous hydrogen fuel cell passenger vessel commissioned on the Ganges River marks the beginning of a <strong>clean energy-based transformation</strong> in India's inland waterways. This project, which has emerged from the convergence of indigenous engineering, public-private collaboration, and infrastructure investment, demonstrates that India is positioning itself not merely as a user but as a <strong>technology developer</strong>. If successful, similar hydrogen-powered vessels are expected to be deployed on other rivers across the country.</p>
<p><br></p>
