<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Russia</strong> continues to import tires produced by <strong>French</strong> company <strong>Michelin</strong> despite sanctions on the sale of civilian and aviation tires to the country. <strong>Guardian</strong> reports that the trade is being conducted through intermediary companies, including one based in the <strong>U.K.</strong> <strong>Guardian</strong> reports that <strong>Michelin</strong> tire shipments valued at 28 million dollars were being acquired in 2023 as of September 2024 — despite the company halting all exports to <strong>Russia</strong> since March 2022 following the full-scale invasion of <strong>Ukraine</strong>. Now, analysis of records shows that significant quantities continue to pass through despite efforts being made to adapt systems to track the diversion of <strong>Michelin</strong> products. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Michelin</strong>, originating from <strong>Clermont-Ferrand, France</strong>, and founded in 1889 by <strong>Édouard Michelin</strong> and <strong>André Michelin</strong>, is one of the world's largest tire manufacturers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to records analyzed by <strong>Ukraine's Economic Security Council of Ukraine</strong> (<strong>ESCU</strong>), between October 2024 and March 2025, <strong>Russia</strong> acquired 2,687 <strong>Michelin</strong> tires valued at more than 7 million dollars. Analysis of the full year 2024 suggests that sales are being facilitated by intermediaries. From a supply chain perspective, the global tire market is dominated by <strong>Michelin</strong>, <strong>Bridgestone</strong> (<strong>Japan</strong>), <strong>Goodyear</strong> (<strong>U.S.</strong>), <strong>Continental</strong> (<strong>Germany</strong>), <strong>Pirelli</strong> (<strong>Italy</strong>; <strong>ChemChina</strong>), <strong>Sumitomo Rubber</strong> (<strong>Japan</strong>), <strong>Hankook</strong> (<strong>Korea</strong>), <strong>Yokohama</strong> (<strong>Japan</strong>), <strong>Maxxis</strong> (<strong>Taiwan</strong>), <strong>Apollo Tyres</strong> (<strong>India</strong>), <strong>Cooper Tire</strong> (<strong>Goodyear</strong>), <strong>Toyo Tire</strong> (<strong>Japan</strong>), <strong>Triangle Group</strong>, and <strong>ZC Rubber</strong> (<strong>China</strong>). In <strong>Russia</strong>, <strong>Nizhnekamskshina</strong>, <strong>Yaroslavl Tire Plant</strong>, and <strong>Cordiant</strong> are local tire manufacturers — but fall short in premium quality and aviation-grade standards.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>EU sanctions</strong> regime, under <strong>Council Regulation (EU) 833/2014</strong> and subsequent regulations targeting <strong>Russia</strong>, prohibits exports including luxury goods, advanced technology, dual-use products, and civilian/aviation tires. The <strong>U.K.</strong>, <strong>U.S.</strong> (<strong>OFAC</strong>), <strong>Canada</strong>, <strong>Australia</strong>, <strong>Japan</strong>, and <strong>Switzerland</strong> apply similar regimes. Circumvention and sanctions evasion employ principal methods including third-country transshipment (<strong>Turkey</strong>, <strong>UAE</strong>, <strong>Kazakhstan</strong>, <strong>Armenia</strong>, <strong>Georgia</strong>, <strong>Kyrgyzstan</strong>, <strong>China</strong>), shell company networks, front companies, <strong>BVI</strong>, <strong>Cyprus</strong>, <strong>Marshall Islands</strong> offshore structures, <strong>OFAC SDN List</strong>, <strong>EU Consolidated Sanctions List</strong>, <strong>UK OFSI</strong>, and compliance screening databases. <strong>Refinitiv World-Check</strong>, <strong>Dow Jones Risk &amp; Compliance</strong>, <strong>LexisNexis</strong>, <strong>Sayari</strong>, <strong>Kharon</strong>, and <strong>Descartes Visual Compliance</strong> are the sector's leading screening tools.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, parallel import has been formally legitimized by the <strong>Russian</strong> government since May 2022 — permitting imports without the trademark owner's approval. The <strong>Russian Federal Customs Service</strong> includes automotive spare parts, electronics, luxury goods, and tires in comprehensive lists under this mechanism. <strong>Michelin</strong> is testing tools such as blockchain-based tracking, VIN-based sales restrictions, and distributor contract clauses to sever <strong>Russia's</strong> distribution channels for <strong>Cromax</strong>/<strong>Davanti</strong>/premium brands. Aviation tires must be certified by <strong>FAA</strong>/<strong>EASA</strong>/<strong>Rosaviatsiya</strong>. Maintenance of <strong>Sukhoi Superjet</strong>, <strong>Aeroflot</strong>, <strong>S7</strong>, and <strong>Ural Airlines</strong> fleets are severely impacted by spare parts sanctions. Cannibalization is distinctly observed in <strong>Russian</strong> aviation post-2022. Consequently, the continued flow of <strong>Michelin</strong> tire shipments to <strong>Russia</strong> clearly demonstrates that the effectiveness of sanctions regimes is deeply linked to supply chain visibility.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Russia</strong> acquires <strong>Michelin</strong> tires through intermediary companies.<br>
2. <strong>Michelin</strong> halted all exports in March 2022.<br>
3. 2023: 28 million dollars; October 2024-March 2025: 2,687 tires valued at $7M+.<br>
4. <strong>ESCU</strong> records expose intermediary chain including a <strong>U.K.</strong> intermediary.<br>
5. <strong>Michelin</strong> is adapting tracking systems but products continue to flow through.</p>
