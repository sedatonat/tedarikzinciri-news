<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Tesla</strong>'s <strong>China</strong> factory shipments continued to decline in October — adding weight to expectations that <strong>Elon Musk</strong>-led automaker faces a challenging final quarter for global sales. The company shipped 61,497 vehicles from its <strong>Shanghai</strong> facility in October — a decline of approximately 10% year-over-year according to preliminary data from <strong>China</strong>'s <strong>Passenger Car Association</strong> (<strong>CPCA</strong>). Wholesale shipments from the facility have fallen in eight of the ten months of this year. <strong>Tesla</strong>'s momentum appears to be weakening globally following a record vehicle delivery quarter. The <strong>China</strong> figures follow a period in which declines have been reported across various <strong>European</strong> countries. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Tesla Inc.</strong> — based in <strong>Austin Texas</strong> (formerly <strong>Palo Alto California</strong>) — was founded in 2003 by <strong>Martin Eberhard</strong> and <strong>Marc Tarpenning</strong>, with <strong>Elon Musk</strong> investing in 2004 and subsequently becoming <strong>CEO</strong>. <strong>Gigafactory Shanghai</strong> opened in 2019 and is <strong>Tesla</strong>'s highest-volume production facility globally, with annual capacity of 950,000+ vehicles. The photograph by <strong>David Paul Morris</strong> was taken at a <strong>Tesla</strong> store in <strong>Colma California</strong> for <strong>Bloomberg</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Tesla</strong>'s <strong>China</strong> troubles are a direct consequence of intensifying local <strong>EV</strong> competition. <strong>BYD</strong> (<strong>Build Your Dreams</strong>; <strong>Shenzhen</strong>) is the world's largest <strong>EV</strong> manufacturer, selling 4.27 million vehicles (<strong>BEV</strong>+<strong>PHEV</strong>) in 2024. <strong>Xiaomi</strong> has entered the market with the <strong>SU7</strong> sedan and <strong>YU7</strong> SUV, delivering 30,000+ units monthly under <strong>Lei Jun</strong>'s leadership. <strong>Nio</strong>, <strong>Li Auto</strong>, <strong>Xpeng</strong> (<strong>XPENG</strong>), <strong>Zeekr</strong> (<strong>Geely</strong>), <strong>Leapmotor</strong> (<strong>Stellantis</strong>), <strong>Avatr</strong>, <strong>IM Motors</strong>, <strong>Aito</strong> (<strong>Huawei</strong>+<strong>Seres</strong>), <strong>Luxeed</strong> (<strong>Huawei</strong>), <strong>Stelato</strong> (<strong>Huawei</strong>+<strong>BAIC</strong>), <strong>Maextro</strong> (<strong>Huawei</strong>+<strong>JAC</strong>), <strong>Onvo</strong> (<strong>Nio</strong>), and <strong>Firefly</strong> (<strong>Nio</strong>) are <strong>China</strong>'s main <strong>EV</strong> players. The <strong>Tesla Model Y</strong> and <strong>Model 3</strong> have long been the best-selling <strong>EV</strong>s in <strong>China</strong>, but local competitors are rapidly gaining market share. <strong>FSD</strong> (<strong>Full Self-Driving</strong>) is launching with a limited version in <strong>China</strong> in 2025 with <strong>Baidu</strong> map data integration.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Tesla</strong>'s global production network includes major facilities in <strong>Fremont California</strong>, <strong>Austin Texas</strong> (<strong>Gigafactory Texas</strong>), <strong>Reno Nevada</strong> (<strong>Gigafactory Nevada</strong>; batteries and drivetrain), <strong>Buffalo New York</strong> (<strong>Gigafactory New York</strong>; solar), <strong>Berlin-Brandenburg Germany</strong> (<strong>Gigafactory Berlin</strong>), and <strong>Shanghai China</strong> (<strong>Gigafactory Shanghai</strong>). <strong>Gigafactory Mexico</strong> (<strong>Monterrey Nuevo León</strong>) was announced in 2023 but is being delayed due to <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> tariffs. <strong>Cybertruck</strong>, <strong>Model S</strong>, <strong>Model X</strong>, <strong>Model 3</strong>, <strong>Model Y</strong>, <strong>Roadster</strong> (delayed), <strong>Semi</strong>, <strong>Robotaxi/Cybercab</strong> (2026 launch), and <strong>Robovan</strong> constitute the core product lineup. The <strong>Optimus</strong> humanoid robot was introduced at <strong>Tesla AI Day</strong> and <strong>We Robot</strong> in 2024, with V3 prototypes in production in 2025. The 4680 cell, 2170 cell, and <strong>LFP</strong> (<strong>Lithium Iron Phosphate</strong>; <strong>Standard Range</strong> supplied by <strong>CATL</strong>) are battery technologies in use. <strong>Panasonic</strong>, <strong>LG Energy Solution</strong>, <strong>CATL</strong>, and <strong>BYD</strong> are <strong>Tesla</strong>'s primary cell suppliers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the global <strong>EV</strong> market is projected to reach approximately 22 million vehicle sales in 2025 — representing over 25% of global vehicle sales. <strong>BEV</strong> (<strong>Battery Electric Vehicle</strong>), <strong>PHEV</strong> (<strong>Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicle</strong>), <strong>HEV</strong> (<strong>Hybrid Electric Vehicle</strong>), <strong>EREV</strong> (<strong>Extended Range Electric Vehicle</strong>), and <strong>FCEV</strong> (<strong>Fuel Cell Electric Vehicle</strong>) are the main electric vehicle categories. <strong>China</strong> leads the world in <strong>EV</strong> penetration with over 50% <strong>NEV</strong> (<strong>New Energy Vehicle</strong>) penetration. <strong>Europe</strong> is rolling back <strong>EV</strong> incentives with many countries seeing market slowdowns. The <strong>U.S.</strong> is significantly curtailing <strong>IRA Section 30D</strong> EV tax credits under <strong>Trump 2.0</strong>. <strong>Norway</strong> has the world's highest <strong>EV</strong> penetration at over 95%. <strong>EV</strong> charging infrastructure standards include <strong>NACS</strong> (<strong>North American Charging Standard</strong>; <strong>Tesla</strong>'s standard, adopted by <strong>Ford</strong>/<strong>GM</strong>/<strong>Hyundai</strong>/<strong>Honda</strong>/<strong>Nissan</strong>), <strong>CCS</strong> (<strong>Combined Charging System</strong>), <strong>CHAdeMO</strong> (<strong>Japan</strong>; declining), and <strong>GB/T</strong> (<strong>China</strong>). The <strong>Supercharger</strong> network gives <strong>Tesla</strong> the world's largest charging network with over 65,000 stalls globally. In conclusion, <strong>Tesla</strong>'s <strong>China</strong> production decline is a clear indicator of structural pressure on <strong>Tesla</strong>'s global market share from intensifying local competition, led by <strong>BYD</strong> and <strong>Xiaomi</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Tesla Shanghai</strong> shipped 61,497 vehicles in October — -10% YoY.<br>
2. Shipments have declined in 8 of 10 months.<br>
3. <strong>Elon Musk</strong> faces a challenging Q4 for global sales.<br>
4. <strong>Europe</strong> is showing a parallel downtrend.<br>
5. <strong>BYD</strong>, <strong>Xiaomi</strong>, and other <strong>Chinese</strong> players are capturing market share.</p>
