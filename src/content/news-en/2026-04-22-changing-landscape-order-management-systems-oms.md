<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; sponsored by <strong>Infios</strong>; analyst insight; an order management system (<strong>OMS</strong>) is a powerful technology platform that coordinates every stage of the purchase journey—from inventory visibility and order processing to fulfillment, payment, and customer notification. Sitting between sales channels and execution tools such as <strong>WMS</strong>, <strong>TMS</strong>, and <strong>ERP</strong>—an <strong>OMS</strong> acts as a command center, orchestrating data and activity to enable seamless fulfillment. With a consolidated view of inventory, order activity, and customer interactions, an <strong>OMS</strong> plays a critical role in optimizing costs, enhancing operational efficiency, and delivering consistent customer experiences. Today's consumers expect fast delivery, real-time updates, and flexible fulfillment. While Tier 1 retailers typically leverage large <strong>ERP</strong> systems, Tier 2 companies still need affordable, nimble solutions that deliver premium customer experiences. The future of an <strong>OMS</strong> lies in modular systems—delivering tailored capabilities without requiring full-scale deployment. This approach is ideal for businesses.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Infios</strong> is the new branding of <strong>Körber Supply Chain Software</strong> as of late 2024—established by <strong>Körber AG</strong> (<strong>Hamburg, Germany</strong>), with core service lines including <strong>WMS</strong>, <strong>OMS</strong>, <strong>TMS</strong>, <strong>YMS</strong>, and voice-directed picking. The CEO of <strong>Infios</strong> is <strong>Chad Collins</strong>. Other leading <strong>OMS</strong> and order orchestration providers include <strong>Manhattan Active Omni</strong> (<strong>Eddie Capel</strong>, CEO; <strong>Atlanta</strong>); <strong>IBM Sterling Order Management</strong> (<strong>Arvind Krishna</strong>, CEO); <strong>Oracle Retail Order Management</strong>; <strong>SAP Subscription</strong> and <strong>SAP Commerce Cloud Order Management</strong>; <strong>Salesforce Commerce Cloud Order Management</strong>; <strong>Microsoft Dynamics 365 Commerce</strong>; <strong>Kibo Commerce</strong>; <strong>fluentcommerce</strong> (<strong>Graham Jackson</strong>, CEO; <strong>London</strong>); <strong>Clódura</strong>; <strong>Deck Commerce</strong>; <strong>Mirakl</strong>; <strong>VTEX</strong>; <strong>Shopify Plus</strong>; <strong>BigCommerce</strong>; <strong>NetSuite Order Management</strong> (owned by <strong>Oracle</strong>)—these are key ecosystem players. <strong>Aptos</strong>, <strong>Radial</strong>, <strong>Cymbio</strong>, and <strong>Newstore</strong> are leading niche <strong>OMS</strong> and omnichannel platforms.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, core <strong>OMS</strong> capabilities include: (1) distributed order management (<strong>DOM</strong>); (2) real-time inventory visibility; (3) order routing and sourcing optimization; (4) buy online pick up in store (<strong>BOPIS</strong>); (5) ship from store; (6) endless aisle; (7) save the sale; (8) backorder and preorder management; (9) returns management; (10) customer communication orchestration. Omnichannel order fulfillment strategies include: (1) store fulfillment; (2) microfulfillment center (<strong>MFC</strong>); (3) dark store; (4) distribution center (<strong>DC</strong>); (5) dropship; (6) 3PL fulfillment; (7) vendor managed inventory (<strong>VMI</strong>). Key modern <strong>OMS</strong> architectural paradigms include composable commerce, headless commerce, microservices architecture, API-first, and cloud-native.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, major retailers deploying <strong>OMS</strong> include <strong>Walmart</strong> (proprietary <strong>OMS</strong>); <strong>Target</strong> (<strong>Manhattan</strong>); <strong>Macy's</strong> (<strong>IBM Sterling</strong>); <strong>Nordstrom</strong>; <strong>Best Buy</strong>; <strong>The Home Depot</strong>; <strong>Lowe's</strong>; <strong>Kohl's</strong>; <strong>Dick's Sporting Goods</strong>; <strong>Kroger</strong> (partnered with <strong>Ocado</strong>); <strong>Albertsons</strong>; <strong>Costco</strong>; <strong>Inditex</strong> (<strong>Zara</strong>); <strong>H&amp;M</strong>; <strong>Uniqlo</strong>; <strong>Adidas</strong>; <strong>Nike</strong> (<strong>Salesforce</strong>). The <strong>MACH Alliance</strong> (<strong>Microservices</strong>, <strong>API-first</strong>, <strong>Cloud-native</strong>, <strong>Headless</strong>) represents the leading modern e-commerce architecture standard. Key industry organizations include the <strong>NRF</strong> (<strong>National Retail Federation</strong>; CEO <strong>Matt Shay</strong>), <strong>Shop.org</strong>, and <strong>RetailX</strong>. In conclusion, <strong>Infios</strong>'s assessment of the <strong>OMS</strong> landscape suggests that globally, omnichannel order orchestration is being fundamentally redesigned—with modular <strong>OMS</strong> deployment and composable commerce emerging as key strategic priorities for supply chain managers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Infios</strong> <strong>OMS</strong> acts as a command center between sales channels and <strong>WMS</strong>/<strong>TMS</strong>/<strong>ERP</strong>.<br>
2. Modular <strong>OMS</strong> represents a key future direction, particularly for Tier 2 companies.<br>
3. <strong>Infios</strong> is the rebranding of legacy <strong>Körber Supply Chain Software</strong> as of 2024.<br>
4. <strong>BOPIS</strong>, ship from store, and endless aisle are key omnichannel use cases.<br>
5. Composable commerce, headless, and API-first are leading modern architecture trends.</p>
