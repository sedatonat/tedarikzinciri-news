<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>European Union</strong> and <strong>India</strong> have agreed to a monumental free trade deal covering almost all goods, marking one of the most expansive trade agreements either side has signed in years and signaling a major step toward deepening economic ties between the two economies. Described by European Commission President <strong>Ursula von der Leyen</strong> as &ldquo;the mother of all deals,&rdquo; the agreement will eliminate tariffs on more than 90 percent of goods traded between the <strong>EU</strong> and <strong>India</strong>, including chemicals, cosmetics, plastics, auto parts, textiles, ceramics, machinery and boats.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The deal will also remove duties on a handful of key European agri-food exports, ranging from olive oil and fruit juices to pastries and pet food. &ldquo;This is a tale of two giants &mdash; the world&rsquo;s second- and fourth-largest economies,&rdquo; <strong>von der Leyen</strong> said at a joint press conference with Indian Prime Minister <strong>Narendra Modi</strong> on January 26. &ldquo;Two giants who choose partnership, in a true win-win fashion.&rdquo;</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The pact also includes a framework agreement for defense and security cooperation between India and the EU, while improving mobility for students and researchers as well as seasonal and skilled workers. The agreement will next undergo legal review and ratification, including approval by the European Parliament and India&rsquo;s cabinet, with the exact timeline depending on whether it requires additional approval from EU member states.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, EU officials framed the agreement as a strategic necessity to counterbalance growing geopolitical and supply chain volatility, particularly as <strong>Trump</strong> administration tariffs have rattled economies and strained trade relationships with key partners across the globe. The deal grants Indian giants such as <strong>Tata</strong>, <strong>Reliance</strong>, <strong>Mahindra</strong>, <strong>Wipro</strong> and <strong>Infosys</strong> expanded European market access, while opening a more competitive gateway to India&rsquo;s 1.4 billion consumers for European players including <strong>Volkswagen</strong>, <strong>Stellantis</strong>, <strong>Mercedes-Benz</strong>, <strong>L&rsquo;Oréal</strong> and <strong>BASF</strong>. It places <strong>India</strong> firmly at the center of <strong>China+1</strong> diversification strategies &mdash; with European-bound container traffic through major ports such as <strong>Mumbai</strong>, <strong>Chennai</strong>, <strong>Mundra</strong> and <strong>JNPT</strong> expected to rise sharply over the 2026&ndash;2030 period.</p>
