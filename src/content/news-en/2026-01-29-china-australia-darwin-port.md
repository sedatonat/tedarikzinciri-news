<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Beijing</strong> is monitoring <strong>Canberra</strong>'s handling of <strong>Darwin</strong> port's status, which is leased to a <strong>Chinese</strong> firm and which the <strong>Labor</strong> government has committed to returning to local control, "very closely" and will take action if necessary, <strong>China</strong>'s ambassador to <strong>Australia</strong> said. Speaking to reporters in <strong>Canberra</strong> on January 28, Ambassador <strong>Xiao Qian</strong> emphasized that if the port is repurchased through forced sale, <strong>Beijing</strong> has an "obligation to protect the legitimate rights of Chinese companies overseas." The remarks coincide with a period of renewed hardening in <strong>Asia-Pacific</strong> port diplomacy, with <strong>Australia</strong>'s strategic port assets re-emerging as a point of contention in a climate where overall trade relations between the two countries are being repaired.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Darwin</strong> port was allocated to <strong>Chinese</strong> company <strong>Landbridge Group</strong> in 2015 under a 99-year lease term; however, Prime Minister <strong>Anthony Albanese</strong> explicitly committed ahead of the previous election to reclaiming control of the strategic asset. Since the <strong>Albanese</strong> government took office in 2022, relations between the two countries have improved markedly; nevertheless, the <strong>Darwin</strong> port lease dispute remains an enduring line of tension awaiting resolution. <strong>Landbridge</strong>'s position at the port in <strong>Northern Territory</strong> carries special weight both commercially and militarily. From a supply chain perspective, this long-term lease agreement has prompted a rethink of the fine line between global-scale port management and national security.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The port is <strong>Australia</strong>'s northernmost maritime facility and is positioned just south of <strong>Indonesia</strong> and generally <strong>Southeast Asia</strong>. It is also located near military facilities hosting <strong>U.S. Marines</strong> rotations deployed to <strong>Northern Territory</strong>; this situation raised concerns among many parties, including then-<strong>U.S.</strong> President <strong>Barack Obama</strong>, when the port lease was announced in 2015. Since <strong>Albanese</strong> committed to returning the port to <strong>Australian</strong> control, public interest has increased, particularly following the May election victory; however, no visible progress has been made on the port's status. If <strong>Beijing</strong> determines that unfair treatment of <strong>Chinese</strong> firms is at play, a forced sale is expected to trigger significant pushback.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Ambassador <strong>Xiao Qian</strong> stated in the press conference that "We'll see when it's time for us to say something, do something, to reflect the Chinese government's position" and while emphasizing <strong>Beijing</strong>'s resolve, added that it expects the matter to be handled in a "properly managed" manner. <strong>China</strong> is <strong>Australia</strong>'s largest trade partner; in this context, <strong>Beijing</strong> has previously expressed clear discomfort with the pressure the <strong>Trump</strong> administration has been applying over ports controlled by a <strong>Hong Kong</strong>-based conglomerate near the <strong>Panama Canal</strong>. Discussions regarding the sale of ports in <strong>Panama</strong> remain ongoing; this parallel case demonstrates that <strong>Darwin</strong> is no longer merely a bilateral port dispute and has become part of a broader framework in which global strategic port portfolios are being restructured.</p>
<p><br></p>

4. The port is located near facilities hosting <strong>U.S. Marines</strong> rotations.<br>
