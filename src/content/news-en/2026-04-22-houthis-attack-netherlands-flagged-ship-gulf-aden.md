<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Houthi</strong> rocket strikes have ignited a fire aboard a <strong>Netherlands</strong>-flagged cargo vessel in the <strong>Gulf of Aden</strong> — with the insurgent group continuing to escalate attacks against commercial shipping transiting the region. The <strong>Houthis</strong> struck the cargo ship <strong>Minervagracht</strong> on September 29 — with no casualties reported. The attack comes a week after a failed strike on the same vessel, according to <strong>French</strong> military officials who spoke to the <strong>Associated Press</strong>. The ship is operated by <strong>Amsterdam</strong>-based carrier <strong>Spliethoff</strong> — which has declined to comment to the <strong>AP</strong> on why its vessels have been targeted twice by the insurgent group. While the <strong>Houthis</strong> provided no official justification for the attack, the strike suggests the vessel appears on a list of <strong>Israeli</strong>-linked targets. Late last month, the <strong>Houthis</strong> vowed to expand their attacks against commercial vessels in the <strong>Red Sea</strong> to include any ship operating at <strong>Israeli</strong> ports, regardless of nationality. Weeks before that announcement, the group attacked two <strong>Greek</strong>-owned vessels — killing four crew members and capturing 11 others in the second strike. Prior to that, as part of a ceasefire that has since collapsed following escalation of <strong>Israel</strong>'s war in <strong>Gaza</strong>, the group had not attacked a commercial vessel in the <strong>Red Sea</strong> since December 2024. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Spliethoff Group</strong> is <strong>Amsterdam</strong>, <strong>Netherlands</strong>-based, founded in 1921 — and ranks among global leaders in specialized project cargo, multipurpose and heavy-lift shipping — operating a fleet of 100+ vessels. <strong>Spliethoff</strong> operates major brands including <strong>BigLift Shipping</strong>, <strong>Sevenstar Yacht Transport</strong>, <strong>Wijnne Barends</strong>, <strong>Bore</strong>, and <strong>Transfennica</strong>. The <strong>Minervagracht</strong> is part of <strong>Spliethoff</strong>'s <strong>Mineralengracht</strong> class multipurpose vessels.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Houthi</strong> maritime attack crisis represents the longest-running and most consequential unconventional maritime threat globally — beginning in November 2023. The <strong>Houthi</strong> movement, officially known as <strong>Ansar Allah</strong>, is the armed wing of a <strong>Yemen</strong>i Shi'a Zaydi faction — based in the <strong>Saada</strong> region — with close ties to <strong>Iran</strong>. <strong>Abdul-Malik al-Houthi</strong> is the movement's leader. <strong>Mahdi al-Mashat</strong> is a key figure in the <strong>Houthi</strong>-controlled government in <strong>Sanaa</strong>. The <strong>Bab el-Mandeb</strong> Strait links the <strong>Red Sea</strong> with the <strong>Gulf of Aden</strong> — one of the four most strategically critical maritime chokepoints globally — through which 6-7 million barrels of oil per day and 12 percent of global container traffic transit. The <strong>Suez Canal</strong>, at the northern end of the <strong>Red Sea</strong>, carries 12-15 percent of global commerce — but <strong>Houthi</strong> attacks have forced major global container carriers to redirect to the <strong>Cape of Good Hope</strong> route (<strong>South Africa</strong>) — extending voyage times by 10-14 days and increasing operational costs by more than 30 percent.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, international maritime protection operations launched in response to <strong>Houthi</strong> attacks include <strong>Operation Prosperity Guardian</strong> (<strong>OPG</strong>; <strong>U.S.</strong>-led since December 2023, including <strong>UK</strong>, <strong>Bahrain</strong>, <strong>Canada</strong>, <strong>Denmark</strong>, <strong>France</strong>, <strong>Germany</strong>, <strong>Greece</strong>, <strong>Italy</strong>, <strong>Netherlands</strong>, <strong>New Zealand</strong>, <strong>Norway</strong>, <strong>Seychelles</strong>, <strong>Singapore</strong>, <strong>Spain</strong>, <strong>Sri Lanka</strong>); <strong>Operation Aspides</strong> (<strong>EU</strong>-led since February 2024, <strong>EUNAVFOR</strong>); and the <strong>Combined Maritime Forces</strong> (<strong>CMF</strong>, <strong>Bahrain</strong>-based, <strong>CTF-153</strong>) as key multinational maritime protection efforts. Active <strong>U.S. Navy</strong> vessels include <strong>USS Eisenhower</strong>, <strong>USS Carney</strong>, <strong>USS Mason</strong>, and <strong>USS Gravely</strong> — equipped with key defense systems including the <strong>Aegis Combat System</strong>, <strong>SM-2</strong>, <strong>SM-6</strong>, <strong>ESSM</strong>, and <strong>Phalanx CIWS</strong>. <strong>UKMTO</strong> (<strong>United Kingdom Maritime Trade Operations</strong>) and <strong>EUMSC-HOA</strong> (<strong>EU Naval Force Operation Atalanta</strong>) serve as primary monitoring and coordination centers. <strong>BMP5</strong> (<strong>Best Management Practices version 5</strong>) is the anti-piracy and anti-attack guidance for commercial vessels. The <strong>JWC</strong> (<strong>Joint War Committee</strong>, <strong>Lloyd's of London</strong>) has classified <strong>Red Sea</strong>, <strong>Gulf of Aden</strong>, and <strong>Yemen</strong>i waters as a <strong>listed area</strong> — significantly raising insurance premiums.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Cape of Good Hope</strong> routing effects impact major global carriers including <strong>Maersk</strong>, <strong>MSC</strong> (<strong>Mediterranean Shipping Company</strong>), <strong>CMA CGM</strong>, <strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong>, <strong>Evergreen Marine</strong>, <strong>Yang Ming</strong>, <strong>HMM</strong>, <strong>ONE</strong> (<strong>Ocean Network Express</strong>), <strong>OOCL</strong>, <strong>COSCO</strong>, <strong>ZIM</strong>, <strong>Wan Hai Lines</strong>, and <strong>PIL</strong> (<strong>Pacific International Lines</strong>). These carriers have rerouted most <strong>Asia-Europe</strong> routes via the <strong>Cape of Good Hope</strong> since December 2023. Key freight rate indicators include the <strong>SCFI</strong> (<strong>Shanghai Containerized Freight Index</strong>), <strong>WCI</strong> (<strong>Drewry World Container Index</strong>), <strong>FBX</strong> (<strong>Freightos Baltic Index</strong>), and the <strong>Baltic Dry Index</strong> (<strong>BDI</strong>). The <strong>Suez Canal Authority</strong> (<strong>SCA</strong>, <strong>Egypt</strong>) has lost more than 50 percent of its revenues during 2024-2025 — representing a significant impact on <strong>Egypt</strong>'s budget. <strong>Osama Rabie</strong> is <strong>Suez Canal Authority</strong> Chairman. As a spillover effect of <strong>Cape</strong> rerouting, <strong>South African</strong> and <strong>Mozambican</strong> port throughput volumes, under the <strong>African Continental Free Trade Area</strong> (<strong>AfCFTA</strong>), have reached record levels. In sum, the <strong>Spliethoff Minervagracht</strong> attack represents a clear indicator of the <strong>Houthi</strong> threat's expanding <strong>Israeli</strong>-linked targeting and the structural entrenchment of <strong>Cape</strong> rerouting.</p>
