<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; <strong>Robert J. Bowman</strong> (<strong>Editor-in-Chief</strong>); how are <strong>U.S.</strong> supply chains responding to <strong>China</strong>'s threat to cut off access to critical minerals? <strong>Carrie Wibben Kaupp</strong>, President of <strong>Exiger</strong>, emphasizes the need for multi-tier visibility. <strong>China</strong>'s dominance over the global critical minerals market is not a new problem—but in response to trade war with the <strong>U.S.</strong>, it is significantly escalating the "weaponization of the periodic table," according to <strong>Wibben Kaupp</strong>. The <strong>U.S.</strong> and its allies carry an "extraordinary" dependency on <strong>China</strong>'s critical mineral supply—including 100% of seven minerals and 50% of another 30 to 40. "This is an area where <strong>China</strong> has a quite paralyzing and devastating strategic advantage in deciding to ban its exports to the <strong>U.S.</strong>," she states. Indeed, <strong>China</strong> is escalating geopolitical tensions, beginning with the <strong>Trump</strong> administration's application of massive new tariffs on <strong>Chinese</strong> goods—recently doing so for a series of rare earth minerals. With the terrifying possibility of core minerals entering a broad range of high-tech products being completely cut off, <strong>U.S.</strong> supply leaders need to gain a complete understanding of what their products are made from. Most manufacturers today lack visibility down to the raw material stage of their contents. Typically, according to <strong>Wibben Kaupp</strong>, they cannot see beyond a single supplier tier.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Exiger</strong>, based in <strong>McLean, Virginia, U.S.</strong>, with CEO <strong>Brandon Daniels</strong>, provides supply chain risk management (SCRM), third-party risk (TPRM), and regulatory compliance artificial intelligence platforms—with <strong>U.S. Department of Defense</strong>, <strong>NASA</strong>, <strong>Lockheed Martin</strong>, <strong>Boeing</strong>, and <strong>Raytheon</strong> as principal clients. <strong>Carrie Wibben Kaupp</strong> is a former <strong>U.S. Department of Defense</strong> supply chain risk leader. Other major SCRM providers include <strong>Resilinc</strong> (CEO <strong>Bindiya Vakil</strong>); <strong>Interos</strong> (Founder <strong>Jennifer Bisceglie</strong>); <strong>Everstream Analytics</strong> (CEO <strong>Julie Gerdeman</strong>); <strong>riskmethods</strong> (owned by <strong>Sphera</strong>); <strong>S&amp;P Global Market Intelligence</strong> (<strong>Panjiva</strong>); <strong>Achilles</strong>; <strong>Avetta</strong>; <strong>SAP Ariba Risk</strong>; <strong>Coupa Risk</strong>; and <strong>JAGGAER</strong>, which are major ecosystem players. Critical minerals include 50 minerals on the <strong>U.S. Geological Survey</strong> (USGS) <strong>2022 Critical Minerals List</strong>—with gallium, germanium, graphite, antimony, tungsten, cobalt, lithium, nickel, magnesium, and rare earth elements (neodymium, dysprosium, terbium, yttrium) among the main categories.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>China</strong>'s share of global mineral processing includes rare earth processing at 85+%, graphite refining at 90+%, gallium production at 98%, germanium at 60%, antimony at 48%, tungsten at 80%, and magnesium at 85%, representing the main areas of dominance. The <strong>China Ministry of Commerce</strong> (MOFCOM) is implementing export licensing requirements in 2025 for seven rare earth minerals (samarium, gadolinium, terbium, dysprosium, lutetium, scandium, and yttrium). <strong>U.S.</strong> responses include: (1) Defense Production Act Title III financing; (2) Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) domestic processing incentives; (3) CHIPS and Science Act; (4) Mineral Security Partnership (MSP; 14 countries); (5) U.S.-Australia Critical Minerals Compact; (6) Quad (<strong>U.S.</strong>, <strong>Japan</strong>, <strong>Australia</strong>, <strong>India</strong>) critical minerals cooperation; and (7) USGS and Department of Energy geological research investments, representing the main strategic steps. <strong>MP Materials</strong> (<strong>Mountain Pass, California</strong>; CEO <strong>James Litinsky</strong>); <strong>USA Rare Earth</strong>; <strong>Energy Fuels</strong>; <strong>Lynas Rare Earths</strong> (<strong>Australia</strong>; CEO <strong>Amanda Lacaze</strong>); <strong>Iluka Resources</strong>; <strong>Vital Metals</strong>; and <strong>Ucore Rare Metals</strong> are major <strong>U.S.</strong> and allied rare earth producers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, multi-tier visibility encompasses: (1) Tier 1 direct supplier; (2) Tier 2-3 sub-supplier mapping; (3) Tier N raw material source tracking; (4) Bill of Materials (BoM) decomposition; (5) Substance of Concern (SoC) tracking; (6) conflict mineral (3TG: tin, tantalum, tungsten, gold) compliance; (7) Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act (UFLPA) supplier screening; (8) EU Battery Regulation; (9) EU Critical Raw Materials Act (CRMA; 2027 targets: 10% domestic extraction, 40% processing, 25% recycling); and (10) SEC Form SD conflict mineral reporting, representing the main compliance requirements. Section 232 (Trade Expansion Act 1962) national security investigations, Section 301 (Trade Act 1974) tariffs, and Section 1260H China military company listings represent main U.S. trade tools. In conclusion, <strong>Wibben Kaupp</strong>'s assessment of multi-tier visibility appears to suggest that raw material supply chain and geopolitical risk management is being fundamentally redesigned globally—with Tier N visibility and mineral diversification emerging as primary strategic priorities for supply chain managers.</p>
