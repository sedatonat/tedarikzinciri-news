<p style="text-align: justify">For the <strong>first time in years, demand for older tankers has surpassed newbuilds</strong>. According to <strong>ShippingWatch</strong>, research firm data documents this unusual movement in the secondhand tanker market. At the same time, new ship orders have also grown significantly — reflecting a dual-speed dynamic in the tanker market.</p><p style="text-align: justify">Core drivers of older tanker demand include prompt capacity needs triggered by <strong>Red Sea and Strait of Hormuz security crises</strong>, efforts to build "shadow fleets" for Russian export shipping, and newbuild delivery lead times extending into 2027-2028. Older tankers offer operators the chance to capture short-term freight opportunities with faster deployment capability.</p><p style="text-align: justify">The rise in newbuilds reflects <strong>long-term green transition</strong> investments. LNG and methanol dual-fuel tankers will ensure compliance with future regulations targeting IMO 2050 carbon goals. Traditional fuel-oil tankers will begin entering scrap markets after amortisation. The two dynamics together show that the tanker market is preparing for a major fleet renewal period. Shippers need to update contract structures and pricing models during this transition.</p>

<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. For the first time in years, demand for older tankers exceeds newbuilds.<br>
2. At the same time, significant orders are being placed for new ships.<br>
3. Older demand stems from Red Sea/Hormuz security crises and the Russian 'shadow fleet'.<br>
4. Newbuilds point to LNG/methanol dual-fuel investment for IMO 2050 carbon targets.<br>
5. The tanker market is preparing for a major fleet renewal period.</p>
