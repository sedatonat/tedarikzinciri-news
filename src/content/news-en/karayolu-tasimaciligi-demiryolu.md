<p style="text-align: justify">As debates on sustainable transport and modal shift continue across Europe, <strong>competition between road transport and rail freight transport</strong> has returned to the spotlight. In RailFreight's assessment titled "Road transport: friend or foe?", a discussion emerges that emphasizes the relationship between the two modes is not black and white, yet perceptions are largely shaped by sectoral interests.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The article addresses this debate through two key figures:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Philippe Degraef</strong> – from the management of Belgium's road transport association <strong>Febetra</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Hans-Willem Vroon</strong> – director of the Netherlands rail freight association <strong>RailGood</strong>.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">Both representatives evaluate the road–rail relationship from entirely different perspectives; however, the discussion points to a fundamental question in European transport policy: <strong>"Are trucks truly privileged, or is rail struggling with its own structural problems?"</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h4 style="text-align: justify"><strong>According to the road sector: Rail cannot solve its own structural problems</strong></h4>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to Philippe Degraef, the rail sector's characterization of road transport as a "preferentially supported sector" does not fully reflect reality. Degraef argues that truck transport's:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>flexibility</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>high service levels</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>door-to-door access</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>processing speed</strong><br>\nare demanded by customers and preferred not because the state favors road, but due to <strong>the natural functioning of the market</strong>.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">Degraef further emphasizes:</p>
<blockquote style="text-align: justify">"Rail must first do its own homework to be able to compete. Schedule reliability is low, infrastructure bottlenecks are widespread, and energy and capacity crises persist."</blockquote>
<p style="text-align: justify">Regarding the road sector's frequently criticized low taxation or external costs, Degraef points out that freight transport is already under pressure due to high motorway tolls, CO₂ regulations, and driver costs.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h4 style="text-align: justify"><strong>According to the rail sector: European policy provides trucks with "discriminatory" advantages</strong></h4>
<p style="text-align: justify">RailGood Director Hans-Willem Vroon approaches the issue from an entirely different angle. According to Vroon, road transport has for years benefited from:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>lower taxation</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>insufficient external cost pricing</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>flexible regulations</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>sector-favorable adjustments to working and driving hours</strong></p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">Vroon's core argument is:</p>
<blockquote style="text-align: justify">"Modal shift policy is strong on paper, but in practice there is a structural system that incentivizes road. Rail freight can only grow when equal conditions are provided."</blockquote>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to RailGood, the phased introduction of <strong>LHV (Long Heavy Vehicles)</strong> – long and heavy truck combinations – into the European market is one of the most critical factors weakening rail's competitive position. Vroon believes that LHV use undermines intermodal operations.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h4 style="text-align: justify"><strong>The real question: Not antagonism, but integration necessity</strong></h4>
<p style="text-align: justify">The fundamental point discussed in the article is: <strong>Are trucks truly the enemy of rail?</strong><br>
In fact, neither Febetra nor RailGood fully accepts this.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Both sides agree on the following:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The European supply chain is not single-modal but <strong>multimodal</strong> in nature,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">An intermodal chain cannot be built without road transport,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Modal shift cannot occur without increased rail efficiency,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Completely restricting road transport risks economic viability.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">Therefore, what matters is not "antagonism" but <strong>balance and sound policy design</strong>.<br>
For rail to attract more freight, the following fundamental steps are needed:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">capacity increases,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">infrastructure modernization,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">digitalization,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">transparent planning,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">improved price competition.<br></p></li>
</ul>
<p><br></p>
<h4 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Conclusion: Road transport is not a rival, but currently more advantaged in the existing system</strong></h4>
<p style="text-align: justify">The outcome of the debate is clear:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Road transport is not rail's "natural enemy",</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">However, European policies and cost structures make road <strong>actually more competitive</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The rail sector faces ongoing need for structural reforms,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Modal shift is possible through <strong>making rail attractive, not by restricting road</strong>.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">For this reason, the framework of future European transport will have room for both modes, but the principle of <strong>fair competition</strong> that redefines the balance will be decisive.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h4 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Notes:</strong></h4>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">According to road associations, truck transport is the market's natural preference; rail's problem lies in its own internal inefficiencies.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">According to rail associations, European policies provide road transport with structural advantages.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">LHVs are at the center of the competition debate.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The key to modal shift: not penalizing road, but strengthening rail.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Road and rail are more <strong>complementary</strong> modes than rivals.</p></li>
</ul>
