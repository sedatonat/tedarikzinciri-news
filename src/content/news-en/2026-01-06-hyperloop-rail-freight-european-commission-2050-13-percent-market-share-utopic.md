<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>European Commission</strong> recently published a new study with ambitious plans and 2050 estimates for <strong>hyperloop</strong>. The study suggests hyperloop could reach a <strong>13 per cent market share</strong> in freight transport by 2050. However, reality may paint a different picture, considering the high expected costs and the stagnation facing the sector.</p><p style="text-align: justify">Hyperloop is a concept that, using electromagnetic levitation and propulsion inside vacuum tubes, can reach speeds of up to 1,000 km/h. As trialled for passengers, it could in theory also carry freight containers. The Commission study assumes that near-term pilot projects would begin in the late 2030s and that a 10,000 km network could be built across the EU by 2050. A few EU initiatives such as Delft Hyperloop in the Netherlands and Zeleros in Spain are in development.</p><p style="text-align: justify">Critics argue the Commission's market share estimates for hyperloop are unrealistic. Even existing rail freight infrastructure struggles to attract investment. Hyperloop's <strong>trillion-euro construction cost</strong>, integration problems with existing networks, and container size mismatch pose practical barriers. Analysts interpret the Commission's projection as aimed primarily at generating political support for R&D funding. In the short term, hyperloop is expected to remain a utopic vision rather than a game-changer for rail freight.</p>

<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. The European Commission foresees hyperloop reaching 13% freight market share by 2050.<br>

3. The Commission assumes a 10,000 km EU network could be built by 2050.<br>
4. Critics call the estimates unrealistic due to trillion-euro construction costs and integration issues.<br>
5. In the short term hyperloop will remain a utopic vision.</p>
