<p style="text-align: justify">The U.S. <strong>truckload</strong> market shows no pronounced recovery signals heading into the final quarter of 2025. The <strong>Curve Report published on November 20 by freight broker RXO</strong> suggests that volumes will remain <strong>muted</strong> in the year's final quarter, though a more meaningful directional shift is possible in 2026. The Curve brand, RXO's analytics division acquired from <strong>Coyote Logistics</strong> last year, is closely monitored by the sector.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">According to the report, while October and November showed relatively better performance on a <strong>year-over-year</strong> basis, this improvement has not created sustained upward pressure on <strong>spot rates</strong>. RXO expects spot prices to experience neither sharp declines nor strong gains in the near term; the market is <strong>trading within a narrow band</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">The report offers a notable assessment:<br>
 <strong>"Though this cycle peak may be atypical to previous — it may very well dip into deflation (without hitting a real trough) — we believe it will rise up to a more traditional peak in 2026."</strong><br>
This statement indicates that the current cycle is evolving differently from past truckload crises; rather than a deep collapse, the market is experiencing <strong>prolonged horizontal weakness</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Demand Problem: The Critical Issue</strong></h3>\n<p style="text-align: justify">The fundamental pressure on the market stems from <strong>demand weakness</strong>. While <strong>Bob Costello</strong>, chief economist of the <strong>American Trucking Associations (ATA)</strong>, suggests that supply-side deterioration could reshape the market, academic circles emphasize that demand-side factors are more critical.</p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Jason Miller</strong>, supply chain professor at <strong>Michigan State University</strong>, argues that the key to recovery in the truckload market is <strong>manufacturing output</strong>. According to Federal Reserve data:</p>\n<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">U.S. manufacturing production is <strong>7.8% below</strong> the 2007 peak.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Excluding high-technology products, this decline reaches <strong>15.6%</strong>.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">This picture demonstrates that <strong>industrial production</strong>, the primary freight source for truckload carriers, remains structurally weak.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>PMI Indicators: Volatile and Uncertain</strong></h3>\n<p style="text-align: justify">The RXO report highlights <strong>Manufacturing Purchasing Managers' Index (PMI)</strong> data. The <strong>New Orders</strong> component is regarded as one of the strongest <strong>leading indicators</strong> for U.S. economic activity.</p>\n<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The PMI New Orders index showed improvement for five consecutive months before <strong>declining in July</strong>.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Though it returned to <strong>expansion</strong> territory in August, it weakened again in subsequent months.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>November PMI</strong> data shows new orders in <strong>contraction for the third consecutive month</strong>.</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p style="text-align: justify">In November, only the <strong>primary metals</strong> segment stood out as one of the few product groups recording growth. This underscores that truckload demand is showing only very limited sectoral improvement.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Rate Cuts: A Source of Hope for 2026</strong></h3>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Despite the weak manufacturing outlook, some supportive factors are emerging. The <strong>Federal Reserve</strong> cut the federal funds rate by <strong>25 basis points</strong> in December, marking the <strong>third rate cut of 2025</strong>. The <strong>Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC)</strong> noted that economic activity is improving at a <strong>moderate pace</strong>, while emphasizing that uncertainty remains elevated.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">According to RXO, if rate cuts continue:</p>\n<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">companies' <strong>cost of capital</strong> could decline,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">manufacturing investment could accelerate,</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">the PMI New Orders index could turn upward again.</p></li>
</ul>\n<p style="text-align: justify">This scenario is viewed as the foundation for truckload demand showing <strong>gradual improvement throughout 2026</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Policy and Regulatory Headwinds</strong></h3>\n<p style="text-align: justify">The report identifies additional market-pressuring factors beyond demand weakness:</p>\n<ul>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Tariff-fueled trade policy</strong> uncertainties (particularly on the U.S.–Canada corridor),</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>non-domiciled CDL enforcement</strong>,</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Tightening on <strong>English language proficiency</strong> requirements.</p></li>
</ul>\n<p style="text-align: justify">These factors create additional pressure on driver supply and cross-border transportation, complicating market equilibration.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Overall Assessment</strong></h3>\n<p style="text-align: justify">RXO's Curve report indicates that the truckload market is displaying a <strong>weak but stable</strong> picture in Q4 2025, with the real inflection point potentially arriving <strong>in 2026 through interest-rate-supported manufacturing recovery</strong>. Rather than a deep crisis, expectations center on a return to a <strong>more traditional cycle peak</strong> following a prolonged period of horizontal weakness.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong></h3>\n<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">RXO forecasts truckload volumes to remain <strong>weak through Q4 2025</strong>.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Spot rates are compressed within a narrow band.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">U.S. manufacturing output is <strong>7.8% below</strong> the 2007 peak.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">PMI New Orders showed <strong>contraction for the third consecutive month</strong> in November.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Fed rate cuts are the primary source of hope for <strong>2026 recovery</strong>.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Trade policy and driver regulation uncertainties are adding pressure.</p></li>
