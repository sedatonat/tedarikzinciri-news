<p style="text-align: justify">Today's supply chains are more technologically sophisticated than at any point in history &mdash; with automation, predictive analytics, digital freight platforms, control towers and end-to-end dashboards supporting nearly every logistics function. Yet despite this progress, the industry continues to suffer from the same chronic breakdowns: mismatched data, unverifiable milestones, inconsistent shipment status and disputes that drag on for weeks or months. From a supply chain perspective, the underlying problem is not visibility. It is in fact <em>trust</em> &mdash; or rather, the <em>absence</em> of a single verifiable record of events shared by all stakeholders.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This is where <strong>blockchain</strong> enters the picture &mdash; as a digital infrastructure that records supply chain events in a way trusted by all parties. Yet vendors keep introducing new dashboards while shippers and carriers describe the chaos that unfolds when disparate systems collide in real-world operations. A shipment may register as "delivered" in a carrier's portal, "in transit" in a 3PL's feed, and be missing entirely from a retailer's platform.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This fragmentation is a structural problem rooted in siloed IT architecture. Each enterprise manages its own internal ledger of activities &mdash; none of which agree without reconciliation. Supply chains can implement as many new tools as they wish; but until the industry establishes verifiable, tamper-proof data as the bedrock of operations, it will continue to operate on digital quicksand. Visibility platforms have promoted themselves as the single source of truth &mdash; but they have struggled to deliver on this promise.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>blockchain</strong>-based solutions are being tested through <strong>IBM Food Trust</strong>, <strong>VeChain</strong>, <strong>TradeLens</strong> (shuttered) and <strong>Maersk-IBM</strong> partnerships. <strong>Smart contracts</strong> enable automatic payment triggers tied to delivery confirmation. <strong>Tokenization</strong> makes it possible to convert raw materials, invoices and bills of lading into digital assets. The industry's core challenge is to mature consortium governance, data standards (GS1; EPCIS) and energy-efficient consensus mechanisms.</p>
