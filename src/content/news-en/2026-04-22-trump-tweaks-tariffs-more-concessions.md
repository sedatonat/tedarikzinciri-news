<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Trump</strong> administration said it will reduce import tariffs on coffee and bananas on November 13 — as part of trade agreements with <strong>Argentina</strong>, <strong>Guatemala</strong>, <strong>El Salvador</strong>, and <strong>Ecuador</strong>, according to <strong>BBC News</strong> reporting. Meanwhile, on November 14, the administration agreed under a "non-binding memorandum of understanding" (<strong>MoU</strong>) to cap U.S. tariffs on <strong>Switzerland</strong> and <strong>Liechtenstein</strong> at "a maximum of 15 percent," according to a statement from the Swiss government. <strong>The Guardian</strong> reported that this brings U.S. tariffs on <strong>Switzerland</strong>, previously set at 39 percent, into alignment with those on neighboring <strong>European Union</strong> countries. From a supply chain perspective, the U.S. consumes approximately 1.5 billion pounds of coffee annually globally — with <strong>Brazil</strong>, <strong>Vietnam</strong>, <strong>Colombia</strong>, <strong>Honduras</strong>, <strong>Ethiopia</strong>, <strong>Peru</strong>, <strong>Indonesia</strong>, <strong>Mexico</strong>, <strong>Guatemala</strong>, <strong>Nicaragua</strong>, and <strong>Costa Rica</strong> being the main coffee supplier countries. <strong>Ecuador</strong> is the world's leading banana exporter — with <strong>Costa Rica</strong>, <strong>Guatemala</strong>, <strong>Colombia</strong>, and <strong>Honduras</strong> as other major banana exporters.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The news does little to end the ongoing uncertainty about which tariffs will be applied when and to which goods and countries. In April 2025, <strong>Trump</strong> declared a "national emergency" over trade deficits with broad trading partners and announced sweeping penalty tariffs. However, multiple revisions have been made to these import duties — and many countries as well as the <strong>EU</strong> trading bloc are also negotiating. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> tariff regime relies on <strong>IEEPA</strong> (<strong>International Emergency Economic Powers Act</strong>), <strong>Section 232</strong>, <strong>Section 301</strong>, and <strong>Section 122</strong> as primary legal instruments. The <strong>U.S. Court of International Trade</strong> and <strong>U.S. Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit</strong> are ruling on the legality of <strong>IEEPA</strong> use — with <strong>SCOTUS</strong> (<strong>Supreme Court of the United States</strong>) final review pending. <strong>Reciprocal tariff</strong>, <strong>fentanyl tariff</strong>, <strong>steel/aluminum tariff</strong>, <strong>auto tariff</strong>, and <strong>semiconductor tariff</strong> are the main <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> tariff categories.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Switzerland</strong> hosts the world's largest gold refining market (<strong>Argor-Heraeus</strong>, <strong>MKS PAMP</strong>, <strong>Metalor Technologies</strong>, <strong>Valcambi</strong>); pharmaceuticals (<strong>Roche</strong>, <strong>Novartis</strong>, <strong>Lonza</strong>); machinery and equipment (<strong>ABB</strong>, <strong>Bobst</strong>, <strong>Bühler</strong>); watchmaking (<strong>Rolex</strong>, <strong>Patek Philippe</strong>, <strong>Audemars Piguet</strong>, <strong>Richemont</strong>, <strong>Swatch Group</strong>); chocolate (<strong>Lindt</strong>, <strong>Nestlé</strong>, <strong>Toblerone</strong>); and finance (<strong>UBS</strong>, <strong>Pictet</strong>, <strong>Julius Baer</strong>, <strong>Lombard Odier</strong>) as major export sectors. <strong>Switzerland</strong>'s annual exports to the U.S. are approximately $60+ billion — a 39 percent tariff poses a serious threat to the economy. <strong>EFTA</strong> (<strong>European Free Trade Association</strong>) comprises <strong>Switzerland</strong>, <strong>Norway</strong>, <strong>Iceland</strong>, and <strong>Liechtenstein</strong>. <strong>Argentina</strong> is building close relations with the U.S. under the <strong>Javier Milei</strong> government. <strong>Guatemala</strong>, <strong>El Salvador</strong> (under <strong>Nayib Bukele</strong>), and <strong>Ecuador</strong> (under <strong>Daniel Noboa</strong>) form <strong>Trump</strong>'s pragmatic allied circle in <strong>Latin America</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the global impact of <strong>Trump</strong> tariffs includes <strong>nearshoring</strong>, <strong>friend-shoring</strong>, <strong>onshoring</strong>, <strong>China+1</strong>, <strong>Mexico nearshoring</strong>, <strong>Vietnam shift</strong>, and <strong>India alternative</strong> as primary strategic responses. <strong>USMCA</strong> (<strong>United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement</strong>) is planned for renegotiation in 2026. The <strong>EU</strong> is negotiating with <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> at a 15 percent reciprocal tariff level — under the leadership of <strong>Ursula von der Leyen</strong>. <strong>Japan</strong>, <strong>South Korea</strong>, <strong>UK</strong>, <strong>Vietnam</strong>, <strong>Indonesia</strong>, <strong>Philippines</strong>, <strong>Thailand</strong>, <strong>Israel</strong>, <strong>Saudi Arabia</strong>, <strong>UAE</strong>, <strong>Kazakhstan</strong>, <strong>Pakistan</strong>, <strong>Bangladesh</strong>, <strong>Sri Lanka</strong>, <strong>Cambodia</strong>, <strong>Brazil</strong>, <strong>Colombia</strong>, <strong>Peru</strong>, <strong>Chile</strong>, <strong>South Africa</strong>, <strong>Nigeria</strong>, and <strong>Egypt</strong> are among countries negotiating bilateral agreements with <strong>Trump 2.0</strong>. Ultimately, the <strong>Switzerland</strong> deal and coffee/banana concessions clearly demonstrate that the <strong>Trump</strong> tariff regime is being developed pragmatically through "reasoned exceptions."</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Trump</strong> reduces coffee/banana tariffs on November 13 — with 4 countries.<br>
2. November 14: <strong>Switzerland</strong>/<strong>Liechtenstein</strong> 39% → 15% maximum.<br>
3. Non-binding <strong>MoU</strong> aligns with <strong>EU</strong> neighbors.<br>
4. April 2025 national emergency — launching point for broad penalty tariffs.<br>
5. Multiple countries and <strong>EU</strong> are engaged in tariff negotiations.</p>
