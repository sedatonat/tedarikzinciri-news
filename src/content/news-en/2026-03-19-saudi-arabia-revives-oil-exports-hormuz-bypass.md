<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Saudi Arabia</strong> is ramping up oil exports to above half normal levels despite the disruptions caused by the <strong>Iran</strong> conflict, marking an early sign of success for the kingdom's ambitious contingency plan to bypass the <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong>. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Saudi Aramco</strong>'s rapid activation of western coast capacity is enabling <strong>Asian</strong> refineries to maintain <strong>spot</strong> supply flows. The shift of <strong>VLCC</strong> and <strong>Suezmax</strong> tonnage toward the <strong>Red Sea</strong> is bringing the <strong>Cape of Good Hope</strong> route back into focus and significantly raising <strong>tonne-mile</strong> demand. For flows heading through <strong>Bab el-Mandeb</strong> toward the <strong>Suez Canal</strong>, there is a need to reassess the <strong>Houthi</strong> threat profile.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">With <strong>Hormuz</strong> nearly closed, <strong>Saudi Arabia</strong> is redirecting oil westward through a 1,200-kilometer (746-mile) pipeline to the <strong>Yanbu</strong> port on the <strong>Red Sea</strong>. At the same time, it is assembling a large tanker fleet rapidly flowing seaward to load crude, and these vessels are now accumulating around the port. According to tracking data compiled by <strong>Bloomberg</strong>, shipments from <strong>Yanbu</strong> have averaged roughly 4.19 million barrels daily over the past five days. This level represents a significant share of the kingdom's pre-war total exports of approximately 7 million barrels and is strikingly higher than the roughly 1.4 million barrels previously moving through this port. From a supply chain perspective, the full utilization of <strong>East-West</strong> pipeline capacity represents a critical stress test for pump station technical constraints, <strong>API gravity</strong> quality compatibility, and <strong>terminal slot</strong> scheduling.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Approximately one-fifth of the world's oil normally flows through the narrow <strong>Hormuz</strong> strait opening into the <strong>Persian Gulf</strong>, and producers in the region are being forced to curtail output as storage fills up. The <strong>International Energy Agency (IEA)</strong> has warned that the conflict is causing the largest oil supply disruption in market history. <strong>Saudi Arabia</strong> is the only major producer with a significant alternative option. The <strong>United Arab Emirates</strong> has a pipeline route to the <strong>Gulf of Oman</strong>, but flows are vulnerable to disruption because the <strong>Fujairah</strong> port it relies on has been forced to halt loading several times due to unmanned aerial vehicle attacks. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>SPR</strong> (<strong>Strategic Petroleum Reserve</strong>) discussions, <strong>OPEC+</strong> supplementary supply coordination, and spikes in <strong>spot premia</strong> are directly impacting global refinery margins and <strong>jet fuel</strong> supply flows.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">As <strong>Saudi Arabia</strong> moves urgently to redirect oil, tankers are piling up offshore the kingdom's <strong>Red Sea</strong> coast. At least 32 supertankers and <strong>Suezmax</strong> vessels are waiting to load near <strong>Yanbu</strong>, with others still en route. Short-term loading data averages tend to be noisy, but <strong>Saudi Arabia</strong> has indicated it intends to increase loadings from <strong>Red Sea</strong> ports to foreign buyers and is offering its long-term customers the option of sourcing through <strong>Yanbu</strong>. State oil producer <strong>Saudi Aramco</strong> is not responding to emails requesting comment on <strong>Yanbu</strong> export levels. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>VLCC</strong> demurrage costs and berthing wait times are necessitating renegotiation of "laytime/demurrage" clauses in <strong>charter party</strong> agreements. Ultimately, <strong>Saudi Arabia</strong>'s <strong>Yanbu</strong> push is becoming a significant case study in how infrastructure redundancy can be mobilized in the global energy supply chain.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. Daily exports from <strong>Yanbu</strong> have reached 4.19 million barrels, above half the pre-war 7 million barrel daily total.<br>
2. The 1,200 km <strong>East-West</strong> pipeline is the primary corridor bypassing <strong>Hormuz</strong>.<br>
3. At least 32 <strong>VLCC</strong>/<strong>Suezmax</strong> vessels await in the <strong>Red Sea</strong>.<br>
4. The <strong>UAE</strong>'s <strong>Fujairah</strong> route is disrupted by drone attacks.<br>
5. The <strong>IEA</strong> has emphasized that the conflict is causing the largest supply disruption in history.</p>
