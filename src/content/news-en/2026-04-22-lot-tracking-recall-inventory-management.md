<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain Think Tank</strong>; <strong>SCB</strong> contributor <strong>Eric Allais</strong>; imagine walking into your local grocery store — only to discover that deli meat is unavailable — or pulling up to your favorite fast-food drive-thru — only to hear that your desired burger is no longer on the menu — situations that highlight the real-world impact of product recalls (<strong>recall</strong>). Such scenarios not only disrupt our daily lives — they also damage the reputation of the businesses involved — underscoring the importance of recall management. This is why any warehouse or distribution center (<strong>DC</strong>) manager must stay on top of the products they sell — as a daily necessity. Whether industrial adhesives, food, building materials, airplane parts, or medical supplies — these products fall under strict regulation. For any manager, this represents a significant responsibility. Recent product recalls underscore the importance of comprehensive inventory and logistics records — with many details to track — including expiration date, product ingredients, manufacturer or supplier, and production date.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>lot tracking</strong> (also called <strong>batch tracking</strong> or <strong>serial number tracking</strong>) is the process of identifying products by their production batch, supplier, and movement date. The <strong>U.S. Food &amp; Drug Administration</strong> (<strong>FDA</strong>), <strong>U.S. Department of Agriculture</strong> (<strong>USDA</strong>), and <strong>Food Safety and Inspection Service</strong> (<strong>FSIS</strong>) are the primary <strong>U.S.</strong> food regulators. The <strong>FDA Food Safety Modernization Act</strong> (<strong>FSMA</strong>) <strong>Section 204</strong> (<strong>Food Traceability Rule</strong>) takes effect on January 20, 2026 — for <strong>Food Traceability List</strong> (<strong>FTL</strong>) products — creating a requirement to maintain records of <strong>Critical Tracking Events</strong> (<strong>CTE</strong>) and <strong>Key Data Elements</strong> (<strong>KDE</strong>) across the supply chain. The <strong>USDA</strong> enforces lot traceability requirements for meat, poultry, and egg products — under the <strong>Bioterrorism Act</strong> (2002) and the <strong>Public Health Security and Bioterrorism Preparedness and Response Act</strong>. The <strong>EU</strong> applies the "one step back; one step forward" principle for food traceability under <strong>Regulation (EC) No 178/2002</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, industries requiring lot tracking include food and beverage, pharmaceuticals and biotechnology, medical devices, cosmetics, automotive (especially safety-critical components), aerospace, defense, electronics, chemicals, building materials, agricultural inputs (fertilizers, pesticides, seeds), and metals/mining. Key <strong>U.S.</strong> recall categories include <strong>FDA Class I/II/III recall</strong>, <strong>USDA Class I/II/III recall</strong>, <strong>NHTSA recall</strong> (<strong>National Highway Traffic Safety Administration</strong>), <strong>FAA Airworthiness Directive</strong>, and <strong>CPSC recall</strong> (<strong>Consumer Product Safety Commission</strong>). Primary traceability standards include <strong>GS1 Global Traceability Standard</strong> (<strong>GTS</strong>), <strong>GS1 Digital Link</strong>, <strong>EPCIS</strong> (<strong>Electronic Product Code Information Services</strong>), <strong>GS1 SSCC</strong> (<strong>Serial Shipping Container Code</strong>), <strong>GS1 GTIN</strong> (<strong>Global Trade Item Number</strong>), and <strong>GS1 GLN</strong> (<strong>Global Location Number</strong>). The <strong>Drug Supply Chain Security Act</strong> (<strong>DSCSA</strong>), the 2013 <strong>U.S.</strong> pharmaceutical traceability law, creates a requirement for lot traceability with serialized <strong>SNI</strong> (<strong>Serialized Numerical Identifier</strong>).</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, lot tracking technologies include <strong>WMS</strong> (<strong>Warehouse Management System</strong>), <strong>ERP</strong>, <strong>QMS</strong> (<strong>Quality Management System</strong>), <strong>LIMS</strong> (<strong>Laboratory Information Management System</strong>), <strong>MES</strong> (<strong>Manufacturing Execution System</strong>), blockchain-based traceability (<strong>IBM Food Trust</strong>, <strong>VeChain</strong>, <strong>FoodLogiQ</strong> — <strong>Trustwell</strong>, <strong>ripe.io</strong>, <strong>Provenance</strong>), <strong>RFID</strong>, <strong>2D Data Matrix</strong> (<strong>GS1 DataMatrix</strong>), <strong>QR code</strong>, and <strong>1D barcode</strong>. Primary <strong>ERP</strong> platforms supporting lot traceability include <strong>SAP S/4HANA</strong>, <strong>Oracle Fusion ERP</strong>, <strong>Microsoft Dynamics 365</strong>, <strong>Infor CloudSuite</strong>, <strong>NetSuite</strong>, <strong>Epicor</strong>, <strong>Plex Systems</strong>, <strong>QAD</strong>, and <strong>SYSPRO</strong>. Leading <strong>WMS</strong> platforms include <strong>Manhattan Active</strong>, <strong>Blue Yonder Luminate</strong>, <strong>Körber WMS</strong>, <strong>Tecsys</strong>, <strong>HighJump</strong>, <strong>Synapse</strong>, <strong>Logiwa</strong>, <strong>Mecalux Easy WMS</strong>, <strong>PathGuide Latitude</strong>, <strong>3PL Central</strong>, and <strong>FishBowl</strong>. As <strong>Eric Allais</strong> emphasizes, lot tracking is a fundamental infrastructure layer of modern supply chains — essential for reducing recall costs, ensuring consumer safety, achieving regulatory compliance, and protecting brand reputation.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Eric Allais</strong> (<strong>SCB</strong>) emphasizes that lot tracking is a vital component of recall management.<br>
2. <strong>FDA FSMA Section 204</strong> takes effect on January 20, 2026.<br>
3. Expiration date, product ingredients, manufacturer, and production date are key elements to track.<br>
4. <strong>GS1 GTIN</strong>, <strong>GS1 SSCC</strong>, and <strong>EPCIS</strong> are primary traceability standards.<br>
5. <strong>WMS</strong>, <strong>ERP</strong>, blockchain, <strong>RFID</strong>, and <strong>2D Data Matrix</strong> are key lot tracking technologies.</p>
