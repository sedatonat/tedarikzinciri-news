<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Libya</strong> has signed a partnership agreement to expand and develop the <strong>Misrata Free Zone</strong> port terminal located on the <strong>Mediterranean</strong>, bringing together <strong>Qatari</strong>, <strong>Italian</strong>, and <strong>Swiss</strong> companies, according to the <strong>OPEC</strong> member nation's announcement. This multinational structure represents a strategic move to reposition the largest free zone on North Africa's coast. The agreement was announced during a period when the Tripoli-based government is simultaneously advancing political stability and economic diplomacy. The capacity expansion in <strong>Misrata</strong> establishes a trajectory that integrates <strong>Libya</strong>'s non-oil trade growth potential more tightly with the broader Mediterranean container network.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to information disclosed by the Tripoli-based government, the agreement aims to attract $2.7 billion in investment, with projections of approximately $600 million in annual operating revenue and 8,400 jobs created. The expansion is structured around the goal of increasing the terminal's annual capacity to 4 million containers. This capacity level will position <strong>Misrata</strong> in the upper tier of ports that balance transshipment loads on <strong>Mediterranean</strong> mainline shipping routes. The terminal expansion also provides a strong foundation for corridor plans with road and rail extensions between <strong>sub-Saharan Africa</strong> and <strong>Europe</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The government disclosed that Geneva-based <strong>MSC</strong> is also playing a role in the project, emphasizing that implementation is based on foreign direct investment and does not impose additional burden on <strong>Libya</strong>'s state budget. <strong>MSC</strong>'s position in global mainline shipping operations provides a critical advantage for the project in terms of service procurement, feeder connections, and cargo attraction potential. The <strong>Misrata Free Zone</strong>, established in 2000, hosts the country's largest commercial port, which manages 60 percent of non-oil trade. This share transforms into an indicator that impacts not only the port's scale, but also the strategic vision of establishing a revenue backbone for <strong>Libya</strong> separate from oil income.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Libya</strong>'s Prime Minister <strong>Abdul Hamid Dbeibah</strong> participated in the agreement signing ceremony alongside his <strong>Qatari</strong> counterpart <strong>Sheikh Mohammed bin Abdulrahman Al-Thani</strong> and <strong>Italian Deputy Prime Minister Antonio Tajani</strong>. High-level political representation underscores that the agreement constitutes a strategic interstate framework beyond being merely a commercial port investment. From a supply chain perspective, the capacity increase in <strong>Misrata</strong> will alter the transshipment balance on Eastern <strong>Mediterranean</strong> and Central <strong>Mediterranean</strong> routes, establishing a new equation in regional competition alongside ports in <strong>Algeria</strong>, <strong>Tunisia</strong>, and <strong>Egypt</strong>. Ultimately, the project stands as one of the largest infrastructure investments structurally redefining <strong>Libya</strong>'s position in the <strong>Mediterranean</strong> logistics landscape.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. The agreement targets attracting a total of $2.7 billion in investment.<br>
2. The expansion increases annual capacity to 4 million containers.<br>
3. Annual operating revenue of $600 million and 8,400 jobs are projected.<br>
4. <strong>MSC</strong> participates in the project through a foreign direct investment model.<br>
5. <strong>Misrata Free Zone</strong> manages 60 percent of <strong>Libya</strong>'s non-oil trade.</p>
