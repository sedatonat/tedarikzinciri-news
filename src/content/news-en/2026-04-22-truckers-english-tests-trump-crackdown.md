<p style="text-align: justify">Truck drivers are encountering a new roadside risk: Law enforcement authorities are being empowered by the <strong>Trump</strong> administration to pull drivers off the road if they fail an English language test. The government argues that the rules are critical for safety. For <strong>Vadym Shpak</strong>, however, they represent a costly and disappointing disruption. The owner of an <strong>Illinois</strong>-based trucking company, <strong>Shpak</strong> must now arrange flights and rental vehicles for drivers forced to abandon their vehicles on the road. Some of his employees, largely <strong>Eastern European</strong>, are refusing to travel to southern states out of fear of being targeted. He says insurance premiums are climbing due to the rising number of violations. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.S.</strong> has approximately 3.5 million professional truck drivers — holders of Class A CDL (Commercial Driver's License).</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">"These are good drivers, experienced drivers, but they're being pulled over and told their English isn't adequate," <strong>Shpak</strong> says. "And you know what? I have to pay for everything." The language crackdown is part of a broader <strong>Trump</strong> administration campaign that is disrupting the trucking industry, a critical pillar of the <strong>U.S.</strong> economy. In September, Transportation Secretary <strong>Sean Duffy</strong> sought to sharply restrict commercial driver's licenses for foreign-born applicants — a move that has since been halted by a federal judge. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>FMCSA</strong> (Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration), positioned under the <strong>U.S. Department of Transportation</strong> (<strong>DOT</strong>), is the primary truck safety regulator. <strong>49 CFR Part 391</strong> governs driver qualification requirements — English proficiency is a requirement but enforcement has been flexible.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.S.</strong> trucking industry has historically relied heavily on immigrant labor — <strong>Sikh</strong>, <strong>Punjabi</strong>, <strong>Latino</strong>, <strong>Eastern European</strong>, <strong>Somali</strong>, <strong>Russian</strong>, <strong>Ukrainian</strong> drivers constitute significant portions of driver demographics. According to the <strong>FMCSA</strong>, over 20% of total truck drivers in the <strong>U.S.</strong> are foreign-born. The <strong>American Trucking Associations</strong> (<strong>ATA</strong>) consistently estimates the driver shortage at 60,000–80,000 — the <strong>Trump</strong> campaign's foreign driver restrictions risk deepening this gap further. <strong>Schneider National</strong>, <strong>J.B. Hunt</strong>, <strong>Werner Enterprises</strong>, <strong>Knight-Swift</strong>, <strong>XPO</strong>, <strong>Old Dominion</strong>, <strong>YRC Worldwide</strong>, <strong>ArcBest</strong>, <strong>Saia</strong>, <strong>Yellow Corp.</strong> (bankruptcy 2023), and <strong>Estes Express</strong> are among the <strong>U.S.</strong>'s leading LTL/TL carriers. <strong>Owner-operator</strong>, <strong>independent contractor</strong>, and <strong>company driver</strong> are driver classifications. <strong>AB5</strong> (<strong>California</strong>) and similar legislation are at the center of regulatory debates surrounding independent contractor classification.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, truckload capacity supply moves approximately 72% of the <strong>U.S.</strong> economy's tonnage. <strong>Spot rate</strong>, <strong>contract rate</strong>, and <strong>fuel surcharge</strong> are fundamental pricing mechanisms in the trucking economy. <strong>DAT</strong>, <strong>Truckstop</strong>, <strong>Convoy</strong> (shutdown 2023), <strong>Uber Freight</strong>, <strong>Loadsmart</strong>, <strong>Transfix</strong>, <strong>Project44</strong>, <strong>FourKites</strong>, <strong>MacroPoint</strong>, and <strong>Trucker Tools</strong> are leading digital load exchange platforms. The <strong>ELD</strong> (Electronic Logging Device) mandate has been in effect since 2017 for <strong>HOS</strong> (Hours of Service) tracking. <strong>Autonomous trucking</strong> — <strong>Aurora Innovation</strong>, <strong>Kodiak Robotics</strong>, <strong>Plus</strong>, <strong>Embark</strong> (shutdown), <strong>Waymo Via</strong> (suspended), <strong>TuSimple</strong> (restructuring) — represents potential long-term solutions to the driver shortage, but commercial-scale deployment remains incomplete. Small fleet owners like <strong>Shpak</strong> face direct impacts from the operational and financial ramifications of <strong>Trump</strong> policies.
