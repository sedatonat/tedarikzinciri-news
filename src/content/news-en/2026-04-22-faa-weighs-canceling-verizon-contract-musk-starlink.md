<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports analyst insight; the U.S. <strong>Federal Aviation Administration</strong> (<strong>FAA</strong>) is nearing a decision on whether to cancel a $2 billion <strong>Verizon Communications Inc.</strong> contract, according to people familiar with the matter. This critical step could clear the way for billionaire <strong>Elon Musk</strong>'s <strong>SpaceX</strong> to assume greater responsibility for upgrading information technology networks for the National Airspace System. Outright cancellation is one option under consideration; alternatives also exist that would allow <strong>Verizon</strong> to continue while transferring some work to <strong>SpaceX</strong>, according to people not authorized to speak publicly. A decision could come by February 28, though the situation is evolving rapidly. This represents a controversial move linked to <strong>Musk</strong>'s <strong>Department of Government Efficiency</strong> (<strong>DOGE</strong>) initiative.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Verizon Communications Inc.</strong>, led by CEO <strong>Hans Vestberg</strong>, is headquartered in New York City and was founded in 1983 as <strong>Bell Atlantic</strong>. It provides telecommunications, mobile, wireless, and cloud services globally. <strong>Verizon Public Sector</strong> serves as its primary federal contracting division. The <strong>FAA Telecommunications Infrastructure Program</strong> (<strong>FTI</strong>) contract was initially won by <strong>Harris Corporation</strong> (now <strong>L3Harris</strong>) in 2002, and was renewed to <strong>Verizon</strong> in 2023 for 15 years at $2.4 billion. <strong>SpaceX Starlink</strong>, founded by <strong>Elon Musk</strong> (Founder/CEO) in 2002 and based in Hawthorne, California, has <strong>Gwynne Shotwell</strong> as COO/President. As of the end of 2024, it operates 7,000+ satellites and serves 4 million+ subscribers as the leading <strong>LEO</strong> (<strong>Low Earth Orbit</strong>) satellite internet constellation. <strong>Starlink Aviation</strong> serves major airline customers including <strong>JSX</strong>, <strong>Hawaiian Airlines</strong>, <strong>United Airlines</strong>, <strong>Air New Zealand</strong>, <strong>Qatar Airways</strong>, and <strong>WestJet</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Department of Government Efficiency</strong> (<strong>DOGE</strong>) is the Trump 2.0 administration's federal cost-reduction initiative, led primarily by <strong>Elon Musk</strong>, officially representing a restructuring of the <strong>U.S. Digital Service</strong>. <strong>DOGE</strong> is reviewing U.S. federal contracts, headcount, and federal agencies, with major actions including: (1) federal employee resignation offers; (2) shuttering <strong>USAID</strong>; (3) downsizing the <strong>Department of Education</strong>; and (4) canceling federal contracts. <strong>DOGE</strong>'s review of the <strong>FAA</strong>/<strong>Verizon</strong> contract raises significant conflict-of-interest concerns for <strong>Musk</strong>, as <strong>SpaceX</strong> would likely benefit as <strong>Verizon</strong>'s successor, a critical issue under scrutiny. <strong>SpaceX</strong> holds 22+ billion dollars in current contracts with federal agencies including the <strong>Department of Defense</strong> (<strong>DoD</strong>), <strong>Space Force</strong>, <strong>NASA</strong>, and <strong>NOAA</strong>. The <strong>Office of Government Ethics</strong> (<strong>OGE</strong>) and the <strong>Government Accountability Office</strong> (<strong>GAO</strong>, headed by <strong>Gene Dodaro</strong>), serve as primary U.S. ethics and accountability authorities.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, major global <strong>LEO</strong> satellite internet competitors include <strong>SpaceX Starlink</strong> (~7,000+ satellites), <strong>Amazon Project Kuiper</strong> (CEO <strong>Andy Jassy</strong>; targeting 3,236 satellites), <strong>OneWeb</strong> (<strong>Eutelsat OneWeb</strong>, CEO <strong>Eva Berneke</strong>; 648 satellites), <strong>Telesat Lightspeed</strong> (CEO <strong>Daniel Goldberg</strong>; Canada-based), <strong>SES O3b mPOWER</strong> (CEO <strong>Ruy Pinto</strong>; Luxembourg-based), <strong>China SpaceSail</strong> (<strong>Qianfan</strong>), <strong>China GuoWang</strong>, and <strong>Europe IRIS²</strong>. Telecom federal contract contractors in the United States include <strong>AT&amp;T</strong> (CEO <strong>John Stankey</strong>), <strong>T-Mobile</strong> (CEO <strong>Mike Sievert</strong>), <strong>Lumen Technologies</strong> (formerly <strong>CenturyLink</strong>), <strong>Comcast Business</strong>, <strong>L3Harris Technologies</strong>, <strong>Leidos</strong>, <strong>SAIC</strong>, <strong>CACI</strong>, and <strong>General Dynamics IT</strong>, among other major federal technology contractors. The <strong>Federal Communications Commission</strong> (<strong>FCC</strong>, Chairman <strong>Brendan Carr</strong>; Trump 2.0 administration) is the primary U.S. federal communications regulatory authority and licenses <strong>Starlink</strong> satellite operations. Ultimately, the <strong>FAA</strong>/<strong>Verizon</strong>/<strong>Starlink</strong> debate appears to fundamentally examine federal contracting competition and <strong>Musk</strong>'s sphere of influence globally, with <strong>LEO</strong> satellite connectivity strategy emerging as a top strategic priority for supply chain executives.</p>

3. The <strong>FTI</strong> contract was renewed to <strong>Verizon</strong> in 2023 for 15 years at $2.4 billion.<br>
