<p style="text-align: justify">Bulgarian and Turkish authorities are confirming new developments regarding the <strong>Kairos</strong> tanker, which is connected to Russia's <strong>"shadow fleet"</strong> network of sanctioned petroleum transport. According to information from <strong>Bulgarian News Agency (BTA)</strong>, the tanker with a capacity of approximately <strong>150,000 DWT</strong> was drifting toward Bulgarian shores following a <strong>Ukrainian maritime drone attack</strong> off Turkish waters and is currently stabilized at a safe anchorage point off <strong>Burgas</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Bulgarian authorities report that the tanker approached Bulgarian shores to within approximately <strong>one nautical mile</strong>, but was secured following intervention operations. The vessel currently has <strong>three crew members</strong> aboard and <strong>no oil spill or marine pollution has been detected</strong>. This situation demonstrates that a significant environmental risk has been mitigated for now, given the escalating military and asymmetric threats in the Black Sea.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>The Attack and Initial Response</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Kairos</strong> suffered damage on <strong>November 28</strong> while <strong>in ballast</strong>. Turkish authorities confirm that the incident resulted from an <strong>"external impact"</strong>; the vessel caught <strong>fire</strong> and <strong>a crew of 25 was completely evacuated</strong> thereafter. On the same day, another tanker named <strong>Virat</strong> was similarly attacked. Turkish authorities subsequently revealed that both vessels were struck by <strong>unmanned surface drones (USV)</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Ukraine's <strong>security service (SBU)</strong> claimed responsibility for both attacks. This indicates that a targeted campaign against <strong>sanctioned Russian petroleum transport</strong> continues in the Black Sea.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Towing Operation and Drift Toward Bulgaria</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">Following initial salvage and securing operations carried out by Turkish authorities, <strong>towing and salvage responsibility was transferred to the ship owner</strong>. The tugboat <strong>Timur Bey</strong>, engaged by the owner, took Kairos in tow on <strong>December 3</strong>. However, the tugboat subsequently altered course toward Bulgaria and released the vessel approximately <strong>six nautical miles offshore</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From this point forward, Kairos began drifting uncontrollably, approaching Bulgarian shores and <strong>anchoring off Burgas</strong>. Bulgarian authorities are closely monitoring the situation due to potential environmental and navigational safety risks it poses.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Crew and Safety Measures</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">Bulgaria, at the request of the ship owner, <strong>evacuated seven riding crew members by helicopter</strong> from the vessel. Following this operation, only <strong>a three-person team</strong> was left aboard. This crew is responsible for maintaining the tanker's safe anchorage and responding to potential emergencies.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Bulgarian authorities are planning to tow the tanker to a <strong>safe roadstead</strong> area to develop a more permanent solution. The waters off Burgas are used as one of the areas where such at-risk vessels are temporarily held in the Black Sea.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Flag Status and Shadow Fleet Dimension</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">Another notable aspect of the incident is the <strong>flag status</strong>. <strong>The Gambia</strong> subsequently announced that <strong>Kairos</strong> and <strong>Virat</strong> tankers were <strong>deregistered as of November 10</strong>. This indicates that the vessels had become flagless approximately <strong>18 days before</strong> the attack.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This development reflects a frequently encountered characteristic of Russia-linked <strong>shadow fleet</strong> vessels:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">frequent flag changes,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">deregistration,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">unclear ownership and insurance structures.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">When combined with military attacks in the Black Sea, this structure creates <strong>high environmental and legal risks</strong> for coastal states.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Implications for Black Sea Security</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">The Kairos incident demonstrates that the Black Sea is increasingly becoming a <strong>high-risk maritime zone</strong>. Vessels engaged in sanctioned petroleum transport are not only becoming military targets; they also pose <strong>secondary environmental and security risks</strong> to coastal states.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Bulgaria's rapid response and the absence of detected pollution have, for now, prevented a potential catastrophe. However, the incident indicates that the risk profile of the Black Sea has permanently shifted for <strong>NATO coastal member states</strong>, insurers, and port authorities.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Overall Assessment</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">While the stabilization of the Kairos tanker off Burgas averted an immediate environmental crisis, risks stemming from the <strong>shadow fleet</strong>, <strong>maritime drone attacks</strong>, and <strong>flagless vessels</strong> continue to mount in the Black Sea. Such incidents reveal that maritime transport in the region is not merely a commercial matter but also a <strong>geopolitical security issue</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Points:</strong></h3>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Kairos</strong> was damaged by a Ukrainian maritime drone attack.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The vessel has a capacity of <strong>150,000 DWT</strong> and was struck while in ballast.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Turkey confirmed that the attack was carried out with <strong>unmanned surface drones</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The vessel was stabilized <strong>off Burgas</strong> following towing operations.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The vessel has <strong>3 crew members</strong> aboard, and <strong>no pollution was detected</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The Gambia</strong> announced that Kairos and Virat were <strong>deregistered on November 10</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The incident demonstrates that <strong>shadow fleet risk</strong> persists in the Black Sea.</p></li>
</ul>
