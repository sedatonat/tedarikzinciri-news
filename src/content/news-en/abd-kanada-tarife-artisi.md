<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>US President Donald Trump</strong> announced that <strong>an additional 10% tariff will be applied to imports from Canada</strong>. The announcement was made on Trump's <strong>TruthSocial</strong> account on <strong>Saturday, October 25, 2025</strong>. However, the announcement did not specify <strong>when the new tariff increase would take effect</strong> or <strong>which products would be covered</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Currently, <strong>goods imported from Canada</strong> are subject to <strong>a 35% tariff rate since August 1, 2025</strong>. This rate applies to <strong>products outside the scope of the United States–Mexico–Canada Agreement (USMCA)</strong>. Additionally, special tariff arrangements exist in sectors such as <strong>automotive</strong>, <strong>lumber</strong>, and <strong>steel</strong>. The decision to increase tariffs is being viewed as <strong>an indicator that tensions between the two countries are escalating again</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The spark for the tensions was lit by <strong>an anti-tariff advertising campaign launched by Ontario's government in the US</strong>. The advertisement used excerpts from a radio address delivered by <strong>then-US President Ronald Reagan in 1987</strong>. In that speech, Reagan explained <strong>temporary tariffs he had imposed on Japan</strong> at the time while also emphasizing that <strong>tariffs could harm free trade</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Following the advertisement's release in the US, the <strong>Ronald Reagan Presidential Foundation and Institute</strong> issued a statement on <strong>X (formerly Twitter)</strong> objecting to the unauthorized use of the speech. Trump subsequently shared this statement on his <strong>TruthSocial</strong> account, alleging that the advertisement was released <strong>to interfere with an upcoming Supreme Court decision on trade tariffs</strong>. In his post, he stated, <strong>"ALL TRADE NEGOTIATIONS WITH CANADA HAVE BEEN TERMINATED HERE"</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Following this announcement, Canada took a <strong>measured approach</strong>. <strong>Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney</strong> said during a press conference on Friday, "We have made significant progress in trade negotiations. When Americans are ready to talk again, we are ready to continue that progress." Carney's remarks were featured in videos published by <strong>CNN</strong> and <strong>the Associated Press</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">On the same day, <strong>Ontario Premier Doug Ford</strong> announced that he had met with Carney and would <strong>suspend the advertising campaign starting Monday</strong>. However, Ford noted that advertisements would continue to air during the <strong>World Series</strong> games being held in Toronto.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">These developments are being viewed as part of <strong>the Trump administration's effort to reshape trade relations with Canada</strong>. The US is intensifying trade pressure on Canada, particularly in sectors such as <strong>lumber, automotive, and agriculture</strong>. However, according to experts, such sudden tariff increases <strong>could adversely affect supply chains</strong> and <strong>could destabilize the USMCA</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In his previous term, Trump similarly used <strong>tariff threats against European and Asian countries</strong> as a negotiating tool. For this reason, analysts suggest that the 10% increase targeting Canada is intended to <strong>create negotiating pressure</strong> and may not necessarily mean a permanent trade war.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">For now, Canada is <strong>maintaining its diplomatic tone</strong> and keeping the door open for negotiations. However, it is assessed that the advertising campaign and mutual statements will leave the relationship between the two countries in <strong>uncertainty in the short term</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>

  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Tensions escalated following <strong>Ontario's anti-tariff advertisement featuring Ronald Reagan speeches</strong>.</p></li>
