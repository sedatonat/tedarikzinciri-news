<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports that <strong>Toyota</strong> on May 8 predicted operating profit for the fiscal year ending March will decline by approximately one-fifth — or $1.3 billion — due solely to <strong>President Donald Trump's</strong> tariffs in April and May. According to the <strong>New York Times</strong>, the automaker is forecasting the impact of automobile tariffs that began in April for only those two months. Beyond that, the effects are "very difficult to forecast," <strong>Koji Sato</strong>, Toyota's chief executive officer, said during a briefing on May 8. "The current environment surrounding the automotive industry, including trade relations, is in extreme flux," he said. The <strong>Times</strong> notes that Toyota's experience of tariff pain underscores the difficult position <strong>Japan</strong> faces in ongoing negotiations with the <strong>Trump</strong> administration. While <strong>Trump</strong> has paused a general 24 percent tax on imports from <strong>Japan</strong> until early July, the 25 percent tariffs on automobiles and auto parts are already hurting the country's main exports to the <strong>U.S.</strong> — automobiles and auto parts. On April 30, <strong>Japan's</strong> central bank more than halved its economic growth forecast, citing the imposition of tariffs at an "unprecedented level" by the <strong>U.S.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply-chain perspective, <strong>Toyota Motor Corporation</strong> (<strong>TYO: 7203</strong>; <strong>NYSE: TM</strong>) is based in <strong>Toyota City, Aichi, Japan</strong>; is led by <strong>Koji Sato</strong> as CEO (since April 2023); with <strong>Akio Toyoda</strong> as Chair; was founded in 1937; and is the world's largest automotive manufacturer — with 11.5 million vehicles sold in fiscal year 2024 and $312 billion in revenue. Its main brands are <strong>Toyota</strong>, <strong>Lexus</strong>, <strong>Daihatsu</strong>, and <strong>Hino</strong>. In the <strong>U.S.</strong>, <strong>Toyota Motor North America</strong> (<strong>TMNA</strong>; <strong>Tetsuo "Ted" Ogawa</strong> as CEO; based in <strong>Plano, Texas</strong>) is the main operating division — with major U.S. manufacturing facilities including <strong>Toyota Texas</strong> (<strong>San Antonio</strong>; Tundra and Sequoia), <strong>Toyota Kentucky</strong> (<strong>Georgetown</strong>; Camry and RAV4 Hybrid), <strong>Toyota Indiana</strong> (<strong>Princeton</strong>; Highlander and Sienna), <strong>Toyota Mississippi</strong> (<strong>Blue Springs</strong>; Corolla), <strong>Toyota Alabama</strong> (engines), and <strong>Toyota North Carolina</strong> (<strong>Liberty</strong>; battery facility opening 2025). Other major <strong>Japanese</strong> automotive manufacturers include <strong>Honda Motor</strong> (<strong>Toshihiro Mibe</strong> as CEO), <strong>Nissan Motor</strong> (<strong>Ivan Espinosa</strong> as CEO as of 2025), <strong>Mazda Motor</strong>, <strong>Subaru Corporation</strong>, <strong>Mitsubishi Motors</strong>, <strong>Suzuki Motor</strong>, <strong>Isuzu Motors</strong>, and <strong>Daihatsu Motor</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply-chain perspective, the <strong>U.S.-Japan</strong> tariff timeline is as follows: (1) February 2025, <strong>Trump</strong> announces a 10 percent baseline tariff on all imports, including from <strong>Japan</strong>; (2) March 2025, a 25 percent sector tariff on automobiles and auto parts; (3) April 2025, reciprocal tariffs — 24 percent for <strong>Japan</strong>; (4) end of April 2025, a 90-day pause on reciprocal tariffs — but the 25 percent auto tariff continues; (5) May 2025, <strong>Japan</strong> sends a delegation led by <strong>Ryosei Akazawa</strong> (<strong>Minister for Economic Revitalization</strong>) to negotiate with the <strong>U.S.</strong> The <strong>Bank of Japan</strong> (<strong>BoJ</strong>; <strong>Kazuo Ueda</strong> as Governor) at its April 30 meeting cut its fiscal 2025 GDP growth forecast from 1.1 percent to 0.5 percent. <strong>Japan's</strong> auto exports to the <strong>U.S.</strong> represent 30 percent of its 2024 annual exports of $410 billion (approximately $50 billion) — making them the main export item. The <strong>Japan Automobile Manufacturers Association</strong> (<strong>JAMA</strong>; <strong>Masanori Katayama</strong> as Chairman) is the principal industry organization. Other major affected <strong>Japanese</strong> exporters include <strong>Sony</strong>, <strong>Panasonic</strong>, <strong>Hitachi</strong>, <strong>Komatsu</strong>, <strong>Bridgestone</strong>, <strong>Denso</strong>, <strong>Aisin</strong>, and <strong>Yokohama Rubber</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply-chain perspective, <strong>Section 232</strong> automobile tariffs in the <strong>U.S.</strong> — under Section 232 of the <strong>Trade Expansion Act of 1962</strong> — allow import tariffs to be imposed on national security grounds, and serve as the primary legal foundation for <strong>Trump's</strong> 2018 steel and aluminum tariffs and 2025 auto tariffs. The <strong>Japanese</strong> automotive supply chain is built on <strong>Toyota's</strong> <strong>JIT</strong> (Just-In-Time) production system and core lean manufacturing principles including <strong>kanban</strong>, <strong>kaizen</strong>, <strong>jidoka</strong>, and <strong>poka-yoke</strong> — now adopted globally. The <strong>Toyota Production System</strong> (<strong>TPS</strong>) is the foundation of <strong>Lean Manufacturing</strong>. The <strong>Center for Automotive Research</strong> (<strong>CAR</strong>, based in <strong>Ann Arbor, Michigan</strong>) estimates that 2025 tariff impacts could lead to consumer price increases of up to $100 billion annually in the <strong>U.S.</strong> Major affected <strong>Japanese</strong> financial institutions include <strong>Mizuho Bank</strong>, <strong>Mitsubishi UFJ Financial</strong>, and <strong>Sumitomo Mitsui</strong> — the country's leading lenders. In conclusion, <strong>Sato's</strong> estimate of a $1.3 billion profit impact suggests that Japan-U.S. trade relations and automotive supply chain economics are undergoing fundamental reassessment globally — with tariff cost absorption and expansion of local U.S. production appearing to be key strategic priorities for supply chain managers.</p>
