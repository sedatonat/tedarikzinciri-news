<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Bloomberg</strong> reports that multiple small vessels have attacked a ship near Yemen's <strong>Al Hudaydah</strong> port in the Red Sea, setting it ablaze and forcing the crew to abandon the vessel—ahead of <strong>Israel's</strong> military conducting airstrikes on <strong>Houthi</strong>-controlled ports in Yemen. The vessels fired on the ship and launched self-propelled grenades, according to a company security officer cited as a source, who did not identify the vessel. <strong>UK Maritime Trade Operations</strong> (<strong>UKMTO</strong>) said in a series of social media posts on 6 July that a commercial vessel passed by and rescued all crew members, with the ship left abandoned. <strong>UKMTO</strong> previously stated that an armed security team returned fire during the attack, approximately 51 nautical miles southwest of <strong>Al Hudaydah</strong>—a stronghold of the Iran-backed <strong>Houthi</strong> militia, which has been targeting ships in the strategic waterway for more than a year. No group claimed responsibility for the attack. In the later hours of 6 July, the <strong>Israel Defense Forces</strong> (<strong>IDF</strong>) conducted airstrikes on <strong>Al Hudaydah</strong>, <strong>Ras Issa</strong>, and <strong>Al-Salif</strong> ports, as well as a power plant in <strong>Al Hudaydah</strong>, over the Red Sea.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Red Sea</strong> and <strong>Suez Canal</strong> represent one of the world's most critical maritime chokepoints—12-15 percent of global container trade, 10 percent of global oil trade, and 8 percent of global <strong>LNG</strong> trade pass through this route. The <strong>Bab-el-Mandeb</strong> strait is a 30 km narrow passage between Yemen and <strong>Djibouti</strong>/<strong>Eritrea</strong>—the <strong>Suez Canal</strong> is operated by <strong>Egypt</strong>—with <strong>Suez Canal Authority</strong> (<strong>SCA</strong>) headed by <strong>Osama Rabie</strong>. The <strong>Houthis</strong> (<strong>Ansar Allah</strong>), originating from <strong>Sanaa, Yemen</strong>, with <strong>Mahdi al-Mashat</strong> as Supreme Political Council leader, are an Iran-backed Shiite militant group that has attacked <strong>Israel</strong>-linked vessels in the Red Sea in solidarity with <strong>Palestine</strong> following <strong>Hamas's</strong> October 2023 attack. <strong>U.K. Maritime Trade Operations</strong> (<strong>UKMTO</strong>) is a subsidiary of the <strong>U.K. Royal Navy</strong> based in <strong>Dubai</strong> that issues risk warnings for commercial shipping. <strong>Operation Prosperity Guardian</strong>, led by the <strong>U.S.</strong>, involving the <strong>U.S. CENTCOM</strong>, <strong>U.K. Royal Navy</strong>, <strong>French Navy</strong>, and the <strong>EU's Operation Aspides</strong>, serve as the principal protection operations. <strong>Combined Maritime Forces</strong> (<strong>CMF</strong>, based in <strong>Bahrain</strong>) and <strong>Combined Task Force 153</strong> (<strong>CTF-153</strong>) are the main coalition structures.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, changes in global trade routing following the Red Sea crisis include: (1) the <strong>Asia-Europe</strong> route via the <strong>Cape of Good Hope</strong> (South Africa) creating an additional 8,000-9,000 nautical miles and 10-14 days of transit time; (2) a 50 percent-plus decline in <strong>Suez Canal</strong> traffic; (3) losses exceeding 8 billion dollars in <strong>Suez Canal</strong> revenues for Egypt; (4) increases of 200-300 percent in <strong>Asia-Europe</strong> freight rates; (5) surges in the <strong>SCFI</strong>, <strong>Drewry WCI</strong>, and <strong>Xeneta XSI</strong> indices; (6) a rise in marine insurance war risk premiums (<strong>P&amp;I</strong>) to 0.7 percent from the previous 0.05 percent; (7) an additional 14-21 days of lead time in supply chains to Europe. <strong>MSC</strong>, <strong>Maersk</strong>, <strong>CMA CGM</strong>, <strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong>, and <strong>ZIM Integrated Shipping</strong> are the major carriers favoring the Cape route. <strong>COSCO</strong> and <strong>OOCL</strong>, along with some Chinese carriers, face fewer disruptions and enjoy a neutrality advantage. <strong>Houthi</strong> attacks employ: (1) ballistic missiles; (2) cruise missiles; (3) one-way attack drones; (4) attack boats; (5) unmanned underwater vehicles (<strong>UUV</strong>); (6) naval mines. Notable vessels impacted include <strong>Galaxy Leader</strong> (November 2023), <strong>True Confidence</strong> (March 2024), <strong>Tutor</strong> (June 2024), <strong>MV Sounion</strong> (August 2024), <strong>Magic Seas</strong> (July 2025), and <strong>Eternity C</strong> (July 2025).</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the maritime security impacts of the conflict in <strong>Yemen</strong> are testing the structural resilience of the global supply chain. The <strong>Israel-Hamas</strong> war, which began in October 2023, has escalated into a broader regional conflict involving <strong>Hezbollah</strong>, <strong>Houthis</strong>, <strong>Iran</strong>, <strong>Syria</strong>, and <strong>Iraq</strong>. The <strong>U.S. Trump 2.0</strong> administration has adopted a hardened approach toward the <strong>Houthis</strong>, launching an intensive air campaign from March 2025 onward. A <strong>Trump-Houthi</strong> ceasefire (May 2025) proved short-lived, with attacks continuing. <strong>Lloyd's of London</strong> and the <strong>Lloyd's Joint War Committee</strong> (<strong>JWC</strong>) are the primary war risk insurers. Organizations such as <strong>BIMCO</strong> (<strong>Baltic and International Maritime Council</strong>), <strong>ICS</strong> (<strong>International Chamber of Shipping</strong>), <strong>INTERTANKO</strong>, and <strong>INTERCARGO</strong> represent the main maritime industry bodies, calling for diplomatic solutions to ensure crew safety and freedom of navigation. In conclusion, the attacks on <strong>Magic Seas</strong> and <strong>Eternity C</strong> stand as concrete evidence that the Red Sea crisis is creating long-term structural changes in global supply chains.</p>
