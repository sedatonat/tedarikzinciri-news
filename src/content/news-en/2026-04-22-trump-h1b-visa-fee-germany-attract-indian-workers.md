<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Trump</strong> administration is implementing a $100,000 fee for new <strong>H-1B</strong> visa applicants, and <strong>Germany</strong> is now actively recruiting <strong>Indian</strong> workers who might otherwise apply for work status in the <strong>U.S.</strong> In a social media video posted on September 23, <strong>German Ambassador to India</strong> <strong>Philipp Ackermann</strong> highlighted <strong>Germany</strong>'s strong demand for skilled <strong>IT</strong> professionals and its political stability—a subtle jab at <strong>President Trump</strong>'s aggressive deportation approach and frequently chaotic foreign policy statements. "Our migration policy works a bit like a German car: reliable, modern, and predictable," <strong>Ackermann</strong> said in the video. "And at maximum speed, you don't have to be afraid of going full throttle—we don't change our rules overnight." Approximately 124,000 <strong>Indians</strong> work in <strong>Germany</strong>, primarily in information technology, science, and technology sectors. According to <strong>Ackermann</strong>, <strong>Indians</strong> working in <strong>Germany</strong> earn more on average than <strong>German</strong> citizens—with the country focused on streamlining visa programs for skilled workers to fill critical gaps in the workforce. The <strong>German Institute of Economic Research</strong> estimates the country has up to 387,000 open technology sector jobs through March and predicts this figure will double by 2027. In the <strong>U.S.</strong>, <strong>Indians</strong> comprise over 70% of <strong>H-1B</strong> visa approvals in 2024—largely for technology companies including <strong>Microsoft</strong>, <strong>Amazon</strong>, and <strong>Meta</strong>, among others.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>H-1B</strong> visa program is created under Section 101(a)(15)(H) of the <strong>Immigration and Nationality Act</strong> (<strong>INA</strong>)—permitting skilled foreign workers to work temporarily in the <strong>U.S.</strong> <strong>USCIS</strong> (<strong>U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services</strong>) is the primary immigration regulatory body under the <strong>U.S. Department of Homeland Security</strong>. The <strong>H-1B</strong> annual cap is 65,000 (regular) + 20,000 (master's exemption) = 85,000. <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> raised the visa fee to $100,000 per application—compared to the previous fee structure of only a few thousand dollars. <strong>Stephen Miller</strong> is <strong>Trump</strong>'s chief immigration strategist and architect of immigration tightening. <strong>Marco Rubio</strong> is <strong>U.S. Secretary of State</strong> overseeing visa policies. <strong>Kristi Noem</strong> is <strong>U.S. Secretary of Homeland Security</strong>. <strong>L-1</strong> (intracompany transferee), <strong>O-1</strong> (extraordinary ability), <strong>EB-1</strong> (extraordinary ability green card), <strong>EB-2</strong> (advanced degree, <strong>NIW</strong>, <strong>National Interest Waiver</strong>), <strong>EB-3</strong> (skilled worker), <strong>EB-5</strong> (investor), are the primary skilled employment visa and green card categories. <strong>Optional Practical Training</strong> (<strong>OPT</strong>) and <strong>STEM OPT extension</strong> allow <strong>F-1</strong> students to work for one year plus two additional years post-graduation.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the global talent migration ecosystem includes <strong>Germany</strong>'s <strong>Skilled Immigration Act</strong> (<strong>Fachkräfteeinwanderungsgesetz</strong>; in effect 2020; 2023 reforms), <strong>EU Blue Card</strong> (<strong>Directive 2009/50/EC</strong>; 2023 reforms), <strong>Germany Chancenkarte</strong> (<strong>Opportunity Card</strong>; in effect 2024; points-based), <strong>UK Skilled Worker Visa</strong>, <strong>UK Global Talent Visa</strong>, <strong>Canada Express Entry</strong>, <strong>Canada Global Talent Stream</strong>, <strong>Australia Skilled Migration</strong> (<strong>Subclass 189</strong>, <strong>190</strong>, <strong>491</strong>), <strong>Australia Global Talent Visa</strong>, <strong>Singapore Tech.Pass</strong>, <strong>Singapore EP</strong> (<strong>Employment Pass</strong>), <strong>UAE Golden Visa</strong>, <strong>UAE Talent Pass</strong>, <strong>Netherlands Highly Skilled Migrant Visa</strong>, <strong>Ireland Critical Skills Employment Permit</strong>, <strong>Portugal Tech Visa</strong>, <strong>France Passeport Talent</strong>, and <strong>Sweden EU ICT Permit</strong> among the major national and regional talent migration programs. <strong>Make-IT in Germany</strong>, the <strong>Federal Employment Agency</strong> (<strong>Bundesagentur für Arbeit</strong>), <strong>BAMF</strong> (<strong>Federal Office for Migration and Refugees</strong>), and <strong>Anerkennung in Deutschland</strong> (foreign credential recognition) are the primary <strong>German</strong> organizations involved.</p>
