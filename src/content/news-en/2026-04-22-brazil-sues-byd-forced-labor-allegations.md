<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports that <strong>Brazil</strong> is suing Chinese automaker <strong>BYD</strong> over allegations that the company subjected workers to slave-like conditions and engaged in human trafficking. According to <strong>Associated Press</strong>, Brazilian labor prosecutors filed suit on May 27 against <strong>BYD</strong> and two contractors, seeking 257 million Brazilian reais ($50 million USD) in damages. The lawsuit stems from the rescue of 220 Chinese workers from a construction site at a <strong>BYD</strong> factory in <strong>Brazil</strong> in December 2024, who prosecutors allege were brought into the country under false pretenses and forced to work under "extremely degrading" conditions. According to prosecutors, workers at five residential facilities managed by <strong>BYD</strong> and the two contractors slept in beds without mattresses, had limited access to toilets, and worked "exhausting hours" without weekly rest. Federal agents also allege that workers had up to 70 percent of their wages withheld, were forced to pay deposits to their employers, and had their passports confiscated. If a worker terminates their contract early, they forfeit their deposit and must repay the value of the airline ticket they received to come to the country.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>BYD Auto Co. Ltd.</strong> (<strong>Build Your Dreams</strong>), headquartered in <strong>Shenzhen, China</strong>, with <strong>Wang Chuanfu</strong> as Founder & Chairman, recorded global vehicle sales of 4.27 million units in 2024 (surpassing Tesla to become the world's largest EV manufacturer), 2024 revenue of $107 billion USD, and main models including <strong>BYD Han</strong>, <strong>Tang</strong>, <strong>Song</strong>, <strong>Yuan</strong>, <strong>Atto 3</strong>, <strong>Seal</strong>, and <strong>Dolphin</strong>. <strong>BYD Brazil</strong> acquired the former <strong>Ford</strong> facility in <strong>Camaçari, Bahia</strong> for $600 million USD, planned as <strong>BYD</strong>'s first major production base in <strong>Latin America</strong> in 2024. The facility is intended to produce SUVs, plug-in hybrids, and battery electric vehicles in <strong>Brazil</strong>. The <strong>Lula</strong> administration (<strong>Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva</strong>, President) is promoting <strong>Brazil</strong> automotive investments through the <strong>Rota 2030</strong> and <strong>Mover</strong> programs. <strong>Geraldo Alckmin</strong> (Vice President, <strong>Ministry of Development</strong>) and <strong>Jinwoo Stella Li</strong> (<strong>BYD</strong> EVP) are among the key executives overseeing the partnership.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Brazil Ministry of Labor</strong> (<strong>Ministério do Trabalho e Emprego</strong>; <strong>MTE</strong>; <strong>Luiz Marinho</strong>, Minister), <strong>Public Ministry of Labor</strong> (<strong>Ministério Público do Trabalho</strong>; <strong>MPT</strong>), <strong>Federal Public Defender</strong>, and <strong>Federal Highway Police</strong> are the main investigating agencies. <strong>Brazil</strong>'s <strong>Lista Suja</strong> (Dirty List) publicly lists companies that employ workers in slave-like conditions, with significant consequences for financing, procurement, and public relations. Other major <strong>BYD</strong> global production and assembly facilities are located in <strong>Hungary</strong> (<strong>Szeged</strong>), <strong>Turkey</strong> (<strong>Manisa</strong>; $1 billion USD), <strong>Indonesia</strong>, <strong>Thailand</strong> (<strong>Rayong</strong>), <strong>Pakistan</strong>, <strong>Vietnam</strong>, and <strong>Mexico</strong>. The <strong>EU CBAM</strong> (<strong>Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism</strong>), <strong>EU Forced Labor Regulation</strong> (approved in March 2024), <strong>Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive</strong> (<strong>CSDDD</strong>), and <strong>U.S. Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act</strong> (<strong>UFLPA</strong>) are the main forced labor compliance frameworks.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, other investments by EV and battery manufacturers in <strong>Latin America</strong> include (1) <strong>Tesla Mexico Gigafactory</strong> (<strong>Nuevo León</strong>; planned but delayed due to <strong>Trump</strong> tariffs), (2) <strong>Stellantis Brazil</strong> (<strong>Goiana, Pernambuco</strong>; $6 billion USD planned), (3) <strong>Volkswagen Brazil</strong> (<strong>Anchieta, São Paulo</strong>; EV transition), (4) <strong>General Motors Mexico</strong> (<strong>Ramos Arizpe</strong>; <strong>Equinox EV</strong>), (5) <strong>Great Wall Motors Brazil</strong> (<strong>Iracemápolis, São Paulo</strong>; former <strong>Mercedes-Benz</strong> facility), (6) <strong>Chery Brazil</strong> (<strong>Jacareí, São Paulo</strong>), (7) <strong>NIO</strong>, <strong>XPeng</strong>, and <strong>Li Auto</strong>, and (8) <strong>JAC Motors Brazil</strong> partnership with <strong>Caoa</strong>. <strong>BYD</strong>'s planned annual production capacity of 150,000 vehicles in <strong>Brazil</strong> is intended to strengthen its <strong>EV</strong> market leadership in <strong>Latin America</strong>; however, the forced labor lawsuit poses serious risks to reputation and investor confidence. As a result, <strong>Brazil</strong>'s forced labor suit against <strong>BYD</strong> appears to be prompting a fundamental reassessment of international construction supply chains and human rights due diligence globally, with contractor monitoring and social compliance emerging as critical strategic priorities for supply chain managers.</p>
