<p style="text-align: justify">It should come as no surprise that the rush to build massive new data centers to support the artificial intelligence boom is putting serious pressure on the supply of high-end memory chips. But other factors are also at play: a near-monopoly controlled by a limited number of suppliers; multiple potential logistics bottleneck points; geopolitical tensions; and rising prices. In a conversation with <strong>Graham Scott</strong>, chief procurement officer at electronics manufacturing giant <strong>Jabil</strong>, all these factors are reviewed. <strong>Scott</strong> identifies where and why the current chip shortage is occurring; discusses the impact of <strong>AI</strong> on the semiconductor supply chain; and suggests survival strategies for manufacturers struggling to understand future demand. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>HBM</strong> (<strong>High Bandwidth Memory</strong>) <strong>HBM3e</strong> and <strong>HBM4</strong> generation memory are produced in a three-company oligopoly by <strong>SK hynix</strong>, <strong>Samsung</strong>, and <strong>Micron</strong>. Up to 192GB of <strong>HBM3e</strong> is used in <strong>NVIDIA H100</strong>, <strong>H200</strong>, <strong>B100</strong>, and <strong>B200</strong> GPUs; <strong>HBM4</strong> is expected to proliferate by 2027.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Jabil</strong> ranks among the leaders in the global <strong>EMS</strong> (<strong>Electronics Manufacturing Service</strong>) market alongside <strong>Foxconn</strong>, <strong>Pegatron</strong>, <strong>Flex</strong>, <strong>Sanmina</strong>, and <strong>Celestica</strong>. Through its <strong>Green Point</strong> and <strong>Nypro</strong> segments, <strong>Jabil</strong> serves the <strong>automotive</strong>, <strong>medical</strong>, <strong>defense & aerospace</strong>, <strong>industrial</strong>, and <strong>cloud & data center</strong> markets. <strong>Graham Scott</strong>'s perspective as <strong>CPO</strong> emphasizes the importance of <strong>strategic sourcing</strong>, <strong>allocation management</strong>, and <strong>multi-tier supply chain</strong> visibility in today's semiconductor crisis. The fact that <strong>direct materials spend</strong> at <strong>Jabil</strong> runs at approximately $25 billion annually makes the company's <strong>buying power</strong> over manufacturer decisions significant.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The problem is that it's a "<strong>feast or famine</strong>" sector. And right now, shortage prevails. The podcast is hosted by <strong>Bob Bowman</strong>, editor-in-chief of <strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong>. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>silicon cycle</strong> in the semiconductor market typically shifts the supply-demand balance over 3-4 year periods. <strong>WSTS</strong> (<strong>World Semiconductor Trade Statistics</strong>) data shows that the 2024-2026 upswing following the 2023 trough is focused on <strong>AI accelerators</strong>, <strong>HBM</strong>, and <strong>advanced packaging</strong>. <strong>TSMC</strong>'s <strong>CoWoS</strong> (<strong>Chip-on-Wafer-on-Substrate</strong>) advanced packaging capacity will reach approximately 35,000 wafers per month by end-2025 and is targeted at 75,000 per month by end-2026; but even this falls short of meeting demand. <strong>ABF</strong> (<strong>Ajinomoto Build-up Film</strong>) substrate supply is concentrated on <strong>Ibiden</strong>, <strong>Unimicron</strong>, and <strong>Shinko Electric</strong>, creating a separate bottleneck.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, survival strategies for manufacturers are multidimensional. <strong>LTA</strong> (<strong>Long-Term Agreement</strong>) based capacity reservations provide preferred customer status alongside <strong>NCNR</strong> (<strong>Non-Cancellable Non-Returnable</strong>) terms. <strong>Second source qualification</strong> enables mitigation of critical supplier dependencies at the <strong>DFM</strong> (<strong>Design for Manufacturing</strong>) and <strong>DFS</strong> (<strong>Design for Supply</strong>) stages. Competition among <strong>TSMC</strong>, <strong>Samsung Foundry</strong>, <strong>Intel Foundry Services</strong>, and <strong>GlobalFoundries</strong> somewhat increases buyer bargaining power in the <strong>pure-play foundry</strong> market. National policies such as the <strong>CHIPS and Science Act</strong>, the <strong>European Chips Act</strong>, <strong>Japan LSTC</strong> (<strong>Leading-edge Semiconductor Technology Center</strong>), and <strong>Korea K-Chips Act</strong> are causing global capacity to be geographically redistributed over the 2026-2030 period. <strong>Rapidus</strong>'s 2nm fab investment in <strong>Hokkaido</strong>, <strong>TSMC</strong>'s Arizona facility, and <strong>Samsung</strong>'s Texas plants are new capacity candidates. In conclusion, <strong>Scott</strong>'s views in the podcast underscore that the shortage in the semiconductor supply chain is multilayered and structural.</p>
