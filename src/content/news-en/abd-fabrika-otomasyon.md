<p style="text-align: justify">The U.S. industrial sector is preparing for the most comprehensive <strong>automation wave</strong> in decades. According to new research published by <strong>RobCo</strong>, <strong>95% of</strong> industrial companies operating in the U.S. plan to deploy new automation solutions by <strong>2028</strong>. The research encompasses <strong>400 senior executives</strong> from different sectors including <strong>manufacturing, construction, engineering, and healthcare</strong>, and demonstrates that automation is becoming the <strong>default operating model</strong> rather than an exception.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The fundamental drivers behind this transformation include <strong>labor shortages</strong>, <strong>reshoring</strong>, and federal incentives. Companies note that with rising domestic production targets, the existing workforce is insufficient to meet this growth. Automation is being positioned as a strategic tool to fill this gap.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Current State: Robots Are No Longer on the Sidelines</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to the research, approximately <strong>one-third</strong> of U.S. companies currently use <strong>robots</strong> actively. However, what is truly striking is how rapidly this proportion will increase. Among participants:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>54%</strong> are currently testing robots or planning to deploy them in the near term.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>47%</strong> are using <strong>AI-powered automation</strong> solutions.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>94%</strong> have connected their machines to <strong>digital systems</strong> at least partially and are monitoring production with data.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">These figures show that automation is not limited to physical robots alone; it is a multi-layered transformation progressing alongside <strong>digitalization, data integration, and artificial intelligence</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Tangible Benefits: Productivity and Resource Utilization</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">Companies already using automation are satisfied with the results. More than half of participants report that through automation they have achieved:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>higher productivity</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>time savings</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>lower error rates</strong></p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">Additionally, many companies report that automation provides <strong>waste reduction</strong> and improves <strong>material and energy utilization</strong> rates. This indicates that automation serves not only to address labor challenges but also to support <strong>sustainability and cost control</strong> objectives.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Labor Shortage: The Primary Driver</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">One of the strongest factors behind the push toward automation is <strong>persistent labor shortage</strong>. According to <strong>Deloitte</strong> projections, the U.S. will need <strong>3.8 million new industrial workers</strong> in the coming years. However, there is a risk that <strong>1.9 million</strong> of this need will go unmet.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Faced with this situation, companies view automation not as a layoff tool but as a <strong>prerequisite for sustaining operations</strong>. According to the research:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>43% of companies</strong> report that after automation, employee workload decreased and <strong>morale improved</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>58% of workers</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>55% of unions</strong>,</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">take a positive view of automation. These figures demonstrate that social acceptance of automation has increased significantly compared to previous periods.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Cost Barrier and New Financing Models</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">Although automation demand is high, <strong>upfront cost</strong> remains a significant barrier. Companies report that:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>47%</strong> cite high initial costs as slowing their investments,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>27%</strong> experience a shortage of <strong>skilled workforce</strong> to install and manage systems.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">To overcome these barriers, companies are turning to alternative solutions instead of traditional machine purchase models:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>leasing</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>state and federal grants</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>robots-as-a-service (RaaS)</strong> models</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">are rapidly gaining traction.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>RobCo CEO and co-founder Roman Hölzl</strong> summarizes this transformation with these words:<br>
 <strong>"Modern robot-as-a-service models significantly lower the barriers to entry into automation."</strong><br>
According to Hölzl, monthly or usage-based payment models allow companies to test new technologies without tying up large amounts of capital. With maintenance, software updates, and service support handled by the provider, downtime risk is reduced and projects can be implemented more quickly.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Toward 2028: Strategic Significance</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">The RobCo research reveals that automation is no longer an "optional" investment but has become a <strong>fundamental condition for competitive survival</strong>. When U.S. manufacturing revival targets, labor shortages, and productivity pressures converge, automation will move to the center of industrial operations over the next three years.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Overall Assessment</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">By 2028, automation, robots, and artificial intelligence in U.S. manufacturing will be the norm, not the exception. For companies, the question is no longer "Should we transition to automation?" but rather <strong>"How quickly and through which model should we transition?"</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong></h3>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>95%</strong> of U.S. industrial companies plan automation by 2028.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>54%</strong> are testing robots or will deploy them soon.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>47%</strong> are using AI-powered automation.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The U.S. faces a <strong>1.9 million industrial labor shortage</strong> risk.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Automation is largely welcomed by workers and unions.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Robot-as-a-service</strong> and leasing models are rapidly expanding.</p></li>
</ul>
