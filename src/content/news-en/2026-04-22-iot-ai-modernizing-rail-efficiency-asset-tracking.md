<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>U.S. rail freight</strong> system serves as the backbone of national commerce—carrying more than 40% of long-distance freight volumes across a network spanning approximately 140,000 miles. Global supply chains face mounting pressures ranging from shifting demand patterns to climate-related disruptions—placing the rail sector under increasing pressure to modernize. A key component of this modernization lies in the adoption of digital technologies. <strong>IoT</strong> (<strong>Internet of Things</strong>) devices, coupled with artificial intelligence-based analytics, are modernizing how rail operators track assets, manage fleets, and make smarter operational decisions. Author <strong>Steven Payne</strong> contributes to <strong>SCB</strong>. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.S.</strong> rail sector is divided into categories: <strong>Class I</strong> (annual revenue >$1 billion); <strong>Class II</strong>; and <strong>Class III</strong> (<strong>short line</strong>). Major <strong>Class I</strong> railroads include <strong>Union Pacific</strong> (<strong>UP</strong>); <strong>BNSF Railway</strong> (<strong>Berkshire Hathaway</strong>); <strong>CSX Transportation</strong>; <strong>Norfolk Southern</strong>; <strong>Canadian National</strong> (<strong>CN</strong>); <strong>Canadian Pacific Kansas City</strong> (<strong>CPKC</strong>; merger of <strong>CP</strong>+<strong>KCS</strong> in 2023); and <strong>Ferromex</strong> (<strong>Mexico</strong>; <strong>UP</strong> joint venture)—representing the major <strong>North American</strong> players.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, rail asset tracking employs <strong>IoT</strong> technologies including <strong>GPS tracking</strong>; <strong>RFID</strong> (<strong>Radio Frequency Identification</strong>); <strong>cellular IoT</strong> (<strong>NB-IoT</strong>; <strong>LTE-M</strong>); <strong>LPWAN</strong> (<strong>LoRaWAN</strong>; <strong>Sigfox</strong>); <strong>satellite IoT</strong> (<strong>Iridium</strong>; <strong>Globalstar</strong>; <strong>Starlink IoT</strong>); and <strong>BLE</strong> (<strong>Bluetooth Low Energy</strong>); <strong>passive/active RFID</strong>—representing the primary connectivity protocols. Key safety and tracking devices include <strong>wayside detectors</strong> (<strong>HBD</strong>; <strong>Hot Box Detector</strong>; <strong>HWD</strong>; <strong>Hot Wheel Detector</strong>; <strong>WILD</strong>; <strong>Wheel Impact Load Detector</strong>; <strong>acoustic bearing detector</strong>); <strong>EOTD</strong> (<strong>End of Train Device</strong>); and <strong>HOTD</strong> (<strong>Head of Train Device</strong>). Major <strong>U.S.</strong> regulatory and industry organizations include <strong>AAR</strong> (<strong>Association of American Railroads</strong>); <strong>FRA</strong> (<strong>Federal Railroad Administration</strong>); <strong>STB</strong> (<strong>Surface Transportation Board</strong>); and <strong>NTSB</strong> (<strong>National Transportation Safety Board</strong>). The <strong>East Palestine derailment</strong> in February 2023 (<strong>Norfolk Southern</strong> hazardous material spill) served as a major catalyst for rail safety reform. The <strong>Railway Safety Act</strong> represents the primary legislative initiative under discussion in <strong>Congress</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, rail <strong>AI</strong>/digital trends include <strong>predictive maintenance</strong> (<strong>PdM</strong>); <strong>condition-based monitoring</strong> (<strong>CBM</strong>); <strong>digital twin</strong> technology (for <strong>locomotives</strong>; <strong>tracks</strong>; <strong>bridges</strong>; <strong>switch yards</strong>); <strong>computer vision</strong> (<strong>track inspection</strong>; <strong>wheel inspection</strong>); <strong>autonomous train</strong> operations (<strong>Rio Tinto AutoHaul</strong> operational in Australia); <strong>positive train control</strong> (<strong>PTC</strong>; mandatory in the <strong>U.S.</strong>); <strong>European Train Control System</strong> (<strong>ETCS</strong>; EU); <strong>European Rail Traffic Management System</strong> (<strong>ERTMS</strong>); and <strong>Chinese Train Control System</strong> (<strong>CTCS</strong>)—representing the primary signaling and control systems. <strong>Precision scheduled railroading</strong> (<strong>PSR</strong>), implemented at <strong>CN</strong>, <strong>CP</strong>, and <strong>CSX</strong> by <strong>Hunter Harrison</strong>, represents an approach aimed at capital efficiency but often at the cost of service quality. Major rail freight car types include <strong>intermodal</strong>; <strong>boxcar</strong>; <strong>tank car</strong>; <strong>hopper car</strong>; <strong>flatcar</strong>; <strong>autorack</strong>; <strong>well car</strong>; and <strong>chassis</strong>. Leading rail equipment manufacturers include <strong>RailPulse</strong>; <strong>Wabtec</strong> (<strong>Westinghouse Air Brake Technologies</strong>); <strong>Trinity Industries</strong>; <strong>Greenbrier Companies</strong>; <strong>FreightCar America</strong>; and <strong>RJ Corman</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the global rail sector includes major <strong>EU</strong> national operators: <strong>Deutsche Bahn</strong> (<strong>DB</strong>; <strong>Germany</strong>); <strong>SNCF</strong> (<strong>France</strong>); <strong>FS Italiane</strong> (<strong>Italy</strong>); <strong>Renfe</strong> (<strong>Spain</strong>); <strong>NS</strong> (<strong>Netherlands</strong>); <strong>SBB</strong> (<strong>Switzerland</strong>); <strong>ÖBB</strong> (<strong>Austria</strong>); and <strong>PKP</strong> (<strong>Poland</strong>). In <strong>Asia</strong>, major operators include <strong>China State Railway Group</strong> (<strong>CSR</strong>); <strong>Indian Railways</strong>; <strong>JR Group</strong> (<strong>Japan</strong>); <strong>KORAIL</strong> (<strong>South Korea</strong>); and <strong>Taiwan Railways</strong> (<strong>TRA</strong>). <strong>High-speed rail</strong> (<strong>HSR</strong>) systems, with <strong>China</strong> operating the world's largest at over 45,000 km, include <strong>Japan Shinkansen</strong>; <strong>French TGV</strong>; <strong>German ICE</strong>; <strong>Spanish AVE</strong>; <strong>Italian Frecciarossa</strong>; <strong>South Korean KTX</strong>; <strong>Taiwanese THSR</strong>; <strong>Saudi Haramain</strong>; <strong>Moroccan Al Boraq</strong>; and <strong>Indonesian Whoosh</strong> (<strong>Jakarta-Bandung</strong>; <strong>Chinese</strong> design). In the <strong>U.S.</strong>, <strong>HSR</strong> remains limited, with <strong>Acela</strong> the closest comparable service; major projects in development include <strong>California High-Speed Rail</strong> (<strong>San Francisco-Los Angeles</strong>); <strong>Brightline West</strong> (<strong>Las Vegas-Los Angeles</strong>); and <strong>Texas Central</strong> (<strong>Dallas-Houston</strong>). The importance of <strong>freight rail</strong> is highest globally in the <strong>U.S.</strong>—it is 4–5 times more energy-efficient compared to highway transport. In conclusion, <strong>Payne</strong>'s vision of <strong>IoT</strong>+<strong>AI</strong> rail modernization represents a tangible indicator of the sector's digital transformation trajectory.</p>
<p><br></p>

2. <strong>IoT</strong> devices + <strong>AI</strong> analytics are modernizing asset tracking and fleet management.<br>
3. <strong>Steven Payne</strong> is a contributor to <strong>SCB Think Tank</strong>.<br>

5. Smarter operational decision-making is a primary outcome of digital adoption.</p>
