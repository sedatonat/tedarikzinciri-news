<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; <strong>Mark Richardson</strong> (<strong>Chief Executive Officer</strong>, <strong>Ocado Intelligent Automation</strong>); interview with <strong>Robert J. Bowman</strong> (<strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong>); analyst insight; visualizing the future of warehouse automation &mdash; including the possibility of fully autonomous "dark" warehouses. In the warehouse automation sector, <strong>Richardson</strong> sees a continuing trend toward labor scarcity and higher costs for available work. As a result, an increasing number of warehouses are considering automation &mdash; even relatively low-cost operations that previously never contemplated the notion. At the same time, there is "a steady march of increased automation among buyers already onboard." <strong>Cobot</strong> (collaborative robots), <strong>AMR</strong> (autonomous mobile robots), and <strong>AS/RS</strong> (automated storage and retrieval systems) are the main categories.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Ocado Group</strong>, based in <strong>Hatfield, United Kingdom</strong>, with <strong>Tim Steiner</strong> as CEO, was founded in 2000 by former <strong>Goldman Sachs</strong> bankers. Initially an online supermarket in the UK, its main business lines now include <strong>Ocado Smart Platform</strong> (<strong>OSP</strong>), <strong>Ocado Intelligent Automation</strong>, and <strong>Customer Fulfilment Centre</strong> (<strong>CFC</strong>). Ocado's main OSP customers include <strong>Kroger</strong> (CEO <strong>Rodney McMullen</strong>), <strong>Casino</strong> (France), <strong>Sobeys</strong> (Canada), <strong>Aeon</strong> (Japan), <strong>ICA</strong> (Sweden), and <strong>Coles</strong> (Australia) among major global customers. Other major warehouse automation platform providers include <strong>Symbotic</strong> (<strong>Rick Cohen</strong> Chairman; major <strong>Walmart</strong> customer), <strong>AutoStore</strong> (CEO <strong>Mats Hovland Vikse</strong>; Norway; cube storage), <strong>Dematic</strong> (owned by <strong>KION Group</strong>; <strong>Hasan Dandashly</strong> President), <strong>Honeywell Intelligrated</strong>, <strong>Vanderlande</strong> (owned by <strong>Toyota Industries</strong>), <strong>Knapp</strong> (Austria), <strong>SSI Schaefer</strong> (Germany), <strong>Witron</strong>, <strong>TGW Logistics</strong>, <strong>Geek+</strong>, <strong>Hai Robotics</strong>, and <strong>Quicktron</strong> as key ecosystem players.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the main <strong>warehouse automation</strong> technology categories are: (1) <strong>AS/RS</strong> (shuttles, cranes, cube storage); (2) <strong>AMR/AGV</strong> (autonomous mobile robots/automated guided vehicles); (3) <strong>goods-to-person</strong> (<strong>G2P</strong>); (4) <strong>person-to-goods</strong> (<strong>P2G</strong> — pick-to-light, voice picking); (5) <strong>conveyor</strong> and <strong>sortation</strong>; (6) <strong>robotic piece picking</strong> (<strong>RightHand Robotics</strong>, <strong>Berkshire Grey</strong>, <strong>Covariant</strong>, <strong>Pickle Robot</strong>); (7) <strong>palletizing</strong> and <strong>depalletizing robots</strong>; (8) <strong>truck loading/unloading</strong> (<strong>Boston Dynamics Stretch</strong>, <strong>Pickle Robot</strong>); (9) <strong>WMS</strong> (<strong>Warehouse Management System</strong>); (10) <strong>WES</strong> (<strong>Warehouse Execution System</strong>) as the main technology stacks. Major <strong>WMS</strong> and <strong>WES</strong> providers include <strong>Manhattan Associates</strong> (CEO <strong>Eddie Capel</strong>), <strong>Blue Yonder</strong> (owned by <strong>Panasonic</strong>), <strong>Oracle WMS</strong>, <strong>SAP EWM</strong> (<strong>Extended Warehouse Management</strong>), <strong>Infios</strong> (formerly <strong>Körber Supply Chain Software</strong>), <strong>Microlistics</strong>, <strong>Tecsys</strong>, <strong>HighJump</strong> (formerly <strong>Körber</strong>), <strong>Made4net</strong>, <strong>Softeon</strong>, and <strong>Logiwa</strong> as key ecosystem players.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, major U.S. and global <strong>3PL</strong> and retailer automation early adopters include <strong>Amazon</strong> (acquired <strong>Kiva Systems</strong> in 2012; now <strong>Amazon Robotics</strong>), <strong>Walmart</strong> (Symbotic, Alphabot), <strong>Kroger</strong> (<strong>Ocado</strong> CFC), <strong>Target</strong>, <strong>Home Depot</strong>, <strong>FedEx</strong> (<strong>Berkshire Grey</strong>), <strong>UPS</strong>, <strong>DHL Supply Chain</strong>, <strong>GXO Logistics</strong> (CEO <strong>Malcolm Wilson</strong>; Symbotic, <strong>6 River Systems</strong>), and <strong>Ryder</strong> (<strong>Locus Robotics</strong>) and <strong>NFI</strong> as major U.S. adopters. <strong>MHI</strong> (<strong>Material Handling Institute</strong>), <strong>ProMat</strong>/<strong>MODEX</strong> are the main industry trade shows. The <strong>Robotic Industries Association</strong> (<strong>RIA</strong>; now <strong>A3 Association for Advancing Automation</strong>) and <strong>International Federation of Robotics</strong> (<strong>IFR</strong>) are the main industry organizations. The concept of "dark warehouse" is not yet widely prevalent — but the fully automated warehouses of <strong>Alibaba Cainiao</strong> and <strong>JD Logistics</strong> in China represent the closest real-world examples. As a result, <strong>Richardson's</strong> vision of warehouse automation suggests the global warehouse sector is being fundamentally redesigned — with <strong>AMR</strong>, <strong>cobot</strong>, and <strong>AS/RS</strong> appearing to be major strategic priorities for supply chain managers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Mark Richardson</strong> (<strong>Ocado Intelligent Automation</strong>) envisions a "dark warehouse" future.<br>
2. Labor scarcity and costs are the primary drivers of automation adoption.<br>
3. <strong>Symbotic</strong>, <strong>AutoStore</strong>, and <strong>Dematic</strong> are major ecosystem players.<br>
4. <strong>Amazon</strong>, <strong>Walmart</strong>, and <strong>Kroger</strong> are leading U.S. retail early adopters.<br>
5. <strong>WMS</strong>/<strong>WES</strong> — <strong>Manhattan</strong>, <strong>Blue Yonder</strong>, <strong>Infios</strong> — are the main software layer.</p>
