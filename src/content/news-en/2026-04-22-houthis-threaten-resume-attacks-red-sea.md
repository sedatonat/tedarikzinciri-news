<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; analyst insight; <strong>Yemen</strong>-based <strong>Houthi</strong> rebels are threatening to resume attacks on commercial vessels moving through the <strong>Red Sea</strong> region unless <strong>Israel</strong> reopens <strong>Gaza</strong> to international aid. <strong>Israel</strong> began imposing a full blockade on all aid and supplies to <strong>Gaza</strong> on March 2—followed the next week by cutting electricity to the region—demanding that <strong>Hamas</strong> return 24 living Israeli hostages the terrorist group continues to hold since the two sides agreed to a ceasefire in January. On March 7, <strong>Yemen</strong>-based <strong>Houthis</strong> issued an ultimatum to <strong>Israel</strong>: lift the blockade against <strong>Gaza</strong> by March 11, or the rebel group will resume attacks on vessels in the <strong>Red Sea</strong> and <strong>Gulf of Aden</strong>—according to <strong>Al Jazeera</strong> reporting.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Houthi</strong> movement (officially <strong>Ansar Allah</strong>; "Supporters of God"), led by <strong>Abdul-Malik al-Houthi</strong>, controls the capital <strong>Sanaa</strong> in north-central <strong>Yemen</strong> and has been a party to the <strong>Yemen</strong> civil war since 2014, backed by <strong>Iran</strong>. From November 2023 through the January 2025 ceasefire, <strong>Houthis</strong> have attacked 100+ commercial vessels in the <strong>Red Sea</strong>—using ballistic missiles, unmanned aerial vehicles, naval mines, and <strong>USV</strong> (unmanned surface vehicles) as primary attack weapons. <strong>Galaxy Leader</strong> (November 2023; <strong>Ray Car Carriers</strong>), <strong>Tutor</strong> (June 2024), <strong>Rubymar</strong> (March 2024), and <strong>True Confidence</strong> (March 2024) are among the main vessels sunk or damaged. The impact of the <strong>Red Sea</strong> crisis has caused <strong>Suez Canal</strong> traffic to decline approximately 70–80 percent—as container vessels divert to the <strong>Cape of Good Hope</strong> route—resulting in +10–14 days additional transit time, +1,500 nautical miles of additional distance, and +30–40 percent increased fuel consumption as major impacts. <strong>Bab el-Mandeb</strong>, the southern entrance to the <strong>Red Sea</strong>, represents a critical chokepoint.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Operation Prosperity Guardian</strong> (launched December 2023) operates under the umbrella of the <strong>Combined Maritime Forces</strong> (<strong>CMF</strong>; <strong>Bahrain</strong>)—a 20+ nation coalition led by the United States, with major U.S. contributions including the <strong>U.S. Navy 5th Fleet</strong>, <strong>USS Dwight D. Eisenhower</strong>, and <strong>USS Theodore Roosevelt</strong>. <strong>EU Operation Aspides</strong> (launched February 2024) represents a joint <strong>European Union</strong> <strong>Red Sea</strong> defense operation—with <strong>France</strong>, <strong>Germany</strong>, <strong>Italy</strong>, <strong>Greece</strong>, <strong>Belgium</strong>, <strong>Netherlands</strong>, <strong>Denmark</strong>, and <strong>Spain</strong> among the main <strong>Aspides</strong> participants. <strong>UK Royal Navy Operation Poseidon Archer</strong> represents an additional major coalition operation. Affected major ocean container carriers—<strong>Maersk</strong>, <strong>MSC</strong>, <strong>CMA CGM</strong>, <strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong>, <strong>ONE</strong>, <strong>Evergreen</strong>, <strong>HMM</strong>, <strong>Yang Ming</strong>, <strong>Zim</strong>—are all avoiding the <strong>Red Sea</strong>. <strong>COSCO Shipping</strong> and <strong>OOCL</strong> (<strong>COSCO</strong> subsidiary) are partially exempted by the <strong>Houthis</strong>—being <strong>China</strong>-flagged and -owned vessels.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Suez Canal Authority</strong> (<strong>SCA</strong>; <strong>Osama Rabie</strong>, Chairman; <strong>Ismailia, Egypt</strong>) saw fiscal year 2023 revenues of 9.4 billion dollars decline to approximately 5 billion dollars in fiscal year 2024—representing approximately 50 percent revenue loss as a result of the <strong>Houthi</strong> crisis as a major economic impact. The <strong>Egyptian General Authority for Maritime Safety</strong> (<strong>EGAMS</strong>) is <strong>Egypt</strong>'s primary maritime security authority. <strong>BIMCO</strong> (<strong>Baltic and International Maritime Council</strong>; <strong>Copenhagen</strong>) and the <strong>International Chamber of Shipping</strong> (<strong>ICS</strong>) are major global maritime shipping sector advocacy groups. The <strong>Joint War Committee</strong> (<strong>Lloyd's</strong> market) lists the <strong>Red Sea</strong> as a high-risk war zone—with additional war risk insurance premiums reaching 0.7–1.0 percent of vessel value. <strong>Drewry World Container Index</strong>, <strong>Freightos Baltic Index</strong> (<strong>FBX</strong>), and <strong>Xeneta XSI</strong> are major container freight rate tracking indices—experiencing 200–300 percent spikes during the <strong>Red Sea</strong> crisis. As a result, renewed <strong>Houthi</strong> threats appear to be fundamentally reshaping global <strong>Red Sea</strong> capacity strategy—making <strong>Cape</strong> route planning and war risk insurance major strategic priorities for supply chain managers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Houthis</strong> are threatening to resume attacks unless the blockade on <strong>Gaza</strong> is lifted.<br>
2. 100+ commercial vessels were attacked between November 2023 and January 2025.<br>
3. <strong>Suez</strong> traffic has declined 70–80 percent; <strong>Cape</strong> route adds +10–14 days additional transit.<br>
4. <strong>Operation Prosperity Guardian</strong> and <strong>EU Aspides</strong> are major coalition operations.<br>
5. <strong>SCA</strong> is experiencing approximately 50 percent revenue loss due to the <strong>Houthi</strong> crisis.</p>
