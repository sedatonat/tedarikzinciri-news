<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports that trade tensions between the <strong>U.S.</strong> and <strong>China</strong> continue to intensify—and American retailers are working to reduce their dependence on Chinese manufacturing. During an earnings call on May 21, <strong>Target</strong> Chief Commercial Officer <strong>Rick Gomez</strong> said the company has reduced the share of products sourced from <strong>China</strong> from 60% in 2017 to 30% today, with plans to further lower that total to 25% by the end of 2026. <strong>Gap</strong> CEO <strong>Richard Dickson</strong> outlined similar plans during an earnings call on May 29—<strong>China</strong> was once among the retailer's top sourcing countries—but expects the company's products sourced there to account for less than 3% by the end of 2025. <strong>Macy's</strong> CEO <strong>Tony Spring</strong> also stated during an earnings call on May 28 that an estimated 18% of products from national brands, which represent the majority of the retailer's sales, are sourced from <strong>China</strong>—down from 20% at the end of the previous fiscal year. Across all three retailers, a common driver of the shift in sourcing strategies has been tariffs—with most concerns centered around <strong>China</strong>. And President <strong>Trump</strong> frequently hesitates to implement his most aggressive tariff plans.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Target Corporation</strong>, based in <strong>Minneapolis, Minnesota, U.S.</strong>, with CEO <strong>Brian Cornell</strong> and Chief Commercial Officer <strong>Rick Gomez</strong>, generated 2024 annual revenue of $107 billion, operates 1,978 stores, and is a leading mass retailer in the <strong>U.S.</strong> <strong>Gap Inc.</strong>, based in <strong>San Francisco, California, U.S.</strong>, with CEO <strong>Richard Dickson</strong> (formerly of <strong>Mattel</strong>), has primary brands including <strong>Old Navy</strong>, <strong>Banana Republic</strong>, and <strong>Athleta</strong>, with 2024 revenue of $15 billion. <strong>Macy's Inc.</strong>, based in <strong>New York, NY</strong>, with CEO <strong>Tony Spring</strong>, has primary brands including <strong>Macy's</strong>, <strong>Bloomingdale's</strong>, and <strong>Bluemercury</strong>, generated 2024 revenue of $23 billion, and is a leading department store operator. <strong>Walmart</strong>, based in <strong>Bentonville, Arkansas, U.S.</strong>, with CEO <strong>Doug McMillon</strong>, generated 2024 revenue of $681 billion and is the world's largest retailer. All four companies are evaluating <strong>Vietnam</strong>, <strong>India</strong>, <strong>Bangladesh</strong>, <strong>Indonesia</strong>, <strong>Cambodia</strong>, <strong>Mexico</strong>, <strong>Honduras</strong>, <strong>Guatemala</strong>, <strong>Pakistan</strong>, and <strong>Turkey</strong> as primary nearshoring and friendshoring target countries.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>U.S.</strong> textile and apparel import data from the <strong>Office of Textiles and Apparel</strong> (<strong>OTEXA</strong>; <strong>U.S. Department of Commerce</strong>) shows that imports from <strong>China</strong> fell to 22% in 2024—down from 40% in 2010—while <strong>Vietnam</strong> rose to 18%, <strong>Bangladesh</strong> accounts for 9%, <strong>India</strong> 8%, <strong>Indonesia</strong> 5%, and <strong>Mexico</strong> 4%. The <strong>U.S. Customs and Border Protection</strong> (<strong>CBP</strong>) automatically detains cotton, tomatoes, polysilicon, and other products originating from <strong>Xinjiang</strong> under the <strong>Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act</strong> (<strong>UFLPA</strong>)—requiring retailers to completely sever supply chain ties to <strong>Xinjiang</strong>. The <strong>U.S. Trade Representative</strong> (<strong>USTR</strong>; <strong>Jamieson Greer</strong>) continues to maintain <strong>Section 301</strong> tariffs on <strong>China</strong>. The <strong>Generalized System of Preferences</strong> (<strong>GSP</strong>), <strong>Caribbean Basin Initiative</strong> (<strong>CBI</strong>), <strong>African Growth and Opportunity Act</strong> (<strong>AGOA</strong>), and <strong>USMCA</strong> are primary trade preference programs—offering retailers alternative sourcing options.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, nearshoring and friendshoring strategies focus on: (1) maquiladora and free trade zones in <strong>Mexico</strong> and <strong>Honduras</strong>; (2) industrial parks in <strong>Bac Ninh</strong> and <strong>Hai Phong</strong> in <strong>Vietnam</strong>; (3) textile clusters in <strong>Tamil Nadu</strong> in <strong>India</strong>; (4) apparel clusters in <strong>Dhaka</strong> in <strong>Bangladesh</strong>; (5) industrial hubs in <strong>Java</strong> in <strong>Indonesia</strong>; (6) apparel centers in <strong>Phnom Penh</strong> in <strong>Cambodia</strong>; (7) textile production in <strong>Pakistan</strong>; (8) export centers in <strong>Bursa</strong>, <strong>Istanbul</strong>, and <strong>Izmir</strong> in <strong>Turkey</strong>; (9) apparel production in <strong>Guatemala</strong> and <strong>El Salvador</strong>; and (10) textile investments in <strong>Egypt</strong>. Establishing new manufacturing facilities typically takes 18–24 months, with full scaling requiring 36–48 months—providing limited short-term flexibility for retailers responding urgently to tariff changes. As a result, the aggressive <strong>China</strong> reduction plans announced by <strong>Target</strong>, <strong>Gap</strong>, and <strong>Macy's</strong> appear to signal a restructuring of the global textile, apparel, and consumer products supply chain—with supply chain managers making source diversification and nearshoring primary strategic priorities.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Target</strong> (<strong>Rick Gomez</strong>) has reduced <strong>China</strong> share from 60% in 2017 to 30% today; 2026 year-end target is 25%.<br>
2. <strong>Gap</strong> (<strong>Richard Dickson</strong>) plans to reduce <strong>China</strong> share to below 3% by end of 2025.<br>
3. <strong>Macy's</strong> (<strong>Tony Spring</strong>) has reduced national brand <strong>China</strong> share to 18%—down from 20%.<br>
4. <strong>Trump</strong> tariffs serve as a common driving factor.<br>
5. <strong>Vietnam</strong>, <strong>India</strong>, and <strong>Bangladesh</strong> are primary alternative sourcing countries.</p>
