<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> video segment features <strong>Robert J. Bowman</strong> discussing the evolution of scenario planning and how it is being "empowered" by technology today with <strong>Ruth Abbott</strong>, senior business consultant at <strong>John Galt Solutions</strong>. Scenario planning provides companies with a "control element" in a world of constant change, complexity, and disruption—according to <strong>Abbott</strong>. It involves creating multiple possible situations and "pulling different levers" to understand the impact of specific decisions on end-to-end supply chains. Elements fed into the model include current events such as tariffs, wars, and major transportation route closures. Planners can also drill down into more localized events such as changes in promotions and production lead times, or the sudden receipt of a large customer order. By virtually testing various options, businesses gain "confidence to take the next step." The <strong>COVID-19</strong> pandemic motivated companies to move away from traditional spreadsheet-based planning toward adoption of "more sophisticated" solutions.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>John Galt Solutions Inc.</strong>, based in <strong>Chicago, Illinois</strong>, is a supply chain planning software provider founded in 1996 with <strong>Annemarie Omrod</strong> as <strong>President &amp; CEO</strong>. Its flagship product, the <strong>Atlas Planning Platform</strong>, encompasses modules for <strong>demand planning</strong>, <strong>supply planning</strong>, <strong>S&amp;OP</strong> (<strong>Sales &amp; Operations Planning</strong>), <strong>IBP</strong> (<strong>Integrated Business Planning</strong>), <strong>scenario planning</strong>, <strong>inventory optimization</strong>, and <strong>distribution planning</strong>. Customers include major U.S. consumer and retail companies such as <strong>Land O'Lakes</strong>, <strong>Krispy Kreme</strong>, <strong>Pernod Ricard</strong>, <strong>Brown-Forman</strong>, <strong>Owens Corning</strong>, <strong>Subway</strong>, <strong>Dunkin Brands</strong>, <strong>Carter's</strong>, <strong>OshKosh B'gosh</strong>, and <strong>Burlington Coat Factory</strong>. <strong>Scenario planning</strong> originates from military and energy sector backgrounds—the methodology pioneered by <strong>Pierre Wack</strong> at <strong>Royal Dutch Shell</strong> in 1970—and was employed to prepare for the 1973 OPEC oil shock. Leading scenario planning theorists include <strong>Herman Kahn</strong> (<strong>RAND Corporation</strong>, <strong>Hudson Institute</strong>) and <strong>Peter Schwartz</strong> (<strong>Global Business Network</strong>).</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, modern <strong>scenario planning</strong> incorporates techniques such as <strong>AI</strong>/<strong>ML</strong>-based simulation, <strong>digital twin</strong> modeling, <strong>Monte Carlo</strong> stochastic analysis, <strong>what-if</strong> querying, <strong>sensitivity analysis</strong>, <strong>optimization</strong>, <strong>stress testing</strong>, and <strong>risk-adjusted forecasting</strong>. Leading planning platforms include <strong>Kinaxis Maestro</strong> (formerly <strong>RapidResponse</strong>), <strong>o9 Solutions</strong>, <strong>Anaplan</strong>, <strong>Blue Yonder Luminate</strong>, <strong>SAP IBP</strong>, <strong>Oracle SCP</strong>, <strong>OMP Plus</strong>, <strong>ToolsGroup</strong>, <strong>Logility Atlas</strong>, <strong>Relex Solutions</strong>, <strong>Manhattan Active Supply Chain Planning</strong>, <strong>Coupa</strong>, <strong>Tecsys</strong>, <strong>John Galt Atlas</strong>, <strong>SAS Demand-Driven Planning</strong>, <strong>Demand Solutions</strong>, <strong>FuturMaster</strong>, and <strong>FlexAI</strong>. Key modern techniques include <strong>concurrent planning</strong>, <strong>continuous planning</strong>, <strong>multi-echelon inventory optimization</strong> (<strong>MEIO</strong>), <strong>probabilistic forecasting</strong>, and <strong>causal forecasting</strong>. Analyst firms such as <strong>Gartner</strong>, <strong>Forrester</strong>, <strong>IDC</strong>, <strong>Nucleus Research</strong>, and <strong>ISG</strong> evaluate scenario planning software.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the 2020-2025 period has made scenario planning a strategic imperative for companies due to: (1) the <strong>COVID-19</strong> pandemic (2020-2022), a global supply shock; (2) the <strong>Suez Canal</strong> blockage (<strong>Ever Given</strong>, March 2021); (3) the <strong>Russia-Ukraine</strong> war (February 2022–), creating energy, food, fertilizer, and metals shocks; (4) the <strong>Red Sea</strong> crisis (<strong>Houthi</strong> attacks, November 2023–), redirecting container ships to the <strong>Cape of Good Hope</strong> route; (5) <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> tariffs (<strong>Liberation Day</strong>, April 2, 2025), altering the global trade order; (6) the <strong>Israel-Hamas</strong> war (October 2023–), creating regional geopolitical tensions; (7) geopolitical tensions in the <strong>Taiwan Strait</strong>, <strong>South China Sea</strong>, and <strong>Persian Gulf</strong>; and (8) climate change extreme weather events—droughts, floods, fires, and hurricanes. <strong>Black swan</strong>, <strong>gray rhino</strong>, <strong>antifragility</strong>, <strong>resilience</strong>, <strong>agility</strong>, and <strong>adaptability</strong> are key scenario planning concepts championed by leading theorists such as <strong>Nassim Nicholas Taleb</strong> and <strong>Michele Wucker</strong>. Ultimately, the vision shared by <strong>Ruth Abbott</strong> of <strong>John Galt Solutions</strong> represents the transition of scenario planning from the spreadsheet era to the <strong>AI</strong>-enabled continuous simulation era—an indispensable decision support tool for modern supply chain leaders.</p>
