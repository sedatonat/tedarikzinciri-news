<p style="text-align: justify">According to an official post from Singapore's foreign ministry, Indonesia's tentative move to explore transit charges on ships using the Strait of Malacca drew immediate regional pushback, with Singapore and Malaysia restating that the artery will remain open and toll-free.</p>

<p style="text-align: justify">The discussion was triggered on 22 April when Indonesian Finance Minister Purbaya Yudhi Sadewa raised the possibility of levying vessels transiting the strait, referencing similar practices in the Strait of Hormuz. No formal policy or government release has followed, and Jakarta's clearest position has come from foreign ministry spokesperson Yvonne Mewengkang, who said Indonesia's approach to international shipping lanes "will always be in line with international law, especially UNCLOS."</p>

<p style="text-align: justify">Singapore moved quickly to shut down the idea. Foreign Minister Vivian Balakrishnan said the littoral states "do not have tolls" and made clear that keeping the route open is a shared strategic interest. Malaysia took the same stance; the strait remains one of the critical arteries of global trade.</p>
