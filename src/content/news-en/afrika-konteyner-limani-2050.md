<p style="text-align: justify">Sea-Intelligence's latest logistics assessment indicates that Africa will become one of the new growth engines for global container volumes through 2050. According to the report, <strong>5% annual container volume growth</strong> is feasible across the continent. This rate reflects a strong demand projection rooted in long-term structural dynamics. However, as of December 2025, operational reality falls far short of this potential. Global route shifts triggered by the Red Sea crisis have subjected African ports to a serious stress test, and most are failing it.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Sea-Intelligence's "<strong>demographics as destiny</strong>" approach explains Africa's containerization potential through population dynamics. The continent's population has reached <strong>1.5 billion</strong> and is expected to grow to <strong>2.5 billion</strong> by 2050. This makes Africa one of the world's last major untapped markets for containerized consumer goods. According to the report, the container density Africa has achieved today resembles <strong>1990s Asia</strong>. Therefore, with strengthened economic integration—such as the <strong>AfCFTA (African Continental Free Trade Area)</strong>—significant volume increases are expected in both imported consumer goods and exported raw materials.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">While the picture supports this long-term potential, <strong>2025 realities</strong> tell a completely different story. Due to attacks in the Red Sea, since late 2023 the majority of vessels have been rerouting via the <strong>Cape of Good Hope</strong>. This has transformed African waters into a global transit corridor. Theoretically, this massive traffic should have created opportunity for African ports; in practice, however, <strong>South African ports</strong> cannot manage it.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Durban</strong> ranked <strong>last</strong> in the World Bank's 2024/2025 container terminal efficiency rankings. <strong>Cape Town</strong> is only a few positions ahead of Durban in performance. The operational inefficiency of these two ports creates significant delays for vessels transiting the Cape route. During the December–January period, Cape Town's chronic problem—the <strong>Cape Doctor</strong> winds—comes into play. Strong winds frequently halt crane operations and cause <strong>7–10 day waiting periods</strong> for some vessels.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This situation produced a clear outcome for logistics operators: major shipping lines have actively deployed an <strong>avoidance strategy</strong> toward Durban and Cape Town. Many services have shifted operations such as bunkering and crew changes to more functional ports like <strong>Port Louis (Mauritius)</strong> and <strong>Walvis Bay (Namibia)</strong>. These ports serve as an "operational buffer" to prevent excessive waiting periods at South African ports.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">During the same period, <strong>asymmetric growth</strong> has become apparent in Africa's northern and western corridors. In other words, growth across the continent is uneven; it is concentrated in certain hubs. <strong>Tanger Med (Morocco)</strong> has become the region's most efficient mega-hub. <strong>Lomé (Togo)</strong> is among West Africa's leading <strong>transshipment node</strong> points. In contrast, major ports in southern Africa lag behind due to infrastructure shortcomings.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>investment gap</strong> highlighted by Sea-Intelligence is the key to this growth potential. Investors such as DP World and APM Terminals are building new container terminals in Africa or modernizing existing facilities. Large-scale projects continue at ports including <strong>Dar es Salaam</strong>, <strong>Maputo</strong>, <strong>Lekki</strong>, <strong>Berbera</strong>, and <strong>Doraleh</strong>. The aim is to bypass the bottleneck effect of traditional hubs like Durban and Cape Town.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In the long term, Africa's container demand presents a strong growth trajectory. However, the reality of December 2025 shows that the continent remains far from the <strong>operational capacity</strong> needed to meet this demand. Despite significant demand potential, the continent continues to be characterized as a <strong>volatile transit route</strong> for shipping lines.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong></h3>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Sea-Intelligence forecasts <strong>5% annual growth</strong> in Africa's container market through 2050.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The population increase from 1.5 billion to 2.5 billion creates substantial consumption potential.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">However, 2025 operational reality is challenging: Durban ranks <strong>last in the world</strong>, and Cape Town causes <strong>7–10 day delays</strong> due to winds.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Shipping lines are shifting toward alternative ports such as Port Louis and Walvis Bay.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Growth is concentrating at efficient hubs like Tanger Med and Lomé.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Despite its potential, Africa remains a <strong>high-risk transit zone</strong> as of 2025.</p></li>
</ul>
