<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Bloomberg</strong> reports that the number of shipping containers carrying U.S. imports declined for a second consecutive month — a key indicator — as President <strong>Donald Trump</strong>'s tariffs disrupt overseas goods purchases, putting the economic metric on track for one of the sharpest annual reversals on record. Veteran industry analyst <strong>John McCown</strong> wrote in a monthly report based on the 10 largest <strong>U.S.</strong> ports on July 20 that inbound container volume in June fell 7.9% compared to the prior year — following a 6.6% decline in May. The declines have completely erased the roughly 10% increase linked to inventory front-loading in April — and left the second quarter 1.8% lower compared to the prior year. "The downward turn in 2025 will depend on tariffs — and there is currently nothing to suggest that it will be short-lived," <strong>McCown</strong> wrote. After last year's 15% gain, "it is now most likely that there will generally be a decline in 2025."</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>U.S. container imports</strong> represent the single largest demand source for global supply chains — with over 25 million <strong>TEU</strong> (<strong>Twenty-Foot Equivalent Units</strong>) in annual imports — over 40% from <strong>China</strong>; 15% from <strong>Vietnam</strong>; 8% from <strong>India</strong>; 5% from <strong>South Korea</strong>; 4% from <strong>Japan</strong>; 4% from <strong>Germany</strong>; 3% from <strong>Taiwan</strong>; 3% from <strong>Indonesia</strong>; 3% from <strong>Thailand</strong> — the leading export sources. The <strong>U.S.</strong>'s 10 largest container ports are <strong>Port of Los Angeles</strong> (1); <strong>Port of Long Beach</strong> (2) — these two in <strong>San Pedro Bay</strong> — with over 18 million <strong>TEU</strong> annually; <strong>Port of New York-New Jersey</strong> (3); <strong>Port of Savannah</strong> (4); <strong>Port of Houston</strong> (5); <strong>Northwest Seaport Alliance</strong> (<strong>Tacoma+Seattle</strong>; 6); <strong>Port of Virginia</strong> (7); <strong>Port of Charleston</strong> (8); <strong>Port of Oakland</strong> (9); <strong>Port of Baltimore</strong> (10). <strong>John McCown</strong> of <strong>Blue Alpha Capital</strong> is the most respected independent analyst for <strong>U.S.</strong> maritime sector analysis — producing the monthly <strong>U.S. Top 10 Port Container Volume Report</strong>. The <strong>National Retail Federation</strong> (<strong>NRF</strong>), <strong>Hackett Associates</strong>, and the <strong>Global Port Tracker</strong> report also publish similar monthly container volume analysis.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the global container shipping sector comprises <strong>MSC</strong> (<strong>Mediterranean Shipping Company</strong>; <strong>Geneva, Switzerland</strong>; the world's largest, with 6.5+ million <strong>TEU</strong> capacity); <strong>Maersk</strong> (<strong>A.P. Moller-Maersk</strong>; <strong>Copenhagen, Denmark</strong>; 4.4 million <strong>TEU</strong>); <strong>CMA CGM</strong> (<strong>Marseille, France</strong>; 3.7 million <strong>TEU</strong>); <strong>COSCO Shipping</strong> (<strong>Shanghai, China</strong>; 3.2 million <strong>TEU</strong>); <strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong> (<strong>Hamburg, Germany</strong>; 2.3 million <strong>TEU</strong>); <strong>Evergreen Marine</strong> (<strong>Taoyuan, Taiwan</strong>; 1.7 million <strong>TEU</strong>); <strong>ONE</strong> (<strong>Ocean Network Express</strong>; <strong>Singapore</strong>; 1.9 million <strong>TEU</strong>); <strong>HMM</strong> (formerly <strong>Hyundai Merchant Marine</strong>; <strong>Seoul, Korea</strong>; 0.9 million <strong>TEU</strong>); <strong>Yang Ming Marine Transport</strong> (<strong>Keelung, Taiwan</strong>; 0.7 million <strong>TEU</strong>); <strong>ZIM Integrated Shipping</strong> (<strong>Haifa, Israel</strong>; 0.7 million <strong>TEU</strong>); <strong>Wan Hai Lines</strong>; <strong>PIL</strong> (<strong>Pacific International Lines</strong>) — the major operators. <strong>Gemini Cooperation</strong> (<strong>Maersk</strong>+<strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong>); <strong>Ocean Alliance</strong> (<strong>CMA CGM</strong>+<strong>COSCO</strong>+<strong>Evergreen</strong>); <strong>Premier Alliance</strong> (<strong>ONE</strong>+<strong>HMM</strong>+<strong>Yang Ming</strong>) are the major vessel alliances post-2025.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the primary drivers of the 2025 container volume decline are: (1) <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> tariffs (<strong>Liberation Day</strong>; April 2, 2025) with import rates from <strong>China</strong> reaching 145%+; (2) elimination of the <strong>de minimis</strong> exemption (shipments under $800) for <strong>China</strong> (<strong>Shein</strong>, <strong>Temu</strong>, <strong>AliExpress</strong> impact); (3) <strong>front-loading</strong>, the piling of orders ahead of tariffs followed by a downturn; (4) declining <strong>consumer confidence</strong>; (5) <strong>USTR Section 301</strong> shipping rate threats; (6) <strong>FMC</strong> (<strong>Federal Maritime Commission</strong>) and <strong>P&I Club</strong> uncertainties; (7) <strong>Red Sea</strong>/<strong>Suez Canal</strong> routing tensions; (8) shifting to alternative suppliers ahead of the <strong>holiday season</strong> (<strong>Vietnam</strong>, <strong>Mexico</strong>, <strong>India</strong>, <strong>Bangladesh</strong>, <strong>Cambodia</strong> rotation). <strong>Drewry World Container Index</strong>; <strong>Shanghai Containerized Freight Index</strong> (<strong>SCFI</strong>); <strong>China Containerized Freight Index</strong> (<strong>CCFI</strong>); <strong>Freightos Baltic Index</strong> (<strong>FBX</strong>) are the primary spot container freight rate indices. In conclusion, <strong>John McCown</strong>'s <strong>Blue Alpha Capital</strong> monthly report crystallizes the direct and long-term impact of the <strong>U.S.</strong> economy's structural adjustment to the tariff regime on global maritime traffic — the second half of 2025 appears likely to be challenging for global carriers in terms of capacity and pricing.</p>
