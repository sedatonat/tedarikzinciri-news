<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Ford</strong> said that a last-minute change to the Trump administration&rsquo;s tariff relief program cost the U.S. automaker $900 million more than expected in 2025. The program is meant to help carmakers offset the levies applied by the administration of U.S. President <strong>Donald Trump</strong>, allowing automakers that import parts for vehicles assembled in the U.S. to apply for credits.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">But, reports <strong>BBC News</strong>, administration officials told the company in December of a new, later effective date for the policy, leading to fewer gains from the credits than anticipated. Chief executive <strong>Jim Farley</strong> said on February 10 that Ford spent double what it had expected on tariffs in 2025 &mdash; roughly $2 billion &mdash; due to &ldquo;the unexpected and late year change in tariff credits for auto parts.&rdquo;</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Separately, <strong>Ford</strong> had previously disclosed a $19.5 billion hit as a result of its retreat from electric vehicle plans, also partly because of Trump administration policies that reversed prior efforts to wean the auto industry off fossil-fuel-powered cars. Those charges also contributed to its fourth-quarter net loss of $11.1 billion. The carmaker&rsquo;s decision to alter its EV plans aligned with a similar announcement made by <strong>General Motors</strong> in October 2025, when GM said it would take a $1.6 billion hit as it rolled back EV ambitions amid weakening demand.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, despite the tariff bill and net loss, the company reported revenue for Q4 2025 that beat analysts&rsquo; expectations. <strong>Ford</strong> sits among the dominant U.S. players alongside <strong>Stellantis</strong>, <strong>GM</strong>, <strong>Toyota</strong>, <strong>Honda</strong>, <strong>Hyundai</strong> and <strong>Volkswagen</strong>, but the tension between <strong>Section 232</strong>, <strong>USMCA</strong>, the <strong>IRA</strong> (Inflation Reduction Act) and <strong>BBB</strong> credit rules is forcing constant supply chain replanning. <em>Tier-1</em> suppliers including <strong>Magna</strong>, <strong>BorgWarner</strong>, <strong>Lear</strong> and <strong>Aptiv</strong> are rebalancing <em>nearshoring</em> footprints across Mexico, the U.S. and Canada. Investments in the <strong>Battery Belt</strong>, lithium, nickel and cobalt offtake deals, and partnerships with <strong>BYD</strong>, <strong>CATL</strong>, <strong>LG Energy Solution</strong> and <strong>SK On</strong> remain at the center of automotive supply chain strategy through 2026.</p>
