<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>According to Bloomberg</strong>, a vessel carrying <strong>radioactive zinc dust</strong> is being held off the coast of <strong>Manila, the capital of the Philippines</strong>, without being allowed to enter port. The ship is transporting <strong>23 containers of radioactive cargo returned from Indonesia</strong> and has been detained by port authorities because <strong>no suitable storage or disposal facility</strong> can be found.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Carlo Arcilla</strong>, Director of the <strong>Philippine Nuclear Research Institute (PNRI)</strong>, stated on October 28 that "<strong>the external radiation levels in the containers are very low, so there is no danger to the public</strong>." He also noted that <strong>there have been no health issues reported among the ship's crew</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Background: Radiation Investigation in Indonesia</strong></h5>\n<p style="text-align: justify">The cargo in question was <strong>originally exported from the Philippines to Indonesia by a Chinese-based trading company, Zannwann International Trading Corp.</strong> However, when inspections were conducted in Indonesia, <strong>traces of cesium-137 were detected in the containers</strong>, and the shipment <strong>was sent back to Manila</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Cesium-137</strong> is <strong>a byproduct of nuclear fission</strong> commonly used in <strong>medical devices, measuring instruments, and radiation testing</strong>. However, exposure to high doses <strong>can increase cancer risk</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Indonesian authorities had detected similar <strong>cesium-137 contamination at a metal processing center in West Java in September 2025</strong> and <strong>temporarily suspended scrap metal imports</strong> following that incident.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Initial Alert from the United States: The Frozen Shrimp Incident</strong></h5>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Behind these developments lies a report issued by the <strong>U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in August 2025</strong>. The report noted that <strong>trace amounts of cesium-137 had been detected in frozen shrimp from Indonesia</strong>, and <strong>major retailers such as Walmart</strong> recalled certain products. Following this, Indonesia <strong>began inspecting all metal and chemical shipments at ports</strong> due to suspicions of <strong>nuclear isotope contamination</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Contamination Suspected to Originate in the Philippines</strong></h5>\n<p style="text-align: justify">According to PNRI Director Arcilla, the <strong>radioactive zinc dust in the returned containers</strong> may have been sourced from <strong>SteelAsia Manufacturing Corp.</strong> and another local steel producer in the Philippines. However, Arcilla noted that <strong>the radioactivity was detected only at SteelAsia's recycling facility in Batangas</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SteelAsia</strong>, the Philippines' largest steel producer, stated that it <strong>had no connection to the containers in question</strong> and <strong>voluntarily suspended facility operations as a precaution</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Zannwann International Trading Corp.</strong> did not respond to requests for comment. The <strong>Philippine Port Authority (PPA)</strong> referred questions to the <strong>Bureau of Customs</strong>, but no official statement has been issued from that agency.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>The Challenge: Where to Dispose of the Waste</strong></h5>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Arcilla identified the primary problem as the inability to find a location where <strong>the 23 containers can be safely disposed of</strong>. The Philippines currently <strong>does not have a permanent disposal facility for radioactive waste</strong>. As a result, the government is <strong>searching for a temporary storage area by consulting with local authorities</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">He also noted that <strong>other potentially radioactive materials at the SteelAsia facility and in Zannwann's warehouse</strong> will need to be <strong>cleaned up</strong>. Arcilla stressed that "there could be significant quantities there" and that the cleanup effort is a national priority.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Regional Nuclear Security Concern</strong></h5>\n<p style="text-align: justify">According to experts, this incident has highlighted <strong>growing concerns in Southeast Asia about radioactive contamination and isotope leaks</strong>. The <strong>scrap metal recycling sectors in the region</strong> typically operate <strong>outside international control chains</strong>, which increases <strong>the risk of nuclear isotopes infiltrating civilian supply chains</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">So far, <strong>no radiation has been detected in Zannwann's previous shipments</strong>, but authorities have <strong>not yet confirmed whether the current incident is connected to the cesium-137 contamination in Indonesia</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">The priority for Philippine officials now is <strong>to safely unload the ship waiting off the coast of Manila</strong> and <strong>to permanently dispose of the radioactive cargo</strong>. However, <strong>the ship will not be allowed to enter port until a suitable facility is found</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Points:</strong></h5>\n<ul>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">A vessel carrying <strong>23 containers of radioactive zinc dust</strong> is <strong>being held off Manila</strong>.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The cargo was <strong>returned by Indonesia following detection of cesium-137</strong>.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>PNRI Director Carlo Arcilla:</strong> "The external radiation of the containers is low; there is no danger to the public."</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The radioactive material is being linked to <strong>SteelAsia's Batangas facility</strong>; the company suspended operations.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Zannwann International Trading Corp.</strong> was identified as the exporter but has not provided comment.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>Philippines</strong> is currently <strong>searching for a facility to dispose of radioactive waste</strong>.</p></li>\n  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The incident has renewed attention to <strong>rising nuclear contamination risks in Southeast Asia</strong>.</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p><br></p>\n<p>----------</p>\n<p><strong>News Link:</strong> <a href="https://www.supplychainbrain.com/articles/42738-ship-with-radioactive-zinc-dust-stuck-near-philippines-port"><u>https://www.supplychainbrain.com/articles/42738-ship-with-radioactive-zinc-dust-stuck-near-philippines-port</u></a></p>\n<p>--------------------</p>\n<p><strong>Author: </strong><a href="http://sedatonat.com/"><u>SedatOnat.com</u></a></p>\n<p>--------------------</p>\n<p><strong>!!!
