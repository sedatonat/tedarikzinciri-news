<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>U.S. President Donald Trump</strong>'s second term is advancing with trade policy <strong>rapidly and frequently shifting direction</strong>. The President signed a series of <strong>executive orders</strong> on <strong>February 1, 2025</strong>, encompassing tariffs on Canada, Mexico, and China; subsequently, he launched <strong>numerous tariff reviews</strong> and product-category investigations throughout the year. During this process, as the scope and timeline of some threats frequently changed, <strong>uncertainty increased among the business community and foreign governments</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The landscape was reshaped following the announcement of the <strong>reciprocal tariff policy</strong> on <strong>April 2</strong>. Over the summer, <strong>new country-specific tariff rates</strong> were codified and took effect on <strong>August 7</strong>. During the same period, the U.S. negotiated various <strong>tariff agreements</strong> and <strong>framework accords</strong> with the <strong>European Union, Japan, and South Korea</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Meanwhile, the "<strong>increasingly hardening</strong>" tariff dispute between the U.S. and <strong>China</strong> escalated throughout the year with tit-for-tat moves. <strong>By end of October</strong>, the White House signaled that a <strong>trade ceasefire</strong> was within reach; following the Trump–Xi meeting in Busan on October 30, <strong>rate reductions and suspensions</strong> were announced on certain items.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Pushback also mounted from <strong>legislative and judicial branches</strong> against executive actions. The <strong>U.S. International Trade Court (CIT)</strong> issued a <strong>preliminary injunction</strong> that would <strong>invalidate certain tariffs</strong> imposed under the <strong>International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA)</strong>. The ruling is <strong>on appeal</strong>, and the <strong>Supreme Court is expected to rule in November</strong>. This process shows that a portion of 2025 tariffs remain under <strong>legal risk</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In this rapidly changing environment, <strong>Supply Chain Dive</strong> published a <strong>live tracking dashboard</strong> compiling the status of <strong>major tariff moves</strong> undertaken by the U.S. and its trade partners since early 2025. The list is filterable by <strong>sector</strong> or <strong>country</strong> and is <strong>updated as new decisions emerge</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The report's main highlights:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Policy flow</strong>: Since January, the executive branch has pursued <strong>multiple executive orders</strong>, <strong>301/232 investigations</strong>, and <strong>country-specific rate updates</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Reciprocal tariffs</strong>: Following the April 2 launch, <strong>country-specific rates</strong> went into effect from <strong>August 7</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Bilateral agreements</strong>: The U.S. announced tariff <strong>reductions/alignment</strong> and <strong>framework</strong> accords with the <strong>EU, Japan, and South Korea</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>China dossier</strong>: Trade tensions marked by <strong>retaliatory moves</strong> throughout the year shifted to a <strong>ceasefire</strong> footing by <strong>end of October</strong> (with rate reductions on certain items, suspension of investigations, and temporary softening in areas such as rare earths).</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Legal challenges</strong>: The <strong>CIT</strong>'s <strong>preliminary injunction</strong> against <strong>IEEPA</strong>-based tariffs will be decided by the <strong>Supreme Court in November</strong>; the outcome could set <strong>precedent</strong> for numerous tariffs.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">The updated dashboard aims to allow companies to <strong>monitor status</strong> across these areas:</p>
<ol>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Tariff levels and effective dates</strong> (general rates, product-specific exemptions),</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Investigations and interim measures</strong> (301/232 scope, port fees, sector-focused reviews),</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Countermeasures</strong> (retaliations by the EU, China, Canada, Mexico, and others),</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Temporary suspension/ceasefire provisions</strong> (annual review clauses, timelines),</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Litigation timeline</strong> (preliminary injunctions, appeals, and Supreme Court schedule).</p></li>
</ol>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Conclusion:</strong> Throughout 2025, the <strong>tariff regime has been volatile</strong>; while <strong>high rates</strong> remain in effect on one hand, <strong>bilateral agreements</strong> and <strong>temporary ceasefires</strong> have complicated the picture on the other. <strong>Cost, uncertainty, and compliance burden</strong> continue for businesses; consequently, <strong>real-time monitoring</strong>, <strong>supply chain restructuring</strong> (near/reshoring, supplier diversification), and <strong>customs strategy</strong> (e.g., tariff engineering, classification, and origin optimization) have become critical.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Important Notes:</strong></h5>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">From <strong>August 7</strong> onward, <strong>country-specific tariff rates</strong> are in effect; <strong>agreement/framework</strong> steps have been taken with the EU, Japan, and South Korea.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">On the <strong>U.S.–China</strong> front, a <strong>ceasefire</strong> footing was established by <strong>end of October</strong>; certain rate reductions/suspensions were announced.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>CIT</strong> issued a <strong>preliminary injunction</strong> against <strong>IEEPA tariffs</strong>; the case is before the <strong>Supreme Court in November</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The trade environment remains <strong>volatile</strong>: <strong>cost management, compliance, and supply chain redesign</strong> are priorities for companies.</p></li>
