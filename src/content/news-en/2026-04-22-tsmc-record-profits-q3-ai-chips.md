<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Taiwanese</strong> chip maker <strong>TSMC</strong> reported approximately 40% year-over-year growth in third-quarter profits, driven by surging global demand for advanced chips running artificial intelligence. According to the <strong>Associated Press</strong>, <strong>TSMC</strong> recorded 15 billion dollars in net profit between July and September, marking a record high for a single quarter and exceeding analyst projections of 13.6 billion dollars. The company also reported revenue surpassing 33 billion dollars, beating analyst projections by over 1 billion dollars. This follows <strong>TSMC</strong>'s report of 61% year-over-year profit growth in the second quarter, which the company attributed to strong demand from major chip customers. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company</strong> (<strong>TSMC</strong>), based in <strong>Hsinchu Taiwan</strong>, was founded in 1987 by <strong>Morris Chang</strong> and is the global leader in advanced semiconductor manufacturing. <strong>C.C. Wei</strong> is <strong>TSMC</strong>'s CEO. <strong>Mark Liu</strong> serves as chairman. <strong>TSMC</strong> controls over 60% of the global foundry market and over 90% of the advanced process market (3-5nm).</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>TSMC</strong>'s global manufacturing network includes <strong>Hsinchu Science Park</strong>, <strong>Tainan Science Park</strong> (<strong>Fab 18</strong>, 3nm), <strong>Taichung</strong> (<strong>Fab 15</strong>), and <strong>Kaohsiung</strong> (2nm planned) as its primary <strong>Taiwan</strong> facilities. Overseas, the company operates <strong>Phoenix Arizona</strong> (<strong>TSMC Arizona</strong>, <strong>Fab 21</strong>, 65 billion dollar investment, 4nm, 3nm, 2nm, with plans for six total facilities), <strong>Kumamoto</strong> in <strong>Japan</strong> (<strong>JASM</strong>, a partnership with <strong>Sony</strong>, <strong>Denso</strong>, and <strong>Toyota</strong>), and <strong>Dresden</strong> in <strong>Germany</strong> (<strong>ESMC</strong>, a partnership with <strong>Bosch</strong>, <strong>Infineon</strong>, and <strong>NXP</strong>). <strong>TSMC</strong>'s major customers include <strong>Apple</strong> (<strong>A18 Pro</strong>, <strong>M4</strong>, <strong>M5</strong> series, accounting for over 20% of total revenue), <strong>Nvidia</strong> (<strong>H100</strong>, <strong>H200</strong>, <strong>B100</strong>, <strong>B200</strong>, <strong>GB200</strong>, <strong>Blackwell</strong> Architecture), <strong>AMD</strong> (<strong>EPYC</strong>, <strong>Instinct MI300X</strong>, <strong>MI325X</strong>, <strong>MI350</strong>), <strong>Qualcomm</strong> (<strong>Snapdragon</strong> series), <strong>MediaTek</strong> (<strong>Dimensity</strong>), <strong>Broadcom</strong> (<strong>AI ASIC</strong>, <strong>Google TPU</strong>, <strong>Meta MTIA</strong>), <strong>Marvell</strong>, <strong>Amazon Annapurna Labs</strong> (<strong>Trainium</strong>, <strong>Inferentia</strong>), <strong>Tesla</strong> (<strong>Dojo</strong>), <strong>Cerebras</strong>, <strong>Groq</strong>, and <strong>SambaNova</strong>, among the major fabless players.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the global <strong>AI</strong> chip boom has made <strong>Nvidia</strong> one of the world's most valuable companies with a market capitalization exceeding 4 trillion dollars. <strong>Data center capex</strong> spending by <strong>hyperscalers</strong> (<strong>Microsoft</strong>, <strong>Google</strong>, <strong>Amazon</strong>, <strong>Meta</strong>, <strong>Oracle</strong>) is projected to exceed 500 billion dollars globally in 2025, with most directed toward <strong>AI</strong> infrastructure. <strong>HBM</strong> (<strong>High Bandwidth Memory</strong>) including <strong>HBM3</strong>, <strong>HBM3E</strong>, and <strong>HBM4</strong>, with <strong>SK Hynix</strong>, <strong>Samsung</strong>, and <strong>Micron</strong> as the major suppliers. <strong>CoWoS</strong> (<strong>Chip-on-Wafer-on-Substrate</strong> from <strong>TSMC</strong>), <strong>SoIC</strong> (<strong>System on Integrated Chips</strong>), and <strong>InFO</strong> (<strong>Integrated Fan-Out</strong>) are the primary advanced packaging technologies, critical for integrating high-bandwidth memory into <strong>AI</strong> chips. <strong>TSMC</strong>'s <strong>CoWoS</strong> capacity remains a bottleneck in global <strong>AI</strong> supply. <strong>ASE</strong>, <strong>Amkor</strong>, <strong>JCET</strong>, <strong>Powertech</strong>, and <strong>SPIL</strong> (merging with <strong>ASE</strong>) are the major <strong>OSAT</strong> players. The <strong>U.S. CHIPS and Science Act</strong> (2022, 52.7 billion dollars), <strong>EU Chips Act</strong> (43 billion Euros), <strong>Japan METI</strong> incentives, <strong>Korea K-Chips Act</strong>, and <strong>India Semiconductor Mission</strong> are the primary government incentive programs.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, global semiconductor geopolitics are tightening under <strong>Trump 2.0</strong>. <strong>U.S. export controls</strong> through the <strong>BIS</strong> (<strong>Bureau of Industry and Security</strong>), <strong>Entity List</strong>, and <strong>Foreign Direct Product Rule</strong> (<strong>FDPR</strong>) are the primary export control tools for <strong>SME</strong> (<strong>Semiconductor Manufacturing Equipment</strong>). <strong>SMIC</strong> (<strong>Semiconductor Manufacturing International Corp</strong>, <strong>China</strong>), <strong>YMTC</strong> (<strong>Yangtze Memory Technologies</strong>), <strong>CXMT</strong> (<strong>ChangXin Memory Technologies</strong>), <strong>Hua Hong</strong>, and <strong>SiEn Qingdao</strong> are the major <strong>Chinese</strong> players. <strong>Huawei HiSilicon</strong> is progressing contentiously with 7nm and 5nm production through <strong>SMIC</strong> via <strong>Kirin</strong> and <strong>Ascend</strong> series. <strong>ASML</strong> is the sole global supplier of <strong>EUV</strong> equipment, with sales to <strong>China</strong> prohibited. <strong>Tokyo Electron</strong> (<strong>TEL</strong>), <strong>Applied Materials</strong>, <strong>Lam Research</strong>, <strong>KLA</strong>, <strong>Screen Holdings</strong>, and <strong>Hitachi High-Tech</strong> are the major <strong>SME</strong> players, with advanced equipment exports to <strong>China</strong> subject to multiple restrictions. The <strong>Taiwan Strait</strong> remains the most strategically critical maritime passage globally, with a potential <strong>China-Taiwan</strong> crisis capable of jeopardizing over 90% of global advanced chip supply. Ultimately, <strong>TSMC</strong>'s record Q3 profit is a concrete indicator of the structural sustainability of <strong>AI</strong>-driven chip demand and <strong>TSMC</strong>'s global geopolitical significance.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>\n1. <strong>TSMC</strong> recorded 15 billion dollars in net profit in Q3 with 40% YoY growth.<br>\n2.
