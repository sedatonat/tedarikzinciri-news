<p><strong>Union Pacific (UP)</strong> and <strong>Norfolk Southern (NS)</strong> claim that their merger could cut transit times by <strong>as much as two days</strong>, particularly in <strong>carload</strong> transportation.</p>
<p><br>
However, shippers looking at the results of past mergers are <strong>taking a cautious view of these promises.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p>The new merger plan aims to eliminate transfer delays at <strong>east-west gateways like Chicago and New Orleans</strong>, making <strong>rail more competitive with trucking.</strong> According to UP and NS, this step would <strong>reduce inventory carrying costs</strong> and allow customers to <strong>reduce their carload fleets.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5><strong>What the Merger Promises: Two Days Faster Shipments</strong></h5>
<p>The merger of UP and NS is expected to deliver the biggest benefits at <strong>transfer points</strong>, which currently cause the most delays.</p>
<p><br>
For example, a shipment from a <strong>lumber mill connected to the NS network in the Southeast</strong> to <strong>Phoenix</strong> on UP's line currently involves <strong>four intermediate transfers.</strong></p>
<p><br>
Following the merger, this number of transfers is planned to be reduced to zero.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p>UP CEO <strong>Jim Vena</strong> stated in August that "with a unified transcontinental line, direct service design under single control would enable cars to reach their destination faster."</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5><strong>Speed Gains in Theory, Uncertainty in Practice</strong></h5>
<p><strong>Railroad analyst C. Tyler Dick from the University of Texas</strong> says, "A unified line could reduce train-to-train connections, improving reliability and enabling more consistent car movement through the system."</p>
<p><br>
However, Dick points out that <strong>numerous mergers since 1980</strong> have not delivered lasting improvements in transit times:</p>
<blockquote>"Previous mergers have not addressed bottlenecks at east-west gateways. The UP–NS merger is focused precisely on this point, but results are not guaranteed."</blockquote>
<blockquote><br></blockquote>
<h5><strong>Chicago: A Chronic Bottleneck</strong></h5>
<p>Chicago remains <strong>the most critical yet most problematic</strong> junction in the U.S. rail network.<br>
According to a retired railroad executive, "UP and NS connections in Chicago are weak. Even slow-moving trains could save 24 hours, but these savings are not automatic."</p>
<p><br></p>
<p>According to the executive's explanation, for example the <strong>North Platte–Bellevue line</strong> has two routing options through Chicago:</p>
<ul>
  <li><p>One is controlled by UP–NS but <strong>conflicts with commuter trains.</strong></p></li>
  <li><p>The other is less congested but <strong>outside the full control of either railroad.</strong></p></li>
</ul>
<p>This situation means that <strong>on-time delivery cannot be guaranteed</strong> even with the UP–NS merger.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5><strong>Past Mergers: Promised Service Improvements Never Materialized</strong></h5>
<p><strong>American Chemistry Council CEO Chris Jahn</strong> states, "Single-line service should theoretically reduce transit times, but we haven't seen that in the past."</p>
<p><br></p>
<p>A <strong>railroad logistics manager at a major energy company</strong> shares a similar view:</p>
<blockquote>"Mergers have not shortened transit times enough to shrink our tank car fleet.<br>
Some routes saw improvements but there was no noticeable difference at the overall level."</blockquote>
<p>The manager points out that even in today's system, companies can operate <strong>run-through trains</strong> on each other's networks, adding, "There's no need for a merger to do that."</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5><strong>A Different Model from Canadian Lines</strong></h5>
<p>One positive example of mergers is the <strong>run-through trains launched by CN (Canadian National)</strong> and <strong>NS in Canada in 2017.</strong></p>
<p><br>
Two pairs of trains between <strong>Winnipeg (CN)</strong> and <strong>Elkhart, Indiana (NS)</strong> completely bypassed <strong>Chicago-area traffic,</strong> reducing transit time by <strong>48 hours.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p>This example shows that <strong>efficiency gains can be achieved on high-volume lines</strong> <strong>without a merger.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5><strong>Is the Merger Hope or a Repeating Story?</strong></h5>
<p>While the UP and NS merger is being received with <strong>high expectations in the railroad industry,</strong> many experts believe <strong>"every merger that promises service improvements repeats the same story."</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p>Consolidation waves since 1980 have shaped modern railroad networks; however, <strong>no lasting breakthrough in transit times and equipment efficiency has been achieved.</strong></p>
<p><br>
According to experts, <strong>the problem is not the merger itself, but the lack of infrastructure and operational investments to solve bottlenecks.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5><strong>Key Points:</strong></h5>
<ul>
  <li><p><strong>Union Pacific (UP)</strong> and <strong>Norfolk Southern (NS)</strong> aim to <strong>cut carload transit time by up to 2 days</strong> through the merger.</p></li>
  <li><p>The largest benefits are expected at <strong>east-west transfer points like Chicago and New Orleans.</strong></p></li>
  <li><p>Experts point out that <strong>mergers since 1980 have not fulfilled similar promises.</strong></p></li>
  <li><p><strong>Chronic traffic congestion in Chicago</strong> could limit potential speed gains.</p></li>
  <li><p>While <strong>single-line operations</strong> are theoretically beneficial, <strong>consistent improvements have not materialized in practice.</strong></p></li>
  <li><p><strong>The CN–NS direct line example in Canada</strong> is one of the rare examples that delivers real time savings on busy routes.</p></li>
  <li><p>Shippers are adopting a <strong>"wait-and-see" approach to witness the real benefits of the new merger.</strong></p></li>
</ul>
