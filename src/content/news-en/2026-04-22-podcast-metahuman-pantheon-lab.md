<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> podcast is examining: <strong>Robert J. Bowman</strong>, <strong>Editor-in-Chief</strong>, asks—is the "person" you're speaking with on that kiosk screen a real human? Or is it a <strong>metahuman</strong>? There is nothing particularly new about virtual humans answering queries, providing customer service, or attempting to resolve problems. However, such entities are becoming increasingly realistic in appearance, behavior, and information sourcing. In fact, we are entering the age of the <strong>metahuman</strong>—voice-enabled interfaces designed to engage users with "human-like" empathy and understanding, even possessing <strong>emotional intelligence</strong>, according to <strong>Ivan Lau</strong>, founder of <strong>Pantheon Lab</strong> and our guest for this episode. He explains how artificial intelligence, and especially large language models, are making it possible for digital humans to detect, interpret, and respond to human emotions in real time. The discussion covers where and when these metahumans will begin to emerge, what they can do, and how they may (or may not) be accepted by real humans. Are we witnessing the end of person-to-person human interaction? What does this mean for social interaction and society?</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Pantheon Lab</strong>, Hong Kong-based, with <strong>Ivan Lau</strong> as Founder &amp; CEO, is a startup specializing in generative AI and digital human technology—one of the leading developers of metahuman and AI avatar technology in the Asia-Pacific region. The company produces real-time, voice-enabled, facial-expression-capable, lip-synchronized artificial intelligence-based human-like interfaces with applications in banking, retail, healthcare, education, and hospitality sectors. Other major digital human and AI avatar providers include <strong>Synthesia</strong> (London, UK; <strong>Victor Riparbelli</strong>, CEO; AI video creation leader); <strong>HeyGen</strong> (<strong>Joshua Xu</strong>, CEO; Los Angeles); <strong>D-ID</strong> (Tel Aviv, Israel); <strong>Soul Machines</strong> (Auckland, New Zealand); <strong>UneeQ</strong> (formerly Digital Humans of New Zealand); <strong>Hour One</strong>; <strong>Replika AI</strong> (<strong>Eugenia Kuyda</strong>, Founder); <strong>Character.AI</strong> (<strong>Noam Shazeer</strong>, Founder; acquired by Google); <strong>Inworld AI</strong>; <strong>NVIDIA Audio2Face</strong>; <strong>NVIDIA Maxine</strong>; <strong>Unreal Engine MetaHuman</strong> (Epic Games); <strong>Rokoko</strong>; and other key ecosystem players.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the large language model (<strong>LLM</strong>) ecosystem includes <strong>OpenAI GPT-5</strong>, <strong>Anthropic Claude Opus 4</strong>, <strong>Google Gemini 2.5</strong>, <strong>Meta Llama 4</strong>, <strong>Mistral Large 2</strong>, <strong>Cohere Command R+</strong>, <strong>xAI Grok 4</strong>, <strong>DeepSeek-V3</strong>, <strong>Alibaba Qwen 3</strong>, <strong>Baidu Ernie 5</strong>, <strong>NVIDIA Nemotron</strong>, <strong>IBM Granite</strong>, <strong>Apple Intelligence</strong>, and <strong>Microsoft Phi-4</strong> as major foundation model providers. Multimodal AI processes text, audio, images, video, and 3D models in an integrated manner—<strong>OpenAI Sora</strong>, <strong>Google Veo</strong>, <strong>Runway Gen-3</strong>, <strong>Pika Labs</strong>, <strong>Kuaishou Kling</strong>, and <strong>Luma Dream Machine</strong> are major video generation models. Speech AI providers include <strong>OpenAI Whisper</strong>, <strong>Deepgram</strong>, <strong>AssemblyAI</strong>, <strong>ElevenLabs</strong> (realistic voice synthesis), <strong>Google WaveNet</strong>, <strong>Microsoft Azure Speech</strong>, <strong>Amazon Polly</strong>, and <strong>Cartesia AI</strong>. Affective computing and emotional AI include: (1) facial expression recognition—<strong>Affectiva</strong> (owned by Smart Eye), <strong>Realeyes</strong>; (2) voice emotion analysis—<strong>Beyond Verbal</strong>, <strong>Cogito</strong>; (3) physiological signal—<strong>Empatica</strong>; (4) multimodal emotion AI—<strong>Hume AI</strong> as major providers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, metahuman and digital human applications include: (1) customer service and call centers; (2) hospitals and healthcare facilities; (3) bank branches and financial services; (4) airport and hotel reception; (5) retail store consultants; (6) education and corporate training; (7) product launches and brand representatives; (8) entertainment and virtual influencers; (9) virtual colleagues and avatars; (10) conversational supply chain assistants in supply chain management. The <strong>EU AI Act</strong> (approved March 2024) requires transparency for deepfakes, synthetic media, and biometric recognition, particularly in high-risk categories. The <strong>Digital Services Act</strong> (<strong>DSA</strong>) mandates that platforms label AI-generated content. The <strong>FTC</strong> (U.S. Federal Trade Commission) is initiating enforcement actions against deceptive AI applications. The <strong>NIST AI Risk Management Framework</strong> (<strong>AI RMF</strong>) is a voluntary standard in the United States. <strong>ISO/IEC 42001</strong> (<strong>AI management systems</strong>) is a global AI governance standard. In conclusion, the metahuman transformation that <strong>Lau</strong> describes reflects an evolving process reshaping global customer interaction, human-machine interfaces, and work structures—AI-based interface design appears to be an emerging strategic imperative for supply chain managers.</p>
