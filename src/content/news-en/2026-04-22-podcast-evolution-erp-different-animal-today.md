<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; <strong>Robert J. Bowman</strong> (<strong>Editor-in-Chief</strong>); "<strong>The Evolution of ERP: Is It a Different Animal Today?</strong>" podcast episode; three decades after its introduction; has the <strong>ERP</strong> beast finally been tamed? <strong>Enterprise Resource Planning</strong>, which emerged under this name in the 1990s as the successor to <strong>material requirements planning</strong> (<strong>MRP</strong>), offered businesses a compelling tool for managing inventory, accounting, finance, and sales. Yet for many, implementation proved to be a massive headache — requiring years and millions of dollars to complete. Is it easier now? How does modern-day <strong>ERP</strong> differ from those earlier incarnations? This episode tackles these and other questions. The episode is sponsored by <strong>Doss</strong>. The history of <strong>ERP</strong> spans: (1) 1960s, inventory control systems; (2) 1970s, <strong>MRP</strong> (<strong>Material Requirements Planning</strong>); (3) 1980s, <strong>MRP-II</strong> (<strong>Manufacturing Resource Planning</strong>); (4) 1990s, <strong>ERP</strong> takes the stage — <strong>SAP R/3</strong> (1992), <strong>Oracle</strong>, <strong>Baan</strong>, <strong>PeopleSoft</strong>, and <strong>JD Edwards</strong> as major early players; (5) 2000s, industry-specific <strong>ERP</strong> and web-based architecture; (6) 2010s, cloud <strong>ERP</strong> (<strong>NetSuite</strong>, <strong>Workday</strong>); (7) 2020s, <strong>AI</strong>, composable <strong>ERP</strong>, and industry cloud as major evolutionary stages.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the major <strong>ERP</strong> providers include: (1) <strong>SAP SE</strong> (<strong>FRA: SAP</strong>; <strong>NYSE: SAP</strong>; CEO <strong>Christian Klein</strong>; <strong>Walldorf, Germany</strong>); principal products include <strong>S/4HANA</strong>, <strong>SAP Business One</strong>, <strong>SAP ByDesign</strong>, and <strong>RISE with SAP</strong>; leading the global <strong>ERP</strong> market; (2) <strong>Oracle Corporation</strong> (<strong>NYSE: ORCL</strong>; CEO <strong>Safra Catz</strong>; CTO <strong>Larry Ellison</strong>; <strong>Austin, Texas</strong>); principal products include <strong>Oracle Fusion Cloud ERP</strong>, <strong>Oracle E-Business Suite</strong>, <strong>NetSuite</strong> (acquired in 2016), <strong>JD Edwards EnterpriseOne</strong>, and <strong>PeopleSoft</strong>; (3) <strong>Microsoft</strong> (<strong>NASDAQ: MSFT</strong>; CEO <strong>Satya Nadella</strong>; <strong>Redmond, Washington</strong>); principal products include <strong>Dynamics 365 Finance</strong>, <strong>Dynamics 365 Supply Chain Management</strong>, and <strong>Dynamics 365 Business Central</strong>; (4) <strong>Workday</strong> (<strong>NASDAQ: WDAY</strong>; CEO <strong>Carl Eschenbach</strong>; <strong>Pleasanton, California</strong>); principal products include <strong>Workday Financial Management</strong> and <strong>Workday HCM</strong>; (5) <strong>Infor</strong> (<strong>CEO Kevin Samuelson</strong>; portfolio company of <strong>Koch Industries</strong>); principal products include <strong>CloudSuite Industrial</strong>, <strong>CloudSuite Distribution</strong>, <strong>M3</strong>, and <strong>LN</strong>; these are among the leading providers. Other major <strong>ERP</strong> providers include <strong>Epicor</strong>, <strong>IFS</strong>, <strong>QAD</strong>, <strong>Sage</strong>, <strong>Acumatica</strong>, <strong>Plex Systems</strong>, <strong>Rootstock</strong>, <strong>Unit4</strong>, <strong>Deltek</strong>, <strong>SYSPRO</strong>, <strong>Odoo</strong>, <strong>SAP Business ByDesign</strong>, and <strong>Sage Intacct</strong>; key ecosystem players.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Doss</strong>, headquartered in <strong>San Francisco, California, U.S.</strong> under CEO <strong>Wiley Jones</strong>, offers a modern <strong>ERP</strong> alternative globally — challenging traditional <strong>ERP</strong> with AI-driven, headless, and composable architecture. The key differentiators of modern <strong>ERP</strong> include: (1) cloud-native and SaaS deployment; (2) API-first architecture; (3) microservices; (4) composable <strong>ERP</strong> — modular, best-of-breed approach; (5) <strong>AI</strong> and generative <strong>AI</strong> integration; (6) industry cloud — industry-specific pre-configuration; (7) low-code/no-code customization; (8) real-time analytics and predictive insights; (9) mobile-first user experience; (10) ecosystem-based integration (<strong>iPaaS</strong>, <strong>Boomi</strong>, <strong>MuleSoft</strong>, <strong>Workato</strong>); these represent the major differentiating dimensions. In <strong>Gartner</strong>'s 2024 <strong>Magic Quadrant for Cloud ERP</strong>, <strong>SAP</strong>, <strong>Oracle</strong>, <strong>Microsoft</strong>, <strong>Workday</strong>, and <strong>Infor</strong> are evaluated as leading providers. <strong>Forrester Wave</strong>, <strong>IDC MarketScape</strong>, and <strong>Nucleus Research</strong> are among the other major evaluation firms.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>ERP</strong> implementation challenges include: (1) cost overruns (50-200% of original budget); (2) schedule overruns; (3) scope creep; (4) business process redesign; (5) data migration; (6) integration complexity; (7) user adoption; (8) training; (9) vendor lock-in; (10) upgrade difficulties; these are the major risk categories. Major <strong>ERP</strong> implementation consulting firms include <strong>Accenture</strong>, <strong>Deloitte</strong>, <strong>PwC</strong>, <strong>EY</strong>, <strong>KPMG</strong>, <strong>IBM</strong>, <strong>Infosys</strong>, <strong>TCS</strong>, <strong>Wipro</strong>, <strong>Capgemini</strong>, <strong>Cognizant</strong>, <strong>HCL Technologies</strong>, <strong>Atos</strong>, <strong>NTT Data</strong>, and <strong>Hitachi Solutions</strong>; key ecosystem players. <strong>Sedat Onat</strong>'s book "<strong>How to Buy ERP?</strong>" is published free of charge on <strong>Google Play Books</strong> — serving as a guide to <strong>ERP</strong> selection, implementation, and go-live. <strong>APICS</strong> (now <strong>ASCM</strong>), the <strong>Project Management Institute</strong> (<strong>PMI</strong>), and <strong>ISACA</strong> are major professional certification organizations. In conclusion, <strong>Bowman</strong>'s <strong>ERP</strong> evolution podcast discussion highlights how enterprise resource planning paradigm is being fundamentally redesigned globally — from monolithic on-premise to composable cloud-AI — with modern <strong>ERP</strong> selection and capability-driven implementation emerging as key strategic priorities for supply chain leaders.</p>
