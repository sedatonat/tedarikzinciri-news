<p style="text-align: justify">Months of attack threats along the Red Sea and Suez Canal route have eased somewhat following <strong>announcements by Houthi forces in Yemen that they would suspend attacks on commercial vessels and Israel-linked targets</strong>. This development offers hope that one of the most critical Asia-Europe container shipping routes could be reopened.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">However, <strong>the risk level in the region remains elevated</strong>. <strong>Jack Kennedy</strong>, S&P Global Market Intelligence Director of Country Risk for the Middle East and North Africa, notes that the absence of attacks over the past six weeks should not create a false sense of security. According to Kennedy, Houthi forces are using this period to <strong>repair infrastructure, rearm, and strengthen prospective attack capacity</strong>. While the situation may be characterized as de-escalation, the threat has not entirely dissipated.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>
<h4 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Carriers maintain cautious stance</strong></h4>
<p style="text-align: justify">Among container lines, <strong>CMA CGM</strong> is the only major operator continuing limited sailings under EU naval escort. Several smaller lines are making restricted transits. However, global carriers such as <strong>MSC, Maersk, Hapag-Lloyd, COSCO, and Evergreen</strong> are still routing through <strong>the Cape of Good Hope</strong> on security grounds.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The main reasons for this cautious approach:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Marine insurance costs are excessively high</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The risk of renewed Houthi attacks persists,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Navigation control in the Red Sea remains fragile,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Naval escort does not provide complete assurance.</p></li>
</ul>
<p><br></p>
<h4 style="text-align: justify"><strong>What would ships returning to the Red Sea mean?</strong></h4>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to experts, a mass return of major carriers to the Red Sea-Suez route could create <strong>a two-way impact</strong> on trade networks:</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>1) Asia-Europe transit times would shorten</strong></h5>
<p>The additional 10-14 days caused by the Cape of Good Hope route would be eliminated and costs would fall.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><br></p>
<h5><strong>2) Severe congestion would emerge at European, Mediterranean, and Asian ports</strong></h5>
<p>Recent schedule disruptions and vessel clustering could trigger <strong>sudden port congestion</strong> if lines rapidly return to Suez. This could lead to:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">bottlenecks at transshipment hubs,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">delays in berth discharge and loading,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">hinterland capacity constraints<br>\n.</p></li>
</ul>
<p><br></p>
<h4 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Insurance remains the critical barrier</strong></h4>
<p style="text-align: justify">Marine insurance providers continue to classify the Red Sea as <strong>a high-risk zone</strong>. According to data from Inchcape, BIMCO, and various brokers:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Premiums remain near crisis-period levels,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Open ocean operational risks have not fully subsided,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Carriers will not return without lower insurance premiums.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">While the Houthis' pause on attacks is a positive sign, <strong>normalization has not begun across the industry</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h4 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Market outlook</strong></h4>
<p style="text-align: justify">Whether container vessels return to Suez will depend on in the coming weeks:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Insurance premium trends</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Actual cessation of Houthi activity</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The effectiveness of maritime security operations</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Carriers' risk appetite</strong><br>\n.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">Industry experts emphasize that as 2026 approaches, the Red Sea remains <strong>the most critical vulnerability point for global shipping</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h4 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key takeaways:</strong></h4>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Houthi forces have paused attacks, but <strong>risk levels remain high</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Except for CMA CGM, major carriers are not transiting Suez.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Insurance premiums have not normalized, making <strong>a mass return unlikely</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Houthi rearmament concerns are mounting.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Should the route fully open, <strong>severe congestion risks loom at European and Asian ports</strong>.</p></li>
</ul>
