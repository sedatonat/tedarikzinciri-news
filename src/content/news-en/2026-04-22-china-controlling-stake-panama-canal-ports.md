<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>China</strong> is reported to be pressing for state-owned ocean carrier <strong>Cosco</strong> to take a controlling stake in a proposed $22.8 billion sale encompassing two controversial ports at the <strong>Panama Canal</strong>. Following pressure from President <strong>Donald Trump</strong> on <strong>Hong Kong</strong>-based operator <strong>CK Hutchison</strong> to transfer ownership of shipping centers, U.S. investment firm <strong>BlackRock</strong> and Swiss container shipping operator <strong>MSC</strong> initially reached a deal in March to acquire the two ports flanking the <strong>Panama Canal</strong>'s entrances. According to <strong>The Wall Street Journal</strong>, <strong>China</strong> is threatening to block the deal unless <strong>Cosco</strong> is granted a controlling stake—escalating from its previous demand for an equal share. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Panama Canal</strong> carries 5% of global trade and 40% of container traffic to and from the U.S.—serving as the vital shortcut between the <strong>Atlantic</strong> and <strong>Pacific</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In early 2025, <strong>Trump</strong> reiterated threats that the U.S. would reclaim control of the <strong>Panama Canal</strong>—claiming that <strong>China</strong>'s influence over the <strong>CK Hutchison</strong>-owned ports poses a national security risk. Months of negotiations between the <strong>Trump</strong> administration and canal authorities culminated in the announcement of the ports' sale to a <strong>BlackRock</strong>-led group, though <strong>CK Hutchison</strong> stated in August that it is unlikely the deal will close before 2026. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>CK Hutchison Holdings</strong> is part of the <strong>CK Hutchison Group</strong> controlled by <strong>Li Ka-shing</strong>—operating with 295 teams across 53 ports globally. <strong>Hutchison Ports</strong> is among the leading players in the global container port market. <strong>Balboa</strong> (on the <strong>Pacific</strong> side) and <strong>Cristobal</strong> (on the <strong>Atlantic</strong> side) are the two <strong>Panama</strong> ports subject to the sale.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Cosco Shipping</strong> (<strong>China Ocean Shipping Company</strong>) was formed through the 2016 merger of <strong>Cosco</strong> and <strong>China Shipping</strong>—based in <strong>Shanghai</strong>, it is the world's fourth-largest container carrier. <strong>OOCL</strong> (<strong>Orient Overseas Container Line</strong>) is a company that <strong>Cosco</strong> acquired from <strong>Hong Kong</strong> in 2018. <strong>BlackRock</strong>, led by <strong>Larry Fink</strong>, is the world's largest asset manager—based in <strong>New York</strong> and managing assets under management of $11 trillion. <strong>BlackRock Global Infrastructure Partners</strong> (<strong>GIP</strong>, acquired in 2024) specializes in infrastructure investments. <strong>MSC</strong> (<strong>Mediterranean Shipping Company</strong>), controlled by the <strong>Aponte</strong> family and based in <strong>Geneva, Switzerland</strong>, is the leader in the global container market. <strong>Terminal Investment Limited</strong> (<strong>TIL</strong>) serves as <strong>MSC</strong>'s terminal arm, operating at 70+ terminals globally. The <strong>BlackRock</strong>-<strong>MSC</strong>-<strong>TIL</strong> consortium covers <strong>Hutchison</strong>'s 43 ports, which is a far broader package than <strong>Panama</strong> alone.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Panama Canal</strong> opened in 1914 and was transferred from the U.S. to <strong>Panama</strong> in 1999 under the <strong>Torrijos-Carter Treaties</strong>. The <strong>Panama Canal Authority</strong> (<strong>ACP</strong>) operates the canal. The <strong>Neopanamax</strong> expansion, completed in 2016, allows larger vessels to transit—container ships carrying up to 14,000 <strong>TEU</strong> can now pass through the canal. The <strong>Suez Canal</strong>, <strong>Strait of Malacca</strong>, <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong>, <strong>Bosphorus</strong>, <strong>Bab-el-Mandeb</strong>, <strong>Strait of Gibraltar</strong>, <strong>Cape of Good Hope</strong>, and the <strong>Northern Sea Route</strong> are other critical chokepoints in global maritime trade. The <strong>State Administration for Market Regulation</strong> (<strong>SAMR</strong>), <strong>China</strong>'s antitrust regulator, has the authority to block the closing of the <strong>BlackRock</strong>-<strong>MSC</strong> deal due to <strong>Hutchison</strong>'s <strong>Hong Kong</strong> connections—similar to <strong>CFIUS</strong> (<strong>Committee on Foreign Investment in the U.S.</strong>) national security reviews, which may be applied bidirectionally on the U.S. side. Ultimately, <strong>China</strong>'s demand for <strong>Cosco</strong> to hold a controlling stake demonstrates clearly how U.S.-China geopolitical tensions are playing out over global port infrastructure.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. <strong>China</strong> is demanding that <strong>Cosco</strong> receive a controlling stake.<br>
2. The <strong>BlackRock</strong>-<strong>MSC</strong> March consortium is valued at $22.8 billion.<br>
3. <strong>Trump</strong> is pressuring <strong>CK Hutchison</strong> on national security grounds.<br>
4. <strong>CK Hutchison</strong> says the deal is unlikely to close before 2026.<br>
5. The previous equal stake demand has been escalated to a controlling interest.</p>
