<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Germany</strong> has halved its 2026 growth forecast after the U.S. war against <strong>Iran</strong> has triggered energy price spikes for industry and households. The Economy Ministry in <strong>Berlin</strong> stated on April 22 that <strong>German</strong> output will grow by 0.5% this year, down from the 1% expansion projection issued in January. The ministry also trimmed next year's outlook from 1.3% to 0.9%. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Germany</strong>'s energy-intensive industries—<strong>chemical</strong>, <strong>steel</strong>, <strong>automotive</strong>, and <strong>machinery</strong> sectors operating under the <strong>Energy Intensive Industry Alliance</strong>—require integrated coordination. Following the <strong>Nord Stream</strong> sabotage, <strong>LNG</strong> dependency has made capacity utilization at the <strong>Wilhelmshaven</strong>, <strong>Brunsbüttel</strong>, and <strong>Lubmin</strong> FSRU terminals critical.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Economy Minister <strong>Katherina Reiche</strong> told a press conference in <strong>Berlin</strong>, "The escalation in the Middle East and the war in Iran have set us back." <strong>Reiche</strong> added, "The situation remains highly volatile." The deteriorating environment is increasing pressure on Chancellor <strong>Friedrich Merz</strong> and his fracturing coalition. The conservative leader has built his government around reviving <strong>Europe</strong>'s largest economy and tackling welfare system reforms that consume an ever-larger share of the nation's resources, but he faces mounting difficulties. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Germany</strong>'s manufacturing share in <strong>GDP</strong> composition of roughly 20% means energy price shocks directly impact <strong>industrial output</strong>. <strong>BASF</strong>, <strong>Volkswagen</strong>, <strong>Siemens</strong>, <strong>BMW</strong>, and <strong>ThyssenKrupp</strong> are accelerating operation rationalization and <strong>offshoring</strong> decisions under energy cost pressure.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Merz</strong> and Finance Minister <strong>Lars Klingbeil</strong> from the center-left <strong>Social Democrats</strong> are negotiating deep cuts in benefits for <strong>German</strong> voters while trying to close a €140 billion ($164 billion) budget deficit through 2029. Both parties are polling near record lows as the far-right <strong>Alternative for Germany</strong> siphons support from disaffected workers, leaving neither the chancellor nor the finance minister much room to maneuver toward a compromise. Their dilemma is sharpened by slower-than-expected growth hitting tax revenues and the need to increase spending to support the unemployed, whose numbers have been trending upward over the past four years. <strong>Helena Melnikov</strong>, managing director of the <strong>DIHK</strong> industry lobby group, said <strong>Germany</strong>'s recovery is "not only fragile, it is under acute threat," adding that 83% of companies are already feeling the crisis's negative consequences, including shortages in oil, gas, other fuels, plastic products, and construction materials. From a supply chain perspective, petrochemical feedstock shortages in terms of <strong>input-output linkage</strong> may trigger a shift to allocation regimes in <strong>polyethylene</strong>, <strong>polypropylene</strong>, and <strong>PVC</strong> production.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>German</strong> economy is being buffeted by U.S. tariff attacks and rising competitive pressure from <strong>Chinese</strong> firms in key export industries such as high-tech machinery and automotive. Inflation risks are also mounting, raising the likelihood of interest rate increases from the <strong>European Central Bank</strong> this year. The ministry projects consumer prices will rise 2.7% this year and 2.8% in 2027. <strong>Bundesbank</strong> and <strong>ECB</strong> <strong>main refinancing operations</strong> rate decisions directly impact <strong>working capital</strong> costs and <strong>CAPEX</strong> financing across Europe. Throughput losses at <strong>Hamburg</strong>, <strong>Bremen</strong>, and <strong>Duisburg</strong> port and intermodal hubs create ripple effects in the <strong>ARA</strong> (<strong>Amsterdam-Rotterdam-Antwerp</strong>) corridor and <strong>Rhine-Main</strong> river transport flows. In sum, <strong>Germany</strong>'s growth revision stands as one of the most tangible macroeconomic impacts of the <strong>Iran</strong> war on <strong>European</strong> industrial infrastructure, raising recession risks across the <strong>Eurozone</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p><strong>Key Highlights:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Germany</strong> cuts 2026 growth forecast from 1% to 0.5%; 2027 from 1.3% to 0.9%.<br>
2. <strong>Reiche</strong> states the situation remains "highly volatile."<br>
3. <strong>DIHK</strong> reports 83% of companies experiencing energy and material shortages.<br>
4. <strong>Merz-Klingbeil</strong> attempting to close €140 billion deficit by 2029.<br>
5. Inflation projected at 2.7% in 2026 and 2.8% in 2027.</p>
