<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Rotterdam</strong> Port's terminal operations have been temporarily disrupted following an incident in which seven empty containers fell from an ultra-large container vessel onto an <strong>LNG</strong> bunker vessel. The incident has reignited questions regarding the safety of <strong>LNG</strong> bunkering operations at one of <strong>Europe</strong>'s largest container ports and at the center of the alternative fuel transition. According to details shared by the port, on February 6, five containers from Hapag-Lloyd's <strong>Bangkok Express</strong> fell onto the aft deck of the <strong>LNG</strong> bunker vessel <strong>K. Lotus</strong>, while two containers fell into the sea. From a supply chain perspective, this incident has prompted a reassessment of the operational risk profile of the contact points between container shipping and <strong>LNG</strong> supply.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The accident forced port management to close the <strong>Prinses Amaliahaven</strong> terminal for two hours during recovery efforts. The incident occurred during a routine bunkering operation being conducted from <strong>K. Lotus</strong> to <strong>Bangkok Express</strong>. It has been reported that the containers struck the <strong>accommodation section</strong> of <strong>K. Lotus</strong>, not its <strong>LNG</strong> compartment, and no injuries have been reported. This detail underscores the critical importance of <strong>LNG</strong> tank equipment and related safety barriers in hazardous cargo vessel safety. Operations in the remainder of the port maintained their flow outside of the closure period; however, the two-hour shutdown has caused cascading delays for shipowners with tightly scheduled freight windows.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In a statement to <strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong>, a port spokesman stated that "The safety procedures surrounding LNG bunkering are very strict and extensive," emphasizing that these types of bunker vessels are additionally protected against potential incidents through multi-layered safety measures. In this context, <strong>Rotterdam</strong> continues to maintain its position as one of the leading centers for global <strong>LNG</strong> bunkering services. The reason for the <strong>container stack</strong> collapse remains unclear; however, the <strong>Dutch Safety Board</strong>, <strong>Human Environment and Transport Inspectorate</strong>, and <strong>Port of Rotterdam Authority</strong> are investigating the incident. The activation of multiple oversight mechanisms indicates that findings may influence international standards regarding both container <strong>lashing</strong> equipment and bunkering procedures.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Bangkok Express</strong> departed the <strong>Port of Rotterdam</strong> following the incident. <strong>K. Lotus</strong> remains anchored in the port for repairs to damage sustained to its mooring system. The incident has brought back into focus questions regarding <strong>container stack</strong> design, the frequency of inspection of <strong>twistlock</strong> and <strong>lashing</strong> components, and the sterilization of surrounding areas during <strong>LNG</strong> bunkering operations. From a supply chain perspective, Hapag-Lloyd's service schedule consists primarily of <strong>Asia-Europe</strong> mainline services; consequently, any delays resulting from vessel rotations also affect return cargo planning. Ultimately, this incident at <strong>Rotterdam</strong> contributes to a period in which port safety architecture during the alternative fuel transition is being redesigned with tighter oversight mechanisms.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. Seven empty containers fell from <strong>Bangkok Express</strong>; five struck <strong>K. Lotus</strong>, two fell into the sea.<br>
2. <strong>Prinses Amaliahaven</strong> terminal was closed for two hours.<br>
3. Containers struck the accommodation section, not the <strong>LNG</strong> compartment.<br>
4. Three agencies, including the <strong>Dutch Safety Board</strong>, are investigating.<br>
5. <strong>K. Lotus</strong> remains in port for mooring system repairs.</p>
