<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>U.S.</strong> farmers are receiving $12 billion in assistance from the <strong>Farmer Bridge Assistance</strong> program announced by President <strong>Donald Trump</strong> on December 8—aimed at offsetting the effects of low crop prices and the administration's ongoing trade wars. The <strong>FDA</strong>, in a press statement, said that "one-time bridge payments" are providing relief to <strong>U.S.</strong> farmers from "unfair market disruptions"—including temporary trade market disruptions and rising production costs continuing to impact farmers, "following four years of disastrous <strong>Biden</strong> Administration policies that caused record-high input prices and zero new trade agreements." From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>USDA</strong> (<strong>U.S. Department of Agriculture</strong>), through its <strong>FSA</strong> (<strong>Farm Service Agency</strong>) and <strong>NRCS</strong> (<strong>Natural Resources Conservation Service</strong>) units, is distributing direct assistance to farmers. <strong>Helen Atkinson</strong>, <strong>Managing Editor</strong> of <strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong>, covers the issue.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>BBC News</strong> reports that the majority of the funds—$11 billion—is being allocated to one-time payments to farmers for commodity crops such as corn, cotton, peanuts, sorghum, soybeans, wheat, and canola—with another $1 billion reserved for crops not covered by the program. <strong>Trump</strong> also claimed in his December 8 announcement that <strong>One Big Beautiful Bill</strong>, passed earlier this year, is providing additional support to farmers. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>U.S.</strong> agriculture comprises over 10% of global world food trade—according to <strong>USDA Economic Research Service</strong> data. The <strong>U.S.</strong> accounts for over 30% of global corn exports, over 30% of soybeans, 12% of wheat, and over 35% of cotton. <strong>Cargill</strong>; <strong>ADM</strong> (<strong>Archer Daniels Midland</strong>); <strong>Bunge</strong>; <strong>Louis Dreyfus</strong>; <strong>COFCO</strong> (<strong>China</strong>); <strong>Wilmar</strong> (<strong>Singapore</strong>); <strong>Olam</strong> (<strong>Singapore</strong>) are the <strong>ABCD+</strong> players in global agricultural commodity trading.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>China</strong> is the <strong>U.S.</strong>'s largest soybean buyer—historically purchasing between $14-30 billion annually. The <strong>U.S.-China</strong> trade war has dramatically affected soybean shipments—with soy flows from <strong>Brazil</strong>, <strong>Argentina</strong>, and <strong>Paraguay</strong> to <strong>China</strong> increasing, hurting <strong>U.S.</strong> producers. During <strong>Trump 1.0</strong> (2018-2020), $28 billion in farmer aid was distributed under the <strong>Market Facilitation Program</strong> (<strong>MFP</strong>)—managed by then-<strong>USDA</strong> Secretary <strong>Sonny Perdue</strong>. <strong>Brooke Rollins</strong> is <strong>USDA</strong> Secretary for <strong>Trump 2.0</strong>. <strong>John Deere</strong>; <strong>AGCO</strong>; <strong>CNH Industrial</strong> (<strong>Case IH</strong>; <strong>New Holland</strong>); <strong>Kubota</strong>; <strong>Mahindra</strong> are leading players in the global agricultural equipment market. <strong>Bayer</strong> (<strong>Monsanto</strong>); <strong>Corteva</strong> (<strong>DowDuPont</strong> spinoff); <strong>Syngenta</strong> (<strong>ChemChina</strong>); <strong>BASF</strong>; <strong>FMC Corp.</strong> are leading companies in the agricultural chemicals and seed market.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>U.S.</strong> agriculture's multiple challenges include low commodity prices (<strong>corn</strong>; <strong>soybeans</strong>; <strong>wheat</strong> in the 2022-2024 cyclical downturn); high input costs (<strong>fertilizer</strong>; <strong>diesel</strong>; <strong>seeds</strong>; <strong>labor</strong>); migrant labor constraints; tariff-for-tariff retaliation; climate change impacts (<strong>drought</strong>; <strong>flood</strong>; <strong>heat</strong>); water access (<strong>Ogallala Aquifer</strong>; <strong>Colorado River</strong>); land consolidation; rising <strong>farm bankruptcy</strong>—fundamental components of the modern <strong>farm crisis</strong>. The <strong>Renewable Fuel Standard</strong> (<strong>RFS</strong>); ethanol mandates; soy-based biodiesel are agricultural demand supporters. <strong>Iowa</strong>; <strong>Illinois</strong>; <strong>Nebraska</strong>; <strong>Minnesota</strong>; <strong>Indiana</strong>; <strong>Ohio</strong>; <strong>Kansas</strong>; <strong>Missouri</strong>; <strong>South Dakota</strong>; <strong>North Dakota</strong> are the core states of the <strong>Corn Belt</strong> and <strong>Wheat Belt</strong>. The <strong>USDA Crop Insurance</strong> program, under the <strong>Risk Management Agency</strong> (<strong>RMA</strong>), is the primary instrument for farm income assurance. <strong>commodity exchange</strong> (<strong>CBOT</strong>; <strong>ICE</strong>); <strong>futures</strong>; <strong>options</strong>; <strong>hedging</strong> are instruments used by farmers to manage price risk. In conclusion, <strong>Trump</strong>'s $12 billion aid package is aimed at offsetting <strong>U.S.</strong> agricultural losses stemming from trade policy—but structural problems persist.</p>

3. $11 billion for corn/cotton/peanut/sorghum/soy/wheat/canola; $1 billion for other commodities.<br>
