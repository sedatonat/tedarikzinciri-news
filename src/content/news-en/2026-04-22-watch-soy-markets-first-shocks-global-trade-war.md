<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; <strong>Robert J. Bowman</strong> (<strong>Editor-in-Chief</strong>); <strong>Simi Thambi</strong>, climate and nature economist at <strong>FAIRR</strong>, explains an unexpected environmental consequence of the <strong>Trump</strong> administration's tariffs on products from <strong>China</strong>. <strong>FAIRR</strong> is an investor network engaged in reducing trade-related risks and exploring opportunities in the global food system. It comprises over 400 investors with 80 trillion USD in combined assets under management. "We work together to bring systemic change step by step; everything is interconnected," <strong>Thambi</strong> says. As a result of tariffs imposed on <strong>China</strong> by President <strong>Trump</strong>, one of the most significant risks emerging in the global food supply chain is the impact on soy markets. This situation is causing Chinese buyers to divert soy purchases away from <strong>U.S.</strong> suppliers toward <strong>Brazil</strong>, a country that "has relatively poor traceability and is regularly linked to deforestation," <strong>Thambi</strong> states. The shift began during the previous <strong>U.S.-China</strong> trade war in 2018 and 2019—accelerating deforestation in <strong>Brazil</strong>. Now, in the latest round of tariffs imposed on <strong>China</strong> by the <strong>Trump</strong> administration, this threat has become even more serious—creating "reputational and regulatory risk in the value chain." Deforestation is monitored among environmental issues tracked by the <strong>Coller FAIRR Protein Producer Index</strong>—which evaluates the world's largest 60 animal protein producers against 10 key performance indicators including greenhouse gas emissions, biodiversity, and water use.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>FAIRR</strong> (<strong>Farm Animal Investment Risk &amp; Return</strong>), established by the <strong>Jeremy Coller Foundation</strong> (<strong>Jeremy Coller</strong> Founder; <strong>Coller Capital</strong> CEO), based in <strong>London, UK</strong>, with <strong>Maria Lettini</strong> as Executive Director, provides an investor collaboration platform addressing ESG risks and opportunities in the global animal protein industry. The <strong>Coller FAIRR Protein Producer Index</strong> covers major protein producers including <strong>JBS</strong>; <strong>Tyson Foods</strong>; <strong>Marfrig</strong>; <strong>Smithfield Foods</strong> (owned by <strong>WH Group</strong>; <strong>China</strong>); <strong>Hormel Foods</strong>; <strong>Cargill</strong>; <strong>BRF</strong>; <strong>Charoen Pokphand Foods</strong>; <strong>Mowi</strong>; <strong>SalMar</strong>; <strong>Nutreco</strong>; <strong>WH Group</strong>; and <strong>Pilgrim's Pride</strong>. <strong>Brazil</strong>'s soy production, at 169 million tonnes in the 2024-25 season, makes it the world's largest producer—followed by the <strong>U.S.</strong> with 119 million tonnes, <strong>Argentina</strong> with 50 million tonnes, and <strong>China</strong> with 20 million tonnes. <strong>Brazil</strong>'s major soy regions include <strong>Mato Grosso</strong>; <strong>Goiás</strong>; <strong>Mato Grosso do Sul</strong>; <strong>Paraná</strong>; <strong>Bahia</strong>; and <strong>Maranhão</strong>—where the <strong>Cerrado</strong> and <strong>Amazon</strong> biomes face deforestation pressure.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the global soy supply chain comprises key players: (1) <strong>ABCD</strong> traders—<strong>Archer Daniels Midland</strong> (<strong>ADM</strong>; <strong>Juan Luciano</strong> CEO); <strong>Bunge Global</strong> (<strong>Greg Heckman</strong> CEO); <strong>Cargill</strong> (<strong>Brian Sikes</strong> CEO; private); <strong>Louis Dreyfus Company</strong> (<strong>Michael Gelchie</strong> CEO; <strong>Lévana Louis-Dreyfus</strong> Chair); (2) <strong>Brazil</strong>'s <strong>Amaggi</strong> (<strong>André Maggi</strong>); <strong>SLC Agricola</strong>; <strong>BrasilAgro</strong>; <strong>Vibra Agroindustrial</strong>; and (3) <strong>China</strong>'s <strong>COFCO International</strong> (<strong>Beijing</strong>; state-owned). The <strong>EU Deforestation Regulation</strong> (<strong>EUDR</strong>; approved April 2023) mandates that soy, beef, coffee, cocoa, palm oil, rubber, and wood imports to the <strong>EU</strong> contain no deforestation—with <strong>FERN</strong>; <strong>Mighty Earth</strong>; and <strong>Trase</strong> serving as key monitoring platforms. The <strong>Soy Moratorium</strong> (signed in 2006) commits to not purchasing soy from newly deforested lands in the Amazon—though the <strong>Cerrado</strong> biome remains outside its scope. The <strong>Cerrado Manifesto</strong> calls for a similar moratorium in the <strong>Cerrado</strong>. The <strong>Roundtable on Responsible Soy</strong> (<strong>RTRS</strong>); <strong>ProTerra Foundation</strong>; and <strong>ISCC</strong> represent major certification programs.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>U.S.</strong> soy export share to <strong>China</strong> has declined from 62% in 2017 to 52% in 2024—while <strong>Brazil</strong>'s exports to <strong>China</strong> have risen to 75%. The <strong>U.S. Department of Agriculture</strong> (<strong>USDA</strong>; <strong>Brooke Rollins</strong> Secretary) and the <strong>Foreign Agricultural Service</strong> (<strong>FAS</strong>) are key monitoring agencies. <strong>U.S.</strong> soy farmers, particularly in <strong>Iowa</strong>; <strong>Illinois</strong>; <strong>Minnesota</strong>; <strong>Indiana</strong>; <strong>Ohio</strong>; <strong>Missouri</strong>; <strong>Nebraska</strong>; and <strong>Kansas</strong>—are facing direct harm from the tariff war. The <strong>American Soybean Association</strong> (<strong>ASA</strong>; <strong>Caleb Ragland</strong> President) and the <strong>National Corn Growers Association</strong> (<strong>NCGA</strong>) are major farmer organizations calling on the <strong>Trump</strong> administration to soften tariff negotiations. Ultimately, <strong>Thambi</strong>'s soy market warning suggests that globally the food supply chain, deforestation risk, and ESG investment decisions are undergoing fundamental reassessment—responsible sourcing and deforestation traceability appear to be emerging as key strategic priorities for supply chain managers.</p>
