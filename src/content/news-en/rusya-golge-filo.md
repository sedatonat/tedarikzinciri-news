<p style="text-align: justify">In October 2025, the US and EU announced fresh sanctions against major Russian energy companies, notably <strong>Lukoil</strong>. The official rhetoric is stern, the list lengthy; yet on the ground, a different picture emerges. While Washington and Brussels keep sanctions targeting Russian oil tight on paper, in practice they are deliberately <strong>applying them loosely</strong>. The core aim is to curtail Russian oil flows without cutting them entirely, thereby maintaining control over global prices and inflation, while simultaneously sustaining the political message of "imposing sanctions."</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">At the centre of this grey zone lies a concept frequently cited in 2025's maritime literature: the <strong>"Shadow Fleet."</strong> This fleet comprises hundreds of tankers, mostly <strong>older than 15 years</strong>, with questionable technical condition, partially disconnected from Western financial and insurance systems. Their mission: to circumvent the <strong>G7 price cap</strong> mechanism and transport Russian oil to Asian and other markets.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This mechanism operates through three fundamental elements:</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify">1. False Flag Operations</h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to intelligence reports, by late 2025, Russia-linked tankers are rapidly re-registering under <strong>obscure flag states</strong> (such as <strong>Malawi</strong>, <strong>Eswatini</strong>, or barely active "zombie" registries). This obscures the ship's true ownership and operational connections. While official databases show an "African-flagged" tanker, in reality the vessel has become a shadow vehicle transporting Russian oil.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify">2. "Dark" Logistics – AIS Shutdown and STS Transfers</h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">Shadow tankers frequently disable their <strong>AIS</strong> navigation transponders. This renders critical information—routes, speed, STS locations—invisible. Vessels typically conduct <strong>Ship-to-Ship (STS)</strong> transfers in areas such as <strong>Greek waters</strong>, the <strong>Eastern Mediterranean</strong>, or the <strong>Bay of Bengal</strong>. Cargo is offloaded at sea to another tanker; sometimes it is blended with legitimate crude from other sources, creating a new consignment with "obscured origin." This process effectively functions as <strong>cargo laundering</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify">3. Sanction–Enforcement Gap – The Lukoil Paradox</h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">The fact that <strong>Lukoil</strong>, officially a <strong>blocked</strong> entity, appeared at the <strong>ADIPEC</strong> energy conference in Abu Dhabi in November 2025 exemplifies this contradiction. The US is visibly avoiding imposing <strong>secondary sanctions</strong> on major buyers like <strong>India</strong> and <strong>China</strong> that do business with Lukoil. Stricter enforcement would trigger both diplomatic crises and sharp increases in oil prices.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Although the US Treasury has listed some vessels, <strong>actual enforcement</strong> remains limited. Stopping, inspecting, or seizing vessels requires naval power. Yet navies are already under intense pressure due to <strong>drone threats in the Red Sea</strong>. Moreover, direct intervention in a tanker operation risks escalating to <strong>naval confrontation</strong>.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This landscape produces serious collateral damage for "normal" supply chain players:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Security and Insurance Risk:</strong> Shadow Fleet vessels are technically weak platforms with poor maintenance. In late autumn 2025, such ships experienced fires, collisions, and groundings in the <strong>Black Sea</strong> and other sensitive areas. These incidents create strait blockages, environmental pollution, and navigation hazards for legitimate container vessels using nearby routes.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Port & Bunkering Congestion:</strong> As shadow tankers concentrate in "friendly" or grey-zone ports, the <strong>bunkering zones</strong> and anchorages at these locations become overcrowded. This delays compliant vessels using the same areas.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Compliance Burden:</strong> Carriers must now screen not only their own vessels, but also bunker sources, STS contacts, and trading partners. Even indirect contact with a <strong>dark ship</strong>—such as refuelling at the same STS point—can trigger regulatory scrutiny and internal investigations.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">Reading all the visuals shared together, a clear framework emerges for 2026 strategy:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Ocean becomes a higher-risk environment:</strong> Drone attacks (Red Sea), Shadow Fleet incidents, and complex sanction networks are turning the ocean from "cheap and safe highway" into a hazardous zone.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Response is shaping through "modality shift":<br>
Rail:</strong> Barcelona's major rail investment makes inland distribution carbon-free and more reliable.<br>
<strong>Air/Land Hub:</strong> DHL's Riyadh investment creates air–land corridors that completely bypass the Red Sea.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Target Markets:</strong> As Africa emerges as a major long-term demand hub, access to these markets is becoming difficult due to current security and infrastructure constraints.</p></li>
</ul>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">For this reason, 2026 planning still uses maritime shipping for the Africa–Middle East–Europe triangle; however, risk levels are "elevated" and insurance and compliance costs are accepted as "structural."</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong></h3>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Although the US/EU formally blocks Russian energy majors, it is not fully cutting oil flows; on the ground, <strong>soft sanctions</strong> are being applied.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Shadow Fleet</strong> comprises aged, risky tankers used to breach the G7 price cap mechanism.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">False flags, AIS shutdowns, and STS "cargo laundering" have become standard 2025 operations.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Lukoil's attendance at ADIPEC illustrates the contradiction between sanctions and enforcement.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Shadow ship incidents and port congestion create serious indirect risks for compliant supply chains.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Entering 2026, maritime routes appear <strong>risky and expensive</strong>, while rail and air–land solutions are <strong>increasingly attractive</strong>.</p></li>
</ul>
