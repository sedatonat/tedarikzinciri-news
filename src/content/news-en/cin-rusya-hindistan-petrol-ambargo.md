<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Fresh U.S. sanctions</strong> are sending shockwaves through global energy markets. Following <strong>the Trump administration's</strong> imposition of sanctions on <strong>Rosneft</strong> and <strong>Lukoil</strong> this week, <strong>China and India, Russia's two largest buyers,</strong> have temporarily halted Russian crude oil imports. This development has the potential to create a significant contraction in energy revenues that have financed Moscow's war in Ukraine for the past three and a half years.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Sources report that China's major energy giants <strong>Sinopec, CNOOC,</strong> and <strong>PetroChina</strong> have suspended their seaborne purchases of Russian crude not as a political stance but due to <strong>compliance calculus</strong> concerns. According to experts, this decision reflects the view that the risk of exposure to secondary sanctions carries more weight than short-term commercial gains.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Similarly, <strong>India</strong> is reducing its Russian oil imports. According to information relayed by <strong>Splash,</strong> this development is viewed as a direct result of <strong>the first sanctions package announced following Trump's return to office.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Scope of Sanctions and Market Effects</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">With the latest U.S. sanctions, Russia's major oil producers <strong>Rosneft, Lukoil, Gazprom Neft,</strong> and <strong>Surgutneftegaz</strong> are now fully covered by <strong>OFAC (Office of Foreign Assets Control)</strong> sanctions. This means that virtually all of Russia's seaborne crude oil exports are now under sanctions.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Following this decision, <strong>crude prices are rising,</strong> while <strong>forward freight agreements (FFAs)</strong> in the tanker market are climbing sharply through year-end.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Jorge Leon,</strong> director of geopolitical analysis at <strong>Rystad Energy,</strong> points out that the sanctions could cause major disruptions to Russia's oil production and exports, with some facilities potentially facing <strong>forced production shut-ins.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Global Energy Balance Being Reshaped</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">In comments to <strong>Reuters, Kuwait's Oil Minister Tariq Al-Roumi</strong> suggested that OPEC could decide on a <strong>production cuts reversal</strong> to counter potential supply tightness. Rising demand from the <strong>Gulf and Middle East regions</strong> in particular is repositioning producer nations.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">These developments signal a major realignment in global energy flows. Given that <strong>China and India</strong> account for approximately <strong>75%</strong> of Russia's seaborne crude exports, their withdrawal is creating <strong>significant pressure</strong> on the Russian economy.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Opportunity for Tank Shipping, Constraint for Russia</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to projections by investment bank <strong>Jefferies,</strong> <strong>30% to 40%</strong> of Russia's seaborne crude exports are currently transported by <strong>G7 price cap-compliant</strong> tankers. However, the new sanctions are making even these shipments risky.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Experts note that this situation will increase pressure on the tanker fleet, drive up freight rates, and deepen <strong>logistical complexity.</strong> <strong>Jefferies analysts</strong> describe this sanction regime as creating a "positive but volatile opportunity environment" for the tanker sector.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Simultaneous Moves from the European Union and United Kingdom</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">Following the U.S. sanctions, the <strong>European Union</strong> swiftly approved <strong>its 19th sanctions package against Russia.</strong> The package targets <strong>117 vessels</strong> while extending the <strong>ban on Russian LNG imports</strong> through <strong>2027.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Additionally, EU sanctions have been expanded to include Chinese importers. The <strong>United Kingdom</strong> added <strong>Yulong Refinery in Shandong</strong> to its sanctions list last week and implemented sanctions against <strong>Rosneft</strong> and <strong>Lukoil.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Conclusions and Possible Scenarios</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">Russia's seaborne crude oil exports have ranged between 3 and 3.5 million barrels per day since the Ukraine invasion began in February 2022. However, with the new sanctions and the pullback by China and India, this figure is expected to decline significantly.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">While this scenario could trigger a supply shock in the global oil market, it also carries potential for <strong>OPEC countries to increase production</strong> and for <strong>the United States to expand its energy diplomacy influence.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In conclusion, the Trump administration's sanctions policy is reshaping not only the Russian economy but also the <strong>global energy supply chain.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>

  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Chinese majors such as Sinopec, CNOOC, and PetroChina</strong> are temporarily halting purchases of <strong>Russian crude due to compliance risk.</strong></p></li>
