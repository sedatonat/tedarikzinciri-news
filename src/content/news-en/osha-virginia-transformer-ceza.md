<p style="text-align: justify">The U.S. Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) has announced comprehensive enforcement action against <strong>Virginia Transformer Corporation (VTC)</strong>. Following inspections at the company's Pocatello manufacturing facility in Idaho, the agency determined that workers were exposed to serious and repeated safety hazards and proposed an administrative penalty of <strong>approximately $1 million</strong>. A total of <strong>53 serious and repeated violations</strong> focused on crane operations, machine guarding, fall protection measures, and deficiencies in <strong>personal protective equipment (PPE)</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">OSHA inspectors, during a follow-up inspection conducted in <strong>June 2025</strong>, found that <strong>similar hazards</strong> persisted that had been identified in two separate inspections carried out in <strong>April and June 2024</strong>. The inspections uncovered <strong>faulty brakes and switches</strong> on cranes used to transport heavy loads, conditions that pose elevated injury risk to workers. These findings demonstrate that the company failed to adequately remedy previously identified risks.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to OSHA's findings, violations extended beyond cranes alone. Significant gaps were identified in fundamental workplace safety areas including <strong>inadequate machine guarding</strong>, workers exposed to <strong>fall hazards while working at height</strong>, and failure to provide appropriate <strong>PPE</strong>. The total proposed penalty for these violations was announced at <strong>$986,888</strong>. The company is required, within <strong>15 business days</strong> of notification of the penalties, to implement necessary corrections, request an <strong>informal conference</strong> with OSHA, or contest the citation by bringing the case before the <strong>Occupational Safety and Health Review Commission (OSHRC)</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">As of its publication date, the status of this case for 2025 remains <strong>open</strong>, and detailed documentation of the violations has not yet been released to the public. OSHA recommends that interested parties regularly monitor developments through the <strong>establishment search</strong> page.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Examining the facility's history reveals that safety performance issues are not new. From when VTC acquired the Pocatello facility in <strong>2003 through the end of 2024</strong>, OSHA records show <strong>99 violations</strong>. The initial total penalty for these violations was <strong>$602,059</strong>, which was reduced to <strong>$363,150</strong> following discounts. Early inspections highlighted <strong>amputation injuries</strong>, <strong>falls from height</strong>, <strong>struck-by hazards</strong>, and <strong>electrical hazards</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">While the facility was not penalized during the 2011–2023 period, safety issues resurfaced with two inspections conducted in <strong>2024</strong>. Following these two inspections, <strong>18 violations</strong> were identified, with the initial penalty of <strong>$228,834</strong> later reduced to <strong>$171,625</strong>. The focus of the inspections centered on <strong>crane and forklift hazards</strong> and exposure to <strong>respirable crystalline silica</strong> covered under OSHA's <strong>National Emphasis Program (NEP)</strong>. Both inspections were initiated following employee complaints.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Employee perspectives also support the on-site conditions. In reviews posted on the <strong>Indeed</strong> platform, the work environment at the Pocatello facility is characterized as "<strong>fear-based</strong>" and "<strong>unprofessional</strong>". Comments emphasize inadequate training, poor management communication, excessive overtime practices, and high employee turnover rates. Some workers contend that no investment has been made in safety training and that this represents a deliberate choice.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">VTC operates a total of <strong>six production facilities</strong> across North America. In addition to the Idaho facility, the company operates in <strong>Georgia</strong>, <strong>Virginia</strong>, and <strong>Chihuahua, Mexico</strong>. Inspections conducted at the Virginia facilities between <strong>2020–2025</strong> identified a total of <strong>29 violations</strong> across <strong>21 inspections</strong>; some cases remain open.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The Pocatello facility's operational structure involves high-risk manufacturing processes. The facility transports <strong>100-ton transformers</strong> using <strong>air pallets</strong>, while <strong>50-ton cores</strong> are lifted with <strong>overhead crane</strong> systems. Production areas employ a <strong>positively pressurized winding shop</strong> for dust control, with targets for <strong>partial discharge</strong> values below <strong>ANSI</strong> limits. Despite this advanced technical infrastructure, the deficiencies in workplace safety practices reveal a disconnect between operational excellence and field safety.</p>
