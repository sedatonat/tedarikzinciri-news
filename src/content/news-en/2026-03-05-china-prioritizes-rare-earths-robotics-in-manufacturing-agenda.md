<p style="text-align: justify">China has pledged to double down on production of high-end tech goods to bolster its status as a manufacturing powerhouse &mdash; even as Beijing's ambition raises alarms in foreign capitals. China unveiled a series of steps to improve its manufacturing prowess in its 15th Five-Year Plan draft released on March 5. To strengthen the country's supply chains, China will continue investing in rare earths, rare metals and superhard materials to maintain a competitive edge, the report said.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Critical minerals have become a key point of leverage for China as it flexes its muscles in tariff negotiations with U.S. President <strong>Donald Trump</strong>. China dominates the mining of rare earths, the essential raw materials in powerful magnets ubiquitous across modern manufacturing. The country controlled 90% of global market share in 2024 &mdash; but that is poised to dip to 69% by 2030 according to Bloomberg Intelligence, as other countries seek to boost their own extraction and mining capabilities.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">China also seeks to invest more in foundational capabilities &mdash; including advanced ceramics, super-precision bearings, domestic software and high-end <strong>CNC</strong> machine tools used in aerospace and automotive manufacturing. Beijing is further targeting several emerging technologies, including advanced chips, robotics, batteries and <em>brain-computer interface</em>, according to the draft. China launched a major effort in 2015 to upgrade its manufacturing capabilities to move away from low-cost goods to higher-end technologies under its "Made in China 2025" plan.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, this plan reinforces China's strategic dominance in the global critical minerals supply chain. For <strong>EV</strong> manufacturers (<strong>Tesla</strong>, <strong>BYD</strong>, <strong>Volkswagen</strong>), wind turbine producers (<strong>Vestas</strong>, <strong>Siemens Gamesa</strong>) and the defense industry (particularly the <strong>F-35</strong> program), strategies to reduce dependence on China &mdash; such as <strong>USA Rare Earth</strong>, <strong>MP Materials</strong> and Australia's <strong>Lynas Rare Earths</strong> &mdash; become vital. Meanwhile, in robotics and <strong>HBM</strong> memory, China is accelerating its domestic ecosystem &mdash; ushering in a new phase of global tech competition.</p>
