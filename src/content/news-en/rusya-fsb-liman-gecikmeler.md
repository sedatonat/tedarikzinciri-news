<p style="text-align: justify">Russia's renewed attacks in the Black Sea and surrounding areas are making ship operations in the region extremely complex, while Moscow's new port-entry procedures for foreign-flagged vessels are creating an additional regulatory burden on supply chains. With the decree taking effect on <strong>November 25, 2025</strong>, all <strong>foreign-flagged vessels</strong> seeking entry into Russia must now obtain a <strong>port-entry clearance</strong> issued by the <strong>Federal Security Service (FSB)</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This new regime, as underscored in an alert from the <strong>American Club</strong> dated December 8, is creating <strong>significant delays, cost increases, and operational uncertainties</strong> for ship owners and charterers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>The Entry Process: Initial Vetting 48 Hours, Full Cycle Up to 53 Hours</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">Under the new regulation, when a vessel requests entry to a Russian port, port authorities:</p>
<ol>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Collect information on the ship's <strong>last 10 ports of call</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Transmit this information to the <strong>FSB within 1 hour</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The FSB then initiates a vetting procedure that can take <strong>up to 48 hours</strong> to issue an entry decision.</p></li>
</ol>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to the American Club, this process can stretch to <strong>49 hours</strong> for the FSB vetting portion alone. Additionally, when accounting for delays in information flow, internal port communication procedures, and operator approvals, the <strong>full clearance cycle</strong>—excluding underwater inspections—can extend to <strong>53 hours</strong>.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This does not even include other operational delays such as <strong>anchorage delays</strong>, <strong>tug availability</strong>, and <strong>terminal scheduling conflicts</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Mandatory Underwater Inspections: Additional Cost and Timeline Uncertainty</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">Russia introduced a requirement for <strong>underwater hull inspections</strong> for all foreign vessels a few weeks ago. These inspections involve scanning for mines, explosive devices, sabotage equipment, or objects that could cause <strong>external impact</strong>.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Under the new regulation:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Port operators can request underwater inspections.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">All costs of this inspection are borne by the <strong>shipowner</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">When the inspection will be conducted, how long it will take, and how many specialist divers it will require remain uncertain.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">As a result, ship owners face challenges not only in terms of time but also regarding <strong>cost predictability</strong> on voyages to Russia.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Black Sea Risk Environment Tightening</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">In recent weeks, <strong>Black Sea war-risk premiums</strong> have risen significantly in the insurance market. According to data from Marsh and Vessel Protect, premiums on some voyages have climbed from <strong>0.25% → 1%</strong>. Behind this increase are factors including:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Ukraine's attacks on Russia-linked vessels,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>External blast</strong> incidents such as the Kairos supertanker case,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Russia's signals of expanding limited retaliation threats,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Operational risks stemming from <strong>shadow fleet</strong> activity in the Black Sea and Mediterranean</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">such factors.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Following the Kairos attack and other incidents, Russian President <strong>Vladimir Putin</strong> declared that "retaliation may not be limited solely to Ukraine-linked vessels and could extend to ships flying the flags of <strong>Ukraine-supporting countries</strong>." This development is raising the geopolitical risk level in the region further.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Allegations that Ukrainian special forces may have been involved in a tanker operation off Senegal indicate the potential for <strong>geographical spillover</strong> of the conflict.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Operational Impact: Triple Pressure on Supply Chains</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">Along with the new FSB regime, carriers planning to call at Russian ports must prepare for <strong>a three-dimensional risk matrix</strong>:</p>
<ol>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Regulatory friction:</strong> 48–53 hour clearance delays, schedule disruptions.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Financial exposure:</strong> Mandatory underwater inspections + rising war-risk insurance premiums.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Geopolitical unpredictability:</strong> Russia's potentially expanding retaliation policies.</p></li>
</ol>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Laycan flexibility</strong> is diminishing in the tanker and bulk segments; container operators are being forced to add <strong>buffer time</strong> to their Russia services.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The American Club particularly recommends that charterers add provisions covering <strong>FSB-delay clauses</strong>, <strong>underwater inspection cost allocation</strong>, and <strong>war-risk deviation</strong> to their contracts.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Points:</strong></h3>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>FSB clearance</strong> is mandatory for all foreign-flagged vessels entering Russia.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The vetting process takes <strong>48–49 hours</strong>, with the full cycle extending to <strong>53 hours</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Mandatory <strong>underwater hull inspections</strong> impose additional costs on ship owners.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Black Sea war-risk insurance premiums are showing increases approaching 250%.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Putin implied that retaliation could extend to vessels flagged by other nations.</p></li>
</ul>
