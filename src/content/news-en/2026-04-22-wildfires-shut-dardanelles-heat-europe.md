<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Bloomberg</strong> reports that wildfires briefly closed the <strong>Dardanelles Strait</strong> to traffic on August 11, as extreme heat and powerful winds fueled blazes across the Mediterranean. A vital maritime chokepoint for oil and gas from the <strong>Black Sea</strong> and <strong>Central Asia</strong>, the <strong>Dardanelles</strong> reopened in the evening after authorities closed it to allow firefighting aircraft to draw water from the sea to battle nearby fires. Turkish firefighters are bringing wildfires under control that have ravaged northern Aegean shores, though towns and pine forests in Çanakkale province remain under threat, with approximately 2,900 residents evacuated, according to reporting by state-run <strong>Anadolu Agency</strong>. Vast swaths of Europe face wildfire threats as scorching heat bakes the region this week. Blazes have erupted across the continent, from suburbs of Madrid and London to Greek islands and forests spanning the Balkans. The fires are fueled by powerful winds and extreme temperatures.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Dardanelles Strait</strong> connects the <strong>Mediterranean Sea</strong> to the <strong>Marmara Sea</strong>, serving alongside the <strong>Bosphorus Strait</strong> as the sole maritime passage from the <strong>Black Sea</strong> to the <strong>Mediterranean</strong>. The <strong>Montreux Convention</strong> of 1936 is the international agreement establishing Turkish sovereignty over the <strong>Turkish Straits</strong> and governing transit. The <strong>Dardanelles</strong> and <strong>Bosphorus</strong> handle 3 to 4 million barrels of crude oil daily, along with LNG, grain, containers, and dry bulk—forming a critical chokepoint for exports originating from <strong>Russian</strong> ports (<strong>Novorossiysk</strong>, <strong>Tuapse</strong>); <strong>Kazakh</strong> sources (<strong>CPC Pipeline</strong>—<strong>Tengiz</strong>); <strong>Azerbaijani</strong> supplies (<strong>BTC Pipeline</strong>—<strong>Ceyhan</strong>, <strong>Baku-Supsa Pipeline</strong>); and <strong>Ukrainian</strong> sources (<strong>Odesa</strong>, <strong>Pivdennyi</strong>, <strong>Chornomorsk</strong>). The <strong>U.S. Energy Information Administration</strong> (<strong>EIA</strong>) classifies the <strong>Turkish Straits</strong> as one of the world's most important maritime chokepoints for global oil flows, alongside the <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong>, <strong>Strait of Malacca</strong>, <strong>Bab el-Mandab</strong>, <strong>Suez Canal</strong>, <strong>Panama Canal</strong>, <strong>Cape of Good Hope</strong>, and <strong>Danish Straits</strong> as major global chokepoints.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the 2025 Mediterranean fire season is well above average according to <strong>European Forest Fire Information System</strong> (<strong>EFFIS</strong>) data, with <strong>Spain</strong>, <strong>Greece</strong>, <strong>Portugal</strong>, <strong>Italy</strong>, <strong>France</strong>, <strong>Croatia</strong>, <strong>Albania</strong>, <strong>North Macedonia</strong>, <strong>Cyprus</strong>, and <strong>Turkey</strong> among the most heavily affected countries. The <strong>Copernicus Climate Change Service</strong> (<strong>C3S</strong>) reports that 2024 was globally the warmest year on record, the first year to exceed the 1.5°C threshold. The <strong>Mediterranean basin</strong> is warming 20 percent faster than the global average—defined as a "<strong>climate change hotspot</strong>" according to the <strong>IPCC</strong> (<strong>Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</strong>). Wildfires impact supply chains through maritime strait closures, reduced air visibility, damage to railway tracks, highway closures, power outages, health impacts (PM2.5/PM10), tourism revenue losses, agricultural and livestock effects, and increased insurance premiums. <strong>Turkey</strong>'s principal wildfire and disaster response agencies are the <strong>Ministry of Agriculture and Forestry</strong> (<strong>OGM</strong>; <strong>General Directorate of Forests</strong>) and <strong>AFAD</strong> (<strong>Disaster and Emergency Management Authority</strong>).</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the impacts of <strong>Turkish Straits</strong> closure include: (1) increased tanker waiting times—higher demurrage fees; (2) widening spreads between <strong>Brent Crude</strong> and <strong>Urals</strong> oil prices; (3) trade effects on the <strong>BSEC</strong> (<strong>Black Sea Economic Cooperation</strong>) region; (4) delays in <strong>Russian</strong>, <strong>Kazakh</strong>, and <strong>Ukrainian</strong> grain exports (under the <strong>Black Sea Grain Initiative</strong> or individually); (5) LNG carrier delays—affecting flows from <strong>Russia</strong> to <strong>Turkey</strong> and <strong>Russia</strong> to the <strong>EU</strong>; (6) rerouting of container ships. <strong>Turkey</strong>'s primary <strong>Strait</strong> crossing infrastructure includes the <strong>Çanakkale Bridge</strong> (<strong>1915 Çanakkale Bridge</strong>; opened in 2022—the world's longest suspension bridge), <strong>Marmaray</strong> (Asia-Europe rail tunnel), and the <strong>Avrasya Tunnel</strong> (highway). The <strong>Dardanelles</strong> region has historically hosted major events including the <strong>Trojan War</strong> and the <strong>Battle of Gallipoli</strong> from 1915–1916, and is home to <strong>UNESCO World Heritage</strong> sites such as the <strong>Troy Archaeological Site</strong> and <strong>Gallipoli Battlefields</strong>. In conclusion, the brief closure of the <strong>Dardanelles</strong> once again demonstrates the persistent and growing threat that extreme weather events caused by climate change pose to global supply chains—requiring <strong>Turkey</strong> and all <strong>Mediterranean</strong> countries to invest in wildfire preparedness.</p>

3. Approximately 2,900 residents evacuated from Çanakkale province—<strong>Anadolu Agency</strong> reports.<br>
4. <strong>Mediterranean basin</strong> warming 20 percent faster than global average.<br>
5. <strong>Spain</strong>, <strong>Greece</strong>, <strong>Portugal</strong>, <strong>Italy</strong>, <strong>France</strong>, and <strong>Turkey</strong> among the most affected by 2025 wildfires.</p>
