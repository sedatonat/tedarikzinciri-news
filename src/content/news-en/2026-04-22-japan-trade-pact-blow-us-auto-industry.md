<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports that <strong>U.S.</strong> automakers claim that the Trump administration's recently announced trade agreement with Japan places American companies and workers at a disadvantage — as they now face higher tariffs than their Japanese competitors. The agreement announced on July 22 sets tariffs on Japanese vehicles at 15% — down from the 25% rate that President <strong>Trump</strong> had previously threatened to impose. By contrast, vehicles produced by <strong>U.S.</strong> companies with engines and transmissions sourced from Canada and Mexico are currently subject to 25% tariffs as part of a broader tax affecting all foreign vehicles and auto parts. In a statement to the <strong>Associated Press</strong>, the <strong>United Auto Workers</strong> union said it was "deeply angered" by the Japan deal — with the <strong>American Automotive Policy Council</strong> (<strong>AAPC</strong>), representing <strong>General Motors</strong>, <strong>Ford</strong> and <strong>Stellantis</strong>, sharing similar sentiments. "Any deal that applies a lower tariff to Japanese imports with virtually no <strong>U.S.</strong> content than to North America-built vehicles with high <strong>U.S.</strong> content is a bad deal," the statement said.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.S. auto industry</strong> is centered around the <strong>Big Three Detroit</strong> — <strong>General Motors</strong>, <strong>Ford Motor Company</strong>, and <strong>Stellantis</strong> — which account for 3-4% of the <strong>U.S.</strong> economy and directly and indirectly support 10 million-plus jobs. <strong>General Motors</strong> (<strong>Detroit Michigan</strong> — <strong>Mary Barra</strong> CEO) has principal brands <strong>Chevrolet</strong>, <strong>GMC</strong>, <strong>Cadillac</strong>, and <strong>Buick</strong>. <strong>Ford Motor Company</strong> (<strong>Dearborn Michigan</strong> — <strong>Jim Farley</strong> CEO) has principal brands <strong>Ford</strong> and <strong>Lincoln</strong>. <strong>Stellantis NV</strong> (incorporated in <strong>Amsterdam Netherlands</strong>; <strong>Antonio Filosa</strong> CEO) has principal brands <strong>Jeep</strong>, <strong>Ram</strong>, <strong>Dodge</strong>, <strong>Chrysler</strong>, <strong>Fiat</strong>, <strong>Peugeot</strong>, <strong>Citroën</strong>, <strong>Opel</strong>, <strong>Vauxhall</strong>, <strong>Alfa Romeo</strong>, <strong>Maserati</strong>, and <strong>DS Automobiles</strong>. The <strong>United Auto Workers</strong> (<strong>UAW</strong>), headquartered in <strong>Detroit Michigan</strong> with <strong>Shawn Fain</strong> as <strong>President</strong>, is the union representing 400,000-plus members organized at the <strong>Big Three</strong> plus <strong>Tesla</strong>. The <strong>American Automotive Policy Council</strong> (<strong>AAPC</strong>), with <strong>Matt Blunt</strong> as <strong>President</strong>, is the industry representation organization for the <strong>Big Three</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, Japan's automotive exports to the <strong>U.S.</strong> exceed 1.5 million vehicles annually — valued at more than 50 billion dollars — making the <strong>U.S.</strong> its largest single market. <strong>Toyota Motor Corporation</strong> (<strong>Toyota City Japan</strong> — <strong>Koji Sato</strong> President) has principal brands <strong>Toyota</strong>, <strong>Lexus</strong>, <strong>Daihatsu</strong>, <strong>Hino</strong>, and a 20% stake in <strong>Subaru</strong>. <strong>Honda Motor Co. Ltd.</strong> (<strong>Tokyo Japan</strong> — <strong>Toshihiro Mibe</strong> President &amp; CEO) has principal brands <strong>Honda</strong> and <strong>Acura</strong>. <strong>Nissan Motor Co. Ltd.</strong> (<strong>Yokohama Japan</strong> — <strong>Iván Espinosa</strong> CEO) has principal brands <strong>Nissan</strong>, <strong>Infiniti</strong>, and <strong>Datsun</strong> — and is part of the <strong>Renault-Nissan-Mitsubishi Alliance</strong>. <strong>Mazda Motor Corporation</strong>, <strong>Subaru Corporation</strong>, <strong>Mitsubishi Motors Corporation</strong>, <strong>Suzuki Motor Corporation</strong>, <strong>Daihatsu</strong> (Toyota), <strong>Isuzu Motors</strong>, and <strong>Hino Motors</strong> (Toyota) are the major Japanese automotive manufacturers. The <strong>USMCA</strong> (<strong>United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement</strong>), the 2020 successor to NAFTA, created a requirement for 75% or greater North America content — but <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> tariffs are effectively suspending this agreement in practice.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the global nature of the automotive supply chain reflects the fact that a vehicle assembled in the <strong>U.S.</strong> has thousands of parts sourced from dozens of different countries. <strong>Tier 1</strong>, <strong>Tier 2</strong>, <strong>Tier 3</strong>, and <strong>Tier N</strong> supplier layers include major global <strong>Tier 1</strong> suppliers such as <strong>Bosch</strong>, <strong>Denso</strong>, <strong>ZF Friedrichshafen</strong>, <strong>Magna International</strong>, <strong>Continental AG</strong>, <strong>Aisin</strong>, <strong>Hyundai Mobis</strong>, <strong>Yazaki</strong>, <strong>Lear</strong>, <strong>Adient</strong>, <strong>Faurecia</strong> (<strong>Forvia</strong>), <strong>Valeo</strong>, <strong>Schaeffler</strong>, <strong>Mahle</strong>, <strong>BorgWarner</strong>, <strong>JTEKT</strong>, and <strong>Toyota Boshoku</strong>. The <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> 25% automotive tariff effectively nullifies the <strong>USMCA</strong>'s comprehensive 75% content requirement — disadvantaging Japanese vehicles at 15% has prompted protests from the <strong>UAW</strong> and the <strong>Big Three</strong>. Japanese automotive production facilities in the <strong>U.S.</strong> (<strong>Toyota</strong> in <strong>Texas</strong>, <strong>Kentucky</strong>, <strong>Indiana</strong>, <strong>Mississippi</strong>; <strong>Honda</strong> in <strong>Ohio</strong>, <strong>Alabama</strong>, <strong>Indiana</strong>; <strong>Nissan</strong> in <strong>Tennessee</strong>, <strong>Mississippi</strong>; <strong>Subaru</strong> in <strong>Indiana</strong>; <strong>Mazda-Toyota</strong> in <strong>Alabama</strong>) provide annual production capacity exceeding 1.5 million vehicles — but a significant portion of engines and transmissions still come from Japan. As a result, the <strong>Trump-Japan</strong> 15% automotive tariff agreement has sparked deep controversy in the <strong>U.S.</strong> auto industry — with the <strong>UAW</strong>, <strong>AAPC</strong>, and <strong>Big Three</strong> voicing strong objections — and the internal consistency of tariff policy has come under question.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. The <strong>Trump-Japan</strong> agreement announced July 22 sets a 15% tariff on Japanese vehicles.<br>
2. <strong>U.S.</strong> vehicles importing engines and transmissions from Canada and Mexico are subject to a 25% tariff.<br>
3. The <strong>UAW</strong> and <strong>AAPC</strong> (<strong>GM</strong>, <strong>Ford</strong>, <strong>Stellantis</strong>) have expressed deep anger at the agreement.<br>
4. The <strong>USMCA</strong>'s 75% North America content requirement is effectively rendered moot in practice.<br>
5. Japan exports more than 1.5 million vehicles annually to the <strong>U.S.</strong></p>
