<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; <strong>Bloomberg News</strong> report; analyst insights; U.S. President <strong>Donald Trump</strong> has announced a delay in some tariff plans — yet the first signs of an economically damaging slowdown in global trade are already emerging — companies worldwide are hitting their own pause buttons as <strong>Trump</strong> continues to escalate the trade war with <strong>China</strong>. <strong>Trump</strong> announced on April 9 that he would raise import duties on <strong>Chinese</strong> goods to 125% (later 145%) — while simultaneously announcing a 90-day pause on plans to impose higher tariffs on dozens of other countries. <strong>Amazon</strong> has begun canceling orders from <strong>China</strong> and other regions of <strong>Asia</strong> — reducing inventory. Buyers are re-evaluating orders with cargo shippers — container volumes are dropping across the <strong>Pacific</strong>. Apparel, footwear, toys, and consumer electronics are being hit hard.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Bloomberg L.P.</strong>, based in <strong>New York</strong> and founded by <strong>Michael Bloomberg</strong> with <strong>John Micklethwait</strong> as Editor-in-Chief, is a leader in global financial news. <strong>Bloomberg News</strong>, <strong>Bloomberg Terminal</strong>, and <strong>Bloomberg.com</strong> are its primary distribution channels. <strong>Amazon</strong>, based in <strong>Seattle, Washington U.S.</strong>, with CEO <strong>Andy Jassy</strong>, CEO Worldwide Amazon Stores <strong>Doug Herrington</strong>, and AWS CEO <strong>Matt Garman</strong> (replacing <strong>Adam Selipsky</strong>), provides e-commerce, cloud computing, and logistics services globally. <strong>Amazon</strong>'s imports from <strong>China</strong> — particularly <strong>Amazon Basics</strong>, <strong>Amazon Marketplace</strong> third-party sellers (3P sellers), and <strong>FBA</strong> (<strong>Fulfillment by Amazon</strong>) inventory — are the primary impacted segments. U.S. imports from <strong>China</strong> in consumer electronics, apparel, footwear, toys, furniture, and home goods are the main categories — estimated at 450-500 billion dollars annually. U.S. retail giants — <strong>Walmart</strong> (CEO <strong>Doug McMillon</strong>; <strong>Bentonville, Arkansas</strong>), <strong>Target</strong> (CEO <strong>Brian Cornell</strong>), <strong>Costco</strong>, <strong>Home Depot</strong>, <strong>Lowe's</strong>, <strong>Best Buy</strong>, <strong>Macy's</strong>, <strong>Kohl's</strong>, <strong>Dollar Tree</strong> (most dependent on <strong>China</strong> — 40% of merchandise) — are the retailers most impacted.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the main trans-Pacific container shipping operators — <strong>MSC</strong> (<strong>Mediterranean Shipping Company</strong>; Chairman <strong>Diego Apont</strong>), <strong>Maersk</strong> (CEO <strong>Vincent Clerc</strong>; <strong>Copenhagen</strong>), <strong>CMA CGM</strong> (CEO <strong>Rodolphe Sadé</strong>; <strong>Marseille</strong>), <strong>COSCO Shipping</strong> (Chairman <strong>Wan Min</strong>; <strong>Shanghai</strong>), <strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong> (CEO <strong>Rolf Habben Jansen</strong>; <strong>Hamburg</strong>), <strong>ONE</strong> (<strong>Ocean Network Express</strong>; CEO <strong>Jeremy Nixon</strong>; <strong>Singapore</strong>), <strong>Evergreen Marine</strong>, <strong>HMM</strong> (<strong>Korea</strong>), <strong>Yang Ming</strong>, <strong>ZIM</strong> (CEO <strong>Eli Glickman</strong>) — are the main ecosystem players. <strong>Gemini Cooperation</strong> (<strong>Maersk</strong>+<strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong>), <strong>Premier Alliance</strong> (<strong>ONE</strong>+<strong>HMM</strong>+<strong>Yang Ming</strong>), and <strong>MSC</strong> operating independently are the main alliance structures post-2025. <strong>2M Alliance</strong> (<strong>Maersk</strong>+<strong>MSC</strong>; ending), <strong>Ocean Alliance</strong> (<strong>CMA CGM</strong>+<strong>COSCO</strong>+<strong>Evergreen</strong>+<strong>OOCL</strong>) are the older/continuing structures. The main U.S. entry ports are <strong>Port of Los Angeles</strong> (Executive Director <strong>Gene Seroka</strong>), <strong>Port of Long Beach</strong> (CEO <strong>Mario Cordero</strong>), <strong>Port of New York/New Jersey</strong> (Port Director <strong>Bethann Rooney</strong>), <strong>Port of Savannah</strong> (<strong>Georgia Ports Authority</strong>; <strong>Griff Lynch</strong>), <strong>Port of Houston</strong>, and <strong>Port of Seattle/Tacoma</strong> (<strong>Northwest Seaport Alliance</strong>).</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the increase in blank sailings from <strong>China</strong> to the U.S. is a key indicator of demand destruction in container shipping. <strong>Drewry World Container Index</strong> (<strong>WCI</strong>), <strong>Shanghai Containerized Freight Index</strong> (<strong>SCFI</strong>), and <strong>Freightos Baltic Index</strong> (<strong>FBX</strong>) are the main freight rate indices. <strong>Sea-Intelligence</strong> (CEO <strong>Alan Murphy</strong>), <strong>Linerlytica</strong>, <strong>Xeneta</strong> (CEO <strong>Patrik Berglund</strong>), and <strong>Container xChange</strong> are the main container shipping analytics firms. U.S. retail inventory strategies include (1) front-loading (accelerating imports ahead of tariffs), (2) nearshoring (<strong>Mexico</strong>, <strong>Vietnam</strong>, <strong>India</strong> shifts), (3) tariff engineering, (4) FTZ, (5) bonded warehouses, (6) order pauses, and (7) consumer price pass-through. <strong>National Retail Federation</strong> (<strong>NRF</strong>; CEO <strong>Matthew Shay</strong>), <strong>U.S. Chamber of Commerce</strong> (CEO <strong>Suzanne Clark</strong>), <strong>Retail Industry Leaders Association</strong> (<strong>RILA</strong>), and <strong>American Apparel and Footwear Association</strong> (<strong>AAFA</strong>) are the main U.S. retail and industry advocacy groups calling for public opposition to tariffs. In conclusion, <strong>Bloomberg</strong>'s report on global trade slowdown suggests that container shipping demand is fundamentally being restructured globally — order management, inventory optimization, and alternative sourcing appear to be the main strategic priorities for supply chain managers.</p>
