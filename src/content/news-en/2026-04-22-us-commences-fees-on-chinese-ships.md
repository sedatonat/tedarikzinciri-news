<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Trump</strong> administration begins imposing fees on <strong>Chinese</strong> vessels calling at American ports on October 14 &mdash; a move aimed at countering <strong>China</strong>&rsquo;s dominance in commercial shipbuilding and revitalizing the <strong>United States</strong>&rsquo; own shipbuilding sector. <strong>China</strong>&rsquo;s Ministry of Transport retaliated on October 10, announcing plans to charge American vessels when they call at <strong>Chinese</strong> ports. <strong>U.S.</strong>-linked vessels currently face charges of 400 yuan (56 dollars) per net ton when calling at <strong>Chinese</strong> ports, according to <strong>Chinese</strong> state media <strong>CCTV</strong>. <strong>The New York Times</strong> reports that this &ldquo;tit-for-tat&rdquo; escalation comes as trade relations between <strong>China</strong> and the <strong>U.S.</strong> deteriorate further. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Office of the United States Trade Representative</strong> (<strong>USTR</strong>) is investigating <strong>China</strong>&rsquo;s shipbuilding dominance under a <strong>Section 301 investigation</strong> &mdash; and the <strong>USTR</strong> has subsequently initiated service fees.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the global shipbuilding sector is dominated by <strong>China</strong>, which produces over 50% of global total tonnage &mdash; <strong>South Korea</strong> accounts for 30%, <strong>Japan</strong> 15%, and the <strong>U.S.</strong> less than 1%. Major <strong>Chinese</strong> shipyards include <strong>China State Shipbuilding Corporation</strong> (<strong>CSSC</strong>); <strong>China Shipbuilding Industry Corporation</strong> (<strong>CSIC</strong>, which merged with <strong>CSSC</strong>); <strong>Yangzijiang Shipbuilding</strong>; <strong>New Times Shipbuilding</strong>; <strong>Hudong-Zhonghua Shipbuilding</strong>; <strong>Jiangnan Shipyard</strong>; <strong>Dalian Shipbuilding Industry</strong>; and <strong>Jiangsu Hantong</strong>. Major <strong>Korean</strong> shipyards include <strong>HD Hyundai Heavy Industries</strong>; <strong>HD Korea Shipbuilding &amp; Offshore Engineering</strong>; <strong>Samsung Heavy Industries</strong>; <strong>Hanwha Ocean</strong> (formerly <strong>Daewoo Shipbuilding &amp; Marine Engineering</strong>, or <strong>DSME</strong>); and <strong>HJ Shipbuilding &amp; Construction</strong>. Major <strong>Japanese</strong> shipyards include <strong>Mitsui E&amp;S</strong>; <strong>Imabari Shipbuilding</strong>; <strong>Tsuneishi Shipbuilding</strong>; <strong>Japan Marine United</strong>; <strong>Mitsubishi Shipbuilding</strong>; <strong>Kawasaki Heavy Industries</strong>; and <strong>Sumitomo Heavy Industries Marine &amp; Engineering</strong>. Major <strong>European</strong> shipyards include <strong>Fincantieri</strong> (<strong>Italy</strong>); <strong>Damen Shipyards</strong> (<strong>Netherlands</strong>); <strong>Meyer Werft</strong> (<strong>Germany</strong>); <strong>Chantiers de l&rsquo;Atlantique</strong> (<strong>France</strong>); <strong>Naval Group</strong>; <strong>BAE Systems Maritime</strong>; <strong>Babcock</strong>; <strong>Navantia</strong> (<strong>Spain</strong>); and <strong>Damen Shipyards</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>U.S. shipbuilding</strong> has structurally contracted since World War II. The <strong>Jones Act</strong> (<strong>Merchant Marine Act of 1920</strong>) requires that commercial vessels operating in <strong>U.S.</strong> inland waterways be <strong>U.S.</strong>-built, <strong>U.S.</strong>-owned, <strong>U.S.</strong>-flagged, and <strong>U.S.</strong>-crewed. Major <strong>U.S.</strong> shipyards include <strong>General Dynamics NASSCO</strong>; <strong>General Dynamics Bath Iron Works</strong>; <strong>General Dynamics Electric Boat</strong>; <strong>HII Newport News Shipbuilding</strong> (<strong>Huntington Ingalls Industries</strong>); <strong>HII Ingalls</strong>; <strong>Bollinger Shipyards</strong>; <strong>Austal USA</strong>; <strong>Eastern Shipbuilding</strong>; <strong>Marinette Marine</strong> (<strong>Fincantieri</strong>); and <strong>Philly Shipyard</strong> (<strong>owned by HD Hyundai</strong>). The <strong>SHIPS for America Act</strong> is legislation pending in the <strong>Senate</strong> designed to revitalize the <strong>U.S.</strong> shipbuilding sector. <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> is establishing a <strong>Maritime Executive Office</strong> at the <strong>White House</strong> to coordinate maritime policy. Programs such as the <strong>Maritime Security Program</strong> (<strong>MSP</strong>), <strong>Tanker Security Program</strong> (<strong>TSP</strong>), and <strong>Cable Security Program</strong> subsidize <strong>U.S.</strong> strategic maritime capacity. The <strong>U.S. Navy</strong> remains the world&rsquo;s largest fleet &mdash; though <strong>China</strong>&rsquo;s <strong>PLA Navy</strong> (<strong>PLAN</strong>) has surpassed it in ship count.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>China</strong>&rsquo;s dominance in global commercial shipping is reflected in major <strong>Chinese</strong> maritime companies including <strong>COSCO Shipping Lines</strong>; <strong>OOCL</strong> (<strong>Orient Overseas Container Line</strong>, acquired by <strong>COSCO</strong> in 2018); <strong>China Merchants Group</strong>; <strong>Shanghai International Port Group</strong> (<strong>SIPG</strong>); <strong>China Shipping</strong> (merged with <strong>COSCO</strong>); <strong>Sinotrans</strong>; and <strong>SITC International</strong>. The <strong>USTR Section 301 Service Fee</strong> applies to <strong>Chinese</strong>-built, <strong>Chinese</strong>-owned, and <strong>Chinese</strong>-operated vessels, with a tariff structure that increases progressively per net ton. <strong>Flags of convenience</strong> (<strong>FOC</strong>) include registries in <strong>Liberia</strong>; <strong>Marshall Islands</strong>; <strong>Panama</strong>; <strong>Hong Kong</strong>; <strong>Singapore</strong>; <strong>Malta</strong>; <strong>Cyprus</strong>; <strong>Bahamas</strong>; <strong>Cayman Islands</strong>; and <strong>Antigua and Barbuda</strong>. Key maritime ownership and regulatory concepts include <strong>true ownership</strong>; <strong>beneficial owner</strong>; <strong>flag state</strong>; and <strong>port state control</strong>. Key sectoral organizations include the <strong>WTO</strong>; <strong>UNCTAD Review of Maritime Transport</strong>; <strong>BIMCO</strong>; and <strong>ICS</strong> (<strong>International Chamber of Shipping</strong>). Ultimately, the <strong>U.S.</strong>&rsquo;s imposition of fees on <strong>Chinese</strong> vessels represents a clear marker of the geoeconomic transformation of global maritime trade.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Trump</strong> administration begins imposing port fees on <strong>Chinese</strong> vessels on October 14.<br>
2. <strong>China</strong> announces retaliation on October 10 &mdash; charging <strong>U.S.</strong>-linked vessels.<br>
3. <strong>Chinese</strong> port fee: 400 yuan (56 dollars) per net ton.<br>
4. <strong>U.S.</strong> holds less than 1% of the global shipbuilding market.<br>
5. <strong>USTR Section 301</strong> provides the legal basis for the fee.</p>
