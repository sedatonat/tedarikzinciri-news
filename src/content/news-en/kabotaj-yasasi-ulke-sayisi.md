<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>According to a new report published by Seafarers' Rights International (SRI) on behalf of the International Transport Federation (ITF)</strong>, <strong>the number of countries enforcing cabotage laws has increased from 91 in 2018 to 105 in 2025.</strong></p>
<p><br>
These countries now control <strong>85% of global coastlines</strong> — marking <strong>the fastest expansion period for cabotage rules in maritime history.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The report, titled <em>"Cabotage Laws of the World,"</em> reveals that the findings show <strong>rising geopolitical uncertainties in the post-pandemic era</strong> are driving countries to <strong>strengthen maritime sovereignty.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Cabotage: From Economics to National Security</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to <strong>Deirdre Fitzpatrick, Executive Director of SRI</strong> and co-author of the report, cabotage is no longer viewed solely as an economic tool but rather as <strong>a cornerstone of national security strategy:</strong></p>
<blockquote style="text-align: justify">"Countries are increasingly viewing cabotage not simply as economic policy, but as an integral part of strategic autonomy and national security in an increasingly unstable geopolitical environment."</blockquote>
<p style="text-align: justify">Fitzpatrick noted that these 105 countries <strong>represent a geographically diverse group</strong>, emphasizing that growing awareness is translating into concrete legal measures.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>The U.S. Jones Act: A Global Reference Point</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The United States</strong> stands as the strongest enforcer of cabotage laws.</p>
<p><br>
In the country, <strong>maritime cabotage</strong> is governed by the <strong>Jones Act of 1920.</strong></p>
<p><br>
This law mandates that <strong>cargo transported between U.S. ports must be carried exclusively by U.S.-built, U.S.-flagged, U.S.-owned, and U.S.-crewed vessels.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>David Heindel, President of the Seafarers International Union and Chair of the ITF Seafarers' Section</strong>, stated that the report validates views long championed by the maritime community:</p>
<blockquote style="text-align: justify">"The Jones Act represents the model that best serves the interests of the United States, our seafarers, and our national security.<br>
It is no surprise that countries facing today's geopolitical challenges are strengthening their own cabotage regulations."</blockquote>
<blockquote><br></blockquote>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Economic Impact: 650,000 Jobs and $150 Billion in Value</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">The Jones Act holds significant importance not only from a national security perspective but also economically.</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>U.S. cabotage fleet:</strong> Approximately <strong>40,000 vessels</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Employment impact:</strong> <strong>650,000 people</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Economic contribution:</strong> <strong>$150 billion annually</strong></p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">Additionally, the law supports <strong>strategic sealift capacity for the U.S. Armed Forces.</strong></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Jennifer Carpenter, President of the American Maritime Partnership</strong>, defended the law as follows:</p>
<blockquote style="text-align: justify">"We do not allow foreign airlines or foreign trucking companies to transport passengers or cargo between U.S. cities, and the same principle applies to maritime commerce.<br>
Protecting our own maritime boundaries and supply chains cannot be left to the decisions of foreign carriers."</blockquote>
<blockquote><br></blockquote>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Global Trend: National Sovereignty and Strategic Autonomy</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to the report, the expansion of cabotage laws is not limited to the United States.<br>
Similar strong regulations are being enforced in the following countries:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>South Korea</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Japan</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Israel</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Brazil</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Turkey</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>China</strong></p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">Most of these countries aim to <strong>keep critical maritime shipping services under local control during crises</strong>, protect supply chains from <strong>dependence on foreign operators</strong>, and achieve <strong>self-sufficiency in defense logistics.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Geopolitical Context: Rising Tensions and Maritime Sovereignty</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">In the post-pandemic era, <strong>energy supply, supply chain security, and regional conflicts</strong> have driven countries toward a <strong>"strategic autonomy" approach in maritime shipping.</strong></p>
<p><br>
Cabotage laws protect <strong>local workforces and merchant fleets</strong> while simultaneously functioning as <strong>an economic defense mechanism against foreign influence.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to the SRI report, this trend is accelerating among countries in <strong>Asia, the Middle East, Latin America, and Africa.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Conclusion: Maritime Sovereignty Has Become a Global Priority</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">As of 2025, <strong>105 countries</strong> have placed cabotage laws at the center of their national strategies.</p>
<p><br>
This figure is viewed not merely as an indicator of <strong>economic protectionism, but also of geopolitical resilience.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Cabotage</strong> is no longer merely a mechanism for regulating domestic trade; it has become <strong>a cornerstone of maritime security, strategic infrastructure, and economic independence.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
