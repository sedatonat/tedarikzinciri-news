<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Panama</strong>'s government has issued a decree ordering the <strong>Panama Maritime Authority</strong> to assume control of two ports located at the entrances of the <strong>Panama Canal</strong>, facilities that have long occupied a contentious status. The order responds to a <strong>Supreme Court</strong> decision that declared the operating concession held by Hong Kong-based <strong>CK Hutchison</strong> unconstitutional. From a supply-chain perspective, this move redraws the corridor risk profile for transpacific and transatlantic services between the <strong>U.S. East Coast</strong> and <strong>Asian</strong> main routes overnight. Major liner operators including <strong>Maersk</strong>, <strong>MSC</strong>, <strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong>, and <strong>CMA CGM</strong> are expected to reassess their call planning for <strong>Balboa</strong> and <strong>Cristobal</strong> in the near term.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">According to the <strong>Associated Press</strong>, a decree issued on February 23 orders the <strong>Panama Maritime Authority</strong> to assume control of the ports citing "reasons of urgent social interest," and the order encompasses all cranes, equipment, computer systems, and software at the hubs. The measure effectively places <strong>Balboa</strong> and <strong>Cristobal</strong> terminals under temporary state control, escalating tensions with <strong>CK Hutchison</strong> and reopening the question of who will ultimately operate the ports going forward. From a supply-chain perspective, preserving liner schedule continuity means that container call sequencing stability, turn time performance, and contractual service contract terms may require additional flexibility during annual renewal periods.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Panama</strong> initially announced plans to have <strong>AP Moller-Maersk</strong>'s <strong>APM Terminals</strong> unit temporarily assume administrative control of the terminals pending a new concession tender. Subsequently, <strong>CK Hutchison</strong> has made threats of legal action against the Danish liner operator and claimed that a forced takeover could potentially disrupt regional commerce. The <strong>Panama</strong>n government, however, has emphasized that terminal operations will not be disrupted while the dispute is being resolved. From a supply-chain perspective, <strong>Maersk</strong>'s interim operator position is a headline being closely monitored with competitive sensitivity in the post-2M era. The <strong>FMC</strong> and other regulatory bodies may assess down the road whether this arrangement is intended to be permanent.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Panama</strong>'s <strong>Supreme Court</strong> invalidated <strong>CK Hutchison</strong>'s concession contract in a July 2025 ruling, stating that the original agreement failed to meet constitutional requirements regarding transparency and competitive bidding frameworks. The court decision effectively stripped the Hong Kong-based operator of its legal standing at the ports and now, after months of political maneuvering, has resulted in direct government intervention. From a supply-chain perspective, the global parallel to this case suggests that port portfolios owned by Hong Kong and Chinese entities may be undergoing restructuring under geopolitical pressure. <strong>CK Hutchison</strong>'s global terminal portfolio encompasses numerous strategic ports including <strong>Felixstowe</strong>, <strong>Rotterdam</strong>, and <strong>Antwerp</strong>, and the direct or indirect reverberations of the <strong>Panama</strong> situation may influence long-term investment planning across this network. Ultimately, the <strong>Panama Canal</strong> port dispute is emerging as a critical inflection point where the balance between sovereignty and commercial continuity is being redefined in global port operations.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>\n1. <strong>Panama</strong> is placing <strong>Balboa</strong> and <strong>Cristobal</strong> ports under temporary government control by decree.<br>\n2.
