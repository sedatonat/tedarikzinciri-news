<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; analyst insight; despite progress recorded over decades, child labor remains deeply embedded in global supply chains &mdash; millions of children work in dangerous and exploitative jobs to produce everyday consumer goods. The scope of the problem is shocking. According to <strong>United Nations</strong> data, an estimated 152 million children aged 5-17 globally participate in child labor &mdash; half of whom work in hazardous conditions. Approximately 90% of these children are found in <strong>Africa</strong>, <strong>Asia</strong>, or the <strong>Pacific</strong>, while the remaining 10% are dispersed across <strong>North America</strong>, <strong>Europe</strong>, <strong>Central Asia</strong>, and <strong>Arab States</strong>. As scrutiny intensifies, corporations face mounting pressure to enforce compliance.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>International Labour Organization</strong> (<strong>ILO</strong>; <strong>Gilbert F. Houngbo</strong> Director-General; based in <strong>Geneva</strong>), founded in 1919 with 187 member states, serves as the primary <strong>UN</strong> authority on labor standards. <strong>ILO Convention 138</strong> (<strong>Minimum Age Convention</strong>) and <strong>ILO Convention 182</strong> (<strong>Worst Forms of Child Labour Convention</strong>) are the principal international frameworks addressing child labor. <strong>UNICEF</strong> (<strong>United Nations Children's Fund</strong>; <strong>Catherine Russell</strong> Executive Director), <strong>Save the Children</strong>, <strong>Plan International</strong>, <strong>Human Rights Watch</strong>, and <strong>Amnesty International</strong> are the primary complementary UN and NGO child rights organizations. The principal U.S. child labor regulatory authorities are the <strong>U.S. Department of Labor</strong> (<strong>DOL</strong>; <strong>Lori Chavez-DeRemer</strong> Secretary; Trump 2.0), the <strong>Wage and Hour Division</strong> (<strong>WHD</strong>), and the <strong>Bureau of International Labor Affairs</strong> (<strong>ILAB</strong>; whose <strong>List of Goods Produced by Child Labor or Forced Labor</strong> is the principal annual report). The <strong>Fair Labor Standards Act</strong> (<strong>FLSA</strong>; 1938) is the principal U.S. child labor statute &mdash; prohibiting employment under age 14 in general.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the principal high-risk child labor sectors and geographies are: (1) <strong>Cocoa</strong> (<strong>Côte d'Ivoire</strong>, <strong>Ghana</strong> &mdash; 60-70% of global cocoa; sourced by <strong>Hershey</strong>, <strong>Mars</strong>, <strong>Nestlé</strong>, <strong>Mondelez</strong>, <strong>Cargill</strong>, <strong>Barry Callebaut</strong>); (2) <strong>Cobalt</strong> (<strong>Democratic Republic of Congo</strong>, <strong>DRC</strong> &mdash; 70%+ of global cobalt; sourced by <strong>Tesla</strong>, <strong>Apple</strong>, <strong>Samsung</strong>, <strong>Glencore</strong>, <strong>CMOC Group</strong>); (3) <strong>Cotton</strong> (<strong>Uzbekistan</strong>, <strong>Turkmenistan</strong>, <strong>Pakistan</strong>, <strong>India</strong>, <strong>Xinjiang China</strong> &mdash; covered under <strong>UFLPA</strong>); (4) <strong>Coffee</strong> (<strong>Brazil</strong>, <strong>Ethiopia</strong>, <strong>Honduras</strong> &mdash; sourced by <strong>Starbucks</strong>, <strong>Nestlé</strong>, <strong>JDE Peet's</strong>); (5) <strong>Tobacco</strong> (<strong>Malawi</strong>, <strong>Zimbabwe</strong>); (6) <strong>Mica</strong> (<strong>India</strong>, <strong>Madagascar</strong> &mdash; cosmetics); (7) <strong>Apparel</strong> (<strong>Bangladesh</strong>, <strong>Cambodia</strong>, <strong>Vietnam</strong>, <strong>Ethiopia</strong>); (8) <strong>Footwear</strong>; (9) <strong>Shrimp and fisheries</strong> (<strong>Thailand</strong>); (10) <strong>Mining</strong> (gold, diamonds, rare earth elements). The <strong>Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act</strong> (<strong>UFLPA</strong>; 2021) and <strong>Customs and Border Protection</strong> (<strong>CBP</strong>) <strong>Withhold Release Order</strong> (<strong>WRO</strong>) are the principal U.S. forced labor enforcement tools.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the principal supply chain child labor audit and certification organizations are: (1) <strong>Fairtrade International</strong> (<strong>Bonn Germany</strong>); (2) <strong>Rainforest Alliance</strong>; (3) <strong>UTZ Certified</strong> (<strong>Rainforest Alliance</strong> merger); (4) <strong>Fair Trade USA</strong>; (5) <strong>SA8000</strong> (<strong>Social Accountability International</strong>; <strong>SAI</strong>); (6) <strong>Better Cotton Initiative</strong> (<strong>BCI</strong>); (7) <strong>amfori BSCI</strong> (<strong>Business Social Compliance Initiative</strong>); (8) <strong>Sedex SMETA</strong>; (9) <strong>WRAP</strong> (<strong>Worldwide Responsible Accredited Production</strong>); (10) <strong>Responsible Business Alliance</strong> (<strong>RBA</strong>; formerly <strong>EICC</strong>). The <strong>EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive</strong> (<strong>CSDDD</strong>), the <strong>EU Forced Labour Regulation</strong> (approved March 2024), the <strong>German Supply Chain Due Diligence Act</strong> (<strong>LkSG</strong>; 2023), the <strong>French Duty of Care Law</strong> (2017), the <strong>Norwegian Transparency Law</strong> (2022), the <strong>UK Modern Slavery Act</strong> (2015), and the <strong>Australia Modern Slavery Act</strong> (2018) are the principal global forced labor due diligence statutes. The principal supply chain visibility platforms include <strong>EcoVadis</strong> (<strong>Pierre-François Thaler</strong>), <strong>Sayari</strong>, <strong>Altana</strong>, <strong>Resilinc</strong>, <strong>Sourcemap</strong>, and <strong>Avetta</strong>. In conclusion, the child labor supply chain report indicates that globally, <strong>ESG</strong> and human rights due diligence are becoming fundamentally mandatory &mdash; <strong>UFLPA</strong> and <strong>CSDDD</strong> compliance and <strong>n-tier</strong> visibility appear to be emerging as principal strategic priorities for supply chain managers.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>\n1. <strong>UN</strong> data: globally 152M children in child labor &mdash; 90% in <strong>Africa</strong>/<strong>Asia</strong>/<strong>Pacific</strong>.<br>\n2. <strong>ILO Convention 138</strong>/<strong>182</strong> are the principal international frameworks.<br>\n3. <strong>Cocoa</strong>, <strong>cobalt</strong>, and <strong>cotton</strong> are principal high-risk sectors.<br>\n4. <strong>UFLPA</strong>, <strong>CSDDD</strong>, and <strong>LkSG</strong> are principal regulatory frameworks.<br>\n5. <strong>Fairtrade</strong>, <strong>Rainforest Alliance</strong>, and <strong>SA8000</strong> are principal certification organizations.</p>\n\n<p><strong>!!!
