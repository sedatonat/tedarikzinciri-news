<p><strong>The National Transportation Safety Board (NTSB)</strong> has disclosed preliminary findings regarding the structural failure that caused <strong>UPS's McDonnell Douglas MD-11F cargo aircraft</strong> to crash during takeoff from Louisville–Muhammad Ali International Airport on November 4. According to the report, <strong>severe "fatigue cracks" were detected in the engine pylon and fastening elements</strong> that secured the aircraft's left engine.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h5><strong>Critical failure: left engine separated at takeoff</strong></h5>\n<p>The NTSB report states that the left engine separated from the fuselage along with its pylon <strong>seconds after takeoff</strong>, and the aircraft <strong>struck the ground without gaining more than 30 feet (9 meters) of altitude</strong>.</p>\n<p><br>\nThe crash killed <strong>a total of 14 people, including crew members and individuals on the ground.</strong></p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p>Images shared in the preliminary report show the engine <strong>fracturing under extreme stress</strong> and becoming airborne in a massive fireball.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h5><strong>"Similarities to 1979 AA DC-10 accident"</strong></h5>\n<p><strong>Jeff Guzzetti</strong>, former director of crash investigations for the U.S. Federal Aviation Administration, said the UPS crash bore <strong>striking similarities</strong> to the <strong>1979 American Airlines DC-10 accident</strong>.</p>\n<p><br>\nIn both accidents, the left engine separated from the fuselage during takeoff.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p>However, the NTSB's UPS report <strong>found no evidence of external maintenance errors</strong>. The findings instead point more toward <strong>aging, metal fatigue, and structural deterioration</strong>.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h5><strong>FAA temporarily grounds MD-11 fleets</strong></h5>\n<p>Following the UPS crash, the FAA issued a directive requiring <strong>urgent inspections of all MD-11F aircraft</strong>.<br>\nThe directive was subsequently expanded <strong>to include other aircraft with similar engine–pylon designs</strong>.</p>\n<p>Currently:</p>\n<ul>\n  <li><p><strong>9%</strong> of UPS's fleet,</p></li>\n  <li><p><strong>4%</strong> of FedEx's fleet<br>\nremain temporarily grounded.</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p>UPS announced that it had grounded its MD-11 fleet on its own initiative <strong>as a precautionary measure</strong> even before the FAA announcement.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h5><strong>Maintenance intervals under scrutiny</strong></h5>\n<p>A notable point in the investigation is that the failed component was <strong>still in the middle of its maintenance life</strong>.<br>\nAccording to the NTSB:</p>\n<ul>\n  <li><p>The engine attachment point underwent <strong>visual inspection in October 2021</strong>.</p></li>\n  <li><p>For more detailed inspection, the aircraft needed to complete <strong>8,000 additional takeoff and landing cycles</strong>.</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p>Guzzetti stated:</p>\n<blockquote>"By design, this component should have exhibited fatigue much later. Yet in this case, <strong>it failed much sooner</strong>."</blockquote>\n<p>This raises serious questions about <strong>design strength</strong>, <strong>metal fatigue calculations</strong>, and <strong>the adequacy of maintenance intervals for aging fleets</strong> of MD-11F aircraft.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h5><strong>Aircraft history</strong></h5>\n<p>The aircraft involved in the crash:</p>\n<ul>\n  <li><p><strong>Was manufactured for Thai Airways in the early 1990s</strong>,</p></li>\n  <li><p>Was converted to cargo configuration approximately <strong>20 years ago</strong>,</p></li>\n  <li><p>Was an MD-11F model <strong>powered by three GE CF6 engines</strong>.</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p>The passenger version of MD-11 aircraft <strong>was retired decades ago</strong>; the model is now used exclusively in cargo operations.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h5><strong>Root cause unconfirmed: final report could take 1–2 years</strong></h5>\n<p>The NTSB stated that the preliminary findings represent only initial assessments and that <strong>final analysis and root cause investigation</strong> could take <strong>1–2 years</strong>.</p>\n<p>The investigation will examine:</p>\n<ul>\n  <li><p>metal fatigue,</p></li>\n  <li><p>maintenance records,</p></li>\n  <li><p>pylon design,</p></li>\n  <li><p>vibration loads, and thermal stress<br>\namong other areas.</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p>UPS and Boeing stated they are providing <strong>full support</strong> to the investigation.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<h5><strong>Key Points:</strong></h5>\n<ul>\n  <li><p>The MD-11F crash was caused by <strong>fatigue cracks</strong> in the left engine attachment.</p></li>\n  <li><p>The engine separated seconds after takeoff, leaving the aircraft uncontrollable.</p></li>\n  <li><p>The FAA has mandated <strong>emergency inspections and temporary groundings</strong> for MD-11F aircraft in UPS and FedEx fleets.</p></li>\n  <li><p>The crash shares technical similarities with the <strong>1979 AA DC-10 engine separation incident</strong>.</p></li>\n  <li><p>The failure of the defective component <strong>before its maintenance life expired</strong> suggests design or aging issues.</p></li>\n  <li><p>A final report is expected <strong>within 1–2 years</strong>.</p></li>\n</ul>\n<p><br></p>\n<p>----------</p>\n\n<p>--------------------</p>\n<p><strong>Author: </strong><a href="http://sedatonat.com/"><u>SedatOnat.com</u></a></p>\n<p>--------------------</p>\n\n<p>We would be delighted to receive your feedback.</p>\n<p>Wishing you happy reading.</p>\n
