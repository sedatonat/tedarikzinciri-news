<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Israel</strong>-<strong>U.S.</strong> tensions with <strong>Iran</strong> are affecting shipping, with focus largely on oil and <strong>LNG</strong> tanker traffic; however, analysts warn container shipping faces significant impact well beyond the region. From a supply-chain perspective, the rerouting of <strong>Persian Gulf</strong>-connected <strong>main line</strong> services is redrawing the <strong>port rotation</strong> schemas on <strong>Asia-Middle East</strong>, <strong>Asia-North Europe</strong>, and <strong>Asia-Mediterranean</strong> routes. <strong>2M</strong>, <strong>OCEAN Alliance</strong>, and <strong>Premier Alliance</strong> are simultaneously deploying <strong>blank sailing</strong>, <strong>port omission</strong>, and <strong>port substitution</strong> strategies, measures that are introducing gradual tightening in the capacity-rate balance.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Judah Levine</strong>, <strong>Head of Research</strong> at online freight marketplace <strong>Freightos</strong>, noted in a March 17 statement that container carriers initially suspended all services to the <strong>Persian Gulf</strong>. Now, however, lines including <strong>CMA CGM</strong> and <strong>Maersk</strong> are accepting new bookings by redirecting volume to accessible alternative ports in the region—including ports in <strong>Oman</strong>, <strong>UAE</strong>, and <strong>Saudi Arabia</strong>—with containers continuing via <strong>landbridge</strong>. <strong>Levine</strong> noted that carriers are also heavily relying on ports in <strong>India</strong> and deploying new <strong>shuttle</strong> services to transport containers to accessible <strong>Middle East</strong> ports. However, some hubs, such as <strong>Fujairah</strong> in the <strong>UAE</strong> (pictured), are now being directly targeted by <strong>Iran</strong>. From a supply-chain perspective, this secondary targeting exposes the fragility of <strong>regional hub</strong> strategies and is pushing <strong>BCO</strong>s toward preferring <strong>direct call</strong> options on <strong>Asia-Europe</strong> routes.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Congestion is mounting at these alternative ports and in <strong>India</strong>; the <strong>Port of Colombo</strong> in <strong>Sri Lanka</strong> is refusing to accept <strong>Gulf</strong>-bound volumes due to existing congestion. Meanwhile, carriers are entering into tension with customers over rising rates. <strong>Karin Ström</strong>, <strong>VP</strong> at supply and procurement advisory <strong>Proxima</strong>, is calling on shippers to "push back firmly and ensure pricing reflects actual risk, not opportunism." In a March 10 statement, <strong>Ström</strong> noted that ocean freight rates are rising primarily on <strong>China</strong> origins—a situation partially linked to <strong>China</strong>'s recent role as a major buyer of <strong>Iranian</strong> oil. With <strong>Iran</strong> restricted in whom it can sell to, <strong>China</strong> is benefiting from heavily discounted volumes; any disruption in these flows or elevated risk is now feeding into <strong>Chinese</strong> export pricing and capacity dynamics.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Freightos</strong> notes that carriers are announcing global flat-rate emergency fuel surcharges of hundreds of dollars per <strong>FEU</strong> to take effect early next week. However, these are not unprecedented fuel adjustments; the market is being hit with an impact that is "expensive, but not destructive," though rates could likely be pushed back toward 2024 levels. Meanwhile, <strong>Ström</strong> reports that some carriers are attempting to apply "war surcharges" on routes with no direct exposure. <strong>Levine</strong> from <strong>Freightos</strong> states, "there is some skepticism that market dynamics will see these increases succeed fully," and notes that <strong>beneficial cargo owners</strong> engaged in annual ocean contract negotiations are pushing back against carrier fees related to <strong>Iran</strong> war disruptions that do not directly affect these shippers' volumes. As a result, the <strong>Iran</strong> war is triggering a multiple shock wave in which <strong>landbridge</strong>, <strong>India shuttle</strong>, <strong>BAF</strong>/<strong>EBS</strong> surcharges, and <strong>war surcharge</strong> headings are being simultaneously reintroduced.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>\n1. <strong>CMA CGM</strong> and <strong>Maersk</strong> are redirecting to ports in <strong>Oman</strong>, <strong>UAE</strong>, and <strong>Saudi Arabia</strong>.<br>\n2. <strong>Shuttle</strong> services via <strong>India</strong> are being deployed; <strong>Fujairah</strong> is being targeted.<br>\n3. <strong>Colombo</strong> is refusing <strong>Gulf</strong> cargo due to existing congestion.<br>\n4. Hundreds of dollars in emergency fuel surcharges per <strong>FEU</strong> are being announced.<br>\n5. <strong>BCO</strong>s are resisting <strong>war surcharge</strong> on routes with no direct exposure.</p>\n<p>---<br>\n<strong>News Link:</strong> <a href="https://www.supplychainbrain.com/articles/43673-war-in-iran-roils-containership-traffic-rates"><u>https://www.supplychainbrain.com/articles/43673-war-in-iran-roils-containership-traffic-rates</u></a><br>\n-------------------</p>\n<p><strong>!!!
