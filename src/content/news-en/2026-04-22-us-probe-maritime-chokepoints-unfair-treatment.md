<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; analyst insight; the <strong>Federal Maritime Commission</strong> (<strong>FMC</strong>) is investigating global maritime chokepoints — to assess whether a country, ocean carrier, or other maritime operator is creating conditions unfavorable to U.S. shipping and foreign trade. "Recent events have led to transit constraints at several critical points in the global shipping supply chain, creating conditions appropriate for Commission investigation," the U.S. agency states in a <strong>Federal Register</strong> notice. The <strong>Panama Canal</strong>'s vulnerability to drought has recently caused lengthy wait times and costly disruptions for shippers. The <strong>FMC</strong> is the principal U.S. federal maritime trade regulatory authority — authorized under the <strong>Shipping Act of 1984</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Federal Maritime Commission</strong> (<strong>FMC</strong>), with Chairman <strong>Louis Sola</strong> (Trump 2.0), is based in <strong>Washington DC</strong> and was established in 1961 with five commissioners — serving as the principal U.S. ocean shipping trade regulatory authority. The <strong>FMC</strong>'s principal jurisdictional areas include: (1) ocean carrier tariff filing; (2) <strong>Vessel Operating Common Carriers</strong> (<strong>VOCCs</strong>); (3) <strong>Non-Vessel Operating Common Carriers</strong> (<strong>NVOCCs</strong>); (4) marine terminal operators; (5) port authorities; (6) enforcement of the <strong>Ocean Shipping Reform Act of 1998</strong>; and (7) the <strong>Ocean Shipping Reform Act of 2022</strong> (<strong>OSRA-22</strong>, signed by <strong>Joe Biden</strong>) — forming the core regulatory frameworks. Major global maritime chokepoints include: (1) the <strong>Suez Canal</strong> (approximately 22,000 ships annually; 12 percent of global trade; under the <strong>Suez Canal Authority</strong> chaired by <strong>Osama Rabie</strong>); (2) the <strong>Panama Canal</strong> (approximately 14,000 ships; 5-6 percent of global trade); (3) the <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong> (20-30 percent of global oil; between <strong>Iran</strong> and <strong>Oman</strong>); (4) the <strong>Strait of Malacca</strong> (approximately 94,000 ships; 30 percent of global trade; among <strong>Singapore</strong>, <strong>Malaysia</strong>, and <strong>Indonesia</strong>); (5) <strong>Bab el-Mandeb</strong> (entrance to the <strong>Red Sea</strong>); (6) the <strong>Turkish Straits</strong> (<strong>Bosphorus</strong>/<strong>Dardanelles</strong>); (7) the <strong>English Channel</strong>/<strong>Strait of Dover</strong>; (8) the <strong>Cape of Good Hope</strong>; (9) the <strong>Bering Strait</strong>; and (10) the <strong>Strait of Gibraltar</strong> — representing the principal global chokepoints.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the 2024 major maritime chokepoint crisis events center on <strong>Houthi</strong> (<strong>Ansar Allah</strong>; <strong>Yemen</strong>) attacks in the <strong>Red Sea</strong> — since November 2023, reducing <strong>Suez Canal</strong> traffic by approximately 70-80 percent; container vessels are now routing via the <strong>Cape of Good Hope</strong> — adding 10-14 days of additional transit time; with <strong>Operation Prosperity Guardian</strong> (a U.S.-led coalition of over 20 countries) — representing the major <strong>Red Sea</strong> crisis. The <strong>Panama Canal</strong> drought of 2023-2024, caused by <strong>El Niño</strong>-driven declines in <strong>Lago Gatun</strong> water levels — has reduced daily transits from 36-38 to 22-24 — representing the major climate disruption. Principal global ocean container carriers — under <strong>FMC</strong> oversight — include <strong>MSC</strong> (chaired by <strong>Diego Aponte</strong>; <strong>Geneva</strong>); <strong>Maersk</strong> (<strong>Vincent Clerc</strong> CEO); <strong>CMA CGM</strong> (<strong>Rodolphe Saadé</strong> CEO; <strong>Marseille</strong>); <strong>COSCO Shipping</strong> (Chairman <strong>Wan Min</strong>; <strong>Shanghai</strong>); <strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong> (<strong>Rolf Habben Jansen</strong> CEO); <strong>ONE</strong> (<strong>Ocean Network Express</strong>); <strong>Evergreen Marine</strong> (<strong>Taiwan</strong>); <strong>HMM</strong>; <strong>Yang Ming</strong>; <strong>Zim</strong>; and <strong>Wan Hai</strong> — representing the core ecosystem players. <strong>Gemini Cooperation</strong> (<strong>Maersk</strong>+<strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong>); <strong>Premier Alliance</strong> (<strong>HMM</strong>+<strong>Yang Ming</strong>+<strong>ONE</strong>); and <strong>Ocean Alliance</strong> (<strong>CMA CGM</strong>+<strong>COSCO</strong>+<strong>Evergreen</strong>+<strong>OOCL</strong>) — represent the major 2025 container alliances.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, principal additional maritime regulatory authorities in the U.S. include: (1) the <strong>U.S. Coast Guard</strong> (<strong>USCG</strong>; Commandant <strong>Linda Fagan</strong>; under the <strong>DHS</strong>); (2) the <strong>U.S. Maritime Administration</strong> (<strong>MARAD</strong>; Acting Administrator <strong>Sang Yi</strong>; under the <strong>DOT</strong>); (3) <strong>U.S. Customs and Border Protection</strong> (<strong>CBP</strong>; Acting Commissioner <strong>Pete Flores</strong>); (4) the <strong>U.S. Trade Representative</strong> (<strong>USTR</strong>; <strong>Jamieson Greer</strong>, Trump 2.0); and (5) the <strong>Office of Foreign Assets Control</strong> (<strong>OFAC</strong>) — representing the principal U.S. maritime regulatory authorities. The <strong>Department of Defense</strong> (<strong>DoD</strong>), the <strong>U.S. Navy 5th Fleet</strong> (<strong>Bahrain</strong>; based in <strong>Manama</strong>), and the <strong>U.S. Indo-Pacific Command</strong> — represent the principal U.S. maritime security forces. Principal global maritime communications and visibility platforms include <strong>MarineTraffic</strong>, <strong>VesselFinder</strong>, <strong>Sea-Intelligence</strong>, <strong>Drewry</strong>, <strong>Xeneta</strong>, <strong>Freightos</strong>, and <strong>Container xChange</strong> — representing core ecosystem players. The <strong>International Maritime Organization</strong> (<strong>IMO</strong>; Secretary-General <strong>Arsenio Dominguez</strong>; <strong>London</strong>) — serves as the principal <strong>UN</strong> maritime regulatory authority. In conclusion, the <strong>FMC</strong>'s chokepoint investigation signals a fundamental intensification of global maritime shipping regulatory scrutiny — and supply chain managers should view <strong>Suez</strong>/<strong>Panama</strong> route strategy and regulatory compliance preparation as principal strategic priorities.</p>
<p><br></p>

3. <strong>Suez</strong>, <strong>Panama</strong>, <strong>Hormuz</strong>, <strong>Malacca</strong>, and <strong>Bab el-Mandeb</strong> are the major chokepoints.<br>
4. <strong>Houthi</strong> <strong>Red Sea</strong> attacks are reducing <strong>Suez</strong> traffic by 70-80 percent.<br>
