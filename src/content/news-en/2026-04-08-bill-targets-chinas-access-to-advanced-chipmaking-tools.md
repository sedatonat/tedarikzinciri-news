<p style="text-align: justify">Republican Congressman <strong>Michael Baumgartner</strong> has introduced a bill that would close loopholes in export controls on advanced chipmaking equipment &mdash; as a way to prevent China from acquiring the tools needed to build cutting-edge semiconductors. Dubbed the <em>Multilateral Alignment of Technology Controls on Hardware</em>, or MATCH Act, the bill would ban the sale of most essential semiconductor manufacturing equipment (SME) to any destination inside a country of concern.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The bill would also blacklist some of China's biggest chip companies, including <strong>XMT</strong>, <strong>Hua Hong</strong>, <strong>Huawei</strong>, <strong>SMIC</strong> and <strong>YMTC</strong> &mdash; and give U.S. allies 150 days to show progress in aligning on these controls. "China has made it abundantly clear that it intends to dominate the technologies that underpin both our economy and our national defense," <strong>Baumgartner</strong> said in an April 2 release. "The United States cannot afford to leave open back doors that allow the Chinese Communist Party to acquire the tools it needs to leap ahead in semiconductor manufacturing."</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The bill has bipartisan support in the U.S. House of Representatives, with co-sponsors spanning both major parties &mdash; and companion legislation being introduced in the Senate by Republican Senator <strong>Pete Ricketts</strong> and Democrat <strong>Andy Kim</strong>. This comes as concerns have grown over China exploiting gaps between the U.S. and allied export controls &mdash; with chipmaking currently ranking as the largest export from the Netherlands to China and the second largest from <strong>Japan</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, this bill is a strategic inflection point for the global semiconductor equipment market, shaped by Dutch <strong>ASML</strong>, Japan's <strong>Tokyo Electron</strong> and U.S. firms <strong>Applied Materials</strong>, <strong>Lam Research</strong> and <strong>KLA</strong>. Although China is the world's largest source of rare earth minerals used in semiconductors, it currently lacks the ability to mass-produce some of the most advanced chips without access to Western equipment &mdash; particularly <strong>EUV</strong> (<em>extreme ultraviolet</em>) lithography. The constraints on <strong>SMIC</strong>'s transition to 7nm production are a concrete example of this dynamic.</p>
