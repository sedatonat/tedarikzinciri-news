<p style="text-align: justify">Elevated fuel surcharges and annual rate increases from <strong>FedEx</strong> and <strong>UPS</strong> resulted in another record-high quarter for ground delivery costs, according to the <strong>TD Cowen/AFS Freight Index</strong> released Tuesday. Per-package ground delivery rates in Q1 were 39.3% above the index's January 2018 baseline, exceeding previous forecasts. That percentage is expected to climb further to 42% in Q2 as diesel prices and associated fuel fees continue to pressure prices. Shippers also contended with differing discount activity from <strong>FedEx</strong> and <strong>UPS</strong> in Q1; <strong>FedEx</strong> deployed higher ground shipping discounts, while <strong>UPS</strong> discounts stalled and decreased for certain customer segments.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The two carriers are raising fuel fees as the <strong>Iran war</strong> and <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong> disruptions impact oil supply, and <strong>Amazon</strong> and the <strong>U.S. Postal Service</strong> are following close behind. Ground fuel surcharges rose 26.7% year over year in Q1, outpacing a 10% jump in diesel fuel prices. Relief is not expected in Q2, "as oil prices are unlikely to see near-term relief even if geopolitical tensions ease," per the index presentation. <strong>Mingshu Bates</strong>, <strong>AFS Logistics</strong> chief analytics officer and president of parcel, said: "Everybody is concerned about it and just trying to do some kind of modeling to predict their cost increase."</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to the index, a five-pound package shipped via ground from <strong>Atlanta</strong> to a <strong>New York City</strong> home is 41.8% more expensive this year than in 2022. Cost increases are not limited to ground shipments; the express parcel rate per package increased to 8.5% above the January 2018 baseline and is expected to jump further to 10.3% in Q2. March fuel surcharges were about 46% higher for express shipments than the levels seen in Q1 of 2025. Even before the <strong>Iran war</strong>, fuel surcharge pressures had been a thorn in shippers' sides, combining with other fees and escalating base rates to challenge transportation budgets.</p>
