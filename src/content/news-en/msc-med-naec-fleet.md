<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Mediterranean Shipping Company (MSC)</strong> is reshaping the structure of the Mediterranean–North America East Coast (MED–NAEC) trade route by deploying ultra-large vessels in the 13,000–15,000+ TEU segment. Analysis within <strong>Sea-Intelligence Sunday Spotlight issue 741</strong> reveals a marked divergence in fleet composition on the route during the second half of 2025. This divergence has driven up the average vessel size while keeping the median value lower, indicating that growth is concentrated among only a limited number of large ships.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Sea-Intelligence has conducted a graphical examination of all vessels deployed on the route since 2012, clearly distinguishing between standard fleet evolution and the current outlier vessels. The analysis shows that the MED–NAEC route remains dominated by a dense cluster of vessels in the <strong>800 TEU to 9,600 TEU</strong> range. This segment is defined as the "primary cluster," representing the route's traditional operational structure.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">However, as of 2025, a second and distinct vessel cluster has emerged in the <strong>13,000–15,000+ TEU</strong> band. This "second cluster" represents a previously unseen magnitude of capacity increase on the MED–NAEC route. According to Sea-Intelligence data, this clustering is entirely driven by <strong>MSC's deployments</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The analysis highlights two distinct services in particular:</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>1. EMUSA Service (Vessels shown in blue)</strong></h5>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">A few ultra-large vessels deployed by MSC operate in this service.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">However, these vessels represent <strong>temporary outliers</strong> departing from the standardized fleet composition operating in the 8,000–9,000 TEU band.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The overall structure of the EMUSA service remains built around mid-capacity vessels.</p></li>
</ul>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>2. Dragon Service (Vessels shown in orange)</strong></h5>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Dragon is the first service on the MED–NAEC route to <strong>continuously deploy 13,000–15,000+ TEU vessels</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">This indicates that large tonnage is transitioning from a singular experiment to an operational norm on the route.</p></li>
</ul>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Sea-Intelligence emphasizes that this new two-tier fleet structure generates significant operational consequences for <strong>North America East Coast (NAEC)</strong> terminals. The requirement for ultra-large vessels to handle substantially higher container volumes per call creates pressure on terminal resources. Capacity constraints are concentrated in areas such as crane capacity, yard layout, gate traffic management, and shift planning, directly impacting terminal efficiency.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This trend also leads to sharper congestion peaks at NAEC terminals during peak-stress periods. Compared to traditional 8,000–9,000 TEU vessels, 13,000–15,000+ TEU ships generate significantly larger workloads in shorter timeframes. Sea-Intelligence notes that this directly strains <strong>auxiliary berth area utilization, equipment rotation, and in-port operation cycles</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This asymmetric growth in capacity structure reflects MSC's individual fleet strategy rather than broader-scale route evolution. As a result, while average vessel size rises rapidly, the median value remains relatively stable, meaning that <strong>genuine structural transformation has not yet occurred</strong>, but rather specific operators are rescaling their operations.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In conclusion, the MED–NAEC route has developed a <strong>two-tier fleet structure</strong> in the second half of 2025:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>A traditional dense cluster of 800–9,600 TEU vessels</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>A new MSC-focused ultra-large vessel cluster of 13,000–15,000+ TEU</strong>.</p></li>
</ul>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This new structure will operationally challenge NAEC terminals while simultaneously altering competitive dynamics. Sea-Intelligence foresees that this development could produce even more pronounced impacts on terminal infrastructure and service speeds in 2026 and beyond.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong></h3>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">MSC has deployed 13,000–15,000+ TEU vessels on the MED–NAEC route.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">A two-tier fleet structure has emerged on the route.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Average vessel size is rising due to large vessels; the median remains stable.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Dragon service is the first to continuously deploy vessels at this capacity on the route.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">NAEC terminals face heightened operational stress due to large vessels.</p></li>
</ul>
