<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> video interview reporting; supply chain leaders; how they can strengthen forecasting with the help of <strong>AI</strong>? <strong>Vinayak Ramesh</strong>, Chief Customer Officer of <strong>Ikigai Labs</strong>, explains the current state of the technology. Supply chain leaders need to adapt to uncertainty in three distinct areas, says <strong>Ramesh</strong>. First, the ability to incorporate external data such as tariff levels, interest rates, and weather conditions into the forecasting process in addition to historical sales data. The second requirement is the ability to work with limited amounts of data and shorter trends. The third is the ability to forecast multiple scenarios that could materialize. Artificial intelligence makes it possible to address these complexities — but augments rather than replaces people. The output of an <strong>AI</strong> system is only as good as the data being fed into it, notes <strong>Ramesh</strong>. And data accessed through traditional methods — based partly on human experience — needs to be part of this. However, "a person can only process a certain number of data points. <strong>AI</strong> helps you get through all of this and presents the probabilities," he says. <strong>AI</strong> and machine learning systems get better with experience — but only to the point where they can take over the decision-making process entirely. Over time, <strong>AI</strong> can assume approximately 80% of the tasks involved in supply chain planning — allowing people to "focus on the really high-impact 20% of cases."</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Ikigai Labs</strong>, based in <strong>Boston, Massachusetts</strong>, is a startup founded by <strong>Devavrat Shah</strong> (MIT professor) and <strong>Vinayak Ramesh</strong> — specializing in the area of generative tabular AI. <strong>Large Graphical Models</strong> (<strong>LGMs</strong>) are the company's core technology — with the ability to generate high-quality forecasts from small amounts of data. <strong>Sequoia Capital</strong> and <strong>SineWave Ventures</strong> are principal investors. Other major players in generative AI applications for supply chain are <strong>OpenAI ChatGPT</strong>, <strong>Anthropic Claude</strong>, <strong>Google Gemini</strong>, <strong>Meta Llama</strong>, <strong>Mistral AI</strong>, <strong>Cohere</strong>, <strong>xAI Grok</strong>, <strong>DeepSeek</strong>, <strong>Hugging Face</strong>, <strong>NVIDIA NeMo</strong> — principal foundation model providers. <strong>Vector database</strong> providers are <strong>Pinecone</strong>, <strong>Weaviate</strong>, <strong>Qdrant</strong>, <strong>Chroma</strong>, <strong>Milvus</strong>. <strong>RAG</strong> (Retrieval-Augmented Generation), <strong>fine-tuning</strong>, <strong>prompt engineering</strong>, <strong>agentic AI</strong>, <strong>LangChain</strong>, <strong>LlamaIndex</strong>, <strong>AutoGen</strong>, <strong>CrewAI</strong> are principal ecosystem technologies.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the demand forecasting and demand sensing software market encompasses (1) statistical forecasting — <strong>ARIMA</strong>, <strong>Holt-Winters</strong>, exponential smoothing; (2) machine learning forecasting — <strong>XGBoost</strong>, <strong>Random Forest</strong>, <strong>LightGBM</strong>; (3) deep learning forecasting — <strong>LSTM</strong>, <strong>Transformer</strong>, <strong>Temporal Fusion Transformer</strong>, <strong>N-BEATS</strong>, <strong>NeuralProphet</strong>; (4) causal forecasting — <strong>Bayesian Networks</strong>, <strong>Causal Forest</strong> — as principal algorithm categories. Major supply chain planning software providers are <strong>Blue Yonder</strong> (formerly <strong>JDA</strong>; <strong>Panasonic</strong>-owned), <strong>SAP IBP</strong> (Integrated Business Planning), <strong>Oracle Demand Planning</strong>, <strong>Kinaxis Maestro</strong>, <strong>o9 Solutions</strong>, <strong>OMP Plus</strong>, <strong>RELEX Solutions</strong>, <strong>ToolsGroup</strong>, <strong>John Galt Solutions Atlas Planning</strong>, <strong>Logility</strong>, <strong>Anaplan</strong>, <strong>Coupa Supply Chain Design</strong>, <strong>QAD Dynasys</strong>, <strong>Demand Solutions</strong>, <strong>Slimstock</strong>, <strong>Smart Software</strong>, <strong>Vekia</strong>, <strong>NeuroForecaster</strong> — principal providers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the "explainable AI" (<strong>XAI</strong>) that <strong>Ramesh</strong> emphasized is a critical requirement in <strong>AI</strong> applications globally. The limitations of black box models are unacceptable to business users, especially in high-volume, high-value decisions. <strong>SHAP</strong> (SHapley Additive exPlanations), <strong>LIME</strong> (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations), <strong>Integrated Gradients</strong>, <strong>Counterfactual Explanations</strong>, <strong>Attention Visualization</strong> are principal <strong>XAI</strong> techniques. The <strong>EU AI Act</strong> (approved March 2024) mandates explainability for high-risk <strong>AI</strong> systems. The <strong>NIST AI Risk Management Framework</strong> (<strong>AI RMF</strong>) is a voluntary standard in the <strong>U.S.</strong> <strong>ISO/IEC 42001</strong> (AI management systems) is a global <strong>AI</strong> governance standard. The <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> tariff regime concretely necessitates the three adaptation areas that <strong>Ramesh</strong> pointed out — (1) external data integration, (2) working from limited data, (3) multi-scenario modeling. Ultimately, <strong>Ikigai Labs</strong> and other modern <strong>AI</strong>-based supply chain planning platforms appear to be principal determinants of resilience in an era of global geopolitical uncertainty.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Vinayak Ramesh</strong> (<strong>Ikigai Labs</strong>) identifies three adaptation areas for forecasting.<br>
2. External data (tariffs, interest rates, weather conditions) augments historical sales data.<br>
3. <strong>AI</strong> assumes 80% of supply chain planning — humans focus on the 20%.<br>
4. Explainable AI is essential for decisions to be interpretable to business users.<br>
5. Limited data and multi-scenario modeling are core competencies in <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> uncertainty.</p>
