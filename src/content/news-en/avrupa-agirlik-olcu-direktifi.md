<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>16 sector associations</strong> across Europe have sent a joint letter to the European Commission, strongly objecting to the proposed implementation of the <strong>European Modular System (EMS)</strong> under the <strong>Weights and Dimensions Directive (WDD)</strong>. This system would permit the use of <strong>longer and heavier trucks (mega-trucks)</strong> on European roads.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to the signatories, this move would <strong>undermine investments in rail transport</strong>, <strong>weaken intermodal transportation (rail–road–maritime integration)</strong>, and <strong>jeopardize road safety.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Negative impact on rail investments</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">In their letter, the 16 associations emphasized that the WDD <strong>contradicts the Commission's commitment to "increasing rail freight transport."</strong> With EMS implementation, the introduction of <strong>transport units incompatible with rail systems</strong> could render billions of euros invested to date in infrastructure and fleet <strong>obsolete.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">For example, the new <strong>semi-trailer length (exceeding 13.6 meters)</strong> specified by the directive is <strong>incompatible with most existing wagon fleets and inland waterway vessels in Europe.</strong> This would create <strong>serious technical incompatibilities in road–rail transfers (intermodal transport).</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The associations stated in their statement: "<strong>Implementing the EMS system will reverse rather than promote intermodal transport.</strong> According to our estimates, <strong>up to 21% of European rail freight could shift to road transport.</strong>" They noted that this would translate to <strong>6.7 to 13.3 million additional truck trips annually.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Investments could be wasted, new infrastructure costs could emerge</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">European countries have made <strong>substantial investments</strong> in rail infrastructure and logistics terminals in recent years. With EMS implementation, existing investments could become <strong>obsolete</strong>, and new costs would arise due to required <strong>bridge, junction, and terminal modifications</strong> to accommodate the new truck dimensions.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The associations characterized this situation as "<strong>a policy that creates a vicious cycle rather than solving the problem.</strong>"</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Road safety and environmental risks</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">The report emphasizes that <strong>long and heavy vehicles (LHV)</strong> would pose serious threats to road safety.</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Larger vehicles would have <strong>extended stopping distances and reduced maneuverability.</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Accident risk</strong> and <strong>accident severity</strong> would increase.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">If EMS use increases for <strong>dangerous goods transport</strong>, this could <strong>raise the risk of catastrophic accidents.</strong></p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">The associations stressed that dangerous cargo has historically been transported safely via <strong>rail and inland waterways</strong>, and that disrupting this balance would represent <strong>a step backward for public safety.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Driver qualification concerns</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">Another critical point highlighted in the letter is the <strong>absence of special qualification requirements for drivers operating EMS vehicles.</strong></p>
<blockquote style="text-align: justify">"Under current regulations, inexperienced 18-year-old drivers could operate these long and heavy trucks. This would further increase the likelihood of accidents," the statement concluded.</blockquote>
<blockquote><br></blockquote>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>The Commission's approach and sector response</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">The European Commission characterizes the WDD within the <strong>Greening Freight Package</strong> as <strong>a tool supporting more environmentally friendly transport.</strong> However, sector representatives argue that <strong>"larger trucks do not equal sustainable transport,"</strong> and instead represent <strong>a policy favoring road over rail.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The associations also noted that the Commission's signals to withdraw planned revisions to the <strong>Combined Transport Directive</strong> represent <strong>another indication of moving away from intermodal targets.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Conclusion: Risk of modal shift</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">Experts warn that if the WDD is implemented in its current form, <strong>"reverse modal shift"</strong> would occur, meaning transportation would <strong>shift from rail and maritime to road,</strong> putting Europe's climate targets under strain.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The 16 European associations have called on the Commission to <strong>review the directive,</strong> <strong>preserve intermodal compatibility,</strong> and <strong>maintain the priority given to rail freight transport.</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Points:</strong></h5>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>16 European associations</strong> published a joint letter opposing the <strong>Weights and Dimensions Directive (WDD).</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Large truck (EMS)</strong> use is <strong>incompatible with rail investments</strong> and <strong>weakens intermodal transport.</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Forecast: <strong>Up to 21% loss of rail freight,</strong> resulting in <strong>6.7–13.3 million additional truck trips annually.</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Semi-trailers exceeding 13.6 meters</strong> are <strong>incompatible with existing wagons and inland vessels.</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Road safety</strong> and <strong>dangerous goods transport</strong> face serious risks.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Driver licensing requirements are insufficient,</strong> allowing 18-year-old drivers to operate these vehicles.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The associations are demanding the Commission <strong>review the directive</strong> and <strong>maintain rail priority.</strong></p></li>
</ul>
