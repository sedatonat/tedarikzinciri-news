<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Bloomberg</strong> reports that Mexico is setting minimum prices for fresh tomato exports to the <strong>U.S.</strong> — seeking to regain full market access following the <strong>Trump</strong> administration's anti-dumping duty implementation. The price floor is designed to avoid creating "distortion" in tomato export prices, according to a decree published by the Mexican government in the federal gazette on the evening of August 8. <strong>U.S.</strong> producers have accused Mexican counterparts of unfairly selling at depressed prices — and the <strong>U.S.</strong> is withdrawing from a trade agreement regulating exports this year. While the <strong>U.S. Commerce Department</strong> announced duties exceeding 17% in July, President <strong>Donald Trump</strong> delayed a sweeping tariff increase by 90 days to create room for a trade deal with Mexico. "The Mexican government is helping producers prevent future increases in anti-dumping duties," said <strong>Georgina Felix</strong>, operations director of the <strong>Arizona</strong> and <strong>Florida Tomato Exchange</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply-chain perspective, <strong>Mexico</strong> is the world's largest fresh tomato exporter — shipping over $2.5 billion annually to the <strong>U.S.</strong> — and supplies approximately 70% of the <strong>U.S.</strong> fresh tomato market. <strong>Sinaloa</strong>, <strong>Baja California</strong>, <strong>San Luis Potosí</strong>, <strong>Jalisco</strong>, <strong>Michoacán</strong>, <strong>Sonora</strong>, and <strong>Querétaro</strong> are Mexico's primary tomato production regions. The Tomato Suspension Agreement (<strong>TSA</strong>), signed in 1996 and renewed in 2002, 2008, 2013, and 2019, establishes minimum entry prices for Mexican tomatoes into the <strong>U.S.</strong> and suspends anti-dumping investigations. The <strong>U.S. Department of Commerce</strong> (<strong>DOC</strong>), through the <strong>International Trade Administration</strong> (<strong>ITA</strong>), withdrew from the <strong>TSA</strong> on July 14, 2025, implementing a 17.09% anti-dumping duty. <strong>Robert Guenther</strong>, Executive Vice President of the <strong>Florida Tomato Exchange</strong>, represents <strong>U.S.</strong> tomato producers. The <strong>USMCA</strong> (<strong>United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement</strong>), the 2020 successor to NAFTA, governs agricultural trade but does not prevent anti-dumping and subsidy actions.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply-chain perspective, <strong>U.S.-Mexico</strong> agricultural trade exceeds $70 billion annually, with vegetables, fruits, grains, meat, and beverages as primary trade categories. Mexico's main fresh exports to the <strong>U.S.</strong> include tomatoes, avocados, strawberries, raspberries, peppers, cucumbers, squash, mangoes, and cherries. The <strong>U.S.</strong> exports corn, soybeans, dairy products, poultry, beef, and rice as main shipments to Mexico. The <strong>U.S. Department of Agriculture</strong> (<strong>USDA</strong>), <strong>Foreign Agricultural Service</strong> (<strong>FAS</strong>), <strong>Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service</strong> (<strong>APHIS</strong>), and <strong>Agricultural Marketing Service</strong> (<strong>AMS</strong>) are the primary <strong>U.S.</strong> agricultural regulators. <strong>SADER</strong> (<strong>Secretaría de Agricultura y Desarrollo Rural</strong>), <strong>SE</strong> (<strong>Secretaría de Economía</strong>), and <strong>SENASICA</strong> (<strong>Servicio Nacional de Sanidad, Inocuidad y Calidad Agroalimentaria</strong>) are Mexico's primary agricultural regulators. <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> tariff policy is expanding beyond Liberation Day (April 2, 2025) toward Mexico, China, Canada, and the EU — affecting many product categories including agriculture.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply-chain perspective, Mexico's leading agricultural exporters include <strong>Grupo Bafar</strong>, <strong>NatureSweet Tomatoes</strong>, <strong>Driscoll's</strong>, <strong>SunFed</strong>, <strong>Del Campo Supreme</strong>, <strong>Mastronardi Produce</strong>, <strong>Houweling's Tomatoes</strong>, <strong>Eurofresh Farms</strong>, <strong>Wholesum Family Farms</strong>, and <strong>Andrew &amp; Williamson Fresh Produce</strong> — the primary Mexican tomato exporters to the <strong>U.S.</strong> — most of which are integrated with distribution centers in the <strong>U.S.</strong> <strong>U.S.</strong> tomato production centers in <strong>Florida</strong> (<strong>Immokalee</strong>, <strong>Ruskin</strong>, <strong>Palmetto</strong>), <strong>California</strong> (<strong>Central Valley</strong>, <strong>Salinas Valley</strong>), <strong>Ohio</strong>, <strong>Michigan</strong>, and <strong>Pennsylvania</strong> cannot compete on price with Mexico due to climate, labor, and land cost disadvantages. Major fresh tomato categories include greenhouse (protected cultivation), hydroponics, vine-ripened, cherry, grape, roma, beefsteak, and heirloom varieties. In conclusion, Mexico's minimum-price decision represents a diplomatic step to ease ongoing trade tensions with the <strong>U.S.</strong> — but structural differences in the two nations' agricultural trade policies will remain a continuous negotiating point during the <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> era.</p>
