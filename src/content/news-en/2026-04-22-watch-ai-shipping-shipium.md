<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> video interview report; <strong>Robert J. Bowman</strong> (<strong>Editor-in-Chief</strong>); artificial intelligence; is making significant strides in the shipping sector — but where is it really being applied today? <strong>Jason Murray</strong>, CEO and co-founder of <strong>Shipium</strong>, explains. Over the past 10-15 years, artificial intelligence has become "far more sophisticated" in its ability to collect vast amounts of data for decision support in shipping, according to <strong>Murray</strong>. The latest iteration of <strong>AI</strong> is "game-changing on the order of the internet," he says. We are still in the "early innings" — promising to enhance optimization through training forecasting models based on inputs from multiple sources. In the past, "we couldn't scale this — but now it's really interesting," he notes. The second innovation in technology is <strong>agentic AI</strong> — enabling "self-running" tasks executed without human intervention. Again, this is in the early stages of development and deployment. "The ability to communicate with people and tell them how to do things — when applied to the supply chain arena — is very significant," <strong>Murray</strong> says. "If something goes wrong with a shipment, most companies can't scale the response with a team of humans to solve it. But the agentic piece can notify the carrier about it." Machine learning is well-positioned in its ability to work with large datasets and training models. Users can better understand transportation flows — for example, assessing how specific corridors have historically performed. This intelligence can then be combined with other considerations such as seasonal demand patterns. <strong>Generative AI</strong>, which has dominated technology headlines over the past three to four years, offers increasingly advanced capabilities for analyzing information.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Shipium</strong>, based in <strong>Seattle, Washington</strong>, with <strong>Jason Murray</strong> as CEO &amp; Co-Founder and former <strong>Amazon</strong> Vice President, and <strong>Lior Sion</strong> as CTO &amp; Co-Founder, is one of the leading global modern shipping platform providers operating on a <strong>SaaS</strong> model. Its core competencies include intelligent shipping selection, rate optimization, delivery prediction, and <strong>carrier rating</strong> for e-commerce and retail brands. <strong>Insight Partners</strong>, <strong>Madrona Venture Group</strong>, and <strong>Trilogy Equity Partners</strong> are primary investors. Other major shipping software and multi-carrier shipping platform providers include <strong>ShipStation</strong> (owned by <strong>Auctane</strong>; formerly <strong>Stamps.com</strong>), <strong>EasyPost</strong>, <strong>Shippo</strong>, <strong>ShipBob</strong>, <strong>ShipHero</strong>, <strong>Project44</strong>, <strong>FourKites</strong>, <strong>Shippeo</strong>, <strong>Convey</strong> (owned by <strong>project44</strong>), <strong>Logistyx Technologies</strong> (owned by <strong>E2open</strong>), <strong>Pierbridge</strong>, <strong>ProShip</strong>, <strong>BluJay Solutions</strong> (<strong>e2open</strong>), <strong>Sendcloud</strong>, <strong>Packlink</strong>, <strong>nShift</strong>, <strong>Bringg</strong>, <strong>Onfleet</strong>, <strong>Routific</strong>, and <strong>Locus.sh</strong>. Major <strong>Carrier API</strong> and <strong>API gateway</strong> providers include <strong>EasyPost</strong>, <strong>Shippo</strong>, and <strong>EasyShip</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>agentic AI</strong> ecosystem includes <strong>Salesforce Agentforce</strong>, <strong>Microsoft Copilot Studio</strong>, <strong>Google Vertex AI Agent Builder</strong>, <strong>AWS Bedrock Agents</strong>, <strong>OpenAI GPTs</strong>, <strong>Anthropic Claude Computer Use</strong>, <strong>NVIDIA NIM</strong>, <strong>LangChain LangGraph</strong>, <strong>LlamaIndex</strong>, <strong>AutoGen</strong>, <strong>CrewAI</strong>, <strong>Semantic Kernel</strong>, <strong>MetaGPT</strong>, and <strong>Devin</strong> (<strong>Cognition Labs</strong>) as leading frameworks and providers. Supply chain-specific agentic applications include (1) autonomous purchase order management; (2) autonomous inventory replenishment; (3) autonomous route optimization; (4) autonomous exception management; (5) autonomous <strong>RFQ</strong> and negotiation; (6) autonomous <strong>customs declaration</strong>; (7) autonomous <strong>ETA</strong> updates; and (8) autonomous customer communication. <strong>NVIDIA Eureka</strong>, <strong>Optimus AI Agent</strong>, and <strong>Slingshot AI</strong> are key industry-specific players. Machine learning and deep learning-based forecasting rely on algorithms and frameworks such as <strong>XGBoost</strong>, <strong>LightGBM</strong>, <strong>Random Forest</strong>, <strong>LSTM</strong>, <strong>Transformer</strong>, <strong>Temporal Fusion Transformer</strong>, <strong>N-BEATS</strong>, <strong>NeuralProphet</strong>, <strong>Prophet</strong> (<strong>Meta</strong>), <strong>scikit-learn</strong>, <strong>TensorFlow</strong>, <strong>PyTorch</strong>, and <strong>JAX</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>e-commerce shipping</strong> transformation that <strong>Murray</strong> referenced involves (1) <strong>Amazon Logistics</strong> (<strong>Andy Jassy</strong>, CEO); (2) <strong>FedEx Ground</strong>; (3) <strong>UPS Ground</strong>; (4) <strong>USPS</strong> (<strong>U.S. Postal Service</strong>); (5) <strong>OnTrac</strong>; (6) <strong>LaserShip</strong> (merging with <strong>OnTrac</strong>); (7) <strong>UDS</strong> (<strong>United Delivery Service</strong>); (8) <strong>DHL eCommerce</strong>; (9) <strong>Pitney Bowes</strong>; and (10) <strong>FirstMile</strong> as major regional carriers and last-mile delivery providers. The <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> tariff regime and de minimis (exemption below $800) reform are reshaping U.S. shipping flows for Chinese e-commerce players such as <strong>Shein</strong>, <strong>Temu</strong> (<strong>PDD Holdings</strong>), and <strong>AliExpress</strong>. The <strong>Customs and Border Protection</strong> (<strong>CBP</strong>) began tightening de minimis rules at the end of 2024. Major forwarding and customs broker providers include <strong>Flexport</strong> (<strong>Ryan Petersen</strong>, CEO), <strong>Expeditors</strong>, <strong>K&amp;N</strong> (<strong>Kuehne+Nagel</strong>), <strong>DSV</strong>, <strong>DB Schenker</strong>, and <strong>C.H. Robinson</strong>. In summary, the <strong>AI</strong> shipping transformation that <strong>Murray</strong> highlighted appears to reflect the evolving process of global structural reshaping in e-commerce logistics, with <strong>agentic AI</strong> and autonomous decision-making emerging as key differentiators.</p>
