<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Alaska Airlines</strong> is withdrawing its full-year 2026 profit forecast as it grapples with rising jet fuel prices driven by the escalating conflict in <strong>Iran</strong>. According to <strong>CNBC</strong>, <strong>Alaska Airlines</strong> is bracing for a $600 million increase in fuel costs for the second quarter of 2026 and expects to pay as much as $4.75 per gallon in April. By comparison, <strong>U.S.</strong> airlines paid an average of $2.35 per gallon for fuel in January and $2.39 in February. Jet fuel typically accounts for approximately one-quarter of most airlines' operating costs, and average prices have doubled since the <strong>Iran</strong> conflict began. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>jet fuel</strong> (<strong>Jet A-1</strong>) pricing is tied to <strong>Brent</strong> crude oil via <strong>crack spread</strong> and is priced daily through <strong>Platts</strong>, <strong>Argus</strong>, and <strong>OPIS</strong> indices. Data from the <strong>IATA Fuel Price Monitor</strong> serves as the primary reference for airlines' <strong>fuel hedging</strong> decisions.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">To manage these costs, <strong>Alaska</strong> is beginning in March to divert fuel supplies away from the <strong>U.S. West Coast</strong> to account for <strong>West Coast</strong> refiner margins that have risen by 20 cents per gallon, instead transporting fuel from <strong>Singapore</strong> to <strong>Seattle</strong>. The airline is also increasing baggage fees and has begun cutting scheduled flights for May and June, focusing particularly on late-night departures in high-traffic markets. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>fuel tankering</strong> strategy leverages <strong>Singapore</strong>'s <strong>Jurong Island</strong> refinery complex and integration with <strong>Shell</strong>, <strong>ExxonMobil</strong>, <strong>SRC</strong> (<strong>Singapore Refining Company</strong>), and <strong>PetroChina</strong> Singapore operations. The 15-18 day transit time via <strong>clean petroleum product tanker</strong>s across the <strong>Pacific</strong> can absorb <strong>Alaska</strong>'s arbitrage margin.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the contraction in <strong>West Coast</strong> refining capacity is a structural trend. Refineries such as <strong>Phillips 66 Los Angeles</strong>, <strong>Valero Benicia</strong>, <strong>Chevron El Segundo</strong>, and <strong>Marathon Martinez</strong> are either shutting down or transitioning to <strong>renewable diesel</strong> conversion under <strong>CARB</strong> (<strong>California Air Resources Board</strong>) regulations and <strong>LCFS</strong> (<strong>Low Carbon Fuel Standard</strong>) obligations, keeping regional <strong>refining margin</strong>s structurally elevated. <strong>SAF</strong> (<strong>Sustainable Aviation Fuel</strong>) blending mandates and <strong>CORSIA</strong> (<strong>Carbon Offsetting and Reduction Scheme for International Aviation</strong>) compliance continue to create long-term cost pressures for airlines. <strong>Alaska</strong>'s <strong>Seattle-Tacoma International Airport (SEA)</strong> hub manages fuel logistics collectively through <strong>Sea-Tac Fuel Facilities LLC</strong>; however, feedstock supply constraints directly reflect operational decisions.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p style="text-align: justify">Other airlines are similarly facing challenges in recent weeks. In April, <strong>Air Canada</strong> suspended all flights from <strong>Toronto</strong> and <strong>Montreal</strong> to New York's <strong>John F. Kennedy Airport</strong> between June and October; <strong>Delta</strong> has cut summer routes from <strong>JFK</strong>, <strong>Detroit</strong>, and <strong>Boston</strong>; <strong>Lufthansa</strong> has grounded 27 aircraft serving its short-haul <strong>CityLine</strong> subsidiary. From a supply chain perspective, airline capacity cutbacks are creating a domino effect in the <strong>air cargo</strong> market. The reduction in <strong>belly cargo</strong> capacity has driven <strong>air freight rate</strong>s to elevated levels across the <strong>BAI</strong> (<strong>Baltic Air Freight Index</strong>) for <strong>e-commerce</strong>, <strong>pharmaceutical</strong>, and <strong>electronics</strong> segments. <strong>Full-freighter</strong> operators such as <strong>FedEx Express</strong>, <strong>UPS Airlines</strong>, <strong>DHL Aviation</strong>, and <strong>Cargolux</strong> are moving into relatively advantageous positions. In conclusion, <strong>Alaska Airlines</strong>' withdrawal of its profit forecast is further evidence of the concrete financial impact of the <strong>Iran</strong> conflict on the global airline sector.</p>\n<p><br></p>\n<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Alaska Airlines</strong> is withdrawing its full-year 2026 profit forecast.<br>
2. A $600 million fuel cost increase is expected for Q2; April prices reach $4.75 per gallon.<br>
3. The company is beginning to transport fuel from <strong>Singapore</strong> to <strong>Seattle</strong>.<br>
4. May-June flight cutbacks focus on late-night departures.<br>
