<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The U.S. Navy</strong> has made a critical decision in its <strong>Medium Landing Ship (LSM)</strong> program, aimed at strengthening amphibious and littoral operations capabilities, by selecting the <strong>LST 100</strong> landing ship design developed by <strong>Damen Shipyards Group</strong>. According to the company's statement, the Navy plans to construct <strong>up to 35 vessels</strong> based on this design at U.S. shipyards.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The decision was announced to the public on <strong>December 5</strong> by <strong>Secretary of the Navy John C. Phelan</strong>. Phelan described the <strong>LST 100</strong> as "a platform that strikes the right balance in terms of capability, cost, and speed," weighing approximately <strong>4,000 tons</strong> with a <strong>range exceeding 3,400 nautical miles</strong>. For the Navy, these vessels provide a solution capable of rapidly responding to current and future operational requirements.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Why an Existing Design?</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">In a statement following the decision, the <strong>Naval Sea Systems Command (NAVSEA)</strong> emphasized that the selection resulted from a <strong>competitive evaluation process</strong> and highlighted the preference for a <strong>mature design requiring no further development</strong>. According to NAVSEA, this approach:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>enables rapid fielding of a critically needed capability</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>reduces technical risk</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>shortens procurement and acquisition timelines</strong></p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">The U.S. Navy chose to progress with a field-proven design rather than developing a platform from scratch, focusing instead on integration and localization phases.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Distributed Maritime Operations and the LSM's Role</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>LSM</strong> vessels based on the LST 100 are positioned to operate in alignment with <strong>Distributed Maritime Operations (DMO)</strong> and the <strong>U.S. Marine Corps' Expeditionary Advanced Base Operations (EABO)</strong> concepts. These doctrines emphasize rapid, flexible, and low-profile logistics and force movement among <strong>island chains</strong> and <strong>coastal areas with limited infrastructure</strong>, rather than large centralized bases.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The vessels are designed to transport personnel, vehicles, and supplies <strong>between islands</strong> and to <strong>challenging coastal regions</strong>. In this context, LSMs provide a more <strong>distributed</strong>, <strong>less visible</strong>, and <strong>cost-effective</strong> solution compared to traditional large amphibious ships.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Technical Specifications</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>LST 100</strong>, a steel-hulled landing ship, features approximately:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>100 meters in length</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>16 meters in beam</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>3.5–3.9 meters shallow draft</strong></p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">The vessel can achieve a maximum speed of <strong>14 knots</strong> and offers a range of approximately <strong>4,000 nautical miles</strong>.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Key design features include:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>approximately 1,020 m² of Ro-Ro (roll-on/roll-off) cargo space</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>clamshell doors and ramps</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>stern ramp</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>integrated Ro-Ro and vehicle decks</strong></p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">This configuration enables rapid loading and unloading of vehicles and wheeled cargo.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The standard crew complement is approximately <strong>18 personnel</strong>. However, the vessel provides accommodation for <strong>over 280 personnel</strong>, enabling the transport of an <strong>augmented Marine rifle company</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The ship's <strong>aft flight deck</strong> supports mid-size helicopters such as the <strong>NH90</strong>, while the <strong>heavy-lift crane</strong> positioned forward enhances cargo-handling capability. <strong>U.S. Marine Corps Commandant General Eric Smith</strong> stated that the vessel represents an <strong>excellent choice</strong> for the LSM role, given its <strong>cargo capacity, helicopter capability, accommodation space, and crane equipment</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Production Timeline and Global References</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">Program officials indicate that construction of the first vessel could commence in <strong>2026</strong> and delivery is feasible around <strong>2029</strong>, subject to funding approvals. Damen and NAVSEA emphasize that, through a <strong>joint technical data package</strong>, vessels can be constructed in parallel at <strong>multiple U.S. shipyards</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>LST 100</strong> currently serves in the <strong>Nigerian Navy</strong> inventory and has been selected by <strong>Australia</strong> for its <strong>Landing Craft Heavy</strong> program. These references underscore the design's operational maturity.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Damen and U.S. Connection</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">Damen designs are not unfamiliar to the United States. The company's <strong>patrol vessel designs</strong> form the basis of the <strong>U.S. Coast Guard's Sentinel-class Fast Response Cutter</strong> program. To date, <strong>more than 150 Damen-designed vessels</strong> have been constructed in the United States. This track record is viewed as a confidence factor regarding <strong>localized production and system integration</strong> for the LSM program.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Overall Assessment</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">The U.S. Navy's selection of the <strong>LST 100</strong> design stands out as a strong example of a new approach in military ship procurement focused on <strong>speed, risk reduction, and cost-effectiveness</strong>. Proceeding with an existing, field-proven platform could enable the Navy to achieve its strategic transformation objectives in a shorter timeframe. This decision also represents one of the most current examples of <strong>U.S.–European defense industry cooperation</strong> in the maritime domain.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Points:</strong></h3>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The U.S. Navy selected the <strong>Damen LST 100</strong> design for the <strong>LSM program</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Up to 35 vessels</strong> will be constructed at U.S. shipyards.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The LST 100 was chosen as a <strong>mature design requiring no further development</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">The vessels will support <strong>DMO</strong> and <strong>EABO</strong> doctrines.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">First construction is expected in <strong>2026</strong>, with delivery around <strong>2029</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Damen designs are already widely employed in the <strong>U.S. Coast Guard</strong> fleet.</p></li>
</ul>
