<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Retail</strong> giant <strong>Target</strong> is appointing a new <strong>chief executive</strong> as it struggles to reverse declines in sales and share price — according to <strong>BBC News</strong> reporting. The effects of <strong>U.S.</strong> tariffs are raising concerns about consumer spending across all <strong>U.S.</strong> retailers — particularly for discretionary products such as apparel and electronics. Outgoing CEO <strong>Brian Cornell</strong>, who has served for 10 years, is expected to retire and will be replaced in February 2026 by the company's <strong>chief operating officer</strong> — <strong>Michael Fiddelke</strong>, who has been with the company for 20 years. <strong>Target</strong>'s shares fell more than 6% on August 20 following the announcement. <strong>Fiddelke</strong> said in a statement that the company "has work to do" and needs to move "faster, much faster." He also pledged to improve the quality of products offered and embed more technology into operations. <strong>Target</strong> has been losing ground to competitors such as <strong>Walmart</strong>, <strong>Costco</strong>, and <strong>Amazon</strong> — and saw weak sales last year.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Target Corporation</strong> is a Minneapolis, Minnesota-based retailer ranked 30th in the <strong>Fortune 500</strong> — founded in 1902 by <strong>George Dayton</strong> as <strong>Dayton Dry Goods</strong> and opening its first discount store under the <strong>Target</strong> brand in 1962. <strong>Brian Cornell</strong> has been <strong>Target</strong> CEO since 2014 — bringing prior experience from <strong>PepsiCo America Foods</strong> and <strong>Sam's Club</strong>. <strong>Michael Fiddelke</strong> has been at <strong>Target</strong> since 2004 — serving in roles including <strong>Executive Vice President and Chief Operating Officer</strong>, former <strong>Chief Financial Officer</strong>, <strong>SVP Merchandising</strong>, <strong>SVP Strategy</strong>, and <strong>VP Finance</strong>. <strong>Target</strong> operates 2,000+ stores in the <strong>U.S.</strong> and employs 400,000+ workers — generating annual revenue of more than $105 billion. <strong>Good & Gather</strong>, <strong>Up & Up</strong>, <strong>Cat & Jack</strong>, <strong>A New Day</strong>, <strong>Goodfellow & Co.</strong>, <strong>Threshold</strong>, <strong>Hearth & Hand with Magnolia</strong>, <strong>Wild Fable</strong>, and <strong>Casaluna</strong> are among <strong>Target</strong>'s proprietary brands. <strong>Shipt</strong> (acquired for $550 million in 2017) is <strong>Target</strong>'s same-day delivery platform.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the major <strong>U.S.</strong> retail sector comprises <strong>Walmart</strong> (Bentonville, Arkansas — <strong>Doug McMillon</strong> CEO — $680 billion revenue — the world's largest retailer globally); <strong>Amazon</strong> (Seattle, Washington — <strong>Andy Jassy</strong> CEO — $575 billion revenue); <strong>Costco</strong> (Issaquah, Washington — <strong>Ron Vachris</strong> CEO — $250 billion revenue); <strong>The Home Depot</strong> (Atlanta, Georgia — <strong>Ted Decker</strong> CEO — $152 billion revenue); <strong>The Kroger Co.</strong> (Cincinnati, Ohio — <strong>Ron Sargent</strong> Interim CEO — $150 billion revenue); <strong>Target Corporation</strong> ($105 billion revenue); <strong>Lowe's</strong>, <strong>Albertsons</strong>, <strong>Best Buy</strong>, <strong>TJX Companies</strong>, <strong>Macy's</strong>, <strong>Nordstrom</strong>, <strong>Kohl's</strong>, <strong>Dollar General</strong>, <strong>Dollar Tree</strong>, <strong>Five Below</strong>, <strong>Ulta Beauty</strong>, <strong>Sephora</strong>, <strong>Bath & Body Works</strong>, and <strong>Victoria's Secret</strong> among other major <strong>U.S.</strong> retail companies.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Target</strong>'s challenges in 2024-2025 include (1) <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> tariffs — creating pricing pressure in discretionary categories; (2) controversy surrounding withdrawal of <strong>DEI</strong> policies — risking consumer boycotts; (3) competitive pressure from <strong>Walmart</strong> and <strong>Amazon</strong> — particularly in groceries and essentials; (4) shrink (organized retail theft) — impacting results by more than $500 million in 2023; (5) need for e-commerce and omnichannel investments; (6) food inflation and declining consumer confidence. <strong>Target</strong>'s key digital and omnichannel initiatives include <strong>Drive Up</strong>, <strong>Order Pickup</strong>, <strong>Same Day Delivery</strong>, <strong>Shipt</strong>, <strong>Target Plus</strong>, <strong>Roundel</strong> (media), and <strong>Target Circle</strong> (loyalty program). The <strong>NRF</strong> (<strong>National Retail Federation</strong>) and <strong>RILA</strong> (<strong>Retail Industry Leaders Association</strong>) are primary <strong>U.S.</strong> retail industry organizations. <strong>Black Friday</strong>, <strong>Cyber Monday</strong>, <strong>Prime Day</strong>, <strong>Back-to-School</strong>, and <strong>Holiday Season</strong> are major <strong>U.S.</strong> retail trading periods. In conclusion, with <strong>Michael Fiddelke</strong>'s appointment as <strong>Target</strong> CEO, supply chain efficiency, technology investment, and product quality improvement are primary strategic priorities for the retailer — making this one of the most critical leadership changes in the <strong>U.S.</strong> retail sector during the <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> tariff era.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Target</strong> appoints <strong>Michael Fiddelke</strong> to replace 10-year CEO <strong>Brian Cornell</strong>.<br>
2. <strong>Fiddelke</strong> takes office in February 2026 — an experienced COO with 20 years of company experience.<br>
3. Stock falls 6% following the announcement.<br>
4. <strong>Fiddelke</strong> pledges product quality and technology investment.<br>
5. Competition from <strong>Walmart</strong>, <strong>Costco</strong>, and <strong>Amazon</strong>, plus <strong>U.S.</strong> tariffs, are primary challenges.</p>
