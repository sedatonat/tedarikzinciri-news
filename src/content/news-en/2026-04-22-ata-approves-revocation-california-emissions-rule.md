<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports that the <strong>American Trucking Associations</strong> (<strong>ATA</strong>) is thanking <strong>President Trump</strong> for revoking California's "ruinous" electric vehicle mandates—praising it as "an important step toward establishing realistic and achievable national emission standards." <strong>ATA</strong> President and CEO <strong>Chris Spear</strong> said in a statement released on June 12: "Today, common sense prevailed. We thank <strong>President Trump</strong>, <strong>EPA</strong> Administrator <strong>Zeldin</strong>, and congressional leadership for taking decisive action to end crippling, reality-detached rules that would have devastating economic consequences for American businesses and families." "This is not the divided United States. With the stroke of a pen, <strong>President Trump</strong> has restored the certainty the trucking industry needs to deliver for our nation while continuing to reduce our environmental impact," he said. The solution, approved by <strong>Congress</strong> under the <strong>Congressional Review Act</strong> and signed by <strong>President Trump</strong> on June 12, revokes the federal waiver granted to <strong>California</strong> under the <strong>Biden</strong> administration. On June 20, <strong>The Guardian</strong> reported that the <strong>U.S. Supreme Court</strong> ruled that fossil fuel companies may challenge California's authority to set stricter standards for reducing pollution from cars. The <strong>Supreme Court</strong> supported a seven-to-two vote backing fossil fuel and gas companies' challenge to California's waiver, which it has periodically obtained from the federal government since 1967, along with 17 Republican-led states. The state separately mandates that only zero-emission vehicles may be sold there by 2035.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>American Trucking Associations</strong> (<strong>ATA</strong>), based in <strong>Washington D.C.</strong> with <strong>Chris Spear</strong> as President &amp; CEO, is the largest road transportation industry trade association in the <strong>U.S.</strong>—representing 33,000+ member companies with federation members in all 50 states. The <strong>California Air Resources Board</strong> (<strong>CARB</strong>; <strong>Sacramento, California</strong>; <strong>Liane Randolph</strong>, Chair) administers the <strong>Advanced Clean Trucks</strong> (<strong>ACT</strong>), <strong>Advanced Clean Fleets</strong> (<strong>ACF</strong>), <strong>Heavy-Duty Omnibus</strong>, <strong>Low Carbon Fuel Standard</strong> (<strong>LCFS</strong>), and <strong>Zero-Emission Vehicle</strong> (<strong>ZEV</strong>) authority as the primary <strong>California</strong> emission regulations. The <strong>U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</strong> (<strong>EPA</strong>), under Administrator <strong>Lee Zeldin</strong>, oversees federal-level implementation of the <strong>Clean Air Act</strong>. The <strong>Congressional Review Act</strong> (<strong>CRA</strong>; 1996) empowers <strong>Congress</strong> to revoke federal regulations by simple majority. The <strong>U.S. Supreme Court</strong>, in the case <strong>Diamond Alternative Energy LLC v. EPA</strong>, examines the foundation of <strong>California</strong> waiver legitimacy. The <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> administration is limiting <strong>EV</strong> incentives under the <strong>Inflation Reduction Act</strong> (<strong>IRA</strong>) and reassessing semiconductor investments under the <strong>CHIPS Act</strong>—reflecting a comprehensive reversal of the <strong>Biden</strong>-era green industrial policy.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective globally, the heavy-duty vehicle electrification market includes <strong>Tesla Semi</strong> (<strong>Elon Musk</strong>, CEO); <strong>Daimler Truck</strong> (<strong>Karin Radrström</strong>, CEO; <strong>Freightliner eCascadia</strong>; <strong>Mercedes-Benz eActros</strong>); <strong>Volvo Trucks</strong> (<strong>Roger Alm</strong>, President; <strong>VNR Electric</strong>; <strong>FH Electric</strong>); <strong>PACCAR</strong> (<strong>Kenworth T680E</strong>; <strong>Peterbilt 579EV</strong>); <strong>Navistar</strong> (<strong>Volkswagen Truck &amp; Bus Traton</strong>-owned; <strong>International eMV</strong>); <strong>BYD</strong> (<strong>Wang Chuanfu</strong>, CEO; <strong>BYD 8TT</strong>); <strong>Nikola</strong>; <strong>Hyzon Motors</strong>; <strong>Lion Electric</strong>; <strong>Xos Trucks</strong>; <strong>Workhorse</strong>; <strong>Mack Trucks</strong> (<strong>Volvo Group</strong>); <strong>Scania</strong>; <strong>MAN Truck &amp; Bus</strong>; <strong>Iveco</strong>; <strong>DAF Trucks</strong> (<strong>PACCAR</strong>); <strong>Hino</strong> (<strong>Toyota</strong>); and <strong>Isuzu</strong> as major suppliers. <strong>Hydrogen fuel cell</strong> alternatives include <strong>Hyundai Xcient Fuel Cell</strong>, <strong>Toyota</strong>, <strong>Cummins</strong>, and <strong>Plug Power</strong> as principal providers. <strong>Charging infrastructure</strong> leaders include <strong>ChargePoint</strong>, <strong>WattEV</strong>, <strong>Voltera</strong>, <strong>Forum Mobility</strong>, <strong>TeraWatt Infrastructure</strong>, and <strong>Greenlane</strong> (<strong>Daimler-NextEra-BlackRock</strong>) as major <strong>Class 8</strong> charging network providers. <strong>NACFE</strong> (<strong>North American Council for Freight Efficiency</strong>) tests <strong>Class 8</strong> electrification through <strong>Run on Less</strong> demonstration programs.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the revocation of <strong>California</strong>'s waiver impacts: (1) OEM manufacturers' <strong>EV</strong> production volume commitments come under scrutiny; (2) battery and powertrain investments in the supply chain face reassessment; (3) fleet owners accelerate decisions to remain on diesel fleets; (4) <strong>charging infrastructure</strong> investors encounter uncertainty; (5) <strong>port drayage</strong> and <strong>last-mile</strong> electrification plans are deferred—as major impact categories. Other states adopting <strong>California</strong> emission standards in the <strong>U.S.</strong>—known as <strong>Section 177</strong> states—include <strong>New York</strong>, <strong>Massachusetts</strong>, <strong>Washington</strong>, <strong>Oregon</strong>, <strong>Colorado</strong>, <strong>New Jersey</strong>, <strong>Vermont</strong>, <strong>Maine</strong>, <strong>New Mexico</strong>, <strong>Connecticut</strong>, <strong>Maryland</strong>, <strong>Rhode Island</strong>, <strong>Delaware</strong>, <strong>Virginia</strong>, <strong>Pennsylvania</strong>, and the <strong>District of Columbia</strong> as major members. In the <strong>EU</strong>, <strong>CO2 emission standards for heavy-duty vehicles</strong> (approved March 2024) mandate 45% emission reductions by 2030, 65% by 2035, and 90% by 2040—widening the divergence with the <strong>U.S.</strong> In <strong>China</strong>, <strong>BYD</strong>, <strong>Sany</strong>, <strong>XCMG</strong>, <strong>FAW</strong>, and <strong>Sinotruk</strong> are leading domestic <strong>NEV</strong> (<strong>New Energy Vehicle</strong>) manufacturers. Ultimately, the <strong>ATA</strong>'s support for the <strong>Trump</strong> revocation reflects <strong>U.S.</strong> political divergence on the pace of green transition and regulatory certainty globally—with fleet planning, procurement strategy, and carbon reporting emerging as key strategic issues for supply chain managers.</p>

3. <strong>U.S. Supreme Court</strong> backs fossil fuel companies' right to challenge by a seven-to-two vote on June 20.<br>
