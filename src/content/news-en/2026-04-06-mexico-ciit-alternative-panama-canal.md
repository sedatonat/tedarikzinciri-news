<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Mexico</strong>&rsquo;s government has indicated that the <strong>Interoceanic Corridor of the Isthmus of Tehuantepec (CIIT)</strong> will be fully completed in the first half of 2026, with final construction work set to finish in June of this year. This information has been reported by <strong>Automotive Logistics</strong>. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>CIIT</strong> is emerging as an alternative <strong>landbridge</strong> option to the <strong>Panama Canal</strong> on the <strong>Asia-U.S. East Coast</strong> route. Its <strong>multimodal</strong> design for <strong>RoRo</strong>, finished <strong>vehicle</strong> flows and time-sensitive general cargo segments represents the transformation of <strong>port-rail</strong> integration into a next-generation <strong>landbridge</strong> concept. Capacity expansions at <strong>Salina Cruz</strong> on the <strong>Pacific</strong> and <strong>Coatzacoalcos</strong> on the <strong>Gulf</strong> serve as the natural nodes for this structure.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>CIIT</strong> project, which aims to provide multimodal logistics services by integrating <strong>Mexico</strong>&rsquo;s <strong>National Port System</strong> and local authorities and connecting them through rail transport via the <strong>Isthmus of Tehuantepec Railway</strong>, was formally approved in June 2019 as an alternative to the <strong>Panama Canal</strong>. From a supply chain perspective, this multimodal integration strengthens <strong>Mexico</strong>&rsquo;s strategic position for flows toward the <strong>U.S.</strong> consumer market in the global <strong>nearshoring</strong> wave. The <strong>tariff-free</strong> advantage within the <strong>USMCA</strong> framework enables production clusters in <strong>Bajío</strong>, <strong>Monterrey</strong> and <strong>Guadalajara</strong> to source <strong>Asia</strong>-origin components through a new corridor.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In a pilot program launched in spring 2025, <strong>Hyundai</strong> and its logistics arm <strong>Hyundai Glovis</strong> are transporting 900 vehicles from <strong>Asia</strong> to the <strong>U.S. East Coast</strong> using the 303-kilometer rail route. This route connects the port of <strong>Salina Cruz</strong> on the <strong>Pacific</strong> to the port of <strong>Coatzacoalcos</strong> on the <strong>Gulf</strong>; information shared by <strong>DailyGalaxy.com</strong>. The pilot completes each rail transit in approximately nine hours; full ocean-to-ocean transfer is benchmarked at approximately 72 hours. From a supply chain perspective, the 72-hour transit time offers transit time advantages comparable to the traditional <strong>Panama Canal</strong> route and is significantly faster than the <strong>Cape Horn</strong> alternative. The redistribution of <strong>specialised RoRo</strong> capacity in the <strong>finished vehicle logistics (FVL)</strong> segment is having direct impact on fleet planning at major operators such as <strong>WWL</strong>, <strong>Glovis</strong> and <strong>K-Line</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">A climate study published the same year reveals that the <strong>Panama Canal</strong> could face significantly more frequent extreme droughts by century's end if greenhouse gas emissions remain high. From a supply chain perspective, the long-term risk of <strong>Gatun Lake</strong> level decline calls into question the reliability of <strong>Neopanamax</strong> canal capacity in global mainline planning. This situation is leading to all alternatives—including <strong>CIIT</strong>, the <strong>Suez Canal</strong>, the <strong>Cape of Good Hope</strong> and the <strong>Northwest Passage</strong>—being incorporated into long-term strategic equations. Consequently, the full opening of <strong>CIIT</strong> is shaping up as a new balancing factor in the Latin American logistics map, offering a new corridor option for global <strong>RoRo</strong>, <strong>refrigerated</strong> and high-value cargo flows.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. <strong>CIIT</strong> final construction completes in June 2026.<br>
2. The 303 km railroad connects <strong>Salina Cruz</strong> to <strong>Coatzacoalcos</strong>.<br>
3. <strong>Hyundai Glovis</strong> is moving 900 vehicles in the 2025 pilot.<br>
4. Full ocean-to-ocean transfer takes approximately 72 hours.<br>
5. Climate drought risk at the <strong>Panama Canal</strong> is accelerating the search for alternatives.</p>
