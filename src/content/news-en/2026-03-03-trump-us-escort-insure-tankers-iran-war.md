<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>President Donald Trump</strong> has announced that the <strong>U.S.</strong> will provide insurance guarantees and naval escort to ensure the safe passage of oil tankers and other vessels transiting through the <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong>. The purpose of this announcement is to avert a potential energy crisis that could result from the ongoing conflict with <strong>Iran</strong>. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>marine war risk</strong> premiums and <strong>K&amp;R</strong> insurance rates are key determinants for <strong>VLCC</strong>, <strong>Suezmax</strong>, <strong>LR2</strong>, and <strong>Aframax</strong> tankers. The state-backed insurance layer creates an alternative support mechanism to capacity outflows from <strong>P&amp;I clubs</strong> and the private reinsurance market. This structure serves as a bridge for crude supply flows to <strong>Asian</strong> refineries and contributes to softening risk premiums in the global oil market.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In a statement made on March 3, <strong>Trump</strong> noted that the <strong>U.S. International Development Finance Corporation (DFC)</strong> will provide insurance <strong>at a very reasonable price</strong>, with the aim of maintaining the flow of energy and other commercial goods in the <strong>Gulf</strong>. Additionally, <strong>Trump</strong> stated that <strong>if necessary, the United States Navy will begin escorting tankers through the Strait of Hormuz, as soon as possible</strong>. In a social media post, <strong>Trump</strong> also conveyed the message: <strong>No matter what, the United States will ensure the FREE FLOW of ENERGY to the WORLD</strong>. From a supply chain perspective, since the <strong>DFC</strong> is an institution established to direct private capital to low and middle-income countries, the technical architecture of the insurance instrument will need to be clarified in the coming weeks. The <strong>U.S. Navy</strong>'s <strong>Fifth Fleet</strong> is based in <strong>Bahrain</strong>, providing operational readiness advantages.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Oil prices briefly surrendered gains following the news, with global benchmark <strong>Brent</strong> trading at approximately $80 per barrel post-settlement. Recent measures appear to have removed some of the risk premium from energy markets; however, traders remain skeptical about whether crude flows through the critical passage will quickly return to normal levels. Prices have risen more than 10 percent since the <strong>U.S.</strong> and <strong>Israel</strong> launched strikes on <strong>Iran</strong> over the weekend, causing widespread disruptions in the region. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Asian</strong> buyers are increasingly turning to <strong>Atlantic Basin</strong> crude as an alternative source diversification, along with <strong>WTI</strong> and <strong>Forties</strong> flows; this development is driving up <strong>tanker tonne-mile</strong> demand.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Bob McNally</strong>, chief of consulting firm <strong>Rapidan Energy Group</strong> and former <strong>White House</strong> official, states that <strong>The announcement may help to reassure traders, but escorting and insuring will take some time to implement</strong>, adding that the <strong>U.S.</strong> military will first want to suppress <strong>Iran</strong>'s capacity to lay mines on ships and attack vessels with cruise missiles and unmanned aerial vehicles. From a supply chain perspective, this preparation period is increasing the frequency of security advisories published by <strong>BIMCO</strong> and other maritime organizations. Updates to high-risk area designations on the <strong>JWC</strong> (<strong>Joint War Committee</strong>) list require shipowners to activate <strong>BIMCO War Risks</strong> provisions in their <strong>charter party</strong> agreements. Ultimately, the <strong>U.S.</strong> announcement of escort and insurance services represents an important case demonstrating the renewed prominence of state-layer risk mitigation tools in the global energy supply chain.</p>
