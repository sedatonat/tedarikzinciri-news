<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Kaja Kallas, the EU's foreign policy chief</strong>, has called on <strong>Iran</strong> to drop <strong>plans for transit fees</strong> in the <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong>. Kallas stressed that the strategic sea lane must remain <strong>open and free of charges</strong>. Her statement came after Iran's parliament prepared a draft bill to impose fees on passages through the strait.</p><p style="text-align: justify">The Strait of Hormuz is a critical choke point through which roughly <strong>20 per cent of world oil supply</strong> flows. The narrow corridor between Iran and Oman links the Persian Gulf to the Gulf of Oman. Daily <strong>17-20 million barrels of oil</strong> and millions of tons of gas/chemical cargoes pass through this route. Transit fees could generate a serious cost increase for the global energy market.</p><p style="text-align: justify">Kallas's statement invokes the right of innocent passage in international straits under the <strong>UN Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS)</strong>. Iran has raised the fee demand as a retaliation tool amid the Gaza war and Western sanctions. As the US increases economic pressure on Iran, European leaders highlight keeping the strait open for commercial ships as a critical condition. A chaos scenario could drive oil prices into the <strong>$120-150 band</strong> and create a domino effect on global supply chains.</p>
