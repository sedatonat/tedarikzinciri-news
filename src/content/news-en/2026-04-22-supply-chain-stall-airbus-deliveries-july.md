<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Bloomberg</strong> reports that <strong>Airbus SE</strong> delivered approximately 63 aircraft in July—roughly 18% fewer than the same month last year—with engine shortages in its best-selling <strong>A320neo</strong> model hampering customer deliveries, according to people familiar with the matter. The world's largest planemaker has delivered approximately 370 aircraft in the first seven months of 2025—less than half its annual target. July delivery figures are preliminary information, and could shift slightly, according to people who spoke on condition of anonymity to avoid discussing confidential data. An <strong>Airbus</strong> spokeswoman refrained from commenting on the July total before official figures are published in early August. Deliveries are closely monitored by investors because they represent when airline customers pay the bulk of an aircraft order. <strong>Airbus</strong> Chief Executive Officer <strong>Guillaume Faury</strong> is maintaining the company's guidance around 820 full-year deliveries, saying supply chain issues will push a significant portion of shipments into the second half.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Airbus SE</strong>—registered in <strong>Leiden, Netherlands</strong> with operational headquarters in <strong>Toulouse, France</strong>—is Europe's largest planemaker. <strong>Guillaume Faury</strong> serves as CEO, succeeding <strong>EADS</strong> (European Aeronautic Defence and Space Company). The <strong>A320neo family</strong> (<strong>A319neo</strong>, <strong>A320neo</strong>, <strong>A321neo</strong>, <strong>A321XLR</strong>) is <strong>Airbus</strong>'s best-selling narrow-body aircraft family and the direct competitor to <strong>Boeing 737 MAX</strong>. <strong>A320neo</strong> engines are supplied by one of two providers: <strong>CFM International</strong> (a joint venture of <strong>GE Aerospace</strong> and <strong>Safran</strong>) with the <strong>LEAP-1A</strong>, and <strong>Pratt &amp; Whitney</strong> (<strong>RTX</strong>) with the <strong>PW1100G GTF</strong> (Geared Turbofan). The <strong>Pratt &amp; Whitney GTF</strong> created a mandatory rapid turbine blade inspection and replacement requirement in 2023 due to metallurgical powder metal contamination—grounding thousands of aircraft—with <strong>RTX</strong> writing down $7 billion in losses. The <strong>CFM LEAP</strong> faces similar production challenges in nickel supply, carbon composite wing manufacturing, and issues comparable to those in the Trent XWB. <strong>RTX Corporation</strong> (formerly <strong>Raytheon Technologies</strong>) is led by <strong>Christopher Calio</strong> as President &amp; CEO. <strong>GE Aerospace</strong> is led by <strong>Larry Culp</strong> as Chairman &amp; CEO and is based in <strong>Cincinnati, Ohio</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, global commercial aircraft manufacturing is divided between <strong>Airbus</strong> and <strong>Boeing</strong> in a "duopoly" market. <strong>Boeing</strong>—registered in <strong>Arlington, Virginia</strong> with production in <strong>Seattle, Washington</strong>/<strong>Renton</strong> and <strong>Charleston, South Carolina</strong>, headed by CEO <strong>Kelly Ortberg</strong>—has core products including <strong>737 MAX</strong>, <strong>787 Dreamliner</strong>, <strong>777</strong>, <strong>777X</strong>, and <strong>767</strong>. The <strong>737 MAX</strong> is operating under FAA production rate constraints following the Alaska Airlines door plug separation incident in January 2024. <strong>Comac</strong> (Commercial Aircraft Corporation of China), based in <strong>Shanghai, China</strong>, is entering the market with <strong>C919</strong> (narrow-body) and <strong>C929</strong> (wide-body) aircraft, though market share remains limited due to lack of global certification. <strong>Embraer</strong> (based in <strong>São José dos Campos, Brazil</strong>), <strong>Mitsubishi Aircraft</strong>, <strong>Sukhoi</strong> (SuperJet), <strong>Bombardier</strong> (formerly; <strong>CSeries</strong> sold to <strong>Airbus</strong> as <strong>A220</strong>), and <strong>ATR</strong> (an <strong>Airbus</strong> and <strong>Leonardo</strong> joint venture) are major regional and smaller aircraft manufacturers. Key <strong>Airbus Tier-1</strong> suppliers include <strong>Spirit AeroSystems</strong> (being acquired by <strong>Boeing</strong>), <strong>Collins Aerospace</strong> (<strong>RTX</strong>), <strong>Honeywell</strong>, <strong>Liebherr-Aerospace</strong>, <strong>Safran</strong>, <strong>Thales</strong>, <strong>Latecoere</strong>, <strong>Premium Aerotec</strong>, and <strong>Stelia Aerospace</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Airbus</strong>'s 2025 production challenges include: (1) <strong>P&amp;W GTF</strong> engine inspection and repair queues; (2) <strong>CFM LEAP</strong> engine production capacity; (3) <strong>Spirit AeroSystems</strong> wing and aerostructure manufacturing (transition period following <strong>Boeing</strong>'s acquisition); (4) titanium supply (reducing dependence on Russia's <strong>VSMPO-AVISMA</strong>); (5) cabin furnishings and seat delivery delays; and (6) workforce shortage—skilled labor constraints in aerospace manufacturing. The <strong>Airbus order book</strong> hit a record 8,500+ aircraft at the start of 2025, with delivery times extending to 8-10 years. <strong>IATA</strong> (International Air Transport Association), led by Director General <strong>Willie Walsh</strong>, reports that the global airline market will carry 4.5 billion passengers in 2025—a record level. <strong>Boeing</strong>'s <strong>737 MAX</strong> production challenges are creating an opportunity for <strong>Airbus</strong> to gain market share, but engine supply bottlenecks are preventing <strong>Airbus</strong> from fully capitalizing on this opportunity. Ultimately, <strong>Airbus</strong>'s July 2025 delivery decline is a direct indicator of structural engine and aerostructure supply constraints in the global aviation supply chain—viewed as a sector-wide bottleneck extending through 2026-2027.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Notes:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Airbus</strong> delivered 63 aircraft in July 2025—18% fewer on an annual basis.<br>
2. <strong>A320neo</strong> engine constraints are the main delivery bottleneck.<br>
3. <strong>Airbus</strong> is maintaining its 2025 annual target of 820 deliveries—pushed into H2.<br>
4. <strong>Pratt &amp; Whitney GTF</strong> and <strong>CFM LEAP</strong> are the primary engine suppliers.<br>
5. <strong>Guillaume Faury</strong>, <strong>Airbus</strong> CEO, states that supply chain challenges will push deliveries into H2.</p>
