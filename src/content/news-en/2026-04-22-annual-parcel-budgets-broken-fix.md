<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Brad McBride</strong>, CEO of <strong>Zero Down</strong>, says that companies basing their shipping budgets entirely on what they did in the previous year is a <strong>"recipe for disaster."</strong> <strong>McBride</strong> argues that package rates set against the prior year are <strong>"totally off-base, and not even close to what the real budget should be."</strong> The reason: parcel carriers no longer limit themselves to a single annual <strong>general rate increase (GRI)</strong>, as they did for many years when <strong>UPS</strong> and <strong>FedEx</strong> were typically the only carrier options. Now there can be seven or eight rate increases in a year. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>parcel</strong> market operates with different dynamics than the <strong>less-than-truckload (LTL)</strong> and <strong>full truckload (FTL)</strong> segments, and technical details such as <strong>zone skipping</strong>, <strong>dim weight</strong> (<strong>dimensional weight</strong>), and <strong>accessorial</strong> charges can account for 30-40% of total transportation costs.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>McBride</strong> states: <strong>"If customers don't change the way they manage their parcels, their budgets are going to spiral out of control."</strong> Revenue leakage can occur at multiple points. Sometimes rates aren't good from the start. Other times, carriers may wait two or three years before renegotiating with their carriers. <strong>McBride</strong> says: <strong>"They need to be doing that on an annual basis, to make sure they're paying market-competitive rates."</strong> From a supply chain perspective, running <strong>RFQ</strong> (<strong>Request for Quotation</strong>) processes on an annual cycle requires access to benchmark data. <strong>Parcel audit</strong> and consulting firms such as <strong>Shipware</strong>, <strong>71lbs</strong>, <strong>AFS Logistics</strong>, and <strong>Reveel</strong> offer contract optimization services through <strong>contract intelligence</strong> platforms.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Large parcel carriers still exert considerable market power, but shippers can mitigate this dominance by knowing their own data <strong>"better than ever before."</strong> <strong>McBride</strong> adds that a qualified third party with expertise in market conditions can assist in negotiations. Despite the power of major carriers, room for negotiation exists. <strong>UPS</strong> and <strong>FedEx</strong> typically make adjustments for shippers to prevent competitors from gaining ground in the market. From a supply chain perspective, the rise of <strong>regional parcel carrier</strong>s such as <strong>Amazon Logistics</strong>, <strong>OnTrac</strong>, <strong>LaserShip</strong>, and <strong>DHL eCommerce</strong> partially breaks the <strong>duopoly</strong> structure and creates <strong>carrier diversification</strong> opportunities for shippers. <strong>USPS</strong> channels such as <strong>Ground Advantage</strong> and <strong>First-Class Package Service</strong> serve as strategic alternatives for last-mile delivery cost optimization.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to <strong>McBride</strong>, a healthy parcel logistics strategy begins with careful carrier selection based on the best available rates. Equally important is having a good carrier mix and understanding customers' specific needs. From a supply chain perspective, a <strong>multi-carrier</strong> approach requires <strong>rating engine</strong> and <strong>rate shopping</strong> capabilities within a <strong>TMS</strong> (<strong>Transportation Management System</strong>). <strong>Shipping API</strong> platforms such as <strong>ShipStation</strong>, <strong>ShipEngine</strong>, <strong>EasyPost</strong>, and <strong>Shippo</strong> provide carrier comparison and label generation from a single point for <strong>e-commerce</strong> sellers. Cost line items such as <strong>address correction</strong>, <strong>residential surcharge</strong>, <strong>delivery area surcharge (DAS)</strong>, and <strong>fuel surcharge</strong>—which have equivalents in the <strong>parcel</strong> segment analogous to <strong>demurrage/detention</strong>—play a critical role in calculating total package costs. In conclusion, <strong>McBride</strong>'s views demonstrate that parcel logistics is no longer simply a cost item but rather a strategic function that must be actively managed.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Points:</strong><br>
1. <strong>McBride</strong> defines basing annual budgets on the prior year as a <strong>"recipe for disaster."</strong><br>
2. Parcel carriers now implement rate increases 7-8 times per year.<br>
3. Revenue leakage can occur at multiple points.<br>
4. Shippers should renegotiate with carriers on an annual basis.<br>
5. <strong>UPS</strong> and <strong>FedEx</strong> are open to adjustments under competitive pressure.</p>
