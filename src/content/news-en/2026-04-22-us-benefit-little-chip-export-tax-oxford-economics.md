<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>China</strong> exports of advanced semiconductor chips under export licensing arrangements involving a 15% government cut will constitute an export tax unlikely to generate significant revenues and may potentially harm U.S. efforts to narrow the trade deficit, according to <strong>Oxford Economics</strong>. The economic consulting firm characterizes the fee recently accepted by chip makers <strong>Nvidia</strong> and <strong>AMD</strong> as a "surprising move." "Although the parties involved appear to deliberately avoid characterizing this arrangement as an export tax, it is one," the firm stated in its August 26 report. "Furthermore, <strong>U.S. Treasury Secretary Bessent</strong> has also raised the idea of expanding export taxes to other industries." <strong>Oxford Economics</strong> estimates that the tax on U.S. semiconductor exports to <strong>China</strong> could generate a maximum of approximately $1.5 billion in revenue—an insignificant amount in the context of the <strong>U.S.</strong> budget deficit exceeding $2 trillion—according to lead economist <strong>Adam Slater</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>NVIDIA Corporation</strong>, based in <strong>Santa Clara, California</strong>, is the world's largest designer of <strong>GPU</strong> and <strong>AI</strong> chips—with <strong>Jensen Huang</strong> as Founder, President &amp; CEO—commanding a market capitalization exceeding $3.5 trillion. <strong>NVIDIA</strong> produces flagship <strong>AI</strong> accelerator chips including <strong>H100</strong>, <strong>H200</strong>, <strong>B100</strong>, <strong>B200</strong>, and <strong>GB200</strong>, while manufacturing restricted variants for <strong>China</strong> such as <strong>H20</strong>, <strong>L20</strong>, and <strong>L2</strong>. <strong>Advanced Micro Devices Inc.</strong> (<strong>AMD</strong>), also based in <strong>Santa Clara, California</strong>, manufactures <strong>x86 CPUs</strong>, <strong>GPUs</strong>, and <strong>AI</strong> accelerators—with <strong>Lisa Su</strong> serving as Chair &amp; CEO. <strong>AMD</strong>'s principal <strong>AI</strong> accelerators include the <strong>MI300X</strong>, <strong>MI325X</strong>, <strong>MI350</strong>, and <strong>MI400</strong>. <strong>Scott Bessent</strong> serves as <strong>U.S. Treasury Secretary</strong>, assuming office during the <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> administration, and is founder of <strong>Key Square Group</strong>. <strong>Oxford Economics</strong>, based in <strong>Oxford, England</strong>, is a global economic forecasting and consulting firm with <strong>Adam Slater</strong> as Lead Economist. <strong>Adrian Cooper</strong> serves as <strong>Oxford Economics</strong> CEO.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the global semiconductor industry reached a market size of $627 billion in 2024 and is expected to reach $1 trillion by 2030. <strong>TSMC</strong> (<strong>Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company</strong>, based in <strong>Hsinchu, Taiwan</strong>) is the world's largest foundry—with <strong>C.C. Wei</strong> as Chairman &amp; CEO—commanding over 60% of the global foundry share. Major foundries also include <strong>Samsung Foundry</strong>, <strong>Intel Foundry</strong>, <strong>GlobalFoundries</strong>, <strong>UMC</strong>, <strong>SMIC</strong> (<strong>China</strong>), <strong>Hua Hong</strong>, <strong>PSMC</strong>, <strong>VIS</strong>, <strong>Tower Semiconductor</strong> (<strong>Intel</strong>), <strong>X-Fab</strong>, and others. <strong>ASML</strong>, based in <strong>Veldhoven, Netherlands</strong>, is the sole global provider of <strong>EUV</strong> (<strong>Extreme Ultraviolet</strong>) lithography equipment—with <strong>Christophe Fouquet</strong> as President &amp; CEO. Major semiconductor manufacturing equipment suppliers include <strong>Applied Materials</strong>, <strong>Lam Research</strong>, <strong>KLA Corporation</strong>, <strong>Tokyo Electron</strong>, <strong>Screen Holdings</strong>, <strong>Hitachi High-Tech</strong>, <strong>Canon</strong>, and <strong>Nikon</strong>. Leading <strong>EDA</strong> (<strong>Electronic Design Automation</strong>) software providers include <strong>Synopsys</strong>, <strong>Cadence Design Systems</strong>, and <strong>Siemens EDA</strong> (<strong>Mentor Graphics</strong>).</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>U.S.-China</strong> technology export controls are administered by the <strong>BIS</strong> (<strong>Bureau of Industry and Security</strong>) within the <strong>Commerce Department</strong>, which oversees semiconductor export licensing. Primary <strong>U.S.</strong> export control tools include the <strong>Entity List</strong>, the <strong>SDN List</strong> (<strong>Specially Designated Nationals</strong>), and the <strong>Foreign Direct Product Rule</strong> (<strong>FDPR</strong>). Major <strong>Chinese</strong> companies on the <strong>Entity List</strong> include <strong>Huawei</strong>, <strong>SMIC</strong>, <strong>YMTC</strong>, <strong>CXMT</strong>, <strong>HiSilicon</strong>, <strong>Phytium</strong>, <strong>Sugon</strong>, <strong>Inspur</strong>, <strong>Megvii</strong>, <strong>SenseTime</strong>, <strong>iFlytek</strong>, <strong>DJI</strong>, <strong>Hikvision</strong>, and <strong>Dahua</strong>. Key Biden-era semiconductor export control measures include the <strong>October 2022 export controls</strong>, <strong>October 2023 update</strong>, and <strong>December 2024 expansion</strong>. Under <strong>Trump 2.0</strong>, sales of <strong>NVIDIA H20</strong> and <strong>AMD MI308</strong> chips to <strong>China</strong> are being permitted—but in exchange for the 15% export revenue cut. The <strong>CHIPS and Science Act</strong>, signed by <strong>Biden</strong> in 2022, provided $280 billion in semiconductor manufacturing and <strong>R&amp;D</strong> incentives. Ultimately, the <strong>NVIDIA-AMD</strong> 15% export revenue cut arrangement represents an unprecedented mechanism in <strong>U.S.</strong> trade policy—functioning more as a geopolitical maneuver than a meaningful financial contribution, according to <strong>Oxford Economics</strong>' analysis.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Nvidia</strong> and <strong>AMD</strong> are accepting a 15% government cut on chip sales to <strong>China</strong>.<br>
2. <strong>Oxford Economics</strong> emphasizes that the arrangement constitutes an export tax.<br>
3. Expected annual revenue is a maximum of $1.5 billion.<br>
4. The <strong>U.S.</strong> budget deficit exceeds $2 trillion.<br>
5. <strong>Treasury Secretary Bessent</strong> has proposed extending export taxes to other industries.</p>
