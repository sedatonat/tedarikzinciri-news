<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Apparel</strong> sector-generated global greenhouse gas emissions grew 7.5% in 2023 — driven by increased production fueled by mounting dependence on carbon-intensive polyester and rising demand from the fast-fashion industry. "It is encouraging to see progress across the value chain — with meaningful case studies from brands and suppliers — but this data is a sharp reminder of how much further we still need to go as an industry," said <strong>Lewis Perkins</strong>, president of environmental nonprofit <strong>Apparel Impact Institute</strong> (<strong>Aii</strong>). According to a report released by <strong>Aii</strong> on July 23, the apparel sector accounted for 2% of total global emissions in 2023 — leaving the industry substantially behind its target to halve its carbon footprint by 2030. <strong>Aii</strong> found that polyester accounts for 57% of total global fiber production — by far the largest of any material. By comparison, cotton is the second most commonly used fiber at 20% — followed by man-made cellulosic fibers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply-chain perspective, <strong>Apparel Impact Institute</strong> (<strong>Aii</strong>) is a global nonprofit organization established to reduce the environmental impact of the apparel and footwear industries — <strong>Lewis Perkins</strong> serves as President — headquartered in <strong>San Francisco, California</strong> and <strong>Hong Kong</strong>. <strong>Aii</strong>'s core programs include <strong>Climate Solutions Portfolio</strong>, <strong>Clean by Design</strong>, <strong>Mill Improvement Program</strong>, <strong>Carbon Leadership Project</strong>, and <strong>Fashion Climate Fund</strong>. The apparel sector is valued at more than 1.5 trillion dollars annually on a global basis — and generates 2–8% of total emissions (estimates vary by source) — producing emissions across its life cycle: production, shipping, retail, use, and disposal. <strong>Polyester</strong> is a petroleum-derived synthetic fiber — popular due to its low cost, high durability, quick-drying properties, and color retention — but is a major source of microplastic pollution, releasing 700,000+ microfibers with each wash. Major global polyester (<strong>PET</strong>) fiber and resin producers include <strong>Indorama Ventures</strong> (<strong>Bangkok, Thailand</strong>), <strong>Reliance Industries</strong> (<strong>Mumbai, India</strong>), <strong>Sinopec</strong>, <strong>Tongkun Group</strong>, <strong>Hengli Group</strong>, <strong>Rongsheng Petrochemical</strong>, <strong>Far Eastern New Century</strong>, <strong>Toray Industries</strong>, <strong>Teijin</strong>, <strong>Mitsubishi Chemical</strong>, and <strong>Eastman Chemical</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply-chain perspective, the global apparel and footwear industry encompasses major brands including <strong>Inditex</strong> (<strong>Zara</strong>, <strong>Pull&amp;Bear</strong>, <strong>Massimo Dutti</strong>, <strong>Bershka</strong>, <strong>Stradivarius</strong>, <strong>Oysho</strong>, <strong>Zara Home</strong>, <strong>Lefties</strong>); <strong>H&amp;M Group</strong> (<strong>H&amp;M</strong>, <strong>COS</strong>, <strong>&amp;Other Stories</strong>, <strong>Monki</strong>, <strong>Weekday</strong>, <strong>ARKET</strong>, <strong>H&amp;M Home</strong>); <strong>Fast Retailing</strong> (<strong>Uniqlo</strong>, <strong>GU</strong>, <strong>Theory</strong>); <strong>Shein</strong>; <strong>Temu</strong> (<strong>PDD Holdings</strong>); <strong>Boohoo Group</strong>; <strong>ASOS</strong>; <strong>Primark</strong>; <strong>Gap Inc.</strong> (<strong>Gap</strong>, <strong>Old Navy</strong>, <strong>Banana Republic</strong>, <strong>Athleta</strong>); <strong>VF Corporation</strong> (<strong>The North Face</strong>, <strong>Vans</strong>, <strong>Timberland</strong>, <strong>Dickies</strong>); <strong>PVH Corp.</strong> (<strong>Tommy Hilfiger</strong>, <strong>Calvin Klein</strong>); <strong>Tapestry Inc.</strong> (<strong>Coach</strong>, <strong>Kate Spade</strong>, <strong>Stuart Weitzman</strong>); <strong>Capri Holdings</strong> (<strong>Michael Kors</strong>, <strong>Versace</strong>, <strong>Jimmy Choo</strong>); <strong>LVMH</strong>; <strong>Kering</strong>; <strong>Richemont</strong>; <strong>Burberry</strong>; and <strong>Hermes</strong>. Major sportswear and footwear brands include <strong>Nike</strong>, <strong>Adidas</strong>, <strong>Puma</strong>, <strong>Under Armour</strong>, <strong>Lululemon</strong>, <strong>New Balance</strong>, <strong>ASICS</strong>, <strong>Skechers</strong>, <strong>Crocs</strong>, <strong>Deckers</strong> (<strong>UGG</strong>, <strong>HOKA</strong>, <strong>Teva</strong>), and <strong>On Holding</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply-chain perspective, apparel-sector sustainability initiatives include <strong>Better Cotton Initiative</strong> (<strong>BCI</strong>), <strong>Textile Exchange</strong>, <strong>ZDHC Foundation</strong> (<strong>Zero Discharge of Hazardous Chemicals</strong>), <strong>Sustainable Apparel Coalition</strong> (<strong>SAC</strong>), <strong>Higg Index</strong>, <strong>Fashion Pact</strong>, <strong>UN Fashion Industry Charter for Climate Action</strong>, <strong>Cascale</strong> (formerly <strong>SAC</strong>), <strong>Global Recycle Standard</strong> (<strong>GRS</strong>), <strong>Recycled Claim Standard</strong> (<strong>RCS</strong>), <strong>Organic Content Standard</strong> (<strong>OCS</strong>), <strong>Global Organic Textile Standard</strong> (<strong>GOTS</strong>), <strong>OEKO-TEX</strong>, <strong>Bluesign</strong>, and <strong>Cradle to Cradle</strong>. Major regulatory frameworks include the <strong>EU Strategy for Sustainable and Circular Textiles</strong>, <strong>EU Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation</strong> (<strong>ESPR</strong>), <strong>EU Digital Product Passport</strong> (<strong>DPP</strong>), <strong>EU Extended Producer Responsibility</strong> (<strong>EPR</strong>), <strong>France AGEC Law</strong>, and <strong>California AB 405</strong> (<strong>Responsible Textile Recovery Act</strong>). Key sustainable textile materials include <strong>recycled polyester</strong> (<strong>rPET</strong>), <strong>biobased polyester</strong>, <strong>chemical recycling</strong>, <strong>mechanical recycling</strong>, <strong>regenerative cotton</strong>, <strong>hemp</strong>, <strong>linen</strong>, <strong>Tencel/lyocell</strong>, <strong>Modal</strong>, <strong>mushroom leather</strong> (<strong>Mylo</strong>), and <strong>cactus leather</strong> (<strong>Desserto</strong>). Ultimately, the <strong>Aii</strong> report clearly demonstrates that the global apparel industry is falling behind its 2030 emissions targets due to polyester dependence and accelerating fast-fashion demand — requiring urgent structural transformation across the sector.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. Global apparel-sector emissions grew 7.5% in 2023.<br>
2. Polyester accounts for 57% of global fiber production; cotton accounts for 20%.<br>
3. The apparel sector accounts for 2% of total global emissions.<br>
4. <strong>Lewis Perkins</strong> (<strong>Aii</strong>) emphasizes that the industry is behind on its 2030 targets.<br>
5. Fast-fashion demand growth and carbon-intensive polyester are the primary emission drivers.</p>
