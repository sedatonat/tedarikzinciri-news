<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; analyst view; as dust settles on President <strong>Donald Trump</strong>'s stunning announcement of punitive "reciprocal" tariffs on practically every country in the world — including some uninhabited islands — a notable omission emerges from the target list: <strong>Russia</strong>. The U.S. outlet <strong>Axios</strong> reported that White House Press Secretary <strong>Karoline Leavitt</strong> explained on April 2 that the current U.S. sanctions on <strong>Russia</strong> "preclude any meaningful trade" — and for the same reason, <strong>Cuba</strong>, <strong>Belarus</strong>, and <strong>North Korea</strong> are not included. According to <strong>BBC News</strong>, <strong>Russian</strong> media argued on April 3 that the reason their country was not on <strong>Trump</strong>'s reciprocal tariff list was due to sanctions. U.S.-<strong>Russian</strong> trade continues despite the war in <strong>Ukraine</strong> — though major crude oil and gas flows have been severed.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the history of the main U.S.-<strong>Russian</strong> sanctions regime comprises: (1) the <strong>Magnitsky Act</strong> 2012 (human rights); (2) <strong>Crimea Sanctions</strong> 2014 (<strong>Executive Order 13660</strong>); (3) <strong>CAATSA</strong> 2017 (<strong>Countering America's Adversaries Through Sanctions Act</strong>); (4) comprehensive sanctions following the 2022 invasion of <strong>Ukraine</strong> (<strong>SDN List</strong> — <strong>Specially Designated Nationals</strong>); (5) the <strong>G7</strong> oil price cap ($60/barrel); (6) removal of major <strong>Russian</strong> banks from the <strong>SWIFT</strong> financial network; (7) freezing of <strong>Central Bank of Russia</strong> reserves; (8) threats of <strong>secondary sanctions</strong> via the U.S., <strong>China</strong>, <strong>India</strong>, the <strong>UAE</strong>, and <strong>Turkey</strong>. The <strong>Office of Foreign Assets Control</strong> (<strong>OFAC</strong>; <strong>Bradley Smith</strong> Acting Director) and the <strong>U.S. Treasury Department</strong> are the main U.S. sanctions enforcement authorities. The <strong>Bureau of Industry and Security</strong> (<strong>BIS</strong>), the <strong>Entity List</strong>, and the <strong>Foreign Direct Product Rule</strong> are the principal export controls.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the 2025 U.S.-<strong>Russian</strong> trade volume is estimated at $5 billion (down from $35 billion in 2021) — principal import categories include fertilizers (<strong>urea</strong>, <strong>potash</strong>); <strong>uranium</strong> (via <strong>Kazatomprom</strong>); <strong>palladium</strong> (<strong>Norilsk Nickel</strong>); titanium (<strong>VSMPO-AVISMA</strong>); main energy and metals categories. U.S. exports to <strong>Russia</strong> consist mainly of pharmaceuticals and medical devices (under humanitarian exemptions). <strong>Russia</strong>'s primary <strong>shadow fleet</strong> tanker strategy involves circumventing the <strong>G7</strong> oil price cap through tankers flagged under <strong>Liberia</strong>, <strong>Panama</strong>, and <strong>Marshall Islands</strong>; principal price cap evasion methods. <strong>Lloyd's List Intelligence</strong>, <strong>Kpler</strong>, <strong>Vortexa</strong>, <strong>S&P Global Commodity Insights</strong>, and <strong>OPIS</strong> are the main crude oil trading analytics firms. <strong>Ukraine</strong>-U.S. relations and <strong>Trump</strong>'s 2025 mineral deal negotiations (with <strong>Volodymyr Zelensky</strong>) are principal geopolitical developments.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the U.S.'s main import dependencies from <strong>Russia</strong> comprise: (1) enriched <strong>uranium</strong> — approximately 30% of U.S. nuclear reactors (being banned under the 2024 <strong>Russian Uranium Ban</strong> — by end of 2027); (2) <strong>palladium</strong> — automotive catalytic converters; (3) fertilizers — agricultural production; (4) titanium — aerospace (<strong>Boeing</strong>, <strong>Airbus</strong>); principal strategic dependencies. <strong>Westinghouse Electric</strong> (<strong>Brookfield</strong>-owned), <strong>Centrus Energy</strong>, <strong>Cameco</strong> (<strong>Canada</strong>), and <strong>Orano</strong> (<strong>France</strong>; formerly <strong>Areva</strong>) are the main Western uranium enrichment firms. The <strong>U.S. Geological Survey</strong> (<strong>USGS</strong>) and its <strong>Critical Minerals List</strong> are the main U.S. strategic minerals analyses. The <strong>Defense Production Act</strong> (<strong>DPA</strong>) and the <strong>CHIPS and Science Act</strong> are the principal U.S. domestic production push statutes. <strong>Trump</strong>'s decision not to include <strong>Russia</strong> on the tariff list is being interpreted as a potential signal of <strong>Ukraine</strong> ceasefire diplomacy — talks between <strong>Trump</strong> special envoy <strong>Steve Witkoff</strong> and <strong>Vladimir Putin</strong> represent the main diplomatic channel. In conclusion, <strong>Russia</strong>'s absence from the tariff list reflects a fundamental redesign of U.S. geopolitical messaging globally — for supply chain managers, geographic diversification of <strong>Russian</strong> dependencies and <strong>secondary sanctions</strong> compliance appear to be the principal strategic priority.</p>

3. <strong>Cuba</strong>, <strong>Belarus</strong>, and <strong>North Korea</strong> are excluded for the same reason.<br>
