<p style="text-align: justify">Germany-based global container shipping company <strong>Hapag-Lloyd</strong> has taken a significant step in fleet transformation by launching its <strong>first methanol-centered newbuild program</strong>. Under an agreement with Chinese shipyard <strong>CIMC Raffles</strong>, the company has ordered <strong>eight 4,500 TEU container ships</strong>. The vessels, scheduled for delivery in 2028 and 2029, represent a total investment <strong>exceeding 500 million USD</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The newly constructed ships will be equipped with <strong>dual-fuel methanol engines</strong>. These systems deliver <strong>fuel efficiency improvements of up to 30%</strong> compared to existing older tonnage and enable a reduction of <strong>up to 350,000 tonnes of CO₂e emissions annually</strong> through methanol use. The order is regarded as one of the most concrete steps Hapag-Lloyd has taken in line with its carbon reduction targets.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>The Methanol Era Begins</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">This order marks Hapag-Lloyd's <strong>first methanol-focused newbuild programme</strong>. To date, the company has primarily focused on <strong>LNG</strong> in its alternative fuel strategy. Hapag-Lloyd currently operates or holds in its order book <strong>37 LNG-capable vessels</strong>. These ships have the capability to run on <strong>biomethane</strong> as well as LNG.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">With the methanol order, Hapag-Lloyd is diversifying its fuel strategy through a <strong>multi-fuel pathway</strong> approach. This strategy enhances operational flexibility during a period of rapidly changing regulations and fuel availability.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Retrofit and Fuel Supply Initiatives</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">In addition to the newbuild orders, Hapag-Lloyd continues to transform its existing fleet. The company plans to <strong>retrofit five 10,100 TEU vessels</strong> during 2026–2027 to operate on <strong>dual-fuel methanol</strong>. This conversion, alongside the newly built ships, aims to reduce the carbon intensity of the fleet.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">On the fuel supply side, Hapag-Lloyd secured a commitment to procure <strong>250,000 tonnes of green methanol annually</strong> under an agreement signed with <strong>Goldwind</strong> in late 2024. This methanol is expected to deliver <strong>at least 70% emission reduction</strong> compared to conventional fuels. This agreement stands out as a critical element ensuring that alternative fuel-powered ships remain <strong>operationally sustainable</strong>, not merely technically viable.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Growth in the Sub-5,000 TEU Segment</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">Hapag-Lloyd is not limiting itself to methanol orders alone; it is also strengthening its presence in the <strong>sub-5,000 TEU</strong> segment. Under long-term charter arrangements, the company plans to add:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>1,800 TEU (4 ships)</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>3,500 TEU (6 ships)</strong></p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>4,500 TEU (4 ships)</strong></p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">a total of <strong>14 new vessels</strong> to its fleet. Delivery of these ships is expected between 2027 and 2029. This brings Hapag-Lloyd's total investment in this segment size to <strong>22 vessels</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">This strategy will enable the company to operate with younger, more efficient, and lower-emission tonnage, particularly in <strong>regional and feeder trades</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Strategy 2030 and Cost Dimension</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">Hapag-Lloyd CEO <strong>Rolf Habben Jansen</strong> emphasized in his remarks on the order that fleet renewal plays a central role within the company's <strong>Strategy 2030</strong>. According to Jansen, the new ships enable:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">gradual <strong>replacement</strong> of older tonnage,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">acceleration of the fleet's <strong>decarbonisation</strong> process,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">reduction of <strong>charter market</strong> dependence,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">achievement of long-term <strong>operating cost efficiency</strong></p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The next-generation vessels are expected to create competitive advantage not only environmentally but also <strong>economically</strong> through their superior fuel efficiency. Given that fuel costs and carbon pricing will become increasingly significant in the years ahead, this investment is viewed as critical for Hapag-Lloyd's risk management perspective.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Overall Assessment</strong></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify">Hapag-Lloyd's order for eight methanol-fueled ships is a strong indicator that <strong>methanol is becoming one of the primary alternative fuels in global container shipping</strong>. The multi-fuel strategy employing LNG, biomethane, and methanol together aims to balance both regulatory compliance and the company's long-term cost structure. When these vessels enter service after 2028, Hapag-Lloyd's fleet profile will visibly rejuvenate and its carbon intensity will decline.</p>
<p><br></p>
