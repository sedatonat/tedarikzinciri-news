<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Trump</strong> administration is moving to roll out a reinsurance program for vessels transiting the <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong>. From a supply chain perspective, this move establishes a government-backed backstop at a time when <strong>P&amp;I clubs</strong>, <strong>Lloyd's</strong>, and <strong>IUMI</strong>-member reinsurers are withdrawing capacity from the <strong>marine war risk</strong> market. The sustainability of <strong>K&amp;R</strong>, <strong>H&amp;M</strong>, and war risk insurance premiums for <strong>VLCCs</strong>, <strong>Suezmax</strong> vessels, <strong>LNG carriers</strong>, and <strong>LPG</strong> tankers emerges as a critical condition for keeping <strong>Asia-Middle East</strong> crude supply corridors open. The program structure distributes total risk that reinsurers struggle to underwrite alone through a centralized public channel, creating conditions for <strong>WS</strong> tariffs stuck in the <strong>charter</strong> market to ease.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to <strong>CNBC</strong>, the <strong>U.S. International Development Finance Corporation (DFC)</strong> will cover up to $20 billion in losses on a rolling basis. The <strong>White House</strong> is focused on ensuring commercial shipping continues to flow through the critical transit point despite elevated risks stemming from ongoing conflict with <strong>Iran</strong>. <strong>President Donald Trump</strong> had previously pledged to offer insurance guarantees and potential <strong>U.S. Navy</strong> escorts for oil tankers transiting the <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong>. The proposed reinsurance program essentially provides a floor for private insurers and helps stabilize premiums at a time when shipping lines are concerned about transits. From a supply chain perspective, this structure enables <strong>BCOs</strong> on the charterer side and major refineries to reconsider <strong>spot</strong> purchasing decisions.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>DFC</strong> <strong>chief executive officer</strong> <strong>Ben Black</strong> stated in a March 6 press release: "<strong>We are confident that our reinsurance plan will get oil, gasoline, LNG, jet fuel, and fertilizer through the Strait of Hormuz and flowing again to the world</strong>." Approximately 20% of global crude oil exports transit this strait, and roughly 20% of liquified natural gas (<strong>LNG</strong>) exports use the same passage. London-based reinsurers have begun canceling war risk coverage following threats by the <strong>Iranian Revolutionary Guard</strong> to burn ships passing through the <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong>, and are demanding premiums exceeding 200% to reinstate policies. From a supply chain perspective, this squeeze complicates trade in <strong>FOB</strong> delivery terms for major supply actors like <strong>QatarEnergy</strong>, <strong>ADNOC</strong>, and <strong>Saudi Aramco</strong>, and has prompted a shift toward <strong>CIF</strong>-based contracts.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>U.S.</strong> crude oil prices have risen 35% due to stalled tanker traffic in the region, and <strong>Gulf</strong> nations are beginning to cut production as they cannot export crude through the <strong>Strait of Hormuz</strong>. Concerns persist about the open-ended nature of the conflict in <strong>Iran</strong>; the war enters its second week on March 9, with no clear signs of imminent de-escalation. Most recently, more than 30 people were injured in an <strong>Iranian</strong> attack near an oil refinery in <strong>Bahrain</strong>, and <strong>Israel</strong> launched night strikes on <strong>Tehran</strong>, <strong>Iran's</strong> capital, between March 8 and 9. From a supply chain perspective, this situation is redirecting <strong>Asian</strong> refinery alternative supply flows toward the <strong>Atlantic Basin</strong>, <strong>U.S. Gulf</strong>, and <strong>West Africa</strong>, and could permanently increase <strong>tanker tonne-mile</strong> demand. In essence, the <strong>DFC</strong>'s reinsurance model represents a case where government capacity is filling a private market gap in the global energy supply chain.</p>
