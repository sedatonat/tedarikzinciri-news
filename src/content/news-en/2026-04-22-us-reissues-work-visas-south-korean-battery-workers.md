<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>U.S. State Department</strong> is reissuing work visas for some <strong>South Korean</strong> workers who were removed from a <strong>Hyundai Motor Co.</strong>-<strong>LG Energy Solution Ltd.</strong> battery facility during an immigration raid in early September. Some are returning to work in the <strong>U.S.</strong> — according to <strong>The New York Times</strong>. In a move aimed at reversing the political turmoil created by the raid, approximately 180 people holding <strong>B-1</strong> business visas in the <strong>U.S.</strong> are having their visas returned — said <strong>Kim Min-su</strong>, one of those detained, to <strong>Times</strong>. At least 30 of these workers are returning to the battery facility — <strong>Kim</strong> said — and former detainees are preparing a <strong>class-action</strong> lawsuit against <strong>U.S.</strong> immigration authorities, claiming their detention was illegal because they held valid work visas. They are also claiming that the detention centers where they were held were "unhealthy." From a supply chain perspective, <strong>HL-GA Battery Company</strong> is a joint venture between <strong>Hyundai Motor Group</strong> and <strong>LG Energy Solution</strong> in <strong>Bryan County, Georgia</strong> — planned to produce <strong>NCM</strong> (<strong>Nickel Cobalt Manganese</strong>) cells with an annual capacity of 30 GWh.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>South Korea</strong>'s Foreign Ministry said in a statement that <strong>U.S.</strong> officials have been acting "swiftly and flexibly" to help advance a diplomatic resolution to the crisis. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>Hyundai</strong> is building an EV production facility called "Metaplant America" in <strong>Bryan County, Georgia</strong> with annual capacity for 300,000 vehicles — a $7.6 billion investment. The <strong>HL-GA</strong> battery facility is located next to <strong>Metaplant</strong> — representing one of the largest <strong>South Korean</strong> direct foreign investments with combined funding exceeding $9 billion. <strong>Hyundai Motor Company</strong>, based in <strong>Seoul, South Korea</strong>, was founded in 1967 by <strong>Chung Ju-yung</strong>. <strong>Hyundai Motor Group</strong> — with brands <strong>Hyundai</strong>, <strong>Kia</strong>, and <strong>Genesis</strong> — is globally the third-largest automotive group. <strong>LG Energy Solution</strong>, a subsidiary of <strong>LG Group</strong>, is the third-largest lithium-ion battery manufacturer globally after <strong>CATL</strong> and <strong>BYD</strong>. Primary <strong>LG Energy</strong> facilities in the <strong>U.S.</strong> include <strong>Holland, Michigan</strong>; <strong>Lansing, Michigan</strong>; <strong>Lordstown, Ohio</strong> (<strong>Ultium Cells</strong>; <strong>GM</strong> JV); <strong>Spring Hill, Tennessee</strong> (<strong>Ultium Cells</strong>); <strong>Queen Creek, Arizona</strong>; and <strong>Bryan County, Georgia</strong> (<strong>Hyundai</strong> JV).</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, global EV battery production is dominated by: <strong>CATL</strong> (<strong>China</strong>); <strong>BYD</strong> (<strong>China</strong>); <strong>LG Energy Solution</strong> (<strong>South Korea</strong>); <strong>Panasonic Energy</strong> (<strong>Japan</strong>); <strong>SK On</strong> (<strong>South Korea</strong>); <strong>Samsung SDI</strong> (<strong>South Korea</strong>); <strong>CALB</strong> (<strong>China</strong>); <strong>Gotion High-Tech</strong> (<strong>China</strong>); <strong>EVE Energy</strong> (<strong>China</strong>); <strong>Sunwoda</strong> (<strong>China</strong>); <strong>Northvolt</strong> (<strong>Sweden</strong>; bankruptcy 2024); <strong>ACC</strong> (<strong>Stellantis</strong>; <strong>Mercedes-Benz</strong>; <strong>TotalEnergies</strong>); <strong>Verkor</strong> (<strong>France</strong>); <strong>Italvolt</strong>; and <strong>Britishvolt</strong> (closing). The <strong>U.S. Inflation Reduction Act</strong> (<strong>IRA</strong>; 2022) — specifically <strong>30D</strong> (<strong>EV tax credit</strong>) and <strong>45X</strong> (<strong>Advanced Manufacturing Production Credit</strong>) — are the primary incentives attracting battery and EV manufacturing to the <strong>U.S.</strong> <strong>Trump 2.0</strong> is partially rolling back the <strong>IRA</strong> but protecting EV/battery investments. <strong>Battery cell</strong>, <strong>module</strong>, <strong>pack</strong>, <strong>BMS</strong> (<strong>Battery Management System</strong>), <strong>cathode</strong>, <strong>anode</strong>, <strong>separator</strong>, and <strong>electrolyte</strong> are the main components of the battery value chain.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>U.S.</strong> foreign worker visa policy includes these main categories: <strong>H-1B</strong> (specialty occupation); <strong>L-1</strong> (intra-company transfer); <strong>E-1</strong> (treaty trader); <strong>E-2</strong> (treaty investor); <strong>O-1</strong> (extraordinary ability); <strong>B-1</strong> (business visitor; normally does not permit work); <strong>TN</strong> (<strong>USMCA</strong>); <strong>EB-1</strong>/<strong>EB-2</strong>/<strong>EB-3</strong> (employment-based green card); and <strong>EB-5</strong> (investor). The <strong>B-1 visa</strong> technically does not permit work, but is used for "business visit" activities including meetings, training, consulting, and installation supervision. <strong>U.S. ICE</strong> (<strong>Immigration and Customs Enforcement</strong>) and <strong>HSI</strong> (<strong>Homeland Security Investigations</strong>) have begun intensified workplace raids under <strong>Trump 2.0</strong>. Besides <strong>Hyundai</strong>, other <strong>South Korean</strong> companies with major <strong>U.S.</strong> investments include <strong>Samsung</strong>, <strong>SK Group</strong>, <strong>LG Group</strong>, <strong>POSCO</strong>, <strong>Lotte</strong>, <strong>HL Mando</strong>, and <strong>Hanwha</strong>. The <strong>Lee Jae-myung</strong> government is engaging in diplomatic negotiations with <strong>Trump</strong>. <strong>Anseong</strong>, <strong>Cheonan</strong>, and <strong>Pyeongtaek</strong> are <strong>South Korea</strong>'s primary battery production hubs. In conclusion, the reissuance of <strong>B-1</strong> visas clearly demonstrates that <strong>U.S.-Korea</strong> diplomatic relations are being pragmatically repaired.</p>
