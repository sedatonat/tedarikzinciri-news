<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; <strong>BBC News</strong> report; analyst insight; <strong>DHL Express</strong> is suspending deliveries worth more than $800 to the U.S. due to a "significant increase" in customs clearance stemming from <strong>Donald Trump</strong>'s new tariff regime. <strong>BBC News</strong> reports that previously packages worth up to $2,500 could enter the U.S. with minimal paperwork — but the threshold is being lowered to $800. The German delivery giant said it will temporarily halt shipments to American consumers from companies in all countries starting April 21 "until further notice." Business-to-business shipments will continue, "though they may also experience delays," the company said. <strong>DHL</strong> said the changes in customs requirements are causing "an increase in formal customs clearances that we handle around the clock." The company said it will continue to deliver packages worth less than $800 with minimal checks thanks to the <strong>de minimis</strong> rule. However, <strong>Shein</strong> and <strong>Temu</strong> are warning of approaching "price adjustments" for the U.S. market.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>DHL Group</strong>, headquartered in <strong>Bonn, Germany</strong>, led by CEO <strong>Tobias Meyer</strong>, founded in 1969 (formerly <strong>Deutsche Post</strong>), provides global express shipping, international mail, supply chain and contract logistics services — with <strong>DHL Express</strong>, <strong>DHL Global Forwarding</strong>, <strong>DHL Supply Chain</strong>, and <strong>DHL eCommerce</strong> being its main service lines. <strong>DHL Express</strong>'s main competitors in the international express market are <strong>FedEx Express</strong> (CEO <strong>Raj Subramaniam</strong>; <strong>Memphis</strong>), <strong>UPS</strong> (CEO <strong>Carol Tomé</strong>; <strong>Atlanta</strong>), <strong>TNT Express</strong> (<strong>FedEx</strong>-owned; <strong>Europe</strong>), <strong>Aramex</strong> (CEO <strong>Othman Aljeda</strong>; <strong>Dubai</strong>), <strong>SF Express</strong> (<strong>Wang Wei</strong>; <strong>Shenzhen</strong>, <strong>China</strong>), <strong>Cainiao</strong> (<strong>Alibaba</strong>-owned), and <strong>JD Logistics</strong>. <strong>Royal Mail International</strong>, <strong>La Poste International</strong>, <strong>Australia Post International</strong>, and <strong>Japan Post International</strong> are major national postal operators — operating under <strong>Universal Postal Union</strong> (<strong>UPU</strong>; Director General <strong>Bishar Hussein</strong>; <strong>Bern, Switzerland</strong>) agreements.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>de minimis</strong> rule (<strong>Section 321</strong> of the <strong>Tariff Act of 1930</strong>) began at $1 in 1938 in the U.S., was raised to $200 in 1990, and to $800 in 2016. <strong>Trump</strong>'s April 2, 2025 "Liberation Day" order eliminates <strong>de minimis</strong> for all shipments originating from <strong>China</strong> — affecting approximately 1.4 billion packages annually. An estimated 60-65 percent of <strong>de minimis</strong> shipments originate from <strong>China</strong> — the cross-border e-commerce sector is the main affected industry. Affected companies include <strong>Shein</strong> (CEO <strong>Sky-Xu Yangtian</strong>; Singapore-headquartered but China-based sourcing), <strong>Temu</strong> (<strong>PDD Holdings</strong>; Co-CEO <strong>Lei Chen</strong>), <strong>AliExpress</strong> (<strong>Alibaba</strong>), <strong>Wish</strong> (<strong>ContextLogic</strong>), and <strong>TikTok Shop</strong> among the major affected companies. Major U.S.-based customs brokers include <strong>Expeditors International</strong> (CEO <strong>Daniel Wall</strong>; <strong>Seattle</strong>), <strong>C.H. Robinson</strong>, <strong>Kuehne+Nagel</strong>, <strong>DSV</strong>, <strong>Livingston International</strong>, <strong>Geodis</strong>, and <strong>Maersk Customs Services</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.S. Customs and Border Protection</strong> (<strong>CBP</strong>; Acting Commissioner <strong>Pete Flores</strong>), <strong>Office of Trade</strong>, and <strong>Office of Field Operations</strong> are the major U.S. federal customs authorities. <strong>Automated Commercial Environment</strong> (<strong>ACE</strong>) and <strong>Automated Broker Interface</strong> (<strong>ABI</strong>) are the main U.S. customs electronic systems. <strong>Harmonized Tariff Schedule</strong> (<strong>HTS</strong>), <strong>Schedule B</strong>, and <strong>Country of Origin</strong> determination are the main classification tools. <strong>Type 11 informal entry</strong> and <strong>Type 86</strong> (<strong>de minimis section 321</strong>) are the main entry types. <strong>UPS</strong> and <strong>FedEx</strong> face similar flow restrictions to the U.S. — <strong>UPS</strong> added surcharges on April 4 for China-origin e-commerce packages; <strong>FedEx</strong> temporarily suspended its <strong>FedEx International Connect Plus</strong> service for ground transportation. The <strong>National Customs Brokers and Forwarders Association of America</strong> (<strong>NCBFAA</strong>; President <strong>Jose D. Gonzalez</strong>) is the major U.S. customs brokerage industry organization. As a result, <strong>DHL</strong>'s decision to temporarily suspend service appears to signal that cross-border e-commerce flows are being fundamentally redesigned globally — customs brokerage capacity, HTS classification accuracy, and alternative service tiers appear to be becoming key strategic priorities for supply chain managers.</p>

2. <strong>Trump</strong>'s new tariff regime is lowering the customs threshold from $2,500 to $800.<br>
3. <strong>De minimis</strong> remains in effect for packages under $800.<br>
4. <strong>Shein</strong> and <strong>Temu</strong> are warning of price adjustments for the U.S. market.<br>
