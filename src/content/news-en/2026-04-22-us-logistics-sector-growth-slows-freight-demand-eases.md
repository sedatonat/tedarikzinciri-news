<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>U.S.</strong> logistics sector is slowing in September — with softer freight demand and a cooling warehouse market pulling the sector's growth index to a six-month low. Each month, a coalition of university business schools and the <strong>Council of Supply Chain Management Professionals</strong> (<strong>CSCMP</strong>) publish the <strong>Logistic Managers' Index</strong> (<strong>LMI</strong>) — measuring the rate of expansion or contraction in key areas of <strong>U.S.</strong> supply chain performance and providing a snapshot of the sector's health and momentum. Any reading above 50 indicates expansion, but downward trending readings — even when above 50 — signal that growth is slowing, offering an early indicator of cooling demand in logistics networks or shifting capacity. According to the <strong>LMI</strong> released on October 9, total logistics activity recorded a reading of 57.4 in September — roughly two points below August's 59.3. This marks the index's lowest reading since March, suggesting that the late-summer recovery in freight and storage activity is beginning to lose momentum — as companies adjust inventories and prepare for a softer peak season. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Logistic Managers' Index</strong> is published monthly through a partnership with <strong>Colorado State University</strong>, <strong>Rutgers Business School</strong>, <strong>Rochester Institute of Technology</strong>, <strong>University of Nevada Reno</strong>, <strong>Florida Atlantic University</strong>, and <strong>CSCMP</strong> — calculated from surveys of hundreds of logistics managers each month. <strong>Zac Rogers</strong>, of <strong>Colorado State University</strong>, is the lead researcher of the <strong>LMI</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the sub-indices of the <strong>LMI</strong> show transportation capacity in the sector declined 2.2 points between August and September, reading 55.1 — indicating that available cargo capacity is expanding, though at a slower pace, as carriers adapt to softer shipment volumes with demand leveling off. Transportation utilization values also dropped nearly five points month-over-month, reading 55.0 — well below September's eight-year average of 65.1. On the storage side, capacity readings rose roughly one point between August and September to 51.6 — while utilization gained more than three points to 65.3 — suggesting strong inventory movement alongside modest warehouse capacity expansion. <strong>Manufacturing PMI</strong> (<strong>ISM</strong>; <strong>Institute for Supply Management</strong>), <strong>Services PMI</strong>, <strong>S&P Global U.S. Manufacturing PMI</strong>, <strong>Cass Freight Index</strong>, <strong>Cass Truckload Linehaul Index</strong>, <strong>DAT Truckload Volume Index</strong>, <strong>FreightWaves SONAR</strong>, <strong>OTVI</strong> (<strong>Outbound Tender Volume Index</strong>), and <strong>OTRI</strong> (<strong>Outbound Tender Reject Index</strong>) are the primary <strong>U.S.</strong> freight and logistics indicators. The <strong>Logistics Managers Index</strong> is unique in providing a simultaneous view of inventories, storage, and transportation — particularly in that regard.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.S.</strong> trucking market's principal industry organizations are <strong>OOIDA</strong> (<strong>Owner-Operator Independent Drivers Association</strong>), <strong>ATA</strong> (<strong>American Trucking Associations</strong>), <strong>NPTC</strong> (<strong>National Private Truck Council</strong>), and <strong>TIA</strong> (<strong>Transportation Intermediaries Association</strong>). The primary federal regulatory authorities are <strong>FMCSA</strong> (<strong>Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration</strong>), <strong>FHWA</strong> (<strong>Federal Highway Administration</strong>), and <strong>STB</strong> (<strong>Surface Transportation Board</strong>). Principal transportation modes include <strong>FTL</strong> (<strong>Full Truckload</strong>), <strong>LTL</strong> (<strong>Less than Truckload</strong>), <strong>parcel</strong>, <strong>intermodal</strong>, <strong>drayage</strong>, and <strong>final mile</strong>. Major <strong>U.S.</strong> truck carriers include <strong>Knight-Swift Transportation</strong>, <strong>J.B. Hunt Transport Services</strong>, <strong>Schneider National</strong>, <strong>Werner Enterprises</strong>, <strong>U.S. Xpress</strong>, <strong>Heartland Express</strong>, <strong>Marten Transport</strong>, <strong>Covenant Logistics</strong>, <strong>Old Dominion Freight Line</strong>, <strong>XPO Logistics</strong>, <strong>Saia</strong>, <strong>ArcBest</strong>, <strong>Estes Express</strong>, <strong>R+L Carriers</strong>, <strong>TForce Freight</strong> (formerly <strong>UPS Freight</strong>), and <strong>Yellow Corp</strong> (which filed for bankruptcy in 2023). Primary parcel carriers are <strong>UPS</strong>, <strong>FedEx</strong>, <strong>USPS</strong>, <strong>Amazon Logistics</strong>, <strong>OnTrac</strong>, and <strong>LaserShip</strong>. Major Class I railroad carriers include <strong>BNSF Railway</strong>, <strong>Union Pacific</strong>, <strong>CSX Transportation</strong>, <strong>Norfolk Southern</strong>, <strong>Canadian National</strong> (<strong>CN</strong>), <strong>Canadian Pacific Kansas City</strong> (<strong>CPKC</strong>), and <strong>Kansas City Southern</strong> (now part of <strong>CPKC</strong>).</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>U.S.</strong> warehouse market includes leading industrial REITs such as <strong>Prologis</strong> (the world's largest logistics real estate investment trust; 1.2B+ sq ft), <strong>Public Storage</strong>, <strong>Extra Space Storage</strong>, <strong>Iron Mountain</strong>, <strong>Americold</strong> (cold chain), <strong>Lineage Logistics</strong> (cold chain), <strong>Duke Realty</strong> (being acquired by <strong>Prologis</strong>), <strong>STAG Industrial</strong>, <strong>EastGroup Properties</strong>, <strong>Rexford Industrial</strong>, and <strong>First Industrial</strong>. Principal types of logistics infrastructure include <strong>3PL</strong> (<strong>Third-Party Logistics</strong>), <strong>4PL</strong> (<strong>Fourth-Party Logistics</strong>), <strong>contract logistics</strong>, <strong>fulfillment center</strong>, <strong>distribution center</strong>, and <strong>cross-dock</strong>. Leading global <strong>3PL</strong> and contract logistics players are <strong>DHL Supply Chain</strong>, <strong>XPO Logistics</strong>, <strong>GXO Logistics</strong> (spun off from <strong>XPO</strong>), <strong>Ryder Supply Chain Solutions</strong>, <strong>NFI Industries</strong>, <strong>Penske Logistics</strong>, <strong>FedEx Logistics</strong>, <strong>UPS Supply Chain Solutions</strong>, <strong>C.H. Robinson</strong> (<strong>Robinson Fresh</strong>), <strong>Expeditors International</strong>, <strong>Kuehne+Nagel</strong>, <strong>DSV</strong> (<strong>DSV Air & Sea</strong>), <strong>DB Schenker</strong> (being acquired by <strong>DSV</strong> in 2025), <strong>Maersk Logistics & Services</strong>, <strong>Geodis</strong>, and <strong>Bolloré Logistics</strong> (being acquired by <strong>CMA CGM</strong>). <strong>Peak season</strong> in the <strong>U.S.</strong> traditionally falls between October and December — encompassing retail shipment peaks during <strong>Black Friday</strong>, <strong>Cyber Monday</strong>, <strong>Christmas</strong>, and <strong>Boxing Day</strong>. Ultimately, the <strong>LMI</strong>'s September reading of 57.4 is concrete evidence that the <strong>U.S.</strong> logistics sector is entering the fourth quarter of 2026 with a soft landing.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. <strong>LMI</strong> reads 57.4 in September — the lowest level since March.<br>
2. Transportation capacity at 55.1 with utilization at 55.0 — below the eight-year average of 65.1.<br>
3. Warehouse utilization rises to 65.3 while capacity signals modest expansion at 51.6.<br>
4. <strong>CSCMP</strong> plus university coalition publishes the <strong>LMI</strong> monthly.<br>
5. <strong>U.S.</strong> logistics is preparing for a softer peak season.</p>
