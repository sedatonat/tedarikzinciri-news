<p style="text-align: justify">What does it take to prove that your product is &ldquo;<strong>Made in USA</strong>&rdquo;? <strong>Bashlin Industries</strong> is a manufacturer of equipment for professional line workers. The company claimed that one of its climbing tools was made in the <strong>U.S.</strong> The <strong>National Advertising Division</strong> (<strong>NAD</strong>), an industry self-regulatory body of <strong>BBB National Programs</strong>, ruled that this was not the case. It emerged that the company could not definitively determine the origin of a particular buckle and screw &mdash; components representing less than 5% of the product&rsquo;s total cost but identified as functionally critical &mdash; which caused the company to fail the <strong>FTC</strong> &ldquo;<strong>all or virtually all</strong>&rdquo; requirement. From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>FTC Made in USA Standard</strong> together with <strong>15 U.S.C. &sect; 45a</strong> requires final processing along with all or virtually all components to be of <strong>U.S.</strong> origin. <strong>Section 45a(a)</strong> classifies false <strong>Made in USA</strong> claims as unfair commercial practices under <strong>Section 5</strong> and provides for civil penalties of up to $50,120 per violation.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">A blog post from <strong>Reed Smith</strong>: &ldquo;<strong>The NAD Tells Bashlin to Buckle Up Its &lsquo;Made in USA&rsquo; Claims</strong>&rdquo;. From a supply chain perspective, <strong>country of origin</strong> rules are administered separately by <strong>CBP</strong> (<strong>U.S. Customs and Border Protection</strong>) for <strong>tariff classification</strong> and <strong>HTSUS</strong> (<strong>Harmonized Tariff Schedule of the United States</strong>); by <strong>FTC</strong> for <strong>Made in USA</strong> claims; and by <strong>USDA</strong> for <strong>COOL</strong> (<strong>Country of Origin Labeling</strong>) requirements. <strong>USMCA</strong> (<strong>United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement</strong>) <strong>rules of origin</strong> mandate compliance with the 62.5% <strong>Regional Value Content</strong> (<strong>RVC</strong>) threshold or the <strong>tariff shift</strong> rule for automotive and other product categories.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>BOM</strong> (<strong>Bill of Materials</strong>) level origin tracking requires <strong>multi-tier supplier mapping</strong>. Sharing of origin data by <strong>Tier 1</strong> suppliers from their <strong>Tier 2</strong> and <strong>Tier 3</strong> sub-suppliers is a fundamental component of <strong>strategic sourcing</strong> together with <strong>supplier risk management</strong> programs. <strong>Sourcemap</strong>, <strong>Sayari</strong>, <strong>Altana AI</strong>, and <strong>Resilinc</strong> are platforms positioned in <strong>supply chain mapping</strong> together with <strong>provenance tracking</strong>. <strong>Blockchain</strong>-based <strong>chain of custody</strong> systems are particularly in demand for compliance with <strong>UFLPA</strong> (<strong>Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act</strong>) together with <strong>EU CSDDD</strong> (<strong>Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive</strong>).</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the <strong>Bashlin</strong> case clearly demonstrates the need to make cautious origin claims. <strong>FTC</strong> enforcement actions are visible in cases such as <strong>Williams-Sonoma</strong> ($1M penalty; 2020), <strong>Lions Not Sheep</strong> ($211K penalty; 2022), and <strong>Chaucer Foods</strong> ($102K penalty; 2024). Private lawsuits under the <strong>Lanham Act</strong> may also be filed by competitors, and <strong>Class Actions</strong> may be initiated by consumers. The podcast is hosted by <strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> editor-in-chief <strong>Bob Bowman</strong>. Although <strong>NAD</strong> decisions are not binding, referral to <strong>FTC</strong> in cases of non-compliance is common. In conclusion, the <strong>Bashlin</strong> ruling stands as concrete evidence that components representing even less than 5% of cost share can invalidate origin claims, and serves as a tangible warning for supply chain managers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Bashlin Industries</strong>&rsquo;s climbing tool origin claim is rejected.<br>
2. Buckle and screw representing less than 5% of cost are sufficient to defeat the claim.<br>
3. <strong>FTC</strong> &ldquo;<strong>all or virtually all</strong>&rdquo; threshold is highly stringent.<br>
4. <strong>NAD</strong> is the self-regulatory body of <strong>BBB National Programs</strong>.<br>
5. <strong>Reed Smith</strong> blog analyzes the case.</p>
