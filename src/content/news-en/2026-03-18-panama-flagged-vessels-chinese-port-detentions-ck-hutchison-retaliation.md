<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Panama-flagged vessels</strong> faced an <strong>unusual spike in detentions</strong> at Chinese ports in early March. According to industry sources, this situation is being regarded as a <strong>coordinated response</strong> to Panama's decision to <strong>revoke CK Hutchison's terminal concessions</strong>. Between <strong>8-12 March</strong>, Chinese authorities detained <strong>28 Panama-flagged ships</strong>, representing <strong>75.7 per cent of all vessel detentions</strong> in the country during that period.</p><p style="text-align: justify">According to data from the <strong>Tokyo Memorandum of Understanding on Port State Control (Tokyo MoU)</strong>, no five-day window since January 2025 had recorded more than 11 Panamanian vessel detentions. In previous periods where Panama accounted for 75 per cent of detentions, the numbers had been far smaller. The five-day total of 28 detentions already exceeds the monthly totals for Panama-flagged vessels in 2025, when the share typically ranged between 28 and 48 per cent.</p><p style="text-align: justify">Industry analysts suggest that the inspection spike is used as a <strong>strategic pressure tool, not a temporary one</strong>. Panama is one of the world's largest flag states; many commercial vessels calling at Chinese ports sail under the Panama flag. A prolonged crisis could adversely affect freight rates, ship routes, and Panama's <strong>flag-state revenues</strong>. Diplomatic contacts between the Panamanian and Chinese governments continue intensively.</p>
