<p style="text-align: justify">The leader of <strong>BNSF Railway</strong> has warned that the proposed deal to combine two of its rivals risks pushing up costs for customers &mdash; a sign of industry opposition to the largest tie-up in U.S. railroad history. <strong>BNSF</strong> chief executive <strong>Katie Farmer</strong> said on January 14 that the industry should question whether it is an &ldquo;accurate premise&rdquo; that <strong>Union Pacific</strong>&rsquo;s proposed $72 billion takeover of <strong>Norfolk Southern</strong> will eventually lead to double-digit growth in shipping volume for the combined company.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Farmer</strong> suggested the combined company would raise prices if that growth fails to materialize, pointing to <strong>Union Pacific</strong>&rsquo;s rising revenue per carload over the last decade even as freight volumes declined. Comments from the leader of the Berkshire Hathaway&ndash;owned railroad highlight emerging unease over the proposed combination. If allowed to proceed, the deal would create the country&rsquo;s first continuous transcontinental railroad by linking <strong>Union Pacific</strong>&rsquo;s Western network with <strong>Norfolk Southern</strong>&rsquo;s East Coast tracks. The transaction is valued at $85 billion on an enterprise basis.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">&ldquo;We knew our competitors would oppose the merger, and we understand why,&rdquo; <strong>Union Pacific</strong> chief executive <strong>Jim Vena</strong> said in a statement. &ldquo;This is a transformational merger that will inject more competition into the railroad industry and force them to enhance their service, reduce their price, or do both.&rdquo; &ldquo;While our opponents appear to be stuck in the past, we are taking a bold step that will reinvigorate the rail industry and make the entire U.S. supply chain stronger,&rdquo; Vena added. &ldquo;We are not content to compete for share of a shrinking railroad industry.&rdquo;</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the U.S. <strong>Class I</strong> rail map is built around <strong>BNSF</strong> and <strong>Union Pacific</strong> in the West, <strong>Norfolk Southern</strong> and <strong>CSX Transportation</strong> in the East, and <strong>Canadian National</strong> together with <strong>Canadian Pacific Kansas City</strong> across the Canada-Mexico spine. Approval by the <strong>Surface Transportation Board</strong> (<strong>STB</strong>) hinges on tests of competition, service quality, safety and transition guarantees. The deal&rsquo;s impact on <strong>intermodal</strong> container traffic, agricultural volumes (<strong>Cargill</strong>, <strong>ADM</strong>), energy flows (<strong>Exxon</strong>, <strong>Phillips 66</strong>) and retail logistics (<strong>Walmart</strong>, <strong>Amazon</strong>) will sit at the center of regulatory scrutiny throughout 2026.</p>
