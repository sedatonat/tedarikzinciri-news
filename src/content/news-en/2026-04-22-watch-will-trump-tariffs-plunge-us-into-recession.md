<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports on an interview between <strong>Connor Lokar</strong>, <strong>Senior Forecaster</strong> at <strong>ITR Economics</strong>, and <strong>Robert J. Bowman</strong> of <strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong>. The analyst discussion weighs the probability that the Trump administration's latest round of tariffs could trigger a recession and examines the likelihood of a Great Depression occurring within the next decade. Can the new Trump tariffs bring about a recession? "Not at this point," Lokar states. He emphasizes the potential damage from an escalating trade war—yet maintains that "we're okay" for now. "I think the economy can still grow despite some tariff pressure," he says. Lokar argues that many sectors of the U.S. economy—including single-family housing, inflation-adjusted capital goods, and new orders—remain on solid footing. However, if the trade war worsens, the possibility of a Great Depression becomes a real concern.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>ITR Economics</strong>, based in <strong>Manchester, New Hampshire, U.S.</strong>, with <strong>Brian Beaulieu</strong> as CEO and <strong>Alan Beaulieu</strong> as President, is an independent economic forecasting firm operating through its flagship publications <strong>Trends Report</strong> and <strong>ITR Insider</strong>. Other major economic forecasting and analysis firms include <strong>Moody's Analytics</strong> (with <strong>Mark Zandi</strong> as Chief Economist), <strong>Oxford Economics</strong> (<strong>Adrian Cooper</strong>, CEO), <strong>S&amp;P Global Market Intelligence</strong>, <strong>The Conference Board</strong> (<strong>Steve Odland</strong>, CEO, publisher of the <strong>Leading Economic Index</strong>), <strong>Wells Fargo Economics</strong>, <strong>Goldman Sachs Economic Research</strong> (led by <strong>Jan Hatzius</strong>), <strong>JPMorgan Economic Research</strong>, <strong>Bank of America Global Research</strong>, <strong>Morgan Stanley Research</strong>, <strong>Deutsche Bank Research</strong>, and <strong>Capital Economics</strong>. Key academic and government sources include the <strong>National Bureau of Economic Research</strong> (<strong>NBER</strong>, which officially dates recessions), the <strong>Federal Reserve Board</strong> (chaired by <strong>Jerome Powell</strong>), the <strong>Bureau of Economic Analysis</strong> (<strong>BEA</strong>, which measures <strong>GDP</strong>), the <strong>Bureau of Labor Statistics</strong> (<strong>BLS</strong>, which tracks <strong>CPI</strong>, <strong>PPI</strong>, and the <strong>jobs report</strong>), and the <strong>Congressional Budget Office</strong> (<strong>CBO</strong>).</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the main U.S. recession indicators include: (1) <strong>yield curve inversion</strong> (10-year to 2-year <strong>Treasury</strong> spread); (2) the <strong>Sahm Rule</strong> (unemployment rising 0.5 percentage points or more); (3) <strong>NBER</strong> dating committee decision (based on <strong>GDP</strong>, employment, income, industrial production, and sales); (4) <strong>ISM Manufacturing PMI</strong> (readings below 50 signal contraction); (5) <strong>ISM Services PMI</strong>; (6) <strong>Conference Board LEI</strong>; (7) consumer confidence indices (<strong>University of Michigan</strong> and <strong>Conference Board</strong>); and (8) housing starts. Key economic impact analyses of Trump's 2025 tariffs come from the <strong>Tax Foundation</strong>, which projects a 0.7% GDP contraction from a 10% universal tariff; the <strong>Yale Budget Lab</strong>, which forecasts a 2.3 percentage point increase in 2025 inflation from 145% tariffs on China plus tariffs on other countries; and the <strong>Peterson Institute for International Economics</strong> (<strong>PIIE</strong>). Major U.S. recession periods include the 1973-75 period (Oil Crisis), 1980, 1981-82 (Volcker disinflation), 1990-91 (Gulf War), 2001 (dot-com bubble), 2007-09 (Great Recession triggered by the <strong>Lehman Brothers</strong> collapse), and 2020 (COVID-19).</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the primary triggers of the 1929-1939 <strong>Great Depression</strong> included: (1) the 1929 stock market crash (<strong>Black Tuesday</strong>); (2) the 1930 <strong>Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act</strong> (sponsored by <strong>Reed Smoot</strong> and <strong>Willis Hawley</strong>); (3) widespread bank failures; (4) the collapse of the global gold standard; and (5) deflationary spirals. While China's current situation differs, a critical point of historical analysis is that the Trump tariff regime threatens to exceed historical <strong>Smoot-Hawley</strong> levels. Key supply chain economic management strategies include: (1) scenario planning (three-way scenarios—base case, optimistic, and pessimistic); (2) <strong>Monte Carlo</strong> simulation; (3) <strong>tariff hedging</strong> (using futures and options); (4) geographic diversification; (5) product portfolio diversification; (6) contract renewal strategies (escalation clauses); and (7) flexible demand planning models. The <strong>Coalition of Service Industries</strong>, <strong>Business Roundtable</strong> (<strong>Joshua Bolten</strong>, CEO), and the <strong>National Association of Manufacturers</strong> (<strong>NAM</strong>, led by <strong>Jay Timmons</strong>) are major U.S. business advocacy groups calling for public opposition to the tariffs. In summary, Lokar's economic forecast suggests that global economic uncertainty dynamics are being fundamentally reshaped—making scenario planning and flexible demand modeling seem like essential strategic priorities for supply chain managers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong><br>
1. <strong>Connor Lokar</strong> of <strong>ITR Economics</strong> states that there is no recession at this time—the economy is growing.<br>
2. A major concern is that the Trump tariff regime threatens to exceed historical <strong>Smoot-Hawley</strong> levels.<br>
3. Single-family housing, capital goods, and new orders remain on solid footing.<br>
4. If the trade war escalates, the possibility of a Great Depression is a genuine concern.<br>
5. The <strong>NBER</strong>, <strong>Federal Reserve</strong>, <strong>BEA</strong>, and <strong>BLS</strong> are the primary U.S. economic data authorities.</p>
