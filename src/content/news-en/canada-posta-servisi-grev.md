<p style="text-align: justify">The <strong>rotating strikes</strong> launched by Canada Post workers following the collapse of collective bargaining negotiations pose a critical risk for shippers as the holiday season approaches. While less disruptive than a full-scale national stoppage, the sporadic short-term work stoppages in specific regions are causing mail and packages to face <strong>unpredictable delays</strong>.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The CUPW (Canadian Union of Postal Workers) called a <strong>national strike</strong> last holiday season, bringing Canada Post operations to a standstill. This year, the union is employing a <strong>rotating strike strategy instead of a complete shutdown</strong>, aiming to maintain pressure while reducing the burden on workers.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The action, which began in mid-October, has so far affected more than 18 locations at different times, including <strong>London and Cobourg</strong> in Ontario. The company states that deliveries in affected regions will resume at the fastest possible pace once the strike ends, but experts believe <strong>the risk of delays will increase</strong> as the holiday rush approaches.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Packages are still moving during strikes — but risk is growing</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">ePost Global VP <strong>Alison Layfield</strong> notes that while there are no significant delays in the Canada Post network currently, this situation could change as the holiday season nears.</p>
<p><br>
Since CUPW does not announce strike timing and duration in advance, shippers find it difficult to prepare.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">However, rotating strikes do not create a complete collapse like a national work stoppage.<br>
CUPW President <strong>Jan Simpson</strong> explains it this way:</p>
<blockquote style="text-align: justify">"Rotating strikes slow down the flow of mail and parcels somewhat, but do not stop them completely."</blockquote>
<blockquote><br></blockquote>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>What should shippers do?</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to experts, companies reliant on the Canada Post network should <strong>strengthen customer communications immediately</strong> and diversify their shipping options.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h6 style="text-align: justify"><strong>1. Strengthen customer communications</strong></h6>
<p style="text-align: justify">eShipper VP <strong>Imtiaz Kermali</strong> says businesses need to clearly communicate potential delays to customers:</p>
<blockquote style="text-align: justify">"Customers want to know what is happening, even if delivery is delayed."<br>
Kermali also notes that offering customers <strong>express or premium shipping options</strong> would be a good alternative.</blockquote>
<blockquote><br></blockquote>
<h6 style="text-align: justify"><strong>2. Turn to alternative carriers</strong></h6>
<p style="text-align: justify">Carriers such as UPS and FedEx are facing increased volume from shippers due to the disruptions at Canada Post.</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>UPS</strong> has <strong>temporarily suspended service refund guarantees</strong> for domestic Canadian shipments due to increased volume.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>FedEx</strong> stated that it has taken necessary measures to manage the increased load but did not provide details.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to both companies, the holiday season already creates high volume pressure. For this reason, companies should <strong>maintain alternative carriers even during turbulent periods</strong>.<br>
As Kermali puts it:</p>
<blockquote style="text-align: justify">"Strike or no strike, the holiday season already creates congestion across all networks — backup options are always necessary."</blockquote>
<blockquote><br></blockquote>
<h6 style="text-align: justify"><strong>3. Local delivery networks in major cities are critical</strong></h6>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>John McClymont</strong> from Operational Innovations warns that CUPW could concentrate rotating strikes especially in <strong>major cities with high volumes</strong>.<br>
For this reason, particularly in:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Toronto</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Montreal</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Vancouver</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Calgary</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Edmonton</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">having strong <strong>local delivery partners</strong> becomes critical.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Risk management during the holiday season</strong></h5>
<p style="text-align: justify">NRF and Hackett Associates data show that retail inventory levels are already strong and most demand is being met early. However, due to the unpredictable nature of rotating strikes:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">high-volume e-commerce orders,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">B2B shipments,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">shipments to rural areas</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">become particularly at-risk.</p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Canada Post still plays a unique role:</p>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>The only operator providing PO Box delivery</strong>,</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Unmatched coverage in rural and remote areas</strong>.</p></li>
</ul>
<p style="text-align: justify">For this reason, <strong>hybrid shipping strategies</strong> are recommended rather than switching entirely to alternative carriers during the strike.</p>
<p><br></p>
<h5 style="text-align: justify"><strong>Key Takeaways:</strong></h5>
<ul>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Canada Post workers are implementing <strong>rotating strikes</strong>; the holiday season increases delay risk.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Strikes are not announced in advance, making planning difficult.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Shippers should <strong>increase customer communication</strong> and <strong>add alternative carriers</strong>.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">UPS and FedEx are adjusting their networks due to volume increases.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Local delivery partners</strong> are critically important in major cities.</p></li>
  <li style="text-align: justify"><p style="text-align: justify">Canada Post cannot be completely bypassed since it remains unmatched for PO Box and rural deliveries.</p></li>
</ul>
