<p style="text-align: justify">The US termination of its <strong>de minimis exemption</strong> in May 2025 is driving a structural break in trans-Pacific air cargo trade. Low-value e-commerce shipments, long concentrated on the <strong>China–US</strong> route, are declining sharply, replaced by high-volume and more regular air cargo imports originating from <strong>Taiwan</strong> and <strong>Vietnam</strong>. Data published by consulting firm <strong>Trade and Transport Group</strong> indicates this directional shift is no longer a temporary fluctuation but a permanent restructuring of trade patterns.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">According to the data, in the first nine months of 2025, US air cargo imports from <strong>Taiwan</strong> surged approximately <strong>91%</strong> year-on-year. During the same period, air cargo volumes originating from <strong>Vietnam</strong> similarly demonstrated robust growth. By contrast, <strong>China–US air freight</strong> traffic is declining noticeably. This pattern demonstrates that changes to US customs policy are directly reshaping logistical flows.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>Trade and Transport Group</strong> Managing Director <strong>Frederic Horst</strong> emphasizes that the transformation is particularly noteworthy from a product composition perspective. According to Horst, the decline in air cargo traffic from China to the US—principally in <strong>computers and parts</strong> and <strong>industrial equipment</strong>—is being more than offset by shipments from <strong>Taiwan</strong> and <strong>Vietnam</strong>. This signals not only a shift at the country level but also changing preferences regarding <strong>manufacturing footprint</strong> and <strong>assembly hub</strong> positioning within supply chains.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">The sharp decline in <strong>de minimis traffic</strong> particularly affects low-value, high-frequency e-commerce shipments. Conversely, a meaningful increase is being observed in <strong>regular air cargo</strong> volumes. This shift suggests air cargo is increasingly becoming the preferred transport mode for <strong>B2B shipments</strong>, semi-finished goods, and high-value industrial products. Air cargo is thus gradually moving away from the e-commerce-heavy profile it gained during the pandemic.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Analysts note that more than just US regulations underpin this shift; production strategies across Asia also play a role. <strong>Taiwan</strong> stands out as a strong manufacturing and export hub for semiconductors, computer components, and advanced technology equipment. <strong>Vietnam</strong> has rapidly become a production and assembly center for multinational corporations under the <strong>China+1 strategy</strong> in recent years. Both countries offer a structure better suited to the US market's needs in terms of both air cargo capacity and product profile.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a sector perspective, this development presents new balancing challenges for air cargo carriers and <strong>air cargo forwarders</strong>. The repositioning of China-originating capacity necessitates updates to <strong>network planning</strong>, <strong>capacity allocation</strong>, and <strong>yield management</strong> strategies. Simultaneously, capacity pressures and upside risks to freight rates on the <strong>Taiwan–US</strong> and <strong>Vietnam–US</strong> routes are emerging.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">Experts concur that this trend should not be viewed merely as a short-term regulatory effect. The removal of the <strong>de minimis exemption</strong> is understood as part of a broader protectionist approach in US trade policy. Within this framework, country-level shifts in air cargo flows are expected to persist into 2026 and beyond.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">In conclusion, the China-centered structure of US air cargo imports is dissolving, replaced by a trade model centered on <strong>Taiwan</strong> and <strong>Vietnam</strong> that is more regular and industrial in character. This transformation emerges as a critical inflection point for both global supply chains and strategic planning in the air cargo sector.</p>
<p><br></p>
