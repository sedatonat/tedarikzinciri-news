<p style="text-align: justify"><strong>SupplyChainBrain</strong> reports; analyst insight; <strong>National Air Traffic Controllers Association</strong> (<strong>NATCA</strong>); analyzing the fallout from February 14 firings &mdash; affecting hundreds of <strong>FAA</strong> probationary workers. This occurs just weeks after the worst U.S. civil aviation disaster in decades. In <strong>NATCA</strong>'s statement, it says it is "analyzing the effect of reported federal employee terminations on aviation safety, the national airspace system, and our members." Federal workforce reductions focused on <strong>DOGE</strong> (<strong>Department of Government Efficiency</strong>) by the <strong>Trump</strong> administration are affecting <strong>FAA</strong> probationary workers in mid-February. The <strong>National Transportation Safety Board</strong> (<strong>NTSB</strong>); <strong>Federal Aviation Administration</strong> (<strong>FAA</strong>); are the main U.S. aviation safety authorities.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, <strong>NATCA</strong>; <strong>Nick Daniels</strong> President; based in <strong>Washington DC</strong>; founded 1987; with ~20,000 members; is the leading union representing U.S. <strong>FAA</strong> air traffic controllers. The <strong>FAA</strong>; <strong>Department of Transportation</strong> (<strong>DOT</strong>; <strong>Sean Duffy</strong> Secretary); <strong>Bryan Bedford</strong> nominee Administrator (Trump 2.0); based in <strong>Washington DC</strong>; founded 1958; is the main U.S. civil aviation authority. <strong>FAA</strong> primary responsibilities are: (1) air traffic control (~14,000 controllers; 313 facilities); (2) airline certification; (3) aircraft certification; (4) pilot licensing; (5) airport oversight; the main responsibilities. The main <strong>en route</strong> traffic control centers (<strong>ARTCC</strong>) in the U.S. are; <strong>Atlanta Center</strong>; <strong>Chicago Center</strong>; <strong>Cleveland Center</strong>; <strong>Denver Center</strong>; <strong>Dallas Center</strong>; <strong>Houston Center</strong>; <strong>Indianapolis Center</strong>; <strong>Jacksonville Center</strong>; <strong>Kansas City Center</strong>; <strong>Los Angeles Center</strong>; <strong>Memphis Center</strong>; <strong>Miami Center</strong>; <strong>Minneapolis Center</strong>; <strong>New York Center</strong>; <strong>Oakland Center</strong>; <strong>Salt Lake Center</strong>; <strong>Seattle Center</strong>; <strong>Washington Center</strong>; the main 22 U.S. <strong>ARTCC</strong>s. The <strong>FAA Academy</strong>; based in <strong>Oklahoma City</strong>; is the main U.S. air traffic controller training center &mdash; a typical controller undergoes 2-3 years of full training.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the January 29, 2025 <strong>DCA</strong> (<strong>Reagan National Airport</strong>) mid-air collision; between an <strong>American Airlines</strong> Bombardier CRJ-700 and a <strong>U.S. Army</strong> Sikorsky UH-60 Black Hawk; 67 fatalities &mdash; is the deadliest U.S. civil aviation disaster since 2001. The <strong>NTSB</strong>; <strong>Jennifer Homendy</strong> Chair; is the main federal accident investigation authority. Other recent U.S. accidents include; January 31 <strong>Philadelphia</strong> Learjet crash (7 fatalities); February 6 <strong>Alaska</strong> Bering Air crash (10 fatalities); the main recent accidents. <strong>DOGE</strong> (led by <strong>Elon Musk</strong> and <strong>Vivek Ramaswamy</strong> &mdash; Ramaswamy departed January 2025); is the <strong>Trump</strong> administration's federal efficiency initiative. The <strong>Office of Personnel Management</strong> (<strong>OPM</strong>; <strong>Charles Ezell</strong> Acting Director); is the main coordinator of U.S. federal workforce reductions, including the <strong>FAA</strong>. <strong>FAA</strong> probationary workers; typically controllers and technical personnel within their first 1-2 years &mdash; account for ~5-10% of all <strong>FAA</strong> personnel. The <strong>FAA</strong>'s controller shortage following the 1981 <strong>PATCO</strong> strike &mdash; when <strong>Reagan</strong> fired 11,000 striking controllers &mdash; has had decades-long impacts on U.S. aviation.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p style="text-align: justify">From a supply chain perspective, the main civil aviation stakeholders in the U.S. aviation sector include; <strong>Air Line Pilots Association</strong> (<strong>ALPA</strong>; <strong>Jason Ambrosi</strong> President; U.S. pilot union); <strong>Allied Pilots Association</strong> (<strong>APA</strong>; <strong>American Airlines</strong> pilots); <strong>Association of Flight Attendants</strong> (<strong>AFA-CWA</strong>; <strong>Sara Nelson</strong> President); <strong>International Association of Machinists</strong> (<strong>IAM</strong>; airline mechanics); <strong>Professional Aviation Safety Specialists</strong> (<strong>PASS</strong>; <strong>FAA</strong> technical personnel union); the main union organizations. The main U.S. airlines include; <strong>American Airlines</strong> (<strong>Robert Isom</strong> CEO); <strong>Delta Air Lines</strong> (<strong>Ed Bastian</strong> CEO); <strong>United Airlines</strong> (<strong>Scott Kirby</strong> CEO); <strong>Southwest Airlines</strong> (<strong>Bob Jordan</strong> CEO); <strong>Alaska Airlines</strong> (<strong>Ben Minicucci</strong> CEO); <strong>JetBlue</strong>; <strong>Spirit Airlines</strong>; <strong>Frontier Airlines</strong>; the main U.S. passenger airline players. <strong>FedEx</strong>; <strong>UPS Airlines</strong>; <strong>Atlas Air</strong>; <strong>Amazon Air</strong>; are the main U.S. cargo airlines. <strong>Airlines for America</strong> (<strong>A4A</strong>; <strong>Nicholas Calio</strong> President); is the main U.S. airline lobby organization. <strong>Aircraft Owners and Pilots Association</strong> (<strong>AOPA</strong>); <strong>National Business Aviation Association</strong> (<strong>NBAA</strong>); are the main U.S. general aviation and business aviation organizations. In conclusion, <strong>NATCA</strong>'s analysis of <strong>FAA</strong> firings appears to position the U.S. aviation safety regime as fundamentally under scrutiny globally &mdash; for supply chain managers, <strong>FAA</strong> capacity risk and air cargo reliability appear to be main strategic priorities.</p>
